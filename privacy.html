Digital Privacy Guide

Practical defenses against tracking and data leakage

Most tracking is passive and invisible. The best strategy is layered defense: harden your browser, reduce unnecessary scripts, and use network-level filtering.

Want the background and rationale? Read: Why Digital Privacy Is Important.

For a threat-model view, read: Levels of Digital Privacy.

1. Browser Settings

  • Block third-party cookies to reduce cross-site ad profiling.
  • Enable tracking protection or enhanced protection mode.
  • Disable third-party sign-in widgets you do not use.
  • Auto-clear cookies/site data for high-risk sites after closing tabs.
  • Disable notification prompts globally to avoid abusive sites.

2. Extensions

  • uBlock Origin: blocks ads, trackers, malicious domains, and known nuisance scripts.
  • Privacy Badger: learns and blocks behavioral trackers.
  • uMatrix: granular request-control for advanced users.
  • NoScript: script whitelist model for strict control.

Start with uBlock Origin first. Add NoScript/uMatrix only if you can tolerate breakage and manual tuning.

3. Browser Choices

  • Firefox: strong built-in privacy controls and configurable protections.
  • Brave: aggressive default blocking and anti-fingerprinting features.
  • Tor Browser: strongest anonymity posture for sensitive browsing.

Use separate browsers or profiles for different roles: personal, work, and financial accounts.

4. Network Tools

  • Pi-hole: blocks known tracking/ad domains at DNS level across your network.
  • DNS filtering: use providers that block malware, telemetry, and ad domains.
  • Router DNS hardening: enforce DNS-over-HTTPS/TLS where supported.

Network filters reduce background tracking from apps and smart devices that browser extensions cannot touch.

Quick Setup Checklist (30 minutes)

  1. Set browser to block third-party cookies and enable strict tracking protection.
  2. Install uBlock Origin and remove redundant ad-blockers.
  3. Turn on multi-factor authentication for email, banking, and password manager.
  4. Switch DNS to a filtering provider or deploy Pi-hole if you control your network.
  5. Create separate browser profiles for daily browsing vs. sensitive accounts.
  6. Audit extension permissions and remove anything unused.

Operational Habits That Matter

  • Use a password manager and unique passwords for every service.
  • Keep OS, browser, and extensions updated to close known exploit paths.
  • Limit social login use to reduce cross-platform identity linkage.
  • Review account recovery settings to avoid phone/email takeover chains.
  • Treat public Wi-Fi as hostile; avoid sensitive actions unless using a trusted VPN.
