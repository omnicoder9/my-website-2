J00 · The base

Moravec's paradox was an understatement

In 1988 Hans Moravec noticed something that has not stopped being true: it is comparatively easy to make a computer play chess at grandmaster level, and comparatively impossible to give it the perceptual and motor skills of a one-year-old.[1] The explanation he offered was evolutionary. Sensorimotor competence is the product of a billion years of ruthless optimisation, and because it runs beneath introspection, it feels effortless and therefore looks simple. Abstract reasoning is a recent, thin, badly-debugged layer, and because it feels hard, we assumed it was.

Thirty-eight years on, the paradox has not been dissolved. It has been localised. Deep learning did to perception roughly what Moravec would have predicted it eventually would: a modern network labels a cluttered scene far better than a 2005 pipeline, and language models have collapsed the gap between an instruction and a plan. What survives is the part of the problem where the robot's atoms meet the world's atoms. That residue turns out to have three components, and it is worth being precise about them, because they are not three flavours of the same difficulty.

The first is energy. A robot that moves must carry its own metabolism, and the chemistry available to it is roughly forty times worse than the chemistry available to the animal it is imitating. The second is contact. A robot that manipulates must touch objects whose geometry, mass, friction and compliance it does not know, using a mathematical model of contact that is non-smooth, combinatorially explosive, and not reliably differentiable. The third is entropy. A robot that leaves the factory must operate in a world that does not repeat, whose tail of rare events is not bounded, and which therefore cannot be validated by testing.

Two lineages, and the gap between them

Industrial robotics succeeded, spectacularly, by deleting the problem. George Devol's 1961 patent for “programmed article transfer” described a machine that repeats a taught trajectory.[2] The Unimate that followed did not perceive, did not plan, and did not adapt. It succeeded because the factory was rebuilt around it: parts arrive in fixtures, the arm is bolted to the floor, the world holds still, and the machine repeats to a thousandth of an inch for a decade. That is not a robot solving an unstructured problem. That is a structured problem, engineered on purpose, and then solved.

The other lineage starts at SRI with Shakey, which between 1966 and 1972 became the first machine to reason about the consequences of its own actions.[3] Shakey ran its planner on a mainframe over a radio link and could take a substantial fraction of an hour to work out how to push a box across a room. The room in question had been painted to make it legible: large uniform blocks, high-contrast baseboards, controlled lighting. Even the first autonomous robot needed the world to meet it halfway.

Rodney Brooks's response, twenty years later, was to throw away the world model entirely. The subsumption architecture coupled sensing to action through layered behaviours with no central representation, on the grounds that “the world is its own best model.”[4,5] It produced insect-like machines that were robust in exactly the way the deliberative planners were not, and useless in exactly the way they were not. It also produced the Roomba, which remains the most commercially successful autonomous robot ever built, and which works because a floor is nearly as structured as a fixture.

The community has periodically tried to write the gap down. Science Robotics' grand-challenge exercise is the most careful recent attempt, and its list — new materials and fabrication, bio-inspired robots, power and energy, robot swarms, navigation and exploration, AI for robotics, brain–machine interfaces, social interaction, medical robotics, ethics — is notable for how much of it is physical.[6]

Everything interesting lives in the gap between those two lineages: machines that must be as reliable as the arm in the cage while operating in the world that Shakey needed painted. The three walls are what is in that gap.

On the framing

Treating power, dexterity and robustness as three separate hurdles is a useful lie. They are one hurdle with three faces, and every serious attempt to relieve one presses harder on the other two. That coupling is the subject of J04, and it is the real reason progress feels so much slower than the demo reels suggest.

What follows is an engineering account: the physics that sets the limits, the history of the attempts, the numbers from the machines that were actually built and measured, the code you would write, and an honest reading of where the field is. Where a claim has a source, it is cited. Where it does not, it is an estimate and says so.

J01 · Hurdle one

Power density: the machine must carry its own metabolism

Start with an arithmetic problem that no amount of software will solve. A Boston Dynamics Spot carries a 605 Wh battery weighing 4.2 kg, and the manufacturer quotes an average runtime of 90 minutes with no payload.[7] That is an average draw of roughly 400 W to walk a 30–35 kg quadruped around a building at a leisurely pace — about four times the resting metabolic rate of the adult human it is following. Its pack delivers 144 Wh/kg at the pack level. A human doing the same job runs on a sandwich.

The Ragone plane, and the hydrocarbon gap

The right way to see the problem is the plot David Ragone introduced for electric vehicles in 1968: specific energy on one axis, specific power on the other, with lines of constant discharge time running diagonally across it.[8] An energy store is not characterised by one number. A supercapacitor will hand you enormous power and almost no energy; a fuel cell will hand you enormous energy and almost no power. Robots need both, which is precisely the region of the plane where nothing lives.

The best commercial lithium-ion cells — high-nickel NMC and NCA chemistries — deliver 250–300 Wh/kg at the cell level, and materials science has been grinding that number upward at a few percent a year for three decades.[9,10] Packaging, cell balancing, thermal management and structure take a further 30–45% off, which is how 250 Wh/kg cells become a 144 Wh/kg pack on a Spot. Gasoline, by contrast, carries about 46 MJ/kg of lower heating value, which is 12,800 Wh/kg. Body fat carries roughly 37 MJ/kg, or about 10,300 Wh/kg. The raw ratio is a factor of forty to fifty.

That comparison is not quite fair, and it is worth doing properly, because the honest version is still devastating. Chemical fuel has to go through a heat engine or through muscle, and both are bad converters: a small internal-combustion engine delivers 20–30% of the fuel's energy as shaft work, and skeletal muscle delivers about 25% of the metabolic energy as mechanical work. An electric drivetrain, meanwhile, is good: a decent brushless motor and drive will return 75–85% of what the pack gives it. Correcting for conversion, the comparison is:

A factor of twenty-five is not a detail. It is the reason the first genuinely capable rough-terrain quadruped, BigDog, ran on a two-stroke internal combustion engine driving a hydraulic pump, and was consequently as loud as a chainsaw.[11] It is the reason the DARPA-era hydraulic Atlas began its life on a tether, and why Boston Dynamics retired the hydraulic platform in April 2024 in favour of an all-electric machine — trading raw force density for maintainability, quiet, and a plausible product.[12] And it is why every humanoid demonstration video you have ever watched is short.

The chemistry roadmap does not rescue this. Lithium–sulfur has a theoretical specific energy near 2,600 Wh/kg and a practical one, today, of 350–500 Wh/kg with poor cycle life.[13] Solid-state cells promise 400–500 Wh/kg with better safety.[14] Take the optimistic end of both: you double the specific energy, and you double your endurance. You do not close a 25× gap by doubling. The energy wall is not going to be knocked down by a battery announcement. It has to be gone around.

Cost of transport, and the 16× that shamed a generation of humanoids

The clean way to measure how well a machine goes around it is the dimensionless total cost of transport, c_et = P / (m g v): the power you burn, divided by the weight you carry times the speed you carry it at. Vance Tucker popularised the measure across animals and machines in 1975, and it remains the only honest way to compare a beetle, a cyclist and a quadruped.[15,16]

The number that should have changed the field appeared in Science in 2005. Tad McGeer had shown in 1990 that a machine with no motors and no controller at all could walk stably down a shallow ramp, powered only by gravity, with a gait that looked disconcertingly human.[17] Collins, Ruina, Tedrake and Wisse built three powered walkers on that principle — passive dynamics with a small active energy source substituted for the slope — and measured them against the state of the art.[18] The Cornell biped achieved a total cost of transport of 0.20, the same as a walking human. Honda's ASIMO, the most celebrated humanoid in the world, came in at 3.2. A factor of sixteen.

ASIMO was not badly engineered. It was engineered to a different objective. Its gait was generated by zero-moment-point methods that keep the projected ground reaction inside the support polygon at all times, tracked by high-gear-ratio joints under stiff position control.[19] The machine never falls, because it never lets itself fall. But a human walk is a controlled fall: you let the body vault over a stiff stance leg, you let the swing leg pendulum forward for free, and you inject energy once per step at push-off. Refusing to fall means fighting gravity continuously with actuators, and actuators that fight gravity continuously dissipate heat continuously.

The most persuasive demonstration of the alternative is Cornell's Ranger, which in May 2011 walked 65.2 km in 186,076 steps over roughly 31 hours on a single battery charge, untouched by a human, at a total cost of transport of 0.28.[20] Later, after replacing an intuitively tuned nominal trajectory with one found by numerical optimisation, the same machine walked at a cost of transport of 0.19 — on a total power budget of 11.5 W. That is less than a household light bulb, and it is, as far as the published record goes, the most efficient legged machine ever built.

Where the watts actually go

Two measured power breakdowns tell you almost everything about the engineering trade. They come from machines at opposite ends of the speed range, and they disagree completely about what the problem is.

The MIT Cheetah, a 33 kg quadruped trotting at 6 m/s, draws 973 W from its pack for a cost of transport of about 0.5 — genuinely comparable to a running animal of the same mass. Of that draw, 76% is heat lost in the motor windings, and only 24% ever becomes mechanical work at all.[21,22] At speed, a legged robot is a space heater that happens to move.

The Cornell Ranger, walking at its 11.5 W optimum, spends its budget completely differently: 45% on sensors, processors and communications, 34% on motor dissipation, and 21% on positive mechanical work.[20] At walking pace, the dominant load is the computer. Ranger's “computer” was a handful of microcontrollers. Put a modern perception stack on it and the ratio gets worse, not better.

The motor's iron law

Copper loss is where the energy goes, and copper loss has an unforgiving functional form. A brushless motor produces torque proportional to current, τ = K_t · I, and dissipates P_loss = I²R = (τ/K_t)² · R. Loss goes as the square of torque. Holding a heavy arm out horizontally at zero velocity does no mechanical work whatsoever and costs a fortune.

The figure of merit that captures this is the motor constant K_m = K_t / √R: torque per square-root-watt of loss. Everything in actuator design is an attempt to raise it, and there are only two schools.

Gear it down. A reduction of ratio N multiplies output torque by N, which means the motor needs N times less current, which cuts copper loss by N². This is enormously effective and it is what ASIMO, and every industrial arm, and most humanoids do. The price is paid in four currencies: gear friction, backlash, reflected rotor inertia (which scales as N² · J_rotor, and which is what shatters a harmonic drive when the foot hits the ground unexpectedly), and the loss of backdrivability — and backdrivability is the substrate of both impact tolerance and force sensing.

Or make the motor bigger and gear it barely at all. Gill Pratt and Matthew Williamson's series elastic actuator put a deliberate spring between the gearbox and the load, converting a force-control problem into a much easier position-control problem across a known stiffness, and giving shock tolerance for free.[23] Sangbae Kim's group at MIT went further with proprioceptive actuation: large-air-gap motors, single-stage 5.8:1 planetary reductions, and no force sensor at all, because a nearly-backdrivable drive lets you infer contact force from motor current.[24,21] Dan Koditschek's direct-drive family took the idea to its limit.[25] The Mini Cheetah made it cheap.[26] It is the reason a modern quadruped can be kicked and recover, and the reason a 2010 humanoid could not.

Two further levers matter and are routinely forgotten. Regeneration: negative mechanical work — braking a swinging limb — can be pushed back into the pack rather than burnt in a brake resistor, and the MIT Cheetah's drivers do exactly this.[22] Thermal budget: continuous torque is limited by winding temperature, not by magnetics. A motor that can deliver peak torque for two seconds may only sustain a third of it for two hours. Every impressive humanoid clip you have seen is operating in the two-second regime.

Design consequence

Because loss scales as τ² and thermal limits bind on the time integral of that loss, a controller that plans against a torque budget is solving the wrong problem. It should plan against a thermal budget. Very little deployed software does.

Compute is a load on the same battery

Roboticists talk about the energy budget and the compute budget as if they were separate. They are the same budget. Ranger's number — 45% of total power to electronics — is the canary.

Add up a contemporary autonomy stack honestly. A spinning lidar draws 8–20 W. A stereo depth camera draws 3–5 W. An embedded GPU module running perception at 30 Hz sits at 15–60 W depending on how hard you push it. Run a modern vision–language–action policy on top and you are asking that same module for billions of multiply-accumulates per inference at 10 Hz. Call it 60 W for the autonomy stack alone. On a 1 kWh pack — five kilograms of good cells — that is 6% of the total energy per hour, before the machine has moved a millimetre.

For a 30 kg quadruped drawing 400 W, a 60 W autonomy stack is a 15% tax: annoying, survivable. For a 1 kg drone, it is fatal. And here is the perverse part, which we will return to in J04: robustness is bought with compute, compute is bought with watts, watts are bought with battery mass, and battery mass raises the power required to move. The three walls are already talking to each other.

Small is worse, and small is much worse than you expect

Scaling does not help. Consider hover. Momentum theory gives the ideal induced power to hold a vehicle of weight mg in the air with a rotor disc of area A in air of density ρ:[27]

P_ideal = (mg)^1.5 / √(2 ρ A)   ⇒   P/m ∝ √(disc loading)

Specific hover power scales with the square root of disc loading. Shrink a vehicle and its disc loading rises; worse, at small scale the Reynolds number collapses, rotor figure of merit falls from ~0.7 to ~0.4, and motor and drive efficiency falls with it. Then the batteries betray you: cell packaging, terminals, protection circuitry and the minimum viable cell size impose a hard floor on mass that does not scale down with the airframe.

The definitive data point is the Harvard RoboBee. In 2013, Ma and colleagues achieved the first controlled flight of an insect-scale flapping-wing robot — tethered to off-board power and control, because there was no other option.[28] It took six more years to cut the cord. The 2019 RoboBee X-Wing achieved sustained untethered flight at a total system mass of 259 mg, consuming 110–120 mW — a specific power of roughly 425 W/kg, about double what a consumer quadrotor needs.[29] The flight lasted about half a second, and required illumination roughly three times the intensity of sunlight to feed its photovoltaics. It is a magnificent piece of engineering and a brutal statement about the wall.[30]

Why you cannot fix endurance by adding battery

There is a small, exact result here that deserves to be better known, because it kills a whole class of naive proposals. Take a multirotor with a fixed airframe of mass m0 and a fixed rotor disc area, and let f be the battery's share of total mass. Total mass is m0/(1−f). Stored energy scales with battery mass; hover power scales as mass to the 1.5. Endurance is energy over power:

t(f) ∝ f √(1 − f)   ⇒   dt/df = 0 at  f* = 2/3

The optimum battery fraction is two-thirds, and near it the curve is flat: going from 40% battery to 67% battery buys you perhaps 15% more endurance while making the aircraft heavier, slower to accelerate, and more dangerous. Beyond two-thirds, endurance falls. The battery has become its own payload.

In practice real multirotors sit at 28–42% battery fraction, well left of the theoretical optimum, because the assumption that the airframe mass is independent of total mass is false — structure, motors and props all have to grow with the load, and thrust margin has to be preserved for control authority. The optimum shifts left. But the shape of the curve is the point: there is no battery fraction at which the endurance problem goes away.

What actually helps

Since the wall cannot be knocked down, the working strategies are all forms of avoidance.

  • Don't carry the energy. Tethers remain the professional answer wherever the environment is hostile enough to justify them. The robots that were actually useful at Fukushima Daiichi were tethered, teleoperated, and mechanically simple; autonomy was not the binding constraint.[31,32]
  • Don't carry it far. Docking stations and opportunistic charging convert an endurance problem into a scheduling problem, which is a far easier problem. This is how deployed inspection fleets actually work.
  • Don't waste it. Passive dynamics, series elasticity, gravity-compensating springs, and regeneration.[18,23,33] Morphology is free computation and free energy storage; a tendon that stores elastic energy at heel-strike and returns it at push-off is doing work that a motor would otherwise pay for twice.
  • Don't compute what you can precompute. Ranger's four-fold efficiency improvement came from moving the hard work offline: optimise the nominal trajectory numerically, then stabilise it in real time with a low-bandwidth, event-driven reflex controller that barely needs a CPU at all.[20]
  • Take the chemistry win when it comes — but budget it as a multiplier of two, not a solution.

The listing below is the model you actually want on your desk when someone proposes a new platform: it takes the pack, the drivetrain, the compute load and the duty cycle, and tells you how long the thing runs before it has to go home.

power_budget.pyPython 3
"""Energy budget and endurance for a legged or wheeled mobile robot.

Everything here is first-principles arithmetic; the value is in forcing you
to write the numbers down before anyone builds the thing.
"""
from dataclasses import dataclass, field
import math

G = 9.81


@dataclass
class Pack:
    # Cell-level specific energy, Wh/kg. NMC/NCA today: 250-300.
    cell_wh_per_kg: float = 250.0
    # Fraction of pack mass that is actually cells (rest = BMS, case, tabs,
    # interconnect, thermal). Real packs land at 0.55-0.75.
    cell_mass_fraction: float = 0.60
    mass_kg: float = 4.2
    # Usable state-of-charge window. Never run a cell to 0%.
    usable_soc: float = 0.85

    @property
    def pack_wh_per_kg(self) -> float:
        return self.cell_wh_per_kg * self.cell_mass_fraction

    @property
    def usable_wh(self) -> float:
        return self.mass_kg * self.pack_wh_per_kg * self.usable_soc


@dataclass
class Robot:
    mass_kg: float                 # total, pack included
    cruise_mps: float
    cost_of_transport: float       # c_et = P_locomotion / (m g v)
    compute_w: float               # SoC + accelerator, sustained
    sensors_w: float               # lidar + cameras + IMU + radios
    standby_w: float               # BMS, fans, contactors, leakage
    duty: float = 0.7            # fraction of wall-clock actually moving
    pack: Pack = field(default_factory=Pack)

    def locomotion_w(self) -> float:
        return self.cost_of_transport * self.mass_kg * G * self.cruise_mps

    def average_w(self) -> float:
        moving = self.locomotion_w() * self.duty
        return moving + self.compute_w + self.sensors_w + self.standby_w

    def endurance_min(self) -> float:
        return 60.0 * self.pack.usable_wh / self.average_w()

    def range_km(self) -> float:
        return self.cruise_mps * self.duty * self.endurance_min() * 60.0 / 1000.0

    def breakdown(self) -> dict:
        total = self.average_w()
        return {
            "locomotion": self.locomotion_w() * self.duty / total,
            "compute": self.compute_w / total,
            "sensors": self.sensors_w / total,
            "standby": self.standby_w / total,
        }


def optimal_battery_fraction() -> float:
    """Fixed airframe, fixed rotor disc: t(f) is proportional to f*sqrt(1-f).

    d/df [ f (1-f)^0.5 ] = (1-f)^0.5 - f / (2 (1-f)^0.5) = 0
      =>  2(1-f) = f  =>  f* = 2/3.
    The result is scale-free: it does not depend on rho, A, or the cells.
    """
    return 2.0 / 3.0


if __name__ == "__main__":
    # Calibrated loosely against a commercial inspection quadruped.
    spot = Robot(
        mass_kg=32.5, cruise_mps=1.0, cost_of_transport=1.1,
        compute_w=45.0, sensors_w=25.0, standby_w=12.0, duty=0.75,
        pack=Pack(mass_kg=4.2),
    )
    print(f"average draw   {spot.average_w():6.0f} W")
    print(f"endurance      {spot.endurance_min():6.0f} min")
    print(f"range          {spot.range_km():6.1f} km")
    for k, v in spot.breakdown().items():
        print(f"  {k:12s} {100*v:5.1f} %")

    # The lever that is NOT the battery: halve the cost of transport.
    spot.cost_of_transport = 0.55
    print(f"\nhalve c_et  -> {spot.endurance_min():6.0f} min")
    # The lever everyone reaches for first: double the specific energy.
    spot.cost_of_transport = 1.1
    spot.pack.cell_wh_per_kg = 500.0
    print(f"double Wh/kg -> {spot.endurance_min():6.0f} min")

Run it and the lesson is immediate: halving the cost of transport and doubling the specific energy buy you comparable improvements, but only one of them is available today, and it is the one that requires you to redesign the legs.

J02 · Hurdle two

Dexterity: the machine must touch what it does not know

Here is the specification you are bidding against. The human hand has 27 bones and roughly two dozen kinematic degrees of freedom, driven by around 34 muscles, most of which are not in the hand at all but in the forearm, pulling on tendons that route through a sheath. John Napier's 1956 taxonomy — the split between power grips, where the object is clamped against the palm, and precision grips, where it is held between the pads of the digits — is still the vocabulary the field uses.[34] That is the easy half of the specification. The hard half is the sensing.

Seventeen thousand channels and a seventy-millisecond reflex

Johansson and Vallbo's microneurography work put a number on it: the glabrous skin of a single human hand contains on the order of 17,000 mechanoreceptive units, in four classes with different adaptation rates and receptive field sizes — fast-adapting type I (Meissner) for slip and flutter, slowly-adapting type I (Merkel) for form and texture, fast-adapting type II (Pacinian) for vibration out past 400 Hz, and slowly-adapting type II (Ruffini) for skin stretch and grip direction.[35]

The number that matters more is the latency. When an object you are holding begins to slip, the microslip excites tactile afferents, and grip force is upgraded to a new, safer ratio within about 70 milliseconds — roughly half the minimum latency of a voluntary, intended grip-force change.[36] You do not decide to hold on tighter. You have already done it.

Johansson and Flanagan's synthesis explains what that reflex is embedded in: manipulation is organised as a sequence of action phases — reach, load, lift, hold, replace, release — delimited by mechanical contact events. Before each event, the brain predicts what the afferent signal should look like. At the event, it compares prediction with reality. A mismatch triggers a corrective sub-routine.[37] Human manipulation is a predictive control loop with seventeen thousand feedback channels and a reflex arc faster than conscious thought.

Set that against what is actually bolted to the end of most robot arms in the world: a two-finger parallel jaw with one actuated degree of freedom and zero tactile channels. That is not a hand. It is a clamp with an opinion.

The classical program, and the wall it hit

The field did not fail to notice. Ken Salisbury's three-fingered, nine-degree-of-freedom articulated hand at Stanford in 1982 was built explicitly to study force control and grasp kinematics, and it framed the questions correctly.[38] The Utah/MIT dexterous hand of 1986 went further — four fingers, sixteen degrees of freedom, thirty-two pneumatically-driven tendons — and is one of the most beautiful pieces of mechatronics ever produced.[39] It was also essentially unmaintainable, and the tendons ate themselves.

In parallel, the theory got rigorous, and it is genuinely good theory. Form closure means the object cannot move at all without penetrating a finger, purely geometrically. Force closure means the contacts, with friction, can resist any external wrench — and Nguyen showed how to construct such grasps directly.[40] Ferrari and Canny then gave the field its scoring function: map every unit contact force the fingers can apply (respecting the friction cone at each contact) into the six-dimensional space of wrenches, take the convex hull, and measure the radius of the largest ball centred on the origin that fits inside it.[41] That radius, ε, is the magnitude of the worst-case disturbance wrench the grasp can survive. Bicchi and Kumar's survey remains the clean statement of the whole framework.[42]

And then you try to use it, and you discover that every input to that theory is unknown at run time. You need the object's exact surface geometry, its mass and inertia tensor, and the coefficient of friction at each contact. The first you might get from a depth camera, badly, with a self-occluded back face. The second you cannot get at all without picking the thing up. And the third is not a property of the object: friction is a property of the pair, and it changes with dust, moisture, wear, temperature and sliding speed. A grasp with a beautiful ε computed against μ = 0.6 will drop the object at μ = 0.3.

grasp_quality.pyPython 3 · numpy, scipy
"""Ferrari-Canny epsilon: radius of the largest origin-centred ball inside
the grasp wrench space. The whole of classical grasp analysis in 40 lines --
and a demonstration of why the number is worth less than it looks."""
import numpy as np
from scipy.spatial import ConvexHull


def friction_cone(normal, mu, m=8):
    """Discretise the Coulomb cone into m unit edge directions.

    The true cone is continuous; every practical algorithm replaces it with an
    inscribed polyhedron, which makes the analysis conservative and tractable.
    """
    n = normal / np.linalg.norm(normal)
    # any vector not parallel to n gives us a tangent basis
    t0 = np.array([1.0, 0.0, 0.0]) if abs(n[0]) < 0.9 else np.array([0.0, 1.0, 0.0])
    t1 = np.cross(n, t0); t1 /= np.linalg.norm(t1)
    t2 = np.cross(n, t1)
    edges = []
    for k in range(m):
        a = 2 * np.pi * k / m
        e = n + mu * (np.cos(a) * t1 + np.sin(a) * t2)
        edges.append(e / np.linalg.norm(e))
    return np.array(edges)


def grasp_wrench_space(contacts, mu, com, torque_scale=0.05):
    """contacts: list of (position, inward_normal), both in object frame.

    torque_scale is the characteristic object radius used to make force and
    torque commensurate. It is a fudge, it is arbitrary, and it silently
    changes the ranking of candidate grasps. Everyone uses it anyway.
    """
    wrenches = []
    for (p, n) in contacts:
        r = np.asarray(p) - np.asarray(com)
        for f in friction_cone(np.asarray(n), mu):
            tau = np.cross(r, f) / torque_scale
            wrenches.append(np.concatenate([f, tau]))
    return np.array(wrenches)


def epsilon_quality(W):
    """Distance from the origin to the nearest facet of conv(W).

    Returns 0.0 if the origin is outside the hull -- i.e. the grasp is not
    force-closed and there exists a disturbance it cannot resist at all.
    """
    try:
        hull = ConvexHull(W, qhull_options="QJ")
    except Exception:
        return 0.0              # degenerate: hull is lower-dimensional
    # scipy gives facets as A x + b <= 0, with rows of A unit-normalised

    return float(max(dists.min(), 0.0))

Contact is a non-smooth mathematical object

Even granting perfect knowledge of the object, the dynamics of contact are nasty in a way that the dynamics of free motion are not. The governing statement is a complementarity condition: either the gap between two bodies is zero and the normal force may be positive, or the gap is positive and the normal force is exactly zero. Written compactly, 0 ≤ g ⊥ λ ≥ 0. There is no smooth function that does this.

The modern numerical treatment discretises time and solves a linear complementarity problem at each step, an approach due to Stewart and Trinkle and to Anitescu and Potra.[43,44] It works, and it is what every physics engine you have used is doing under the hood. But it comes with three permanent scars, catalogued in Stewart's survey.[45]

  • Non-convexity. Coulomb friction couples the tangential force limit to the normal force. The resulting problem is not convex, and there exist rigid-body configurations with no consistent solution (the Painlevé paradox) and others with infinitely many. The physics is genuinely ill-posed; the engine picks one answer, and you never find out which.
  • Combinatorial explosion. Each active contact is separating, sticking, or sliding. With n contacts there are 3ⁿ modes. Trajectory optimisation through contact is a mixed-integer program wearing a disguise; a hand wrapped around a mug is comfortably in the thousands of modes.
  • Unreliable gradients. The solution is piecewise-smooth at best. Gradients across a mode boundary are meaningless. This is a problem, because gradients are exactly what both trajectory optimisation and policy gradient reinforcement learning need.

This is the deep reason that sim-to-real transfer is harder for manipulation than for locomotion, and it is not a fact about deep learning. In locomotion, contact is mostly incidental: put the foot down, do not slip. In manipulation, contact is the task. The thing you are least able to simulate accurately is the thing you are trying to do.

Cheating with control

Before reaching for better mechanics, there is a control-theoretic escape that is forty years old and still under-used. Neville Hogan's argument is that when a manipulator contacts an environment, neither position control nor force control is the right question. What you should specify is the dynamic relationship between them — the mechanical impedance the arm presents at its tip.[46]

The reason this matters is a two-line calculation. Command a stiff position, and if the world turns out to be 2 mm closer than your depth camera said, you generate a force of K · 0.002. With an industrial arm's effective tip stiffness of order 105 N/m, that is 200 N — enough to crush the part, trip the drive, or break the wrist. Command instead a spring of 300 N/m and the same 2 mm error produces 0.6 N, the contact converges quietly, and the geometry error becomes a non-event. Impedance control is why a robot can insert a peg it cannot see.

The catch is exactly the one that J01 set up, and it is worth being explicit about the trap: to render a low apparent inertia at the tip — to make a heavy arm feel light to the environment — you need very high control bandwidth, and you need a drivetrain the environment can actually push back through. A high gear ratio, which is the cheapest way to save watts, gives you neither. The control fix and the energy fix pull in opposite directions.

impedance.pyPython 3 · Pinocchio
"""Cartesian impedance control.

Do not command a position. Do not command a force. Command the SPRING, and
let the world decide where the arm ends up. This is the single most useful
idea in contact-rich manipulation and it predates every learned policy in
this article by four decades (Hogan, 1985)."""
import numpy as np
import pinocchio as pin


class CartesianImpedance:
    def __init__(self, urdf, tip, kp=(300, 300, 300, 20, 20, 20), zeta=1.0):
        self.model = pin.buildModelFromUrdf(urdf)
        self.data = self.model.createData()
        self.tip = self.model.getFrameId(tip)
        self.Kp = np.diag(kp)
        # Critically damped against the APPARENT inertia, not the joint inertia.
        # Under-damp this and the arm will buzz against a stiff wall; over-damp
        # it and it will feel like it is moving through treacle.
        self.zeta = zeta

    def tau(self, q, qd, X_des, V_des=None, F_ff=None):
        m, d = self.model, self.data
        pin.forwardKinematics(m, d, q, qd)
        pin.updateFramePlacements(m, d)
        pin.computeJointJacobians(m, d, q)

        X = d.oMf[self.tip]                                # current SE(3) pose
        J = pin.getFrameJacobian(m, d, self.tip, pin.LOCAL_WORLD_ALIGNED)

        # Pose error as a spatial twist. log6 is the right thing here: naive
        # subtraction of rotation matrices is not an error, it is a category
        # mistake that will bite you at large angles.
        e = pin.log6(X.actInv(X_des)).vector
        ed = (V_des if V_des is not None else np.zeros(6)) - J @ qd

        # Operational-space inertia: Lambda = (J M^-1 J^T)^-1. This is what the
        # environment actually feels when it pushes on the tip.
        M = pin.crba(m, d, q)
        Minv = np.linalg.inv(M)
        Lam = np.linalg.inv(J @ Minv @ J.T + 1e-6 * np.eye(6))

        # Damping chosen against the apparent inertia, not against nothing.
        Kd = 2.0 * self.zeta * _sqrtm_psd(Lam @ self.Kp)

        F = self.Kp @ e + Kd @ ed
        if F_ff is not None:
            F += F_ff                                       # e.g. a deliberate 5 N push

        # Gravity + Coriolis compensation is what makes the spring feel like a
        # spring rather than like a spring bolted to a heavy, sagging arm.
        nle = pin.nonLinearEffects(m, d, q, qd)
        return J.T @ F + nle


def _sqrtm_psd(A):
    w, V = np.linalg.eigh(0.5 * (A + A.T))
    return V @ np.diag(np.sqrt(np.clip(w, 0.0, None))) @ V.T

Cheating with mechanics

If you cannot model the contact, buy compliance and stop modelling it. This is the single most productive idea in practical manipulation, and it comes in several flavours.

Underactuation. Aaron Dollar and Robert Howe's SDM hand has fewer actuators than joints; the mechanism's passive compliance lets the fingers conform to whatever they meet, and a single tendon pull closes the whole hand around an unknown shape.[47] It cannot execute a specified grasp. It does not need to. The mechanism computes the grasp.

Jamming. The most elegant demonstration of the principle is a latex bag of ground coffee. Press the bag onto an object — the granular medium flows and conforms — then evacuate the air, and the grains lock into a rigid solid that has already mated with the object's surface. Brown and colleagues' universal jamming gripper picks up objects of arbitrary geometry, with no model, no perception, and no planning.[48] An enormous fraction of the grasp-planning literature is defeated by a vacuum pump and a coffee bag.

Softness generally. Fluidic elastomer actuators, Fin Ray fingers, and the whole soft-robotics programme trade precision for intrinsic safety and intrinsic conformity.[49,50] The cost is control bandwidth, hysteresis, fatigue, and the inability to say precisely where anything is.

And then there is the unglamorous truth of deployed picking: most of it is suction. Suction is fast, cheap, and works on the flat, non-porous, clean surfaces that dominate consumer packaging. It fails on mesh, on porous cloth, on dusty surfaces, on wet surfaces, and on anything with a compound curve smaller than the cup.

The scoreboard nobody likes to quote

Amazon ran a picking challenge at ICRA in 2015 for exactly this reason: to find out what the state of the art could actually do in a warehouse bin. The task was modest. Twenty minutes, a Kiva-style shelf, twelve target items drawn from a published list of twenty-five, fully autonomous, one item per bin.

Team RBO from TU Berlin won, decisively, with 148 points out of a possible 190: they picked 10 of 12 items, at a mean of 87 seconds per pick.[51] Second place scored 88 points on seven items. Third scored 35. Of the 26 teams, about half scored zero — defeated by exhibition-hall lighting, last-minute code changes, and modelling errors.[52]

Giving the robot skin

The most important hardware development in manipulation this decade is not a hand. It is a sensor, and it works by refusing to be a tactile sensor at all.

GelSight replaces an array of discrete transducers with a slab of transparent elastomer whose outer surface is coated with an opaque reflective membrane, illuminated from inside by coloured LEDs and watched by an ordinary camera. When the membrane presses against an object, photometric stereo reconstructs the deformed surface at micrometre-scale resolution; printed markers in the gel track shear and let you estimate the friction state directly.[53] It converts touch into an image — and images are the one thing the machine-learning stack is unambiguously good at. Meta's DIGIT took the same principle and made it compact, cheap and open, specifically so that it could be put on a multi-fingered hand and used in closed loop.[54]

The remaining problems are not signal-processing problems. They are the problems of putting anything on the surface of a robot: the skin is the wear part, sitting exactly where all the abrasion happens; every additional channel is another conductor routed through a joint that flexes a million times; and there is no standard, so every lab's hand speaks a different tactile dialect.[55]

The learning turn, and the wall behind it

The modern approach is to stop specifying the grasp and start collecting data. The arc is short and worth walking.

Brute force real data. Levine and colleagues ran up to 14 arms continuously for two months, gathering roughly 800,000 grasp attempts, and trained a network to predict the success of a candidate motion directly from monocular images — no calibration, no object model, no pose estimation.[56] QT-Opt scaled the idea to a distributed off-policy reinforcement learning system trained on around 580,000 real grasp attempts, reaching 96% success on unseen objects, and — more interestingly — discovering closed-loop regrasping and object-singulation behaviours that nobody had programmed.[57]

Synthetic data with analytic labels. Dex-Net 2.0 inverted the relationship between theory and learning: use the classical wrench-space analysis not as a run-time planner but as a label generator, produce 6.7 million synthetic point-cloud-and-grasp pairs, train a grasp-quality CNN on them, and deploy. The result was 99% precision on a physical bin-picking task.[58] The classical theory turned out to be extremely valuable — just not as something you run online.

Simulation plus randomisation. OpenAI's Dactyl learned in-hand reorientation on a 24-degree-of-freedom Shadow Hand entirely in simulation, and transferred to hardware by randomising everything the simulator could not get right: masses, friction coefficients, actuator gains, latencies, visual textures, lighting.[59,60] The extended system solved a Rubik's cube one-handed.[61] The cost was on the order of thirteen thousand simulated years of experience, and the cube was dropped often. GPU-parallel simulators made the recipe reproducible on an academic budget.[62,63,64]

Imitation, and the ALOHA moment. The most consequential recent result is also the least glamorous: a $32k bimanual teleoperation rig and an action-chunking transformer that learns fine, contact-rich, bimanual tasks from around fifty human demonstrations.[65] Two ideas make it work. First, predict a chunk of future actions rather than a single one, which shortens the effective horizon and kills compounding error. Second, ensemble overlapping chunks temporally, which smooths the command stream. Diffusion Policy pushed the same insight further by modelling the whole conditional distribution over action sequences, which handles the multimodality that dooms a regression loss: when there are two equally good ways to grasp a mug, the mean of them puts your fingers through the handle.[66]

chunked_policy.pyPython 3 · the inference loop that matters
"""Action chunking with temporal ensembling.

Behaviour cloning fails on long horizons because errors compound: a small
deviation puts you in a state the demonstrator never visited, and the policy
has nothing sensible to say about it. Predicting a CHUNK of k future actions
each step and blending overlapping chunks buys two things at once -- a
shorter effective horizon, and a low-pass filter on the command stream."""
from collections import deque
import numpy as np


class ChunkedController:
    def __init__(self, policy, chunk=32, m=0.15, dof=14):
        self.policy = policy       # obs -> (chunk, dof) array of future actions
        self.chunk = chunk
        self.m = m                 # exp weight; small m = trust older chunks more
        self.dof = dof
        # each entry is (age, chunk_of_actions); we keep at most `chunk` of them
        self.pending = deque(maxlen=chunk)

    def step(self, obs):
        # 1. every tick, the policy proposes a fresh plan for the next k steps.
        self.pending.appendleft([0, self.policy(obs)])

        # 2. collect every prediction that speaks to the CURRENT timestep.
        votes, weights = [], []
        for entry in self.pending:
            age, plan = entry
            if age < len(plan):
                votes.append(plan[age])
                # older chunks were made with staler observations but have
                # already been partially executed; exponential decay is the
                # cheap compromise (Zhao et al., ACT).
                weights.append(np.exp(-self.m * age))

        if not votes:
            return np.zeros(self.dof)

        w = np.asarray(weights)[:, None]
        action = (np.stack(votes) * w).sum(0) / w.sum()

        # 3. age every plan by one tick.
        for entry in self.pending:
            entry[0] += 1

        return action

# from the left 50% of the time and the right 50% of the time will learn to
# reach for the MEAN of those two trajectories -- straight through the mug.
#
# Diffusion Policy (Chi et al.) fixes this by learning the score of the
# action-sequence distribution and sampling from it, so the policy commits to
# one mode instead of averaging incompatible ones. The chunking above is
# orthogonal and composes with it.

Generalist policies. The current frontier stacks all of this on top of a pretrained vision–language backbone. RT-1 trained a transformer on 130,000 real episodes collected across 13 robots over 17 months.[67] RT-2 co-trained on web-scale vision–language data so that the policy inherits semantic generalisation it never saw a robot demonstrate — ask it to pick up “the extinct animal” and it reaches for the toy dinosaur.[68] Open X-Embodiment pooled over a million real trajectories from 60 datasets, 22 distinct embodiments and 21 institutions, covering 527 skills, and showed genuine positive transfer across robot bodies.[69] OpenVLA made the recipe open.[70] π0 added flow matching for high-frequency dexterous control.[71] DROID added 76,000 in-the-wild demonstrations across 564 scenes.[72]

The data wall

Now put those numbers next to the corpora that made the vision and language stacks work.

Open X-Embodiment, the largest open robot dataset in existence, is on the order of 106 trajectories.[69] LAION-5B is 5.85 × 109 image–text pairs. The web-text corpora used to train modern language models run to 1013 tokens and beyond. The robotics corpus is roughly eight orders of magnitude smaller than the one that trained the language model sitting inside the robot's own policy — and the deficit is not being closed, because you cannot fast-forward a physical grasp.

And robot data is not merely scarce, it is expensive in a way web data is not. It is collected in real time — you cannot fast-forward a physical grasp. It is collected on hardware that wears out, and the hardware's wear changes the data distribution as you collect it. It does not transfer freely across embodiments, because the action space is different and the kinematics are different. And it is dominated by success: nobody teleoperates a thousand failures on purpose, so the policy never learns what recovery looks like.

The uncomfortable question

The scaling hypothesis is that the data wall is the only wall — that a corpus 1,000× larger produces a policy that manipulates like a person. It might be right. But contact dynamics are non-smooth, tactile feedback is nearly absent from the existing corpora, and the friction coefficient is not in any image. Scale has never yet substituted for a missing sensor. Sünderhauf and colleagues' survey of the limits of deep learning for robotics makes the case carefully, and it has aged well.[73]

Deformables, and the state you cannot write down

Everything above assumed rigid objects. A rigid body's state is six numbers. A shirt's state, in principle, is a field — and the parts of it you most need to know are folded inside the parts you can see. Self-occlusion is not an inconvenience here; it is the structure of the problem.[74]

Cloth, cable, food, dough, biological tissue, and packaging are the majority of the objects that a robot in a home, a kitchen, or an operating theatre must handle. The progress that has been made has come almost entirely from not estimating the state: policies that map raw pixels to actions, plus the mechanical trick of exploiting the environment as a fixture — drag the cloth against the table, let gravity find the hem, use the world to reduce the state you have to infer. That is Brooks's insight, arriving thirty years late in a new suit.

Billard and Kragic's assessment stands as the field's own summary, and it is not triumphant: after four decades, robots are good at grasping known objects in known poses, adequate at grasping novel rigid objects in clutter, and essentially incapable of the in-hand dexterity that a toddler uses to get a sock on.[75,76] Kemp, Edsinger and Torres-Jara's 2007 list of challenges for manipulation in human environments remains uncomfortably current.[77]

Robustness: the world does not repeat

“Unstructured environment” is a phrase that has been worn smooth by use, and it deserves a sharp definition. It does not mean messy. It means that the distribution the robot is tested on is not the distribution it was built on, and that the tail of that distribution is not bounded. A factory is structured because the world is a fixture: it was engineered to hold still. A kitchen, a road, a collapsed building and a sewer are unstructured because the world is a sample, and you do not get to see the whole population before you deploy.

The public record of failure

Robotics has one thing that most of computer science does not: a series of public, adversarial, unrepeatable competitions in which the machines failed on camera. It is the field's most valuable data.

The 2004 DARPA Grand Challenge sent fifteen autonomous vehicles into the Mojave to cross a 142-mile desert course. None finished. The best of them, Carnegie Mellon's Sandstorm, managed 11.8 km — under 5% of the route — before beaching itself on an embankment after a switchback. Every other entrant crashed, broke down, or was disqualified. The million-dollar prize went unclaimed.[78]

Eighteen months later, five of twenty-three vehicles completed a 132-mile course, and Stanford's Stanley did it in six hours and fifty-three minutes.[79] The jump was not caused by a new theory of intelligence. It was caused by better lidar, a properly probabilistic terrain classifier trained on human driving, careful treatment of the vehicle's own pitch-induced sensor error, and a year of relentless engineering. In 2007 the Urban Challenge added moving traffic and traffic law, and six of eleven finished.[80]

Then the field tried humanoids. The DARPA Robotics Challenge Finals in 2015 put twenty-three teams through eight disaster-response tasks — drive a vehicle, get out of it, open a door, turn a valve, cut a wall, handle a surprise, cross rubble, climb stairs — over a deliberately degraded communications link.[81] DARPA's official account records fourteen falls. The compilation video of those falls is, without exaggeration, the most honest artefact the field has ever produced: hundreds of engineer-years of the world's best humanoid robotics, tipping slowly over onto concrete. The post-mortems are worth more than the results.[82]

And in 2021, the Subterranean Challenge: eight teams, an artificial cave-tunnel-urban maze, dense smoke deployed on purpose, forty hidden artefacts to find and localise. Team CERBERUS won with four ANYmal C legged robots that collectively walked over 1.7 km and correctly reported 23 of the 40 artefacts. They tied with CSIRO Data61 on points and won on a tie-break.[83]

Look at the shape of those scores: 0 of 15. 5 of 23. 6 of 11. 23 of 40. These are not the numbers of a solved discipline. They are the numbers of a discipline that has learned to be honest.

Reliability, measured

The quiet field data is worse than the competition data. Carlson and Murphy assembled failure statistics from ten studies of unmanned ground vehicles in urban search-and-rescue and military field use — including robots deployed at the World Trade Center — and reported a mean time between failures of six to twenty hours, with availability under 50%.[84] In one study half of all failures were in the effectors; in another, 54% were in the control system. The failures were not exotic. They were tracks, connectors, radios, and software that had never met a wet slope.

Set that against an industrial arm in a cage, which will run 60,000 to 100,000 hours between failures. It is largely the same actuators. The difference is entirely the world.

Fukushima Daiichi is the case study that should be taught first. When robots were finally sent into the reactor buildings, the ones that were useful were tethered, teleoperated, and mechanically simple; the ones that failed did so because of radiation-induced electronics faults, snagged cables, tracks that could not handle debris, and lost radio links.[31] Autonomy was not the binding constraint. Robin Murphy's account of two decades of disaster deployments makes the same point repeatedly: the robot that shows up and works is worth more than the robot that is clever.[32]

Perception fails silently, which is the worst way to fail

A motor that seizes announces itself. A perception system that is wrong does not. It returns a confident answer, at the usual rate, with the usual latency, and the planner downstream has no way to tell.

The problem is structural. Modern neural networks are systematically overconfident: their softmax outputs do not correspond to empirical frequencies, and the miscalibration gets worse as the models get better.[85] On inputs drawn from outside the training distribution — a pedestrian in a chicken suit, a truck carrying a photograph of a road, a lidar return from smoke — the network has no mechanism to say “I have not seen this.” It says “pole, 0.94.”

This is not a robotics-specific pathology. It is the same distributional-shift problem that Amodei and colleagues catalogued as one of the concrete, unglamorous, still-unsolved problems of deploying learned systems in the world — and robotics is simply where it has a body and a mass.[86]

The mitigations are real and partial. Deep ensembles give a usable disagreement signal at a linear cost in compute.[87] Introspective classification explicitly models the reliability of the classifier's own output.[88] Conformal prediction gives you finite-sample coverage guarantees, at the price of returning a set rather than a label — which is honest, and which most downstream planners are not written to consume.

ood_gate.pyPython 3 · the cheapest useful safety net
"""An abstention gate for a learned perception component.

The goal is NOT to classify better. It is to know when not to answer, and to
hand the decision to a controller that has a conservative default. A robot
that stops when it is confused is a robot that can be debugged."""
import numpy as np


class EnsembleGate:
    def __init__(self, models, tau_entropy=0.55, tau_disagree=0.25):
        self.models = models              # independently initialised + shuffled
        self.tau_entropy = tau_entropy    # normalised predictive entropy
        self.tau_disagree = tau_disagree  # mutual information (epistemic part)

    def __call__(self, x):
        # (M, C) probability simplex, one row per ensemble member
        P = np.stack([m.predict_proba(x) for m in self.models])
        mean = P.mean(axis=0)
        C = mean.shape[-1]
        eps = 1e-12

        # Total uncertainty: entropy of the ensemble mean.
        H_total = -np.sum(mean * np.log(mean + eps)) / np.log(C)

        # Aleatoric part: mean entropy of the individual members.
        H_alea = np.mean([-np.sum(p * np.log(p + eps)) for p in P]) / np.log(C)

        # Epistemic part = mutual information. THIS is the "I have not seen
        # this before" signal. Aleatoric noise (motion blur, fog) is
        # irreducible; epistemic uncertainty means the model is out of its
        # depth, and is what should trigger a fallback.
        mutual_info = H_total - H_alea

        abstain = bool(H_total > self.tau_entropy or
                      mutual_info > self.tau_disagree)

        return {
            "label": None if abstain else int(mean.argmax()),
            "confidence": float(mean.max()),
            "entropy": float(H_total),
            "epistemic": float(mutual_info),
            "abstain": abstain,
        }

# drawn from the training distribution. If your validation set looks like your
# training set, your gate will never fire until the day it matters.
#
# And note the honest limit: an ensemble of networks trained on the same data
# shares the same blind spots. Ensembling reduces variance; it does not
# manufacture knowledge the corpus never contained.

State estimation grew up, and then found its own wall

Localisation and mapping is the one part of the stack that genuinely matured. The field moved from filtering to smoothing — from an extended Kalman filter that irreversibly folded the past into a covariance, to a factor graph over the whole trajectory that can be relinearised and re-solved as new information arrives.[89] ORB-SLAM3 will run visual, visual-inertial and multi-map SLAM on a laptop with metric accuracy.[90] Cadena and colleagues named the period the field was entering the “robust-perception age,” and catalogued exactly what was still missing.[91]

What is still missing includes a failure mode that no amount of robust kernel repairs. In a long, straight, featureless tunnel, the geometric registration problem is rank-deficient along the tunnel axis: there is genuinely no information in the scan about how far you have travelled. Your ICP will not fail. It will converge, quickly, to a confident and wrong answer. Zhang, Kaess and Singh showed how to detect this by examining the conditioning of the optimisation itself and how to solve only in the well-constrained subspace.[92]

That is the correct architectural instinct, and it generalises: the system must be able to detect that its own estimator is not observable, and degrade deliberately rather than lie confidently. It is exactly what the SubT courses were designed to provoke, with their smoke, their dust, their self-similar corridors and their glass-smooth walls.

Control that survives contact it did not expect

The control-theoretic side of robustness has had the field's most genuine recent success, and it happened in legged locomotion.

Model-predictive control at speed came first: a convex MPC formulation running fast enough to plan ground reaction forces over a receding horizon lets a quadruped trot, bound and recover from shoves without a hand-tuned state machine.[93] Then reinforcement learning arrived and beat it, on hardware. Hwangbo and colleagues trained ANYmal's locomotion policy entirely in simulation — using a learned actuator model to close the sim-to-real gap where the analytical model was worst — and transferred it to a physical machine that could run faster than before and get up after being knocked over.[94]

Lee and colleagues went further with a teacher–student scheme: train a privileged policy in simulation that can see the terrain's true friction and geometry, then distil it into a student that sees only proprioception, and put the resulting blind policy on a mountain.[95] Miki and colleagues then added exteroception and, crucially, taught the policy when to stop believing its own map: an attention mechanism learns to fall back on proprioception whenever the height map disagrees with what the foot actually finds.[96]

The idea worth stealing

Miki's architecture is the single best answer the field has produced to the silent-failure problem, and it is not a monitor bolted on top. The policy learns robustness as a behaviour: it learns that its exteroception is sometimes a liar, and it learns the conditions under which to distrust it. Contrast with the usual approach, in which a hand-written health check tries to guess, from outside, whether the perception module is having a bad day.

The caveat is important and is in the papers themselves. Champion-level autonomous drone racing beat human world champions — in an instrumented room, on a known track, with an external motion-capture system providing state, and after training against a carefully identified dynamics model.[97] The result is real and the caveats are real, and confusing the two is how the field earns its reputation for overpromising.

The validation problem you cannot test your way out of

Now the mathematics that should govern every deployment decision, and mostly does not.

Human drivers in the United States kill about 1.09 people per 100 million miles driven. Kalra and Paddock asked the obvious question: how many miles would an autonomous fleet have to drive to demonstrate, statistically, that it is safer? The answer is that to show with 95% confidence that a system's fatality rate is 20% lower than a human's, you would need to drive on the order of hundreds of millions of miles — and for some claims, hundreds of billions. A hundred-vehicle fleet driving 24 hours a day at 25 mph would take decades. And that is the theoretical lower bound, assuming the software never changes.[98]

The distribution is the reason. Common scenarios — lane keeping, a pedestrian at a crossing — are seen a million times in training. Rare ones are not, and there is no finite list of them. A mattress on the freeway. A flock of birds on wet asphalt. A cyclist carrying a mirror. Each individual event has negligible probability; the union of them is the entire safety case. You cannot enumerate the tail, you cannot collect it, and you cannot test it.

Philip Koopman's contribution is to name why this breaks the safety-engineering tradition outright. Functional-safety standards like ISO 26262 assume you can write a specification and then show the implementation satisfies it. A machine-learned perception component has no specification. There is no document that says what “correctly detects a pedestrian” means at the level of precision a safety argument requires; the model is the specification, and it is 200 million parameters long.[99]

The consequence is not defeatist, but it is disciplining: the safety case has to be architectural, not statistical. You cannot prove the learned component is right. You can build a system that survives it being wrong.

Architecture for a system whose components will lie to it

Four ideas, all old, all under-used.

Independent monitors that do not share the failure mode. Lui Sha's simplex architecture pairs a high-performance controller of unknown reliability with a simple, verified, conservative one, and a decision module that switches to the safe controller whenever the state leaves a certified region.[100] The whole value rests on the word independent: a monitor that consumes the same perception output it is supposed to be checking is decoration.

Formal envelopes. Control barrier functions give a certificate that a controller keeps the state inside a safe set, and compose with an arbitrary — including learned — nominal policy by minimally modifying its output.[101] Reachability analysis answers the same question by construction. Neither requires you to trust the policy; both require you to be able to write down the safe set, which is exactly as hard as it sounds.

Runtime monitors with a real semantics. Signal temporal logic lets you state properties over continuous signals — “the end-effector force never exceeds 80 N for more than 50 ms” — and monitor them online with a quantitative robustness margin rather than a boolean.[102]

Explicit recovery, as a first-class behaviour. Behaviour trees make fallback structure legible and composable, which is why they displaced finite state machines in practice.[103] Nav2's recovery subtree — clear the costmap, spin in place, back up, wait, then and only then give up — is the reason a research navigation stack survived contact with real buildings.[104]

safety_supervisor.pyPython 3 · ROS 2 · rclpy
"""A supervisor that assumes its own perception stack is unreliable.

Three defences, deliberately independent of each other:
  1. a liveness deadman on every safety-critical input,
  2. a model-based residual monitor that never reads the perception topic,
  3. a bounded-authority gate on the command that leaves the box.

None of them require the learned components to be correct. All of them
require the learned components to STOP being trusted when they misbehave."""
import rclpy
from rclpy.node import Node
from rclpy.qos import QoSProfile, ReliabilityPolicy
from geometry_msgs.msg import Twist
from sensor_msgs.msg import JointState, Imu
import numpy as np

DEADMAN_S = 0.25        # any critical input older than this = not alive
RESIDUAL_N = 28.0       # external wrench estimate, newtons
RESIDUAL_HOLD = 3       # consecutive ticks before we believe it


class SafetySupervisor(Node):
    def __init__(self, model):
        super().__init__("safety_supervisor")
        self.model = model            # rigid-body dynamics (e.g. Pinocchio)
        self.p_hat = None            # momentum-observer state
        self.r = np.zeros(model.nv)  # residual = estimated external torque
        self.trip = 0
        self.last = {}               # topic -> last receipt time

        # BEST_EFFORT on sensors, RELIABLE on the command we emit.
        sensor_qos = QoSProfile(depth=1, reliability=ReliabilityPolicy.BEST_EFFORT)
        self.create_subscription(JointState, "/joint_states", self.on_joints, sensor_qos)
        self.create_subscription(Imu, "/imu", self.on_imu, sensor_qos)
        self.create_subscription(Twist, "/cmd_vel_raw", self.on_cmd, 10)
        self.pub = self.create_publisher(Twist, "/cmd_vel", 10)
        self.create_timer(0.02, self.tick)   # 50 Hz, independent of any input

    # ---- defence 2: generalised momentum observer -------------------------
    # Haddadin, De Luca and Albu-Schaeffer: the residual
    #     r = K [ p - integral( tau + C^T qd - g + r ) ]
    # converges to the external joint torque WITHOUT differentiating velocity
    # and WITHOUT a force/torque sensor. It reads only motor current and
    # encoders -- so it cannot be fooled by a hallucinating camera.
    def on_joints(self, msg):
        self.last["joints"] = self.get_clock().now()
        q = np.array(msg.position)
        qd = np.array(msg.velocity)
        tau = np.array(msg.effort)
        dt = 0.002

        M = self.model.mass_matrix(q)
        p = M @ qd                                  # generalised momentum
        beta = self.model.gravity(q) - self.model.coriolis(q, qd).T @ qd

        if self.p_hat is None:
            self.p_hat = p.copy()
        self.p_hat += (tau - beta + self.r) * dt
        self.r = 12.0 * (p - self.p_hat)          # K = 12 I, first order

    def on_imu(self, msg):
        self.last["imu"] = self.get_clock().now()

    def on_cmd(self, msg):
        self.last["cmd"] = self.get_clock().now()
        self.desired = msg

    # ---- defence 1 + 3 ---------------------------------------------------
    def alive(self, key):
        t = self.last.get(key)
        if t is None:
            return False
        age = (self.get_clock().now() - t).nanoseconds * 1e-9
        return age < DEADMAN_S

    def tick(self):
        # A silent upstream node is indistinguishable from a crashed one, and
        # both are indistinguishable from a node that is slowly deadlocking.
        # Treat all three the same way.
        if not all(self.alive(k) for k in ("joints", "imu", "cmd")):
            return self.safe_stop("deadman")

        # Debounce: a single spike is an encoder glitch, three in a row is a
        # person's hand.
        if float(np.linalg.norm(self.r)) > RESIDUAL_N:
            self.trip += 1
        else:
            self.trip = 0
        if self.trip >= RESIDUAL_HOLD:
            return self.safe_stop("unexpected contact")

        cmd = Twist()
        cmd.linear.x = float(np.clip(self.desired.linear.x, -0.6, 1.2))
        cmd.angular.z = float(np.clip(self.desired.angular.z, -1.0, 1.0))
        self.pub.publish(cmd)

    def safe_stop(self, reason):
        self.pub.publish(Twist())        # zero command, every tick, forever
        self.get_logger().warn(f"SAFE STOP: {reason}", throttle_duration_sec=1.0)
        # Deliberately NOT auto-clearing. A supervisor that silently resumes
        # has taught the operator to ignore it.

The residual observer in the middle of that node deserves its own paragraph. Haddadin, De Luca and Albu-Schäffer's survey of collision detection shows that you can estimate the external torque acting on a manipulator from motor current and joint encoders alone, with no force sensor and without differentiating a noisy velocity signal, by observing the generalised momentum.[105] It is cheap, it is fast, and — the point here — it is physically independent of the perception stack. When the camera hallucinates, the momentum observer does not care.

Where the stack breaks, layer by layer

Every layer has a defence and none of them is a guarantee, which is the honest summary of the state of the art. The supervision layer is the one that deserves the most suspicion, because it is where the field's favourite mistake lives: the monitor that shares the failure mode of the thing it monitors. A health check that asks the perception node “are you healthy?” will be told yes by a node that is confidently hallucinating.

Why physical safety is nearly solved, and what that teaches

There is one part of robot robustness that is genuinely engineered to an industrial standard, and the reason is instructive.

ISO 10218 governs industrial robot safety; ISO/TS 15066 extends it to collaborative operation and specifies, per body region, the maximum permissible pressure and force a robot may apply to a human before it hurts; ISO 13482 covers personal-care robots.[106,107,108] These standards work. Collaborative arms genuinely do share a workspace with people without cages, using force-limited joints, momentum observers, and speed-and-separation monitoring.[105]

Why did this problem yield when the others did not? Because the requirement was specifiable. “Do not exceed 140 N on the sternum” is a sentence you can write down, test against, and certify. “Do not misunderstand the scene” is not. Every robustness problem that has been solved is one that could be written as a specification, and every one that remains open is one that cannot.

J04 · Synthesis

The coupling: three walls, one problem

Treating the hurdles separately, as this article has done for three sections, is a pedagogical convenience that becomes a lie the moment you try to build anything. The walls are load-bearing for each other. Relieve one and you press harder on the next two, and the reason robotics progress feels so much slower than the demonstration videos suggest is that most of the field's effort is spent going around a triangle.

Robustness taxes power

Every mechanism in J03 costs watts. Ensembles multiply inference cost by the ensemble size. Multi-modal fusion means a lidar and a radar and a camera, each with its own power draw and its own perception pipeline. Redundant actuators are dead mass most of the time. Higher control rates mean a faster processor at a higher clock, and dynamic power scales superlinearly with clock. A monitor that is genuinely independent of the perception stack requires a second computer that does not share a failure mode with the first one, which means a second power rail.

Robustness, in other words, is bought with compute, and compute is bought with watts, and watts are bought with battery mass, and battery mass raises the power required to move the robot at all — which reduces the endurance you were trying to protect. It is the rocket equation's poor cousin, and it is why so many field robots end up with a runtime measured in tens of minutes.

Power taxes dexterity

This is the coupling that is least discussed and most damaging. Section J01 established that copper loss scales as τ² and that the single most effective way to reduce it is a large gear reduction, which cuts current by N and loss by N². So the energy-optimal actuator, considered in isolation, is a small motor behind a big gearbox under stiff position control.

That is precisely the actuator that cannot do dexterity. A high reduction ratio destroys backdrivability, and backdrivability is what lets you infer contact force from motor current without a sensor. It reflects rotor inertia by N², which is what shatters a harmonic drive when a foot or a fingertip meets the world sooner than expected. It introduces backlash and stiction, which put a floor under your force resolution. ASIMO's 3.2 cost of transport and its inability to handle unexpected contact are not two separate facts about ASIMO. They are the same design decision, seen from two directions.

The quasi-direct-drive school pays the energy bill knowingly in exchange for the mechanical transparency that makes contact survivable.[24,25] There is no configuration that gives you both.

Dexterity taxes robustness

And the return path closes the loop. Every capability that makes a hand dexterous is a new surface for failure.

  • A tactile skin lives on the exact surface that does all the abrading. It is a wear part, by construction.
  • A hundred tactile channels means a hundred conductors routed through joints that will flex a million times. Cable fatigue at the flexure is the classic failure mode of every dexterous hand ever built, from the Utah/MIT hand onward.[39]
  • Tendon drives, which are how you get the actuators out of the fingers and into the forearm where there is room for them, introduce friction, hysteresis, creep, and eventual rupture.
  • Soft and compliant actuators fatigue, and elastomers age. A gripper that is intrinsically safe on day one is a gripper with a crack propagating in it on day four hundred.
  • Compliance itself, deliberately introduced to survive unmodelled contact, destroys the positional accuracy that makes the task legible to the planner in the first place.

And every kilogram is spent once

All three walls meet at the mass budget, which is the one constraint that admits no cleverness at all. A kilogram spent on a tactile hand is a kilogram not spent on battery, or on a redundant IMU, or on structure that survives the fall. A kilogram spent on battery raises the power needed to carry the battery.

It is worth doing the humanoid arithmetic explicitly, because it is the machine that everyone is currently promising and almost nobody is characterising honestly.

This is the arithmetic behind an observation you can make for yourself: humanoid demonstration videos are short. They are short because the thermal envelope is short and the energy budget is short, and where they are not short they are usually teleoperated, or a single take from many, or running at a fraction of the speed the edit implies. That is not fraud — every one of those clips represents real, hard, admirable engineering. It is simply that the highlight reel is a poor instrument for measuring a mean time between failures.

The honest reading

A demonstration proves a capability exists. It says nothing about the rate at which the capability succeeds, the duration over which it can be sustained, or the breadth of conditions under which it holds. Those three numbers — success rate, endurance, and coverage — are what separate a research result from a product, and they are exactly the three numbers that the three walls make hard.

J05 · Practice

The toolchain you actually build with

Robotics has an unusually good open-source stack and an unusually large gap in the middle of it. It is worth knowing both.

Middleware, and the tyranny of the topic

ROS began as a research plumbing layer at Willow Garage and became the lingua franca of the field by being good enough and by shipping drivers for everything.[109] ROS 2 rebuilt it on DDS, which brought quality-of-service policies, discovery without a central master, lifecycle-managed nodes, and a credible path to real-time and to safety certification.[110] It is the right default, and it should be used with clear eyes: publish-subscribe makes it very easy to build a system in which no single component knows whether the system as a whole is working. The supervisor in J03 exists because of that property, not in spite of it.

On top of it, Nav2 is the navigation stack that survived contact with real buildings, largely because of its recovery behaviours.[104] MoveIt is the manipulation stack, wrapping OMPL's sampling-based planners — PRM, RRT, and their asymptotically-optimal descendants.[111,112,113]

Dynamics and control

Roy Featherstone's spatial-vector algorithms are the reason forward and inverse dynamics are O(n) instead of O(n³), and every serious library implements them.[114] Pinocchio implements them fast, in C++, with analytical derivatives — which is the unglamorous reason whole-body model-predictive control runs at kilohertz rates on a real machine instead of in a paper.[115] Convex MPC on top of a reduced-order model is the workhorse of legged control.[93]

EEF · End effector

What would actually move

Prediction in robotics has an unblemished record of being wrong, and it is wrong in a specific direction: the field consistently overestimates what a new idea will do and underestimates what a year of unglamorous engineering will do. In March 2004, no vehicle finished the Grand Challenge. In October 2005, five did. Nothing philosophical happened in between. What happened was better lidar, a properly probabilistic terrain model, careful characterisation of the vehicle's own sensor errors, and eighteen months of people not giving up.[79] That is what a step change actually looks like, and it is worth keeping in mind while reading the following.

On energy: stop asking the chemists

The battery will improve by a factor of two over the next decade, and that will be genuinely useful, and it will not change the shape of the problem. The available factors of two are elsewhere and they are additive:

  • The actuator. Raise K_m. Large-air-gap motors, better magnet material, better thermal paths out of the winding. Every point of motor constant is a permanent reduction in the τ²R/K_t² that dominates the budget.[22]
  • The thermal model. Continuous torque is set by winding temperature, not by magnetics. A controller that plans against a thermal state estimate rather than a fixed current limit can extract substantially more work from the same hardware, and will not cook it. Almost nothing deployed does this.
  • The mechanism. Springs are free actuators. Passive dynamics are free control. The Ranger's 11.5 W is not a software achievement; it is a machine that was designed so that the software would have almost nothing to do.[20,18]
  • Not doing the work. Docking, tethering, and fleet scheduling convert an intractable energy problem into a tractable logistics problem. It is not glamorous and it is how every deployed inspection robot on earth actually operates.

On dexterity: touch, then data, in that order

The scaling hypothesis says that manipulation is a data problem and that a corpus three orders of magnitude larger than Open X-Embodiment produces a policy that manipulates like a person. It may be right. But two things have to be true for it to be right, and only one of them is currently being worked on at scale.

The first is tactile sensing that is cheap, durable, and standard. Every existing large robot dataset is, essentially, video of an arm. The friction coefficient is not in the image. The moment of first contact is not in the image. Incipient slip is not in the image. A human deprived of tactile feedback — through anaesthesia, not paralysis — becomes clumsy in a way that vision cannot compensate for, and there is no reason to expect a policy to do better. Vision-based tactile sensors like GelSight and DIGIT turned touch into an image, which is exactly the format the rest of the stack knows how to consume.[53,54] What is missing is durability, a connector standard, and the willingness of one large data-collection effort to instrument every fingertip.

The second is contact simulation good enough to transfer. This is a numerical-methods problem, it is unfashionable, and it is the bottleneck. Locomotion policies transfer from simulation because their contacts are simple. Manipulation policies transfer badly because their contacts are the task, and every simulator regularises friction differently.[45,116] A contact solver that is fast, accurate, and differentiable would do more for manipulation than another order of magnitude of teleoperation data. Both would be better.

On robustness: give up on proving safety, and start engineering for failure

Kalra and Paddock's arithmetic is not going to be repealed.[98] You cannot test your way to a statistical safety case for a system operating in an open world, and Koopman's observation stands: a learned component has no specification to verify it against.[99] The field spent a decade trying to route around this, and it did not work.

What does work is architectural, and it is old:

  • Learned components inside verified envelopes. Let the policy be as clever as it likes; put a control barrier function, a reachability check, or a simplex switch between it and the actuators.[101,100]
  • Monitors that are physically independent of what they monitor. A momentum observer running on encoder data cannot be fooled by a hallucinating camera, which is precisely why it is worth having.[105]
  • Policies that learn to distrust their own sensors. Miki's perceptive locomotion — which learns to fall back on proprioception when the height map disagrees with the foot — is the most important architectural idea in the recent literature, and it is barely being copied.[96]
  • Graceful degradation as a design requirement, not an exception path. A robot that stops when confused is debuggable. A robot that continues confidently is a lawsuit.[103]
  • Honest instrumentation. Report mean time between interventions. Report success rate over a stated distribution. Report endurance under load. The field's most persistent self-inflicted wound is that its public metric is a video.[84]

What the walls are made of

It is worth being clear about the different natures of the three, because they will not fall at the same time or for the same reasons.

The energy wall is made of chemistry and thermodynamics. It is real, it is quantitative, and it is negotiable: it yields, slowly, to better cells, better motors, better mechanisms, and better logistics. A machine that runs for eight hours is an engineering problem with a known solution path, and someone will get there.

The dexterity wall is made of mathematics and missing data. Contact is non-smooth, and that is a permanent property of the world; but the field has repeatedly shown that you can go around a hard model with mechanism (jam a bag of coffee grounds around the object) or with data (learn the policy without ever writing the model down). It is negotiable too, and the current rate of progress in imitation learning is the fastest the field has ever seen.[66,65,69]

The robustness wall is different in kind. It is not made of physics or of mathematics. It is made of the world's refusal to repeat itself — of a long tail with no last term. No amount of engineering closes it, because it is not a gap in capability; it is a gap between a finite training set and an infinite world, and it is structurally the same problem that makes every other deployed machine-learning system unreliable at the margin.

Which means the winning architectures will not be the ones that promise not to fail. They will be the ones that assume failure and stay useful anyway: that detect the moment their own estimator went unobservable, that stop when they are confused, that carry a monitor which does not share their blind spot, and that fail in the direction of a stationary robot rather than a moving one.

Shakey needed the room painted. Sixty years on, the honest engineering position is that we still need the room painted — we have simply become much better at painting it quickly, at noticing when it is not painted, and at standing still when we cannot tell.

Three numbers to ask for

Next time you watch a robot demonstration, ask for three figures that no highlight reel contains: the success rate over a stated distribution of trials, the endurance under representative load, and the mean time between human interventions in the field. If they are not offered, the demonstration is a capability claim, not a reliability claim — and the three walls live entirely in the gap between the two.
