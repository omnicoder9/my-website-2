Introduction
01

The mind between the sensors and the motors

A robot is a loop. Sensors turn the world into numbers, motors turn numbers back into the world, and somewhere in between a decision has to be made about what to do next. That decision-making middle — the part that holds a model of the situation, reasons about goals, and commits to a course of action — is what this article calls the cognitive layer. It is the robot’s planning and decision apparatus: the software that answers the question “given what I know, where should I go and how should I move?”

It helps to fix the layer’s position in the wider system. Beneath it sits perception, which converts raw camera, LiDAR and proprioceptive streams into an estimate of the world’s state; above the actuators sits control, which tracks a desired motion despite friction, backlash and disturbance. The cognitive layer is the deliberative tissue between them — it consumes the perceived state and emits motion goals for control to execute. The classic decomposition of this stack into estimation, decision and control runs through the standard references of the field.[1,2]

Three activities dominate the layer, and they organise everything that follows. Path planning finds a collision-free route from where the robot is to where it wants to be. Trajectory optimization turns that geometric route into a smooth, timed, dynamically feasible motion that conserves energy and spares the mechanism. And AI integration — chiefly learned computer vision and reinforcement learning — supplies the perception that grounds decisions and, increasingly, learns the decision policy itself. The remainder of this piece treats each in turn, after a short detour through the history that produced them, because the modern shape of robot cognition is very much an argument that the field had with itself and partly resolved.

A working definition

The cognitive layer is the deliberative component of a robot’s software that maintains a model of the task and environment, reasons over it to select goals and actions, and produces motion references for lower-level control. It is distinct from perception (which builds the model) and from feedback control (which executes the references).

Lineage
02

A short history of machine deliberation

The first mobile robot to reason about its own actions was Shakey, built at the Stanford Research Institute between 1966 and 1972. Shakey could be asked to push a box off a platform and would, remarkably, work out the sub-goals for itself: find the ramp, push it to the platform, drive up, push the box. Its planner was STRIPS — the Stanford Research Institute Problem Solver — which represented the world as a set of logical facts and searched for a sequence of operators, each with preconditions and effects, that transformed the initial state into a goal state.[3] STRIPS defined a template for symbolic planning that is still recognisable in task planners today.

Shakey also gave the field one of its most durable algorithms. To decide how to move between locations, its designers needed a graph search that was both optimal and efficient, and in 1968 Hart, Nilsson and Raphael published A*, a best-first search that uses an admissible heuristic to find least-cost paths while expanding as few nodes as possible.[4] More than half a century later A* remains the default grid planner in game engines and robots alike.

This early paradigm is now called sense–plan–act: perceive the world, build a complete symbolic model, plan an optimal sequence, execute it. It was intellectually clean and, on Shakey’s tabletop world, it worked. But it was also slow and brittle. Building a complete world model before every action is expensive, and any mismatch between the model and reality — a moved object, a wheel that slipped — could invalidate the whole plan.

The reactive revolt

In 1986 Rodney Brooks detonated the assumption at the centre of the paradigm. His subsumption architecture dispensed with the central world model entirely, decomposing a robot’s behaviour into layers of simple sensor-to-actuator reflexes — avoid, wander, explore — that ran in parallel, with higher layers suppressing lower ones when needed.[5] Intelligence, on this view, could emerge from the interaction of many dumb behaviours with a complex world, with no need for the robot to carry an internal replica of that world. Brooks made the philosophical claim explicit a few years later in an essay whose title became a slogan: intelligence without representation.[6] Reactive robots were fast, robust and cheap; what they could not easily do was pursue a long-horizon goal that required looking several steps ahead.

The hybrid synthesis

The resolution, arrived at through the 1990s, was not to choose. Three-layer architectures put a slow deliberative planner on top, a fast reactive controller on the bottom, and a middle sequencer or executive to mediate between them — dispatching the reflexive skills that carry out each step of the plan and reacting when reality diverges.[7] The best-known instance, the 3T architecture, made this planner–sequencer–skills division a template for intelligent, reactive agents.[8] The cognitive layer, in this settlement, is the top two tiers: it deliberates when it has time and defers to reflexes when it does not.

The executive tier itself later found an elegant formalism in behavior trees, which structure a robot’s task logic as a tree of composable, reactive nodes. Colledanchise and Ögren showed that behavior trees generalise and subsume earlier control structures, including the subsumption architecture and decision trees, while remaining modular and easy to reason about.[9] They are now the orchestration backbone of production robot stacks. With that lineage in place, we can look closely at the three activities the modern cognitive layer performs.

Path Planning
03

Finding a way through

Path planning asks for a collision-free route between two configurations of the robot. The word configuration is doing quiet but essential work. A mobile base on a flat floor has a configuration of three numbers — position and heading — but a robot arm with six joints has a six-dimensional configuration, and a humanoid has dozens. Planning has to happen in that full space, not in the three-dimensional world we see.

Configuration space: making a body into a point

The conceptual key, introduced by Lozano-Pérez in 1983, is configuration space (C-space): the space of all the robot’s possible configurations, in which the entire robot collapses to a single point.[10] Every workspace obstacle casts a shadow in C-space — the set of configurations that would collide with it — and if we “grow” each obstacle by the shape of the robot (formally, a Minkowski sum), then a collision-free motion of the whole sized body becomes nothing more than the motion of a point among the grown obstacles.

This reduction is what lets a single family of algorithms serve robots as different as a warehouse cart and a surgical arm: they all reduce to steering a point through a high-dimensional space punctured by forbidden regions. The two great families of methods differ in how they represent that space — by chopping it into a grid, or by sampling it at random.

Graph search: Dijkstra and A*

Discretise the space into a grid or graph and planning becomes shortest-path search. Dijkstra’s algorithm, published in 1959, expands nodes outward from the start in order of increasing cost and is guaranteed to find the shortest path in a graph with non-negative edge weights.[11] Its weakness is that it explores uniformly in all directions, wasting effort on nodes pointing away from the goal.

A* fixes exactly this. It orders the search not by cost-so-far g(n) alone but by f(n) = g(n) + h(n), where h(n) is a heuristic estimate of the remaining cost to the goal.[4] If the heuristic never overestimates — the admissibility condition, satisfied by straight-line distance on a grid — A* is guaranteed to return an optimal path while expanding the fewest nodes any optimal algorithm could. When the heuristic is zero, A* degenerates exactly into Dijkstra, which makes the relationship between the two vivid: A* is Dijkstra that knows which way the goal is.

The figure above is not a schematic; it is the output of both algorithms run on the same map. Dijkstra expanded 251 cells to find its path; A*, armed with an octile-distance heuristic, expanded 20 to find a path of identical cost. That ratio is the whole argument for heuristic search. A minimal but complete A* is short enough to read in one sitting:

astar.py
import heapq

def astar(grid, start, goal):
    """A* on a 2-D occupancy grid. grid[y][x] == 1 marks an obstacle.
    8-connected; diagonal moves cost sqrt(2) and may not cut corners."""
    h, w = len(grid), len(grid[0])

    def heuristic(a, b):                       # octile distance (admissible)
        dx, dy = abs(a[0] - b[0]), abs(a[1] - b[1])
        return (dx + dy) + (2 ** 0.5 - 2) * min(dx, dy)

    def neighbours(node):
        x, y = node
        for dx in (-1, 0, 1):
            for dy in (-1, 0, 1):
                if dx == dy == 0:
                    continue
                nx, ny = x + dx, y + dy
                if not (0 <= nx < w and 0 <= ny < h) or grid[ny][nx]:
                    continue
                if dx and dy and (grid[y][x + dx] or grid[y + dy][x]):
                    continue                   # forbid corner cutting
                yield (nx, ny), (2 ** 0.5 if dx and dy else 1.0)

    open_heap = [(heuristic(start, goal), 0.0, start)]
    came_from, g_score = {}, {start: 0.0}
    while open_heap:
        _, g, current = heapq.heappop(open_heap)
        if g > g_score.get(current, float("inf")):
            continue                           # a stale, worse entry
        if current == goal:
            path = [current]
            while current in came_from:
                current = came_from[current]
                path.append(current)
            return path[::-1]                  # start -> goal
        for nxt, cost in neighbours(current):
            tentative = g + cost
            if tentative < g_score.get(nxt, float("inf")):
                came_from[nxt] = current
                g_score[nxt] = tentative
                f = tentative + heuristic(nxt, goal)
                heapq.heappush(open_heap, (f, tentative, nxt))
    return None                                # goal unreachable

Replanning, any-angle motion, and driving

Real environments change and are only partially known, which makes replanning from scratch on every update wasteful. D* and its successors were designed for exactly this: they plan a path in a partially known environment and, when new obstacles appear, repair only the affected part of the search rather than restarting.[12] D* Lite rebuilt the same capability on cleaner theoretical foundations and became the more widely implemented of the two.[13] Grid search also has a cosmetic flaw — paths are constrained to grid edges and come out jagged — which any-angle planners such as Theta* remove by allowing path segments to take any heading, producing shorter, more natural routes.[14]

Autonomous driving stresses all of this at once, because a car cannot turn in place: its plans must respect a minimum turning radius and the direction it is facing. The hybrid A* planner developed for Stanford’s entry in the DARPA Urban Challenge searches over a grid but associates a continuous, kinematically feasible vehicle state with each cell, so that the path it returns can actually be driven.[15] It remains a reference design for motion planning in semi-structured spaces like car parks.

Sampling-based planning for high dimensions

Grids do not survive contact with high-dimensional configuration spaces: the number of cells explodes exponentially with the number of joints. The breakthrough that made planning for real arms and humanoids practical was to stop representing the space explicitly and instead sample it. The probabilistic roadmap (PRM) scatters random collision-free configurations, connects nearby ones into a graph, and then answers many queries by searching that graph — ideal for a fixed workspace queried repeatedly.[16]

For single queries, the rapidly-exploring random tree (RRT), introduced by LaValle in 1998, grows a tree from the start configuration by repeatedly sampling a random point and extending the nearest branch toward it.[17] The sampling geometry gives RRT a beautiful property: it is naturally biased toward the large unexplored regions of the space, so the tree rushes to fill gaps and quickly finds a way through. A bidirectional variant, RRT-Connect, grows two trees — one from the start, one from the goal — and tries to join them, which is dramatically faster in practice.[18] RRTs also extend to systems with dynamics: kinodynamic planning samples in state space and respects the robot’s equations of motion, so the tree’s edges are feasible controls rather than straight lines.[19]

The tree above was grown by the build script for this article; it threaded 90 samples through the obstacle field to reach the goal. Basic RRT has one notable shortcoming, though — it finds a path, not a good one. Karaman and Frazzoli closed that gap in 2011 with RRT* and PRM*, variants that rewire the tree as they sample and are asymptotically optimal: given enough time, the path cost converges to the true minimum.[20] This asymptotic-optimality result reoriented a decade of subsequent planning research. A compact RRT reads almost as simply as A*:

rrt.py
import random
from math import hypot, atan2, cos, sin

def rrt(start, goal, obstacles, bounds,
        step=0.5, goal_bias=0.05, max_iter=5000):
    """Basic RRT in 2-D. obstacles: list of circles (cx, cy, r).
    bounds: (xmin, ymin, xmax, ymax). Returns a path of points or None."""
    xmin, ymin, xmax, ymax = bounds
    nodes, parent = [start], {0: None}

    def collision_free(a, b, n=12):            # sample the segment a -> b
        for i in range(n + 1):
            t = i / n
            x = a[0] + t * (b[0] - a[0])
            y = a[1] + t * (b[1] - a[1])
            for cx, cy, r in obstacles:
                if hypot(x - cx, y - cy) <= r:
                    return False
        return True

    for _ in range(max_iter):
        if random.random() < goal_bias:        # occasionally aim at the goal
            sample = goal
        else:
            sample = (random.uniform(xmin, xmax), random.uniform(ymin, ymax))
        nearest = min(range(len(nodes)),
                      key=lambda i: hypot(nodes[i][0] - sample[0],
                                          nodes[i][1] - sample[1]))
        nx, ny = nodes[nearest]
        theta = atan2(sample[1] - ny, sample[0] - nx)
        new = (nx + step * cos(theta), ny + step * sin(theta))
        if not collision_free((nx, ny), new):
            continue
        nodes.append(new)
        parent[len(nodes) - 1] = nearest
        if hypot(new[0] - goal[0], new[1] - goal[1]) <= step:
            nodes.append(goal)
            parent[len(nodes) - 1] = len(nodes) - 2
            return _trace(nodes, parent)        # connected to the goal
    return None

def _trace(nodes, parent):
    path, i = [], len(nodes) - 1
    while i is not None:
        path.append(nodes[i])
        i = parent[i]
    return path[::-1]

Choosing between them

The two families answer different questions. Grid and graph search — Dijkstra, A*, D* Lite — give resolution-optimal paths and shine in low-dimensional spaces like mobile-robot navigation, where the map is essentially two-dimensional. Sampling-based planners — PRM, RRT, RRT* — trade the guarantee of finding the very best path for the ability to plan at all in the high-dimensional configuration spaces of manipulators. Trajectory Optimization
04

From a path to a motion

A planner returns geometry: an ordered list of collision-free configurations, a polyline through space. A robot cannot execute geometry. It needs a trajectory — a function of time that specifies where every joint should be at every instant, along with the implied velocities and accelerations. Turning the first into the second is the job of trajectory optimization, and it matters for two intensely practical reasons: energy and mechanical stress.

The reason is in the derivatives. A raw waypoint path has sharp corners, and at a sharp corner the robot must decelerate to a stop and accelerate away again, because turning instantly would require infinite acceleration. Those stops waste energy and time. Worse, the higher derivatives of position — acceleration, and its own rate of change, jerk — translate directly into the forces and torques the drivetrain must supply and absorb. Discontinuous jerk means impulsive loads that excite vibration, wear bearings and gears, and in extreme cases damage the mechanism. Smoothing a trajectory is therefore not a cosmetic nicety; it conserves energy and protects the hardware.

Optimization-based smoothing

The modern approach frames trajectory generation as an optimization problem: find the trajectory that minimises a cost — typically a weighted sum of smoothness (integrated squared acceleration or jerk) and obstacle proximity — subject to the constraints that it stay collision-free and respect the robot’s velocity, acceleration and torque limits. Several algorithms attack this in complementary ways. CHOMP treats the trajectory as a point in a space of trajectories and follows the covariant gradient of the cost functional downhill, using a distance field so that even an infeasible initial guess is pulled smoothly out of collision.[21] The mature formulation frames this as covariant Hamiltonian optimization and demonstrated it across real manipulation platforms.[22]

Gradient methods can stick in local minima, so STOMP takes a gradient-free route: it samples noisy perturbations of the current trajectory, evaluates their cost, and combines them into an update, which lets it optimise costs that are not even differentiable — such as discrete constraint violations.[23] A third approach, TrajOpt, poses the problem as sequential convex optimization with a clever formulation of continuous-time collision checking, and is often strikingly fast and reliable in practice.[24] These three — CHOMP, STOMP and TrajOpt — are the workhorses of manipulator trajectory optimization.

Flatness, snap, and reactive smoothing

Aerial robots supply the most elegant special case. A quadrotor is differentially flat, meaning its full state and the motor commands can be written in terms of a handful of output variables and their derivatives. Mellinger and Kumar exploited this to generate minimum-snap trajectories — snap being the fourth derivative of position — because minimising snap directly minimises the aggressive motor inputs a quadrotor needs, yielding trajectories that are both smooth and near the limits of what the vehicle can do.[25] It is a clean illustration of the general principle: choose the derivative you penalise to match the physical quantity you want to spare.

Not all smoothing can be done offline. When the world is changing, the robot needs a local method that reacts within a control cycle. The dynamic window approach does this by searching, at each instant, over the small set of velocities the robot can actually reach given its acceleration limits, scoring each by progress, clearance and speed, and committing to the best.[26] It is the reactive complement to global planning — and it closes the circle back to the reactive tradition of Section 02, now operating as one tier of a hybrid stack.

AI Integration · Perception
05

Seeing well enough to decide

Planning presupposes a world model: obstacles, free space, objects, their poses. For decades that model was assembled with hand-engineered vision that was fragile outside the laboratory. The change came in 2012, when a deep convolutional network — AlexNet — cut the error rate on the ImageNet recognition benchmark so sharply that the entire field of computer vision reorganised around learned features within a couple of years.[27] For robotics this meant that the perception feeding the cognitive layer could suddenly be trusted in cluttered, unstructured scenes.

The capability that matters most for a robot is object detection: not just what is in the image but where, as bounding boxes. Two design philosophies emerged. The two-stage R-CNN line first proposes candidate regions and then classifies each, achieving high accuracy; Faster R-CNN folded the region-proposal step into the network itself, making the approach fast enough to be practical.[28,29] The single-stage philosophy, exemplified by YOLO, instead predicts all boxes and classes in one forward pass over the image, trading a little accuracy for the real-time speed a moving robot needs.[30] A robot that must not collide with a person walking across its path cannot wait a second per frame; the real-time detectors are what make responsive behaviour possible.

Detection is only the start of what manipulation demands. To grasp an object a robot needs its full six-degree-of-freedom pose — position and orientation in space — not merely a box around it in the image, along with segmentation to know precisely which pixels are the object and which are the background it must avoid. These perceptual products are exactly the inputs the planners of Sections 03 and 04 assume: the object’s pose becomes a goal, its geometry becomes an obstacle, and the free space becomes the configuration space to search. Perception and cognition are not separable stages so much as two ends of one pipeline — which is precisely why the field has begun trying to learn them jointly, the subject of Section 07.

AI Integration · Learning
06

Learning to decide

Planning computes a decision from a known model. Reinforcement learning (RL) takes the opposite stance: learn a decision policy from experience, without being told the model in advance. The problem is framed as a Markov decision process — states, actions, a reward signal, and transition dynamics — and the goal is a policy that maximises expected cumulative reward over time.[31] The foundational algorithm, Q-learning, estimates the long-run value of taking each action in each state and improves that estimate from experience, provably converging to the optimal policy under suitable conditions.[32]

Classical RL was confined to small, tabular problems until deep networks were used to approximate the value function. The Deep Q-Network learned to play dozens of Atari games directly from pixels, reaching human-level performance on many of them with no game-specific engineering — the result that launched deep RL as a field.[33] For continuous control, where a robot’s joint torques are real numbers rather than a handful of buttons, policy-gradient methods proved more suitable, and Proximal Policy Optimization became the default: a stable, relatively forgiving algorithm that has trained an enormous range of control policies.[34] The same era produced AlphaGo, which combined deep networks with Monte Carlo tree search to master a game long thought out of reach, a striking demonstration that learning and search are complementary rather than competing.[35]

The reality gap

RL needs enormous amounts of trial and error, and a robot cannot fall over a million times to learn to walk — the hardware would not survive, and the wall-clock time would be prohibitive. The dominant workaround is to train in simulation and transfer to reality, which runs straight into the reality gap: no simulator matches the real world exactly, so a policy tuned to the simulator often fails on the robot. The pivotal idea for crossing it is domain randomization — deliberately varying the simulator’s textures, lighting, masses and friction so widely that the real world looks to the policy like just one more variation it has already learned to handle.[36] The technique underwrote one of the most striking demonstrations in the field, in which a five-fingered robot hand learned in randomised simulation to dexterously reorient objects and transferred that skill to physical hardware.[37]

Learning can also be scaled on real robots directly if enough of them run in parallel. The QT-Opt system trained a vision-based grasping policy across a fleet of arms collecting hundreds of thousands of real grasp attempts, and learned closed-loop behaviours — repositioning objects, pausing to let a disturbance settle — that no one programmed.[38] The broader arc of applying RL to physical systems, and the practical obstacles that make it hard, are surveyed thoroughly by Kober, Bagnell and Peters.[39] In practice a modern training loop leans on a mature ecosystem, and the whole apparatus can be expressed in a few lines:

train_policy.py
import gymnasium as gym
from stable_baselines3 import PPO

# A continuous-control task behind the standard Gymnasium interface.
env = gym.make("Pendulum-v1")

# PPO: a stable policy-gradient method (Schulman et al., 2017).
model = PPO("MlpPolicy", env, verbose=0)
model.learn(total_timesteps=200_000)          # trial-and-error in simulation

# Roll out the learned policy.
obs, _ = env.reset()
for _ in range(1000):
    action, _ = model.predict(obs, deterministic=True)
    obs, reward, terminated, truncated, _ = env.step(action)
    if terminated or truncated:
        obs, _ = env.reset()
AI Integration · Convergence
07

When the layers fold together

Everything so far describes a modular cognitive layer: perception, planning and control as separate components with defined interfaces. The most consequential recent development is the attempt to replace that pipeline with a single learned model that maps sensors and instructions directly to actions. The lever is the transformer architecture that reshaped language and vision, now pointed at robot control.

RT-1 demonstrated that a transformer trained on a large, diverse dataset of real robot demonstrations could absorb many tasks in one network and generalise across them.[40] Its successor RT-2 made the crucial leap: by co-training on web-scale image–text data and robot trajectories, it produced a vision–language–action (VLA) model that inherits semantic knowledge from the internet — it can be told to move an object toward a brand it has only ever read about, and act sensibly.[41] This transfer of web knowledge into physical action is the defining trick of the current generation.

Progress then accelerated through shared data and open models. The Open X-Embodiment effort pooled data from many robots and labs into a single dataset and showed that models trained across embodiments outperform those trained on any one platform — robotics’ version of the lesson that scale and diversity beat bespoke tuning.[42] On that foundation, OpenVLA released an openly available seven-billion-parameter VLA trained on that pooled data, putting a capable generalist policy in the hands of the wider research community.[43] A parallel line pushed on the action representation itself: π0 uses a flow-matching formulation to output smooth, high-frequency continuous actions, aiming at the dexterity and fluidity that discrete action tokens struggle to capture.[44]

Language models also entered the layer from a different direction — as high-level planners. SayCan paired a language model’s knowledge of what steps a task requires with a set of learned skills that estimate whether each step is currently feasible, grounding the model’s suggestions in what the robot can actually do right now.[45] Seen together, these systems are re-litigating the argument of Section 02 at a higher level: the VLA is a learned reincarnation of the deliberative planner, and the open question is once again how to marry its slow, knowledge-rich deliberation with fast reactive control — the hybrid problem, returned in new clothes.

Tools & Software
08

What the layer is actually built on

None of these ideas reaches a robot without infrastructure, and the field has converged on a fairly standard stack. At its centre is the Robot Operating System (ROS) — not an operating system but a middleware that lets many small programs (nodes) communicate over named topics, so that perception, planning and control can be developed independently and composed.[46] Its successor, ROS 2, rebuilt the framework for the reliability, security and real-time needs of products rather than prototypes, and is now the industry default.[47]

On top of that middleware sit the planning frameworks. MoveIt is the de facto motion-planning framework for manipulators, wrapping planners, collision checking and execution into a single interface and lowering the barrier to getting an arm moving.[48] It draws its planners from the Open Motion Planning Library (OMPL), a library of sampling-based algorithms — RRT, PRM and their optimal variants among them — that most manipulation systems ultimately call.[49] For mobile robots, the Nav2 stack provides the navigation counterpart, orchestrating planners, controllers and recovery behaviours with a behavior tree and proven durable enough to run robots on kilometre-scale deployments.[50]

Learning-based cognition adds a second toolchain built on physics simulation, where MuJoCo — an efficient engine for contact-rich, model-based control — is a mainstay for training and evaluating control policies before they ever touch hardware.[51] The table maps the pieces to the jobs they do.

Open Problems
09

What remains hard

The cognitive layer is far from solved, and its open problems are the honest measure of the field. The oldest is the tension between optimality and reactivity: the more thoroughly a robot deliberates, the slower it responds, and the hybrid architectures of Section 02 manage this trade-off rather than dissolving it. The arrival of large, slow VLA models has made the question urgent again — how to get web-scale deliberation to run at control-loop speed.

A second is uncertainty. The clean formulation assumes the robot knows its own state, but perception is noisy and the world is only partially observed. Planning correctly under that uncertainty leads to partially observable models whose exact solution is intractable, so practical systems rely on approximations, and the probabilistic treatment of robot state estimation is a field of its own.[1] A third is safety and verification: a learned policy that performs well on average can still fail in rare, unmodelled situations, and we do not yet have mature ways to certify that a black-box cognitive layer will behave when it matters. A fourth is generalisation — the reason the foundation-model programme is so intensely pursued is precisely that classical planners, for all their guarantees, do not transfer knowledge across tasks the way a human plainly does. Underlying all of these is a body of theory — the mathematics of planning algorithms and their completeness and complexity — that continues to frame what is and is not achievable.[2]

Conclusion
10

The argument, and where it stands

The cognitive layer is where a robot stops reacting and starts deciding. Its history is an argument — deliberation against reaction — that the field settled, provisionally, by building systems that do both: a planner that thinks ahead, a controller that responds now, and an executive that decides which to trust. Path planning gave that layer the means to find a way through space; trajectory optimization taught it to move through space gracefully, sparing energy and hardware; and the integration of learned vision and reinforcement learning gave it, first, eyes it could rely on and, increasingly, the ability to learn its own decisions.

What is happening now is that the boundaries inside the layer are dissolving. The vision–language–action models fold perception, planning and control into a single learned mapping, and in doing so they reopen the founding question of the field at a new scale. The tools have matured, the algorithms are well understood, and the data is finally arriving. The robot’s mind — that thin, decisive layer between the sensors and the motors — is being rebuilt, and the argument that shaped it is very much still alive.
