CH 00 The drift problem

A robot does not perceive the world. It perceives voltages. Everything else — the map, the obstacle, the coffee cup it is about to pick up — is an inference, and every inference is wrong by an amount that grows with time unless something stops it.

That last clause is the whole subject. It is tempting to organise robotics perception around sensors, as though the field were a catalogue of transducers, or around algorithms, as though it were a branch of statistics. Both framings miss the thing that actually structures the discipline. Perception in robotics is a running fight against the accumulation of error, and every component in a modern stack — every encoder, every gyroscope, every lidar, every loop-closure detector — earns its place by how it trades one kind of error for another.

The fight has a clean structure. On one side sit the proprioceptive sensors: encoders, inertial measurement units, joint torque sensors, motor current. They report the robot's own internal state. They are fast, cheap, always available, immune to fog and darkness, and they never fail to produce a number. They are also, without exception, integrative. They measure a derivative — a wheel increment, an angular rate, a specific force — and to recover a pose you have to integrate. Integration accumulates. A gyroscope with a bias of one hundredth of a degree per second is an excellent gyroscope, and it will still have rotated your world by a full degree after a hundred seconds, silently, with no indication that anything has gone wrong.

On the other side sit the exteroceptive sensors: cameras, lidar, radar, sonar, tactile skin. They look outward. They are drift-free in the sense that matters — a wall observed at three metres is at three metres, regardless of how long the robot has been running — but they are ambiguous, expensive, slow, and each of them has a set of physical conditions under which it returns confident nonsense. A camera in a dark tunnel. A lidar in a dust cloud. A sonar aimed at a pane of glass at forty degrees. The failure is rarely a missing measurement; it is usually a plausible one.

The synthesis of the two is what the rest of this article is about. Fuse a fast, drifting proprioceptive stream with a slow, drift-free exteroceptive one and you get odometry that is better than either. Add the ability to recognise a place you have been before, and you get something categorically new: an estimator whose error no longer grows with the distance travelled. That capability is called loop closure, and it is the single most important idea in the field. Simultaneous localisation and mapping is the machinery built around it.

The chicken and the egg

To build a map you need to know where you are. To know where you are you need a map. SLAM is not a clever way to break the circularity — it is the observation that you do not have to. Pose the whole thing as a single joint estimation problem over trajectory and map, and the circularity dissolves into a large, sparse, well-conditioned optimisation.1

Two warnings before we start. First, this is an engineering discipline, and almost every failure I have seen in a deployed perception system was a calibration or timing bug rather than an algorithmic one. The mathematics in section 5 is elegant; the reason your robot is driving into a wall is that the lidar timestamps are 40 ms stale. Second, the sensors do not care about the taxonomy. An IMU strapped to a camera becomes, jointly, something neither is alone. The categories below are a way of organising your thinking, not a description of how the hardware behaves.2

CH 01 A short history of machines that look

Between 1966 and 1972, a group at SRI International built a wheeled robot roughly the size of a filing cabinet and called it Shakey, reportedly because of how it moved. Shakey carried a vidicon television camera, an optical rangefinder, and bump detectors, and it was connected by radio to a mainframe that did the thinking.3 Its perception was, by modern standards, almost unbelievably fragile: it worked in a purpose-built world of large, matte, uniformly coloured blocks and wedges, under carefully controlled lighting, and it found edges by thresholding intensity gradients. But Shakey did something no machine had done before. It looked at a scene, built a symbolic model of what was in it, planned a sequence of actions over that model, and executed them — and when the world failed to match its expectations, it re-planned. The architecture of perception feeding a world model feeding a planner is Shakey's, and it has survived essentially unchanged.

What Shakey did not have was any real notion of uncertainty. It believed what it saw.

A decade later, Hans Moravec's Stanford Cart introduced the idea that changed that. The Cart carried a single camera on a horizontal slider; at each stop it took nine images from nine positions along the slider, extracted interest points, and triangulated their depths.4 Crucially, Moravec did not treat the resulting depths as facts. He tracked them with uncertainty, and let the confidence in a feature grow as it was re-observed from more viewpoints. The Cart moved roughly a metre every ten to fifteen minutes, mostly spent waiting for the computer, and it drifted badly, and it occasionally drove into things. It also produced the first genuinely probabilistic map of a real environment built by a machine.

Moravec later gave the field its most durable aphorism. The hard problems in artificial intelligence, he observed, are not the ones that feel hard to us. Reasoning and symbolic manipulation — chess, logic, integration — are comparatively easy to automate, because they are evolutionarily recent and we perform them with conscious effort. Perception and mobility are brutally hard, because a billion years of selection built them and we experience them as effortless.5 Every generation of roboticists rediscovers this the first time they try to make a machine pick up a sock.

Field note · the mathematics arrived before the robots

Rudolf Kalman published his recursive filter in 1960, six years before Shakey was switched on.6 It was developed for aerospace navigation, and it reached the Apollo guidance computer long before it reached a mobile robot. Much of what follows is the story of robotics slowly discovering that the estimation theory it needed had been sitting in the controls literature the whole time.

The 1980s supplied the two representations that still underpin the field. In 1985 Moravec and Alberto Elfes took the worst sensor available to them — a ring of Polaroid ultrasonic rangefinders, with a beam width of about thirty degrees and a pathological tendency to specular reflection — and made it work by refusing to treat any single reading as a measurement of anything. Instead they discretised the world into a grid of cells, and let each sonar return vote probabilistically on the occupancy of every cell in its cone.7 Fuse enough bad measurements and a good map falls out. Elfes formalised this as the occupancy grid, and made the crucial point that free space is as important as occupied space and must be represented explicitly, because free space is what you plan through.8

In 1986, Randall Smith and Peter Cheeseman published the other half. Their paper on the representation of spatial uncertainty made the observation that estimates of landmark positions are correlated with each other, because they are all measured from the same drifting robot.9 Two landmarks seen from the same erroneous pose share that error. You therefore cannot maintain a set of independent landmark estimates; you must maintain a joint distribution over the robot pose and all landmarks, correlations included. That joint distribution — the "stochastic map" — is the SLAM problem, and by the mid-1990s the community had a name for it.10

The 1990s and 2000s were spent making it tractable. Filtering the joint distribution directly with an extended Kalman filter is O(n²) per update in the number of landmarks, because the covariance matrix is dense — every landmark is correlated with every other — and that ceiling arrives at a few hundred landmarks.11 Three escapes were found. Feng Lu and Evangelos Milios reformulated mapping as a network of relative-pose constraints to be relaxed globally, which is the pose graph.12 Michael Montemerlo and colleagues exploited a conditional independence — given the full robot path, the landmarks are independent of one another — to factor the problem into a particle filter over paths with a tiny Kalman filter per landmark per particle.13 And the community eventually noticed that the information matrix, unlike the covariance matrix, is naturally sparse, which turns the whole thing into a sparse nonlinear least-squares problem that modern solvers eat for breakfast.14

Then, in October 2005, a Volkswagen Touareg named Stanley drove 212 kilometres across the Mojave desert without a human in it, and the field changed character.15 Stanley's perception was not exotic: five roof-mounted single-plane laser scanners, a colour camera for looking further ahead than the lasers could reach, radar, GPS and an inertial unit. What was exotic was the discipline. The lasers were fused into a grid, the camera was trained — online, from the laser data — to classify drivable terrain, and the whole thing was tuned against logged human driving. Two years later, at the DARPA Urban Challenge, Carnegie Mellon's Boss navigated live traffic with a sensor payload that included a newly available spinning 64-beam lidar, and the modern autonomous-vehicle stack — dense 360-degree lidar, multiple radars, camera ring, tight inertial fusion — snapped into the shape it still has.16

The rest of the story is compression and learning. Depth sensing fell from tens of thousands of dollars to $150 when Microsoft shipped the Kinect in 2010.17 Within a year, KinectFusion was building dense, real-time, drift-corrected 3-D reconstructions on a consumer GPU.18 MEMS inertial units became free, because every phone needed one. And a 2016 survey by Cesar Cadena and colleagues took stock, named the era it hoped was coming — the "robust-perception age" — and was honest about how far away it still was.1 That paper remains the best single map of the territory, and much of what it listed as open is still open.

CH 02 Proprioception: the robot's inner ear

Close your eyes and touch your nose. You did not need to see your hand, because you have a continuously updated model of where your limbs are, assembled from muscle spindles, Golgi tendon organs and the vestibular apparatus. That is proprioception, and a robot needs the machine equivalent for exactly the same reason: it is the only sensing that is always there, at every instant, with no latency and no dependence on what the world happens to be doing.

Encoders, and the lie of the wheel

The rotary encoder is the most boring sensor in robotics and the one most likely to be quietly wrong. An incremental optical encoder puts a slotted disc between an LED and a pair of photodetectors offset so that their outputs, A and B, are ninety degrees out of phase. The phase relationship gives direction; counting edges gives displacement. Decode both edges of both channels — the classic four-times quadrature scheme — and you get four counts per slot.

The failure mode that matters is not noise. It is the missed edge. If the shaft accelerates faster than your interrupt handler can service, the state machine skips a step, and the count is silently wrong forever after. This is why a real decoder distinguishes an illegal transition from a legal one and counts the illegal ones rather than guessing. A glitch counter that stays at zero across a full-speed run is the only evidence you have that your odometry means anything.

An absolute encoder solves a different problem: it reports position rather than change, usually by reading a Gray-coded disc or a magnetic pattern, so it survives a power cycle without a homing routine. Joint robots use them for exactly that reason — a seven-axis arm that forgets where it is on reboot is a hazard.

What no encoder can tell you is whether the wheel was touching anything. Wheel odometry converts shaft rotation into displacement through a kinematic model — wheel radius, track width, gear ratio — and every term in that model is a lie of a few per cent.19 Johann Borenstein and Liqiang Feng separated the two failure classes precisely: systematic errors, dominated by unequal effective wheel diameters and by uncertainty in the effective track width, which are deterministic and can be calibrated out with a bidirectional square-path test; and non-systematic errors, dominated by wheel slip, which cannot.20 A drive wheel spinning on a patch of oil reports confident, precise, entirely fictional motion. No amount of filtering fixes that, because the sensor is not noisy — it is lying.

The IMU, and why integrating it twice is a bad idea

A strapdown inertial measurement unit is three accelerometers and three rate gyroscopes, rigidly attached to the body. The gyros measure angular rate; the accelerometers measure specific force, which is the sum of true acceleration and the reaction to gravity, and cannot distinguish between them. That last fact is the source of nearly every difficulty that follows.

The standard strapdown mechanisation is: integrate the gyros to get attitude, use that attitude to rotate the accelerometer readings into the world frame, subtract gravity, and integrate twice to get position.21 Each of those steps compounds the error of the previous one. Oliver Woodman's technical report is the clearest analysis of what that costs, and the arithmetic is worth doing yourself, because the answer is genuinely shocking.22

Why open-loop inertial navigation dies in about a minute

Accelerometer bias. A constant bias ba integrates twice into a position error of bat²/2. A bias of 1 mg — 9.8 mm/s², which is a perfectly ordinary consumer MEMS part — gives 4.4 m after 30 s and 17.6 m after 60 s.

Gyro bias is far worse. A constant gyro bias bg makes the attitude estimate tilt at θ(t) = bgt. A tilted attitude estimate fails to fully cancel gravity, so a component g sin θ ≈ g bgt leaks into the horizontal channel. Integrate that twice and position error grows as g bgt³/6 — cubically.

Take bg = 0.01 °/s, an unremarkable number. After 60 s: (1/6) × 9.81 × 1.75×10−4 × 60³ ≈ 62 m. At one minute, the gyro bias — acting purely through gravity leakage — has contributed about three and a half times more position error than the accelerometer bias, and the gap widens without limit, because one term is cubic and the other is merely quadratic. This is why attitude estimation is the whole game in inertial navigation, and why a good IMU is one with a good gyro.

The practical consequence is stark. Nobody navigates open-loop on a MEMS IMU for more than a few seconds. What the IMU is for is filling in the gaps between exteroceptive updates: it is the sensor that tells you what happened during the 100 ms between lidar sweeps, and that propagates the state across a motion blur that blinded the camera. It is a short-term extrapolator, not a navigator, and every successful architecture treats it that way.

Reading a datasheet you can trust: the Allan variance

IMU datasheets are marketing documents. The honest characterisation is the Allan deviation, borrowed from the frequency-standards community and standardised for inertial sensors by the IEEE.23 The recipe is: log the sensor at rest for a couple of hours, integrate the rate to get angle, and compute the deviation of the second difference of that angle over averaging windows of length τ. Plot on log-log axes and the noise processes separate themselves out by slope.24

Three numbers come out. The angle random walk N, read at τ = 1 s, is the white noise; it is what a Kalman filter's process-noise covariance Q should be built from. The bias instability B, taken at the minimum of the curve and scaled by 1/0.664, is the floor — the amount of bias you simply cannot estimate away. The rate random walk K, on the rising branch, tells you how fast the bias wanders, and therefore how aggressively your filter must keep re-estimating it. A filter tuned with numbers invented at a whiteboard will either trust the IMU too much and drift, or too little and become a low-pass filter on the camera. Tuned from a real Allan plot, it will not.

Attitude: complementary filters and the yaw problem

The gyro knows how the robot is turning but not which way is down. The accelerometer knows which way is down, but only when the robot is not accelerating — and a robot that is not accelerating is a robot that is not doing anything. The classical resolution is a complementary filter: take the high-frequency content of the gyro and the low-frequency content of the accelerometer. Robert Mahony's formulation does this directly on the rotation group SO(3), which sidesteps gimbal lock entirely and estimates the gyro bias as a by-product of the same feedback loop.25 Sebastian Madgwick's gradient-descent variant is the one that ended up in every hobby flight controller, for good reasons of computational cost.26

There is a hard limit here that costs people weeks of debugging, and it is not a tuning issue. Gravity carries no information about heading. Roll and pitch are observable from an accelerometer; yaw is not. A gyro-only yaw estimate will walk away at the bias-instability rate, forever, and no amount of accelerometer weighting will pull it back. You need a magnetometer (which is unreliable indoors and near motors), a wheel or visual heading, GNSS, or a loop closure. There is no fourth option. When I ran the filter above against a synthetic gyro with a small z-axis bias, the residual attitude error after 200 seconds was a 5.6 ° rotation whose axis was 96% aligned with gravity — pure, unrecoverable yaw. Roll and pitch were held to within a fraction of a degree.

For anything serious, the modern practice is an error-state Kalman filter: track the nominal state on the manifold and the small error in a local tangent-space parameterisation, which keeps the linearisation valid and the covariance minimal.27 Better still, use an invariant EKF, whose error dynamics are independent of the estimated state and which therefore has provable convergence properties that the ordinary EKF simply does not.28 For legged robots, where the contact points give intermittent, high-quality velocity constraints, the invariant formulation with contact-aided updates has become close to standard.29

Force, torque, and contact

The proprioceptive channel that manipulation cares about most is force. Joint torque sensing — whether from strain gauges, series-elastic elements, or estimated from motor current — is what lets a robot press a peg into a hole without snapping it, and what lets a legged robot know that its foot has actually landed. Contact detection is the quiet workhorse of legged state estimation: a foot in stable contact with the ground is a zero-velocity constraint, and a zero-velocity constraint is free information that bounds IMU drift very effectively. The whole family of leg-odometry systems is built on exploiting it.30

CH 03 Exteroception: photons, echoes, and contact

Exteroceptive sensors are the only thing standing between a robot and unbounded drift, and each of them is a bargain with physics. You can have angular resolution or you can have all-weather robustness. You can have direct metric depth or you can have semantic richness. Nobody gets both from one device, which is why every serious platform carries several and why the interesting engineering is in the fusion.

Cameras: the most information per dollar, and the least metric truth

A camera is a device for throwing away a dimension. The pinhole model maps a 3-D point to an image point by perspective projection, which means a single image constrains a world point to a ray and nothing more. Depth is gone. Recovering it — from two views, from motion, from a learned prior, from anything — is what most of computer vision is about.31,32

Before any of that works, the camera must be calibrated. Zhengyou Zhang's 2000 method — wave a planar checkerboard in front of the lens from a dozen orientations, solve for intrinsics and distortion in closed form, then refine by nonlinear least squares — is still the standard, and is what sits inside OpenCV's calibration module.33 Get it wrong and every downstream geometric algorithm inherits a bias it cannot detect. A focal length that is 1% off produces a reconstruction that is 1% off in scale, consistently, in a way that looks exactly like correct output.

Pitfall · the rolling shutter

Most CMOS sensors expose the image row by row rather than all at once. On a static camera this is invisible. On a robot it means every row of the image was captured from a different pose. At 30 fps with a 20 ms readout, a camera panning at 60 °/s smears more than a degree of rotation across the frame. Feature-based SLAM will happily converge on a geometrically inconsistent solution and report a small reprojection error while doing it. Either buy a global-shutter sensor — which is the correct answer for any robot that moves — or model the readout explicitly in the bundle adjustment.34

What cameras give you, uniquely, is correspondence. Detect distinctive points, describe their local appearance, match them across frames, and you have constraints. SIFT set the standard for what a descriptor should be — invariant to scale and rotation, robust to moderate illumination and viewpoint change.35 ORB made it fast enough for real time by pairing a FAST corner detector with a rotation-aware binary descriptor that can be matched with a Hamming distance, which is one XOR and a popcount.36 For dense, small-baseline tracking, the Lucas–Kanade formulation from 1981 is still what everyone actually runs.37 And because matching is never clean, RANSAC — fit a model to a minimal random subset, count inliers, repeat — remains the load-bearing robustness mechanism of the entire visual pipeline.38

The learned versions have not so much replaced these as widened their operating envelope. SuperPoint learns detection and description jointly and self-supervises through homographic warping;39 SuperGlue treats matching as an optimal-transport problem on a graph and solves it with attention, which lets it match across viewpoint and illumination changes that would defeat any nearest-neighbour descriptor search.40 They cost more compute and they win where classical matching simply fails.

Depth: three ways to get the third dimension back

Stereo is triangulation from a known baseline. Rectify two images so that corresponding points lie on the same row, search along the row for the horizontal offset d (the disparity), and depth follows as Z = fB/d. Structured light replaces the second camera with a projector. The original Kinect projected a fixed pseudo-random dot pattern in the near infrared and triangulated the deformation of that pattern against a factory-stored reference.17 The physics is the same as stereo — it is still triangulation over a baseline, so it still has the Z² wall — but the projector guarantees texture, which removes stereo's worst failure mode: blank walls. It also breaks in sunlight, because the sun is a far brighter infrared source than the projector.

Time of flight abandons triangulation entirely and measures the round-trip time of a light pulse or the phase shift of a modulated one.41 Depth error is now largely independent of range, which is a fundamentally better scaling law. The costs are multipath — light that bounces off two surfaces before returning reports a depth that is the mixture of both, which is why ToF cameras hallucinate rounded corners — and interference between multiple sensors.

Lidar: the workhorse

A 3-D lidar is a time-of-flight rangefinder swept across the scene, historically by physically spinning a stack of laser-detector pairs. The 64-beam spinning units that appeared around the Urban Challenge produce on the order of a million points per second, each one a metric range measurement good to a couple of centimetres, independent of ambient light.42 That combination — dense, direct, metric, and indifferent to darkness — is why lidar dominates ground-vehicle mapping.

The industry's direction of travel is away from spinning mechanics — towards MEMS mirrors, optical phased arrays, and flash architectures — and towards FMCW, for the per-point velocity. Per-point velocity is a bigger deal than it sounds: it lets you segment moving objects from a single sweep, without tracking, which is exactly the information a classical lidar throws away.

What lidar cannot do: see colour, read a sign, distinguish a painted lane line from the asphalt around it, or survive a dust cloud. Airborne particulate returns energy. To a lidar, thick dust is a wall.

Radar: the ugly sensor that saves you

Automotive radar operates around 77 GHz with an FMCW waveform. Range comes from the beat frequency, radial velocity comes directly from the Doppler shift across a chirp sequence, and angle comes from a MIMO array that synthesises a large virtual aperture from a small physical one.44 The angular resolution is dreadful compared to lidar — degrees, not tenths of a degree — and the returns are sparse, noisy, and full of multipath ghosts.

And it does not care about dust, fog, rain, smoke, or darkness. Millimetre waves pass straight through the particulate that stops light dead. In the DARPA Subterranean Challenge, where teams drove robots through smoke-filled tunnels and dust-choked mines, radar and thermal imaging were what kept the state estimator alive when the lidar and the cameras had both gone white.45 The Doppler channel alone is enough to estimate ego-velocity directly, without any correspondence at all, which is an extraordinarily robust thing to have in your back pocket when everything else has failed.46 Radar-based odometry and place recognition now have their own benchmark data, which is a fair sign the modality has stopped being an afterthought.47

Sonar, and a note on respecting bad sensors

Ultrasonic rangefinders are cheap, slow, and have a beam so wide that a return tells you only that something is somewhere within a thirty-degree cone. They suffer specular reflection: a smooth surface at an oblique angle reflects the pulse away entirely and reads as empty space. And yet the first probabilistic maps in robotics were built from them, precisely because Moravec and Elfes stopped trying to treat each return as a measurement and started treating it as weak evidence.7 John Leonard and Hugh Durrant-Whyte's work on tracking geometric beacons made the complementary point: if you model which physical feature produced a return — a plane, a corner, an edge — even sonar becomes a precise localisation sensor.48 The lesson generalises. There is no such thing as a bad sensor, only a bad sensor model.

Event cameras: throwing away the frame

A conventional camera samples every pixel at a fixed rate whether or not anything changed, which is both wasteful and slow. An event camera does the opposite: each pixel independently and asynchronously emits an event the moment its log-intensity changes by more than a threshold.49 There are no frames. The output is a sparse stream of (x, y, t, polarity) tuples with microsecond timestamps and a dynamic range beyond 120 dB.

The properties are exactly what a fast robot wants: no motion blur, microsecond latency, and the ability to see simultaneously into a dark tunnel and out at the sunlit exit — a scene that saturates any conventional sensor.50 The catch is that the entire toolbox of computer vision assumes frames, and essentially none of it applies unchanged. Event-based perception is a genuinely open research area rather than a product category.

Touch: the sensor you only need at the last centimetre

Manipulation is where vision runs out. At the moment of contact the object is occluded by the hand that is grasping it, and the information that matters — is it slipping, how hard am I squeezing, did I actually seat the peg — is not visible from any camera.

The most productive recent idea has been to make touch a vision problem. GelSight and its descendants press a soft elastomer against the object and photograph the inside of that elastomer with an ordinary camera under controlled illumination; the deformation of the gel is reconstructed as a height map, at a spatial resolution finer than a human fingertip.51 DIGIT shrank the concept to something that fits on a robot finger and can be manufactured cheaply.52 A different lineage — the BioTac — uses a fluid-filled bladder with impedance electrodes plus a hydro-acoustic pressure sensor, and detects the micro-vibrations of incipient slip, which is the signal a human uses to tighten a grip before an object falls.53

CH 04 Calibration and time: the unglamorous prerequisites

Here is a claim I will defend: more deployed perception systems are broken by extrinsics and timestamps than by anything in the estimation literature. This section is short because the ideas are simple, and it is here because they are the ideas that will actually bite you.

Extrinsics

Fusing two sensors requires knowing the rigid transform between them to a precision better than the accuracy you want out. A lidar-to-camera extrinsic that is off by half a degree puts a point at 20 m into the wrong lane. The transform must be measured, not read off the CAD model, because the CAD model does not know that the bracket flexed when it was torqued down.

For camera-to-camera and camera-to-IMU, the reference tool is Kalibr, which estimates intrinsics, extrinsics, and the time offset between the camera and the IMU in one continuous-time batch optimisation.54 Its extension handles multiple IMUs and individual-axis scale factors.55 For camera-to-lidar, a single shot of a scene containing several checkerboards is enough to constrain the transform.56 For a camera mounted on a robot arm — the hand-eye problem — the classical formulation is AX = XB, solved by Tsai and Lenz in 1989 and still solved essentially that way.57 AprilTag remains the workhorse fiducial for all of this: it recovers a full 6-DoF pose from a single small printed marker and is robust to occlusion and lighting.58

Pitfall · time is a sensor reading too

Two sensors on the same robot do not share a clock. A USB camera timestamps at the moment the driver receives the frame, which is after exposure, after readout, and after an unpredictable transport delay. A lidar timestamps at the start of a sweep, but the last point in that sweep was measured 100 ms later, from a different pose.

The consequences are quantitative. At 15 m/s, a 20 ms timing error is a 30 cm position error — roughly the width of a pedestrian. Three things fix it, in decreasing order of effectiveness: hardware triggering, so the sensors are physically synchronised; a proper time-transfer protocol such as PTP; and treating the time offset as a state variable and estimating it online, which is what Kalibr does at calibration time and what good VIO systems do continuously.59

And regardless: every lidar sweep from a moving platform must be motion-compensated — each point de-skewed to a common reference time using the best available motion estimate — before it is used for anything. Skipping this step is the most common cause of a lidar odometry system that "almost works".

CH 05 Estimation: turning measurements into belief

Every estimator in robotics is the same algorithm wearing different clothes. You maintain a probability distribution over the state. When the robot moves, you push that distribution through a motion model, which makes it wider, because motion is uncertain. When a measurement arrives, you multiply the distribution by the likelihood of that measurement, which makes it narrower, because information is information. Then you normalise and repeat.60

The Gaussian family

Assume the belief is Gaussian and the models are linear, and the integral above collapses into linear algebra: this is the Kalman filter, and it is not merely convenient but provably optimal under those assumptions.6 The Kalman gain is the ratio of how much you trust the model to how much you trust the sensor, and everything else is bookkeeping.

Robots are not linear. The extended Kalman filter linearises the models about the current estimate at every step, which works fine when the estimate is close and fails badly when it is not — and the failure is insidious, because a diverging EKF reports a small, shrinking covariance right up until it is completely lost. The unscented Kalman filter avoids the Jacobians by propagating a deterministically chosen set of sigma points through the true nonlinear model and refitting a Gaussian, which is both more accurate and easier to get right.61

Three details in that code separate a working EKF from a diverging one, and none of them are the Kalman equations. The innovation on a bearing measurement must be wrapped, because the difference between 179 ° and −179 ° is 2 °, not 358 °. Measurements must be gated: a chi-square test on the Mahalanobis distance of the innovation, rejecting anything beyond a threshold. And the covariance update should use the Joseph form, which stays symmetric and positive semi-definite in finite precision when the naive form does not.62 Run that filter against a simulated robot and it holds a couple of centimetres of error indefinitely; remove the gate, mis-associate one landmark, and it never recovers, because a Kalman filter has no mechanism for un-fusing a measurement it should not have believed.

When Gaussians are the wrong shape

Consider a robot in a long, symmetric corridor with identical doors. Its true belief is genuinely bimodal — it is at one end or the other, with equal probability — and it will stay bimodal until a distinguishing feature arrives. A Gaussian cannot represent that. An EKF asked to do so will average the two hypotheses and confidently place the robot in the middle of a wall.

Monte Carlo localisation represents the belief as a weighted set of samples, which can be any shape at all.60 Move: push every particle through the motion model with its own noise draw. Correct: weight each particle by the likelihood of the observation given that particle's pose. Resample: replace the set by drawing proportional to weight. That is the whole algorithm, and it solves the global-localisation and kidnapped-robot problems that the EKF cannot even express.

The two implementation details that matter are both about diversity. Resample only when the effective sample size collapses — resampling every step throws away hypotheses you will need later, and is the most common way to kill a particle filter. And use low-variance (systematic) resampling rather than naive multinomial draws, because it preserves the sample distribution far better at the same cost.

Stop throwing away the past

A filter is Markovian. It carries a single belief forward and discards everything behind it. That is exactly what you want on an embedded processor with a fixed budget, and it is exactly what makes loop closure impossible: when the robot recognises a place it visited ten minutes ago, a filter has no representation of "ten minutes ago" to correct.

The alternative is to keep all the constraints and re-solve. Write the whole problem as a factor graph: a bipartite graph whose variable nodes are the things you want to estimate (poses, velocities, biases, landmarks) and whose factor nodes are the probabilistic constraints between them (an odometry measurement, a reprojection, a loop closure, a prior).14 The maximum a posteriori estimate is then the assignment of variables that maximises the product of all factors, and under Gaussian noise assumptions taking the negative log turns that product into a sum of squared residuals. SLAM is a sparse nonlinear least-squares problem. That is the whole insight, and it is the one that made everything after 2010 possible.

Why is it sparse? Because a measurement touches only the few variables it physically involves. A camera at pose X3 sees landmark ℓ7; that produces a residual coupling exactly those two variables and nothing else. The information matrix — the inverse covariance — therefore has a nonzero block only where variables co-occur in a factor, and in a real trajectory that is a vanishingly small fraction of all pairs. (The covariance matrix, by contrast, is dense: everything is correlated with everything, which is precisely why EKF-SLAM chokes.)

The tooling is mature. GTSAM builds on the factor-graph formulation directly and provides iSAM2, which maintains a Bayes tree and re-linearises only the variables a new factor actually touches — so adding a loop closure that affects 300 poses costs 300 poses' worth of work, not the whole map.63,64 g2o and Ceres attack the same problem as general sparse optimisers.65,66 The incremental idea traces back to iSAM.67

Manifolds, and why you cannot just add rotations

A rotation is not a vector. You cannot add a small rotation to a big one by adding their components, and if you try, your state will drift off the rotation group and your covariance will stop meaning anything. The correct machinery is Lie theory: represent the state on the manifold, represent the error in the tangent space at the current estimate, and move between them with the exponential and logarithm maps. All the familiar formulae come back, with + replaced by ⊕.68,69

The second manifold idea that made modern VIO possible is IMU preintegration. An IMU runs at 200 Hz; a camera at 20 Hz. Naively, that means 10 IMU factors between each pair of camera poses, and every one of them must be re-integrated from scratch whenever the linearisation point changes. Christian Forster and colleagues showed how to integrate the IMU measurements between two keyframes into a single relative-motion factor, on the manifold, in a way that can be corrected for a change in the bias estimate without re-integrating.70 That one trick is what makes tightly coupled visual-inertial optimisation real-time.

CH 06 SLAM: the map and the chicken-and-egg

Every SLAM system, without exception, has the same two halves.

The front end is where the sensors live. It turns raw measurements into geometric constraints: it tracks features, registers scans, estimates frame-to-frame motion, and — critically — proposes loop closures by recognising previously visited places. The front end is where all the domain-specific engineering is, and it is where systems differ.

The back end takes those constraints and finds the trajectory and map that best explain them. The back end is domain-agnostic: it is sparse nonlinear least squares, and a good one does not care whether the constraints came from a lidar or a camera.71

Most catastrophic SLAM failures are front-end failures. The back end is usually doing exactly what you asked it to.

Registration: the front end's core operation

Given two point clouds, find the rigid transform that aligns them. The Iterative Closest Point algorithm alternates between assigning each source point to its nearest target point and solving for the transform that minimises the distances.72 Its point-to-plane variant minimises instead the distance from each source point to the tangent plane at its target, which lets the two surfaces slide across each other — and on the flat walls and floors that dominate every real scan, that is worth an order of magnitude in convergence rate.73 Szymon Rusinkiewicz and Marc Levoy's survey of the variants remains the practical reference.74

Generalised-ICP models the local surface structure on both clouds and gives a plane-to-plane cost that is more robust to sampling differences.75 The Normal Distributions Transform takes a completely different route: it converts the target cloud into a grid of local Gaussians and maximises the likelihood of the source points under that mixture, which makes the cost function smooth and removes the nearest-neighbour search entirely.76

The check written into that code — inspecting the eigenvalues of ATA before trusting the solve — is the one everybody leaves out. In a long, featureless corridor, or a tunnel, or the middle of a flat field, the geometry does not constrain motion along one direction. The Hessian is rank-deficient, the solution slides freely along the null space, and ICP reports a confident pose that is metres wrong. Ji Zhang, Michael Kaess and Sanjiv Singh characterised this precisely and showed how to detect and constrain the degenerate directions rather than letting them corrupt the estimate.77 Recent work makes localisability a first-class quantity computed before the optimisation commits.78

The 2-D lineage: grids, particles, and submaps

For a wheeled robot on a flat floor with a planar lidar, the problem is small enough to solve almost exactly. GMapping uses a Rao-Blackwellised particle filter — each particle carries a complete trajectory hypothesis and its own occupancy grid — with a proposal distribution that folds in the current scan before sampling, which cuts the required particle count by an order of magnitude.79 It is still what runs on a great many warehouse robots.

Google's Cartographer took the graph route: build small locally consistent submaps from consecutive scans, then run branch-and-bound scan matching to find loop closures against all previous submaps efficiently enough to do it continuously, and optimise the pose graph in the background.80 SLAM Toolbox is the maintained ROS 2 descendant of this line and adds lifelong mapping — the ability to keep refining a map across sessions rather than rebuilding it.81

Visual SLAM

Start with the easier problem. Visual odometry asks only for incremental motion: match features between consecutive frames, estimate the relative pose that explains them, compose, repeat. David Nistér's 2004 paper gave the problem its name and its first real-time pipeline, and fixed the shape everyone still uses — detect, match, estimate under RANSAC, refine.82 Davide Scaramuzza and Friedrich Fraundorfer's two-part tutorial remains the best entry point to the mechanics.83 But VO is odometry: it has no map and no memory, so it drifts without bound, exactly like the wheel encoders it replaces. SLAM is what you get when you add the memory.

Andrew Davison's MonoSLAM was the first system to do SLAM from a single hand-held camera at frame rate, using an EKF over the camera pose and a sparse set of landmarks.84 Georg Klein and David Murray's PTAM then made the observation that reorganised the field: tracking and mapping do not need to run at the same rate. Split them into two threads — track the camera against the existing map at 30 Hz, and run bundle adjustment over keyframes in the background as fast as it can go — and you can afford a far better optimiser.85 Every modern system is built this way.

ORB-SLAM assembled the pieces into the system that became the reference: ORB features throughout, three threads (tracking, local mapping, loop closing), a covisibility graph to keep local bundle adjustment bounded, and bag-of-words place recognition for loop detection.86 ORB-SLAM3 added tightly coupled inertial fusion and a multi-map "Atlas" that lets the system survive tracking loss by starting a new map and merging it later when it recognises where it is.87

A parallel lineage rejected features entirely. Direct methods optimise photometric error — the raw intensity difference between images — rather than reprojection error on detected keypoints. LSD-SLAM built semi-dense maps this way;88 DSO formulated it as a fully joint photometric bundle adjustment that also estimates exposure and vignetting, and it works in textureless scenes where a corner detector finds nothing at all.89 SVO split the difference, using direct alignment for speed and features for the map.90 The learned end of this lineage is DROID-SLAM, which replaces the hand-built front end with a recurrent network that iteratively refines dense pixel correspondences and camera poses through a differentiable bundle-adjustment layer — keeping the geometric back end and learning everything that feeds it.91 Its correspondence machinery descends directly from RAFT, the optical-flow architecture that made all-pairs iterative refinement practical.92

The scale problem

A single moving camera cannot recover metric scale. A large scene viewed from far away and a small scene viewed from close up produce identical images, and monocular SLAM reconstructs the world only up to an unknown scale factor — which then drifts, because nothing pins it down. There are exactly four fixes: a second camera (a known baseline), an IMU (gravity has a known magnitude), a known object in the scene, or a depth sensor. This is why almost every serious monocular system is really a visual-inertial system.87

Visual-inertial odometry

Cameras and IMUs are almost perfectly complementary. The IMU is fast, works in the dark, works during motion blur, and drifts. The camera is slow, needs light and texture, and does not drift. Fuse them tightly — jointly estimating pose, velocity, and IMU biases in a single optimisation — and each covers the other's failure mode.

Anastasios Mourikis and Stergios Roumeliotis's MSCKF keeps a sliding window of past camera poses in the filter state and marginalises the features out, giving a complexity linear in the number of features rather than quadratic — which is what made VIO run on the hardware of 2007, and is still the basis of a great deal of what flies.93 OKVIS moved to keyframe-based nonlinear optimisation with marginalisation of old states.94 VINS-Mono packaged tightly coupled sliding-window optimisation with preintegrated IMU factors, a careful initialisation procedure, and a four-degree-of-freedom pose-graph loop closure (roll and pitch are observable from gravity, so only position and yaw need correcting) into something robust enough to run on a phone.95 ROVIO took the direct, filter-based route.96 Kimera added a real-time 3-D mesh and semantic labels on top.97

Lidar SLAM

LOAM's key idea was to stop treating all points as equal. Classify each point by local surface roughness into edge and planar features, and match edges to lines and planes to planes. Then split the computation: run a fast, coarse scan-to-scan odometry at the sensor rate, and a slow, accurate scan-to-map refinement an order of magnitude slower, and compose them.98 That two-rate architecture dominated lidar odometry for the better part of a decade. LeGO-LOAM specialised it for ground vehicles by segmenting the ground plane and solving for its constrained degrees of freedom separately;99 LIO-SAM restructured the whole thing as a factor graph with preintegrated IMU factors, GPS factors, and loop closures.100

Then the pendulum swung back. FAST-LIO2 discards feature extraction entirely, registers raw points directly against the map using a tightly coupled iterated Kalman filter, and makes it fast with an incremental k-d tree that supports insertion and deletion without rebuilding.101 KISS-ICP went further and showed that a carefully implemented plain point-to-point ICP — with an adaptive correspondence threshold and a constant-velocity motion prior, and no features, no learning, and almost no parameters — is competitive with all of it across wildly different vehicles and sensors.102 It is a genuinely useful corrective to a decade of accumulating complexity.

Recognising a place you have been

Loop closure is the whole point, and it reduces to a retrieval problem: given the current sensor reading, has the robot been here before?

The classical answer borrows from text search. Quantise local descriptors into a "vocabulary" of visual words, represent an image as a bag of those words, and use an inverted index to retrieve candidates in logarithmic time.103 DBoW2 did this with binary descriptors and made it fast enough to query every frame, and it is what sits inside ORB-SLAM.104 NetVLAD replaced the hand-designed aggregation with a learned, differentiable one, trained weakly from geotagged imagery, and dramatically improved robustness to viewpoint and appearance change.105 For lidar, Scan Context encodes a sweep as a polar image of maximum heights, which is naturally rotation-invariant and requires no training at all.106

The hardest case is appearance change over time — the same street in summer sunshine and winter darkness. SeqSLAM's insight was that matching sequences of images against sequences is dramatically more robust than matching single images, because a run of mediocre matches in the right order is far stronger evidence than one excellent match.107 The survey by Stephanie Lowry and colleagues remains the map of this territory.108

Retrieval only narrows the field. To actually relocalise — recover a full 6-DoF pose inside a pre-built map, with no prior at all — the standard recipe is hierarchical: retrieve a handful of candidate images with a global descriptor, then match local features against only those candidates, then solve a perspective-n-point problem under RANSAC. Coarse to fine, cheap to expensive.109

When the front end lies

Perceptual aliasing — two different places that look identical — is the nightmare. A single false loop closure has unbounded leverage on a least-squares problem: it is a constraint saying "these two poses are the same", and the optimiser will fold the map in half to satisfy it.

The back end must therefore be robust to constraints that are simply wrong. Robust kernels (Huber, Cauchy) make the cost grow linearly rather than quadratically past a threshold, so an outlier can be outvoted rather than dominating. Switchable constraints go further and give the optimiser an explicit per-loop-closure switch variable that it can turn off, at a cost.110 Dynamic covariance scaling achieves a similar effect in closed form and much faster.111 Graduated non-convexity solves a sequence of progressively less-convex surrogates, which gets global outlier rejection with the convergence properties of a convex problem.112 And consistency-based methods cluster loop closures and reject clusters that disagree with the rest.113

What the map is

The estimator gives you a trajectory. What you store as the map is a separate decision, driven by what the planner needs.

  • Occupancy grid / octree. Explicit free, occupied and unknown. OctoMap's octree makes 3-D grids memory-feasible at fine resolution.114 Unknown-versus-free is the distinction that exploration planners live on.
  • Signed distance fields. Store the distance to the nearest surface. Truncated SDFs are how KinectFusion fuses depth images into a coherent surface;115,18 Euclidean SDFs give a planner the distance-to-obstacle and its gradient for free, which is exactly what trajectory optimisers want.116
  • Surfels and meshes. ElasticFusion drops the pose graph entirely and deforms the map to close loops, which is map-centric rather than trajectory-centric.117 SuMa does the equivalent for lidar.118
  • Radiance fields. NeRF represents a scene as a neural function you can render, not merely occupy.119 iMAP put one inside a real-time SLAM loop;120 NICE-SLAM made it scale by replacing the single MLP with a hierarchy of feature grids.121 3-D Gaussian splatting then delivered comparable quality with real-time rasterisation instead of ray marching,122 and was folded back into SLAM almost immediately.123

Whether a photorealistic map is what a robot actually needs is an open question. A planner does not care what the wall looks like. But a map you can render is a map you can differentiate, and that turns out to be useful for relocalisation, for simulation, and for closing the loop between perception and control.

CH 07 From geometry to meaning

Everything so far produces geometry: where surfaces are, where the robot is. A geometric map will let a robot avoid a person, but it will not let it understand that the person is a person, that people move, that this particular person is walking towards the door and will therefore be somewhere else in two seconds. For that you need semantics, and semantics is where learning earns its place.

Detection and segmentation in 3-D

In 2-D the problem is largely settled. Mask R-CNN detects objects and segments each instance in a single pass by bolting a mask head onto a region-proposal detector, and it remains the sane default when you want boxes and pixel-accurate masks from one camera.124 The difficulty for a robot is that it needs those labels in metres, not pixels — which means lifting them into 3-D.

Point clouds are awkward for neural networks: they are unordered, irregular, and sparse. PointNet solved the ordering problem with a symmetric function — per-point MLPs followed by a global max-pool, which is permutation-invariant by construction.125 VoxelNet took the opposite route, voxelising space and applying 3-D convolutions, at considerable cost.126 PointPillars found the compromise that actually shipped: collapse the vertical dimension into "pillars", encode each pillar with a small network, and then run an ordinary 2-D CNN on the resulting pseudo-image — fast enough for a car, accurate enough to matter.127 CenterPoint dropped the anchor boxes and predicts object centres directly, which handles the rotational ambiguity of 3-D boxes far more gracefully.128

Everything meets in bird's-eye view

The architectural convergence of the last five years is that all modalities get projected into a common, ego-centric, metric top-down grid, and fusion happens there. Lift-Splat-Shoot showed how to get camera features into that grid without depth ground truth: predict a distribution over depth for every pixel, lift the pixel's feature along its ray weighted by that distribution, and splat the result into BEV.129 BEVFormer replaced the explicit lift with learned BEV queries that attend back into the image features via deformable cross-attention, and added temporal self-attention across the previous BEV frame.130

The current frontier is dense occupancy prediction: rather than fitting boxes around known object classes, predict the occupancy and semantics of every voxel, which sidesteps the problem that a box-based detector is blind to anything not in its class list — the mattress on the motorway, the overturned trailer.131 That is a direct, and slightly ironic, return to Elfes.

Foundation models and the open-set problem

A closed-set detector trained on a fixed taxonomy will confidently misclassify anything outside it, and the world is full of things outside it. Segment Anything produces class-agnostic masks for essentially any object from a point or box prompt.132 DINOv2 learns visual features without labels that transfer to segmentation and depth estimation with only a linear probe on top.133 These are genuinely useful to robotics because they degrade gracefully on the long tail rather than failing silently.

The most aggressive direction collapses perception into the policy entirely. Vision-language-action models take pixels and an instruction and emit actions, with no explicit intermediate representation at all — no map, no pose, no object list.134,135 OpenVLA made this line reproducible in the open.135 Whether this is the future or a detour is genuinely unresolved. What is clear is that a system with no explicit state estimate has no explicit uncertainty either, and cannot tell you that it does not know where it is.

The limits, honestly stated

Niko Sünderhauf and a large group of co-authors laid out why deep learning has not simply eaten robotics perception, and the argument holds up: robots need calibrated uncertainty (a classifier that is 90% confident should be right 90% of the time, and most are not), they need to learn incrementally without catastrophic forgetting, they operate in an open set, and they must reason about causes and not just correlations.136 A network that has never seen a fallen tree will not report that it has never seen a fallen tree.

CH 08 The toolchain

Nothing in this field is written from scratch, and you should not try.

Middleware

ROS provided the thing robotics was missing: a common message layer, so that a lidar driver written by one group could feed a SLAM system written by another.137 ROS 2 rebuilt it on DDS with real-time-capable executors, proper QoS policies, and a security model, because the original was a research tool that ended up in products.138 The single most valuable component in it is tf2, which maintains the time-stamped tree of coordinate frames and answers the question "where was the lidar, relative to the map, at 14:32:07.043?" — a question that is much harder than it sounds and that you will otherwise get wrong.139 Nav2 is the navigation stack built on top.140

That node is a fair sample of what real perception code looks like. The projection is three lines of linear algebra. Everything else is extrinsics lookup, time synchronisation, and frustum culling — and every one of those lines is there because of a bug someone shipped.

Two metrics, and they answer different questions. Absolute trajectory error aligns the estimated trajectory to ground truth with a single rigid transform (or a similarity transform, for monocular, because of scale) and reports the residual — it measures global consistency, and it is the number that loop closure improves. CH 09 How perception fails

A perception system that reports "I don't know" is doing its job. A perception system that reports a confident, precise, wrong answer is a hazard. Almost every serious robotic accident traces back to the second category, so it is worth cataloguing the ways it happens.

Degeneracy

The environment does not always constrain the state. A lidar in a straight, featureless tunnel is unconstrained along the tunnel axis. A camera looking at a blank wall is unconstrained in the direction perpendicular to it. A drone hovering over flat, textureless snow has nothing to lock onto at all. In each case the optimisation problem is rank-deficient, the solution slides freely along the null space, and the estimator reports a pose with a tight covariance that is metres wrong.77

The fix is not a better solver. It is to detect the degeneracy — by inspecting the eigenvalues of the Hessian, or by explicitly computing localisability along each direction before committing to a solve — and to fall back on a different modality, or on a motion prior, or to simply admit uncertainty in that direction.78

Perceptual aliasing

Two places that look identical. Every office corridor, every warehouse aisle, every row of identical server racks. The place-recognition front end produces a high-confidence match, the back end folds the map to satisfy it, and the robot's map now contains two physically distinct rooms superimposed. This is the failure mode that robust back ends exist to survive, and it is why a modern system never trusts a single loop-closure hypothesis.110,112

Dynamic and deformable worlds

The classical SLAM formulation assumes a static world. Real environments are full of people, vehicles, doors, and pallets that were not there yesterday. Points on a moving object produce constraints that are correct about the object and wrong about the robot, and the estimator cannot tell the difference from geometry alone.

Semantics is what breaks the tie. SuMa++ labels the point cloud semantically and simply refuses to use points on classes known to move for the odometry constraint — a small change that produces a large robustness gain in traffic.157 Long-term operation makes this worse rather than better: a warehouse map is stale within a shift, and lifelong SLAM has to decide what to forget, which is a genuinely open problem.1

Degraded visual environments

The DARPA Subterranean Challenge was, essentially, a systematic assault on perception. Robots had to operate in tunnels, caves, and urban rubble: no GPS, no light, thick dust, water, smoke, self-similar geometry, and no prior map. The lessons that came out are the most honest field data we have.45 Every competitive team ran heterogeneous, redundant sensing — lidar and vision and thermal and radar and inertial — with an estimator that could gracefully drop a modality that had failed. Every team invested heavily in detecting failure rather than in preventing it. And multi-robot map merging, which is a research curiosity in the lab, turned out to be a practical necessity.158,159

The covariance is not the uncertainty

The number your EKF reports as its covariance is the uncertainty under the assumption that all of your models are correct. It does not account for the possibility that a landmark was mis-associated, that the extrinsics are stale, that the camera is looking at a mirror, or that the lidar is seeing dust. Those are the errors that actually kill robots, and they are invisible to the filter's own self-assessment. A perception system needs an independent monitor — consistency checks, cross-modality disagreement, innovation whiteness tests — because a filter cannot be its own judge.

CH 10 Open problems, and what to watch

Cadena and colleagues asked in 2016 whether SLAM was solved. Their answer was: it depends on the robot, the environment, and the performance you need — and for most interesting combinations, no.1 A decade on, that remains the right answer, though the frontier has moved.

Robustness, still. The gap between a system that achieves 0.5% drift on KITTI and one that survives a year in a working mine is enormous, and it is not an accuracy gap. It is a gap in failure detection, in graceful degradation, in knowing what you do not know. Certifiable estimation — algorithms that return a solution together with a proof that it is globally optimal, or an honest admission that it is not — is the most promising formal attack on this.160,161

Representation. We still do not agree on what a map should be. A planner wants an ESDF. A relocaliser wants descriptors. A human wants a semantic scene graph. A renderer wants Gaussians. Currently we build several maps and keep them consistent by hand. A representation that serves all these queries, is memory-bounded, and can be updated incrementally over years does not exist.

The uncertainty of learned components. A geometric estimator gives you a covariance that means something. A network gives you a softmax that means almost nothing. Until learned perception produces calibrated uncertainty that a downstream estimator can actually fuse, the two halves of the stack cannot be properly joined — and today they are joined by heuristics and thresholds.136

Active perception. Perception is treated as a passive pipeline in almost every system, and in almost every animal it is not. Where should the robot look? Which viewpoint most reduces the uncertainty that matters for the task? A robot that cannot localise in a featureless corridor should go and find a corner. Closing that loop — letting the estimator's uncertainty drive the planner — remains largely unexploited engineering.

Touch, still. Vision has had four decades of concentrated effort and tactile sensing has had, generously, one. Manipulation in clutter is bottlenecked on the last centimetre, where the object is occluded by the hand and only touch can tell you what is happening.51

Coda

The oldest ideas in this article are the ones that have survived best. Kalman's recursion from 1960 is still the skeleton of every state estimator. Moravec and Elfes's insistence that a bad measurement is still evidence, if you model it honestly, is still how mapping works. Smith and Cheeseman's observation that the errors are correlated is still the reason SLAM is one problem and not two. What changed is that the sensors got a thousand times cheaper and the solvers got a million times faster, and a set of ideas that could barely move a cart across a room in fifteen minutes now runs at 200 Hz on a chip in a drone.

The underlying fight has not changed at all. Proprioception gives you everything, instantly, and it is drifting away from the truth from the moment you switch it on. Exteroception gives you the truth, occasionally, ambiguously, and only when physics is in a cooperative mood. The entire discipline is the machinery for holding those two in tension — and loop closure, the moment a robot recognises a place and reaches backwards through its own history to correct it, remains the closest thing perception has to a way of stopping time.
