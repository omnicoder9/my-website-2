Vegan Activism Series  ·  Pillar IV

The Market Is the Message

How entrepreneurship, capital, and commerce are becoming the most powerful tools in the vegan activist's arsenal

May 6, 2026  ·  by Nicholas Knapp

Back to all articles

Protest signs fade in the rain. Petitions accumulate in ministerial inboxes. But a product that beats conventional animal foods on price, taste, and convenience — that changes what people eat every Tuesday night without anyone needing to be persuaded of anything. The most audacious argument in vegan strategy today is not moral but economic: that the fastest route to a post-animal food system runs directly through the market, using its own logic against itself.

This is not naïve faith in the goodness of capitalism. It is a recognition — increasingly supported by research, investment data, and commercial precedent — that when profit incentives align with ethical outcomes, change scales at a velocity that advocacy alone cannot match. Market-based activism does not ask industries to be merciful; it makes cruelty expensive and compassion profitable.

This pillar of vegan activism examines how entrepreneurs, investors, and consumers are reengineering the food economy from within: building businesses that embody vegan values, directing capital toward alternatives that outcompete animal agriculture, demanding transparency across supply chains, deploying certification systems that signal ethics at the point of sale, and relentlessly pursuing the price parity that transforms a niche preference into a mass-market default.

Section 1

The Scale of What Is Being Built

To understand the stakes, begin with the numbers. The global alternative protein sector — the commercial heart of vegan entrepreneurialism — is one of the fastest-growing segments in modern food history. While estimates vary across market research methodologies, even conservative projections illustrate a dramatic structural shift. One widely cited analysis values the alternative protein market at approximately USD $35.6 billion in 2024, forecast to reach USD $130 billion by 2034 at a compound annual growth rate of 13.9%.1 Other analyses place the 2025 figure even higher, exceeding USD $79 billion when the broadest definition of the sector is applied.2

Plant-based proteins hold the dominant share — over 50% of the market by most measures — reflecting both consumer familiarity and a more mature commercialization pathway than cultivated meat or precision fermentation.1 Food and beverage applications drive demand, accounting for roughly two-thirds of sector revenue.3 North America leads in market share (approximately 36–40%), though Asia-Pacific is projected to be the fastest-growing region through the 2030s, driven by population scale and shifting middle-class dietary preferences.1,4

The breadth and growth of this commercial activity represents something genuinely new. Vegan entrepreneurialism is no longer a cottage industry of small health food stores; it is a sector attracting institutional investors, legacy food conglomerates, and sovereign wealth funds. In January 2025, agribusiness giant Cargill announced investments in both 3D printing and mycoprotein fermentation technologies specifically to improve the taste and texture of alternative proteins.4 When Cargill moves into a space, the space has moved into the mainstream.

Section 2

Vegan Businesses as Activism Infrastructure

Every vegan restaurant, plant-based brand, or cruelty-free cosmetics company is, at minimum, a proof of concept. It demonstrates to the market that ethical production can generate returns, to consumers that their values can be expressed through ordinary purchasing decisions, and to competitors that unmet demand exists. This is a form of activism conducted through commerce rather than communication.

The Entrepreneurial Argument

The structural logic is compelling. New businesses create new supply chains, develop new production technologies, and train new workforces in plant-based manufacturing. Each successful vegan brand expands the infrastructure that makes vegan consumption more convenient and affordable for everyone — including people who never thought of themselves as activists or even as particularly interested in animal welfare.

Independent vegan entrepreneurs have shown particular resilience in this role. Analysis of the 2024 market notes that smaller vegan businesses often serve as what one commentator describes as "the industry's heart," innovating while maintaining ethical standards and avoiding what has been called "welfare-washing" — the practice by which corporations introduce token vegan product lines while maintaining supply chains that contradict vegan principles.5 Smaller operators typically maintain more coherent ethical identities and respond more rapidly to community expectations.

The Welfare-Washing Problem

As major food corporations enter the plant-based space, critics have warned that vegan product launches can serve as ethical cover for unchanged core operations. Some vegan brands have responded by adopting QR-code-linked supply chain transparency tools, allowing customers to trace ingredients from farm to shelf — making ethics verifiable rather than merely claimed.5

From Local to Systemic

The aspiration for market-based activism extends far beyond individual businesses. The Good Food Institute (GFI) — the most influential think tank working at the intersection of alternative protein science and market strategy — frames its corporate engagement work explicitly around "replicating past market transformations by showing companies of all sizes, from startups to multinational corporations, how alternatives to animal products can be profitable while meeting environmental, social, and governance goals."6 This is not advocacy for veganism as a moral cause; it is a commercial development program for a post-animal food economy.

Plant-based meat is a powerful tool for tackling climate challenges. At scale, alternative proteins including plant-based meat, seafood, eggs, and dairy could enable a shift toward less resource-intensive ways of producing protein.

Caroline Bushnell, Good Food Institute, April 20246
Section 3

Impact Investing and the Capital Dimension

Capital is the most potent accelerant available to market-based activism. The question of where investment flows determines which technologies scale, which supply chains develop, and ultimately which products appear on supermarket shelves at what price. Impact investing — directing financial capital toward measurable positive outcomes — has become a serious institutional practice within the alternative protein sector.

The Investment Landscape

The Good Food Institute's analysis of 2024 investment data shows that global private funding for alternative protein companies totalled USD $1.1 billion — down 27% from the previous year amid a broader climate tech venture capital pullback.7 Yet this contraction was uneven. By 2025, investment in plant-based companies had rebounded strongly, rising 39% to USD $450 million, even as cultivated meat and fermentation saw continued declines.8 Simultaneously, public funding for the sector remained robust: governments committed USD $510 million in public investment to alternative proteins in 2024 alone, driven by the US, Denmark, and the EU.9

Since 2016, the private sector has invested at least USD $10.5 billion in the plant-based meat, seafood, egg, and dairy industry, according to GFI analysis.10 Public funding has totalled USD $2 billion since 2020 for the alternative protein sector as a whole.10 These figures represent not just a commercial bet but a structural intervention in global food systems.

ESG Frameworks and Institutional Capital

The integration of alternative protein into mainstream Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) investing frameworks has been a significant development. A survey conducted by Beyond Investing found that 80% of investors who invest on behalf of an ESG or impact fund reported that alternative proteins were part of their funds' core mandates.11 GFI has co-developed ESG frameworks with the Farm Animal Investment Risk and Return Initiative (FAIRR) specifically to equip investors and companies with tools to monitor, measure, and report on risks and opportunities in this space.6

The food and land use sector — within which alternative proteins sit — accounted for over 250 new companies raising money in 2024, making it the second most populated climate tech sector globally by deal numbers, trailing only energy.12 The sector receives a share of climate tech capital (18.5%) that remains somewhat below its proportional contribution to global greenhouse gas emissions (approximately 22%), suggesting continued room for capital deployment growth.12

Blended Finance and the Infrastructure Gap

A recurring theme in the investment literature is that conventional venture capital — focused on near-term returns and relatively modest check sizes — is insufficient to fund the industrial-scale infrastructure that alternative protein production requires. Building a demonstration or commercial-scale fermentation facility in Europe, for instance, can cost between €15 million and €250 million — well beyond what early-stage startups can raise from VCs alone.13 GFI Europe has consequently argued for blended finance models: combinations of guarantees, grants, concessional loans, leasing arrangements, and market-shaping schemes that de-risk investment sufficiently to attract private capital at the required scale.13

The capital structure problem for alternative protein is not one of interest but of instrument. The sector needs patient infrastructure capital — the kind that built conventional agricultural processing facilities over decades — not just the growth equity suited to software startups. Solving this financing architecture problem may be more consequential than any individual technological breakthrough.

Section 4

Ethical Supply Chains and Corporate Accountability

A business can market itself as vegan without having built the supply chain to back the claim. Ethical supply chain management — ensuring that every input, from raw ingredients to packaging, meets the ethical standards the brand projects — is both a commercial differentiator and an activism imperative.

Animal Welfare in the Supply Chain

For vegan businesses, the supply chain challenge is distinct from (though related to) the challenge facing conventional food companies. Sourcing ingredients ethically and sustainably remains genuinely difficult, particularly for speciality inputs like pea protein that face global supply pressures.5 But the ethical stakes are more tractable than in animal agriculture: the central problem is environmental and labour practices in ingredient supply, not the welfare of directly harmed animals.

For companies still operating in animal agriculture, supply chain animal welfare is now a recognised material business risk. The 2019 UN Global Sustainable Development Report included animal welfare as a key overlooked issue within the 2030 Sustainable Development Goals framework.14 USDA enforcement actions in fiscal year 2024 resulted in more than $1 million in penalties across dozens of facilities for animal welfare non-compliance — a figure that, while modest in absolute terms, signals that regulatory attention is increasing.15 A 2023 academic analysis of "animal welfare washing" in supply chains identifies how welfare narratives, standards, and certifications can create the appearance of ethical production without delivering measurable improvements — a systemic risk that vegan entrepreneurs can exploit competitively by offering genuine rather than performative ethics.16

Transparency as Competitive Advantage

Some vegan brands have made transparency itself the product. QR-code-linked supply chain documentation allows customers to trace the provenance of every ingredient, understand the ethical considerations relevant to each input, and verify the actions the company has taken to address supply chain risks.5 This level of disclosure would have been operationally prohibitive a decade ago; digital infrastructure now makes it commercially viable and, for the right consumer segment, highly compelling.

Businesses that prioritise animal welfare and ethical sourcing in their supply chains can meaningfully enhance their competitiveness and appeal to ethically conscious consumers, and consumer awareness and advocacy play a crucial role in shaping market dynamics.14 The direction of causation runs both ways: companies that build ethical supply chains attract ethically motivated consumers, and those consumers' purchasing power creates the financial conditions in which ethical supply chains become economically rational for competitors to adopt.

Section 5

Certification Systems: Signalling Ethics at Scale

In markets characterized by information asymmetry — where consumers cannot directly verify the ethical claims embedded in products — certification serves as a crucial trust mechanism. The growth of vegan and cruelty-free labelling systems represents an institutionalization of the market signal that ethical production exists and can be identified.

Consumer Reliance on Certification

The empirical evidence for the importance of certification to vegan consumer behaviour is striking. Research by The Vegan Society found that 88.9% of primarily non-meat-eating British consumers look for vegan certification on food and drink products when buying vegan items for the first time, with the figure rising to 89.1% for American consumers.17 In healthcare products, 86% of both British and American survey respondents reported relying on vegan certification; in cosmetics and toiletries, 81% did so.17

These figures suggest that certification is not merely a marketing embellishment but a functional component of the vegan consumer decision-making process. Third-party certification reduces the cognitive burden of ethical consumption by replacing the need for research with the shortcut of a trusted mark. For market-based activists, this implies that investment in credible, independently audited certification infrastructure has outsized returns relative to its cost.

The Label Paradox

The relationship between labelling and consumption is, however, more complex than a simple "label = sales" equation. A randomised controlled study published in Appetite found that menu items were significantly less likely to be chosen when labelled as vegan or vegetarian, with larger effects observed in field studies (where choices had real consequences) than in hypothetical online surveys.18 The implication is counterintuitive but consistent with other research in behavioural economics: for non-vegan consumers, the label may trigger avoidance rather than attraction.

The evidence suggests a strategic bifurcation: vegan certification is highly valuable as a signal to already-vegan or vegan-curious consumers, but may be counterproductive when prominently displayed to omnivorous consumers at the point of choice. This argues for differentiated marketing strategies — lean on certification in vegan-specific retail and community contexts; lead with taste and price in mainstream foodservice environments.

Separate research published in Food Quality and Preference found that adding vegan labels to "randomly-vegan" products — foods that happen to be vegan without having been reformulated for the vegan market — biases perceived healthiness, expected taste, and perceived sustainability, but only when consumers do not already expect the product to be vegan.19 This suggests that labelling strategy should be calibrated to the existing expectations of the target consumer segment, rather than applied uniformly.

The Growth of Vegan Consumerism as a Market Force

Despite labelling complexities, the overall trend in ethical consumer markets has been one of growth. The number of consumers embracing veganism or reducing their intake of animal products grew substantially over the past decade, generating a surge in the development of products targeted at vegans and an expansion of vegan labelling across retail categories.20 In the UK, an estimated 7.5% of consumers identify as vegan or vegetarian, with 39.5% identifying as flexitarian — a figure that represents a substantial and commercially meaningful addressable market for ethically certified products.21

Section 6

Price Parity: The Decisive Battleground

All other elements of market-based vegan activism — the businesses, the capital, the supply chains, the certifications — ultimately converge on one question: can plant-based and cultivated alternatives to animal products reach price parity? If they can, the ethical case for veganism gains a commercially irresistible ally. If they cannot, the market remains segmented between an ethical niche and an affordable mainstream.

Where Things Stand

In the US, beef is currently approximately 20% cheaper than plant-based meat on average; when calculated across the full meat market, the price gap expands to around 77%.22 Taste and price remain the top factors influencing consumer purchasing decisions in this space, according to US market data.23 This premium is a principal driver of limited mainstream adoption, and closing it is the sector's most important operational priority.

Good Food Institute research has indicated that the largest group of consumers is willing to pay only the same price for plant-based products as for their conventional counterparts — making price parity not merely an aspiration but a threshold requirement for mass adoption.22 A report from Blue Horizon Corporation and Boston Consulting Group estimated that at price parity, plant-based patties could capture a 10% market share of beef and 22% of all animal protein.24

Progress and Pathways

Progress is real, if uneven. In the Netherlands, manufacturers achieved price parity with conventional beef burgers in 2022, with plant-based versions averaging 78 cents cheaper than their animal-based equivalents.25 In the US foodservice sector, Seattle-based Rebellyous Foods — which supplies over 109 school districts under the USDA National School Lunch Programme — announced in 2023 that it anticipated reaching cost parity with conventional chicken products by 2024 through continuous production technology that raised throughput from 100 to 2,500 lbs per hour.26

The pathways to price parity are increasingly well-understood: economies of scale, more efficient extraction and texturization processes, switching from protein isolates to cheaper protein concentrates, diversifying protein sources beyond soy and pea to mycelium, fava bean, and algae, and continued investment in precision fermentation and AI-optimized production.27,28 Market research suggests that technological innovation and scaling could reduce production costs in alt-protein by 40% by 2035.3

The upward pressure on conventional meat prices also aids the convergence. A combination of supply chain disruption, weather events, inflationary pressures, and labour shortages have materially increased the cost of conventional animal protein, narrowing the gap from the other side. When plant-based producers scale and conventional meat becomes more expensive, the lines will cross — the question is when, not whether.

We are convinced that we will be able to significantly reduce production costs and prices in the coming years — this is part of our business plan to penetrate the mass market. Traditional meat production has worked in the same way for decades.

Chris Jenny, Planted Foods, January 202429

The Limits of Price as Lever

It would be intellectually dishonest to present price parity as a panacea. A peer-reviewed narrative review published in 2025 evaluated the "price, taste, and convenience" (PTC) conjecture — the widespread assumption that if plant-based alternatives matched these attributes, a majority of consumers would switch from animal-based meat. The authors found that the conjecture receives little scientific scrutiny and that, even at price parity, a large majority of present-day consumers would not be expected to switch entirely from conventional meat.10 Price parity is necessary but not sufficient; it unlocks growth, particularly among flexitarians, without guaranteeing mass transformation.

The behavioural literature consistently suggests that food choice is driven by habit, social context, sensory expectation, and identity, in addition to price and taste. Market-based activism that achieves price parity has cleared the most significant structural obstacle to mainstream adoption — but complementary strategies addressing identity, social norms, and culinary culture remain necessary for the deepest transformation.

Section 7

Using Capitalism Rather Than Fighting It

The strategic philosophy underlying market-based vegan activism is not one of accommodation or moral compromise. It is a recognition that structural change at the scale required — reducing and ultimately eliminating the exploitation of tens of billions of land animals annually — cannot be achieved through moral persuasion alone within any meaningful time horizon. The market is the most powerful allocative mechanism in human civilisation. Redirecting it, rather than opposing it, is the more effective form of activism.

This strategic posture does involve real tensions. Market logic is indifferent to animal welfare in itself; it responds to welfare considerations only insofar as they are expressed through consumer demand, regulatory constraint, or investment preference. Vegan entrepreneurs who build businesses must generate returns, serve a market, and make operational compromises that pure advocacy organizations do not face. The risk of value drift — of a business that began with vegan principles gradually diluting them under commercial pressure — is real and well-documented.

But the alternative — ceding the commercial sphere to animal agriculture while veganism remains a moral project conducted outside the market — has not produced transformation at the pace or scale required by the urgency of animal suffering and environmental crisis. The entrepreneurial and investment activity documented in this article represents a serious attempt to engineer a world in which the ethical option is also the convenient, affordable, and delicious one. That world, if achieved, would constitute the most significant advance in the history of veganism.
