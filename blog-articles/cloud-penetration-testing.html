01 — Terrain

The shared-responsibility frontier

Cloud penetration testing is not network penetration testing with a different IP range. The attack surface is not a rack of servers behind a firewall — it is a web of identity policies, service configurations, storage permissions, and trust relationships between managed services, all driven through APIs. An attacker who compromises a single function with an over-permissioned execution role can reach full account takeover without ever touching a virtual machine.1 The skills that find these paths blend cloud-architecture fluency with offensive tradecraft, and the techniques that matter rarely involve a memory-corruption exploit. They involve a misconfiguration that nobody checked.

The conceptual key to the entire discipline is the shared responsibility model. The provider secures the infrastructure it operates — physical hardware, the hypervisor, the internals of managed services. The customer is responsible for everything they configure on top of it: identities, access policies, network rules, and data.2 A cloud penetration test operates almost entirely below that line. You are testing the customer's configuration, not the provider's infrastructure, and the most consequential findings are almost always the customer's to fix. Neither AWS nor Azure nor Google audits your environment for you; an independent assessment is what surfaces the over-privileged role, the public bucket, and the exposed metadata endpoint before an adversary does.3

That distinction has practical teeth. Because the provider's infrastructure is multi-tenant, testing it is both prohibited and pointless — you would be attacking other customers. Because the customer's surface is API-driven and ephemeral, traditional tooling that scans hosts and ports misses most of it entirely.4 Cloud-native attack paths run through IAM privilege escalation, instance-metadata exploitation, inter-service trust abuse, storage exposure, and lateral movement across managed services — categories a conventional web-application or infrastructure test was never scoped to touch.1

This is why "the cloud is secure" is a category error. The platforms are, by and large, robustly engineered. The breaches happen in the orange band — a leaked access key, a role with AdministratorAccess, a bucket left world-readable, a function trusting unvalidated input. Misconfiguration, not novel vulnerability research, is the dominant cause of cloud compromise, and it is precisely what a competent assessment is built to find.3

02 — Authorization

Rules of engagement

Before a single API call, the engagement needs authorization that is both ethical and contractual. Each hyperscaler publishes its own rules, and they differ enough that "I tested my AWS account the same way I'd test my Azure tenant" is a route to a suspended account. The universal constant: you may test what you own, never the provider's shared infrastructure or another tenant.5

Amazon Web Services

AWS permits customers to assess a defined set of services without prior approval. The current Permitted Services list spans the obvious compute and edge surface — EC2 instances, Elastic Load Balancers, NAT gateways, the AWS WAF, RDS and Aurora, CloudFront, API Gateway, AppSync, Lambda and Lambda@Edge, Lightsail, Elastic Beanstalk, ECS and Fargate, OpenSearch, FSx, Transit Gateway, and Bedrock AgentCore.5 What remains firmly off-limits is anything that degrades the platform for other tenants or impersonates an attack on AWS itself: denial-of-service and its simulations, port, protocol, and request flooding, DNS zone walking, hijacking, or pharming through Route 53, and S3 bucket or subdomain takeover are all prohibited.5 Adversarial simulations involving command-and-control, red/blue/purple teaming, malware, or simulated phishing require a separate Simulated Events form submitted for review before testing begins.5 AWS dropped its blanket pre-authorization requirement years ago, but the customer remains solely responsible for ensuring tooling does not stray into DoS behavior or another customer's resources.6

Scope discipline
The single rule that protects you: test your own resources only. If a finding leads toward AWS infrastructure or another tenant — stop, document, and report it through the security disclosure channel rather than pursuing it.5

Microsoft Azure

Azure's penetration-testing rules of engagement permit customers to test their own resources without prior notification for most test types. The prohibitions mirror the spirit of AWS's: social engineering against Microsoft employees, physical-security testing of datacenters, and automated scanning of infrastructure outside your own tenant are all out of bounds.7 Because Entra ID (formerly Azure AD) is internet-facing by default, the tenant boundary — not a network perimeter — is the line that must not be crossed.7

Google Cloud Platform

Google operates the least restrictive policy of the three: it requires no prior approval and has no formal notification process for testing your own resources, provided the activity complies with the Acceptable Use Policy and Terms of Service. The constraint is that testing must stay within your own project and organization.7 Across all three providers the practical takeaway is identical — you can test your own cloud, and a reputable engagement scopes every action inside the provider's rules by default.7

Beyond provider policy, every engagement needs the artifacts a mature pentest always carries: written authorization from the account or tenant owner, a defined scope listing exact accounts, subscriptions, projects, and regions, and a rules-of-engagement document fixing source IP ranges, test windows, and emergency contacts.3 In cloud work, credentialed grey-box access is strongly preferred over pure black-box testing — supplying the tester a low-privilege identity keeps the assessment strictly in-scope and avoids the risk of mis-attributing an attack to a resource the client does not own.8

03 — Method

Methodology & frameworks

A defensible engagement is built on recognized frameworks rather than ad-hoc curiosity. Mature cloud-testing methodology draws on the Penetration Testing Execution Standard (PTES), the OWASP testing guides, the SANS methodology, and NIST SP 800-115 (Technical Guide to Information Security Testing and Assessment), threaded together by the one map written specifically for this terrain: the MITRE ATT&CK Cloud matrix.4 Anchoring findings to a shared taxonomy is what lets a client compare this year's report to last year's, and lets a tester demonstrate coverage rather than anecdote.

The MITRE ATT&CK Cloud matrix

ATT&CK's Cloud matrix extends the Enterprise knowledge base with tactics and techniques that map to specific platforms — AWS, Azure, Google Cloud, plus Microsoft 365 and Google Workspace sub-matrices — giving teams targeted insight into how adversaries actually behave in each environment.9 It is living documentation: version 16, released in October 2024, added more than nineteen techniques, including cloud-relevant additions such as conditional-access-policy manipulation (T1556) and cloud service hijacking (T1496.004).10 Security teams operationalize it in four ways — threat modeling to simulate attacks before they happen, gap analysis to expose detection blind spots, adversary emulation to exercise defenses against real tactics, and incident response to accelerate investigation.11 For a red team, the matrix turns "we poked around" into "we exercised eleven techniques across five tactics, and here is where detection failed."

The cloud kill chain

Regardless of provider, a cloud assessment moves through a recognizable arc that mirrors a real intrusion: reconnaissance of the external surface, an initial foothold (most often a leaked credential or an application flaw), enumeration of what that identity can see and touch, privilege escalation, lateral movement across services and trust boundaries, and finally impact — exfiltration, persistence, or disruption.12

That last point is the methodology's core. Read-only posture tools — covered in detail later — are the right way to start, inventorying resources and flagging misconfigurations without changing anything.13 But their output is a list of weaknesses, not an attack. The value of a penetration test over a configuration scan is the chaining: an exposed storage bucket combined with a credential it contains, combined with a role that credential can assume, becomes a single high-impact narrative that no scanner produces on its own.13

04 — Identity

Identity is the perimeter

In a datacenter, the perimeter is a firewall. In the cloud, it is an identity. Every provider's access model — AWS IAM, Azure RBAC and Entra ID, Google Cloud IAM — answers one question that dominates every engagement: can a low-privilege principal reach administrator? Privilege escalation is the highest-impact finding category in cloud environments, and it almost never requires a vulnerability in the classical sense.7 It requires a permission that looks harmless in isolation.

A developer user with iam:PassRole and Lambda permissions may have a direct path to administrator that is invisible until you graph it. — Rhino Security Labs, on indirect IAM escalation

The AWS escalation catalogue

Rhino Security Labs catalogued the canonical AWS privilege-escalation methods — more than twenty distinct paths — and the through-line is that most of them require no CVE; misconfiguration is sufficient.14 The dangerous permissions are specific and frequently granted by accident:

  • iam:CreatePolicyVersion — create a new version of a policy you can edit, defining arbitrary permissions, using the --set-as-default flag to activate it without needing iam:SetDefaultPolicyVersion. Potential impact: full administrator.14
  • iam:AttachUserPolicy / iam:PutUserPolicy — attach a managed admin policy, or write an inline one, directly to your own user. Granting iam:Put* without action restrictions lets a principal escalate to administrator immediately.15
  • iam:PassRole + ec2:RunInstances — launch an instance with a privileged role attached, then read that role's credentials from the metadata service.3
  • iam:PassRole + lambda:CreateFunction — the most commonly overlooked path. Create a function with a high-privilege execution role, run code inside it, and act with that role's permissions even without direct IAM write access.16
  • iam:CreateAccessKey / iam:CreateLoginProfile — mint a second access key, or set a console password, for any other user — taking over a more privileged identity wholesale.16

None of these is iam:*. Each looks innocuous in a single policy review, and that is exactly the problem: the escalation path is a property of the graph of identities and trust relationships, not of any one document.14 An identity that cannot directly modify IAM may still create a Lambda function with a role more powerful than its own, invoke it, and reach administrator through that indirection in three hops that are invisible when policies are read one at a time.16

Because these paths are graph properties, they are found by graph tooling. PMapper builds a directed graph of an account's users, roles, and the cross-service chains between them, surfacing escalation routes in minutes.13 Rhino's own Pacu framework includes an iam__privesc_scan module that identifies available escalation paths from the current identity and, if chosen, executes each one — handing back administrator-level access through whichever misconfiguration it finds.17 Underneath the automation, the failures are mundane: wildcard actions, credentials nobody rotated, stale keys belonging to deprovisioned users, and the recurring catastrophe of a single IAM user holding AdministratorAccess with a leaked access key — full account compromise, no exploit required.3

A CI deployment user holding iam:PassRole and lambda:CreateFunction can attach an unrelated high-privilege execution role to a new function, invoke it, and execute arbitrary AWS API calls as that role — reaching administrative access without any direct IAM-write permission on its own identity.

The catalogue extends well past these headline cases. A principal able to call iam:UpdateLoginProfile can reset the console password of a more-privileged user and authenticate as them; one holding iam:AddUserToGroup can join a group that carries administrative policies; and the data-plane services each contribute their own PassRole sinks — Glue development endpoints, CloudFormation stacks, Data Pipeline definitions, and SageMaker notebooks all accept a role to run under, and each becomes an escalation primitive whenever a low-privilege identity can both pass a powerful role and create the resource that assumes it.16 What makes these so durable in real estates is that every one of them is a legitimate, documented API working exactly as designed; the vulnerability lives entirely in the policy that grants the permission, never in the service itself.14

Trust policies are the second axis of IAM abuse, and the subtler one. A role's permission to be assumed is governed by its trust relationship, so a principal that can call iam:UpdateAssumeRolePolicy on a high-privilege role can rewrite that role to trust an attacker-controlled account and then assume it directly — a single API turning a role the attacker could merely see into one they fully control.16 Over-broad trust relationships are endemic: roles that trust an entire account rather than a specific principal, third-party integration roles whose external-ID condition is missing, and cross-account roles whose counterpart account has itself been compromised all collapse the boundary the trust policy was meant to enforce. Pacu's privilege-escalation scanner enumerates these paths automatically, which is precisely why the framework is run early in an engagement — it converts a tangle of individually innocuous permissions into a ranked list of concrete routes to administrator.15

The same logic — a low-privilege identity reaching the controls of a higher-privilege one — recurs in Azure through role assignments and managed identities, and in Google Cloud through service-account impersonation. The mechanics differ; the question does not. The remaining provider sections trace how each platform's identity model bends under pressure.

05 — AWS

Amazon Web Services

AWS is the most-assessed cloud, and its defining attack technique is older than most cloud-security programs: abuse of the Instance Metadata Service (IMDS). Every EC2 instance can reach a link-local HTTP server at 169.254.169.254, accessible only from the instance itself, that returns configuration data, user-data scripts, and — when a role is attached — temporary IAM credentials for that role.18 Those credentials are the keys to whatever the instance's role can do, which is why attackers target the endpoint relentlessly.

IMDSv1, SSRF, and credential theft

The original IMDSv1 answers a plain GET with no authentication. If an application running on the instance contains a server-side request forgery (or XXE) flaw, an attacker can coerce that application into fetching the credentials from the metadata endpoint without ever obtaining code execution on the host.18 Mandiant has observed adversaries running automated scans precisely to harvest IAM credentials from internet-facing web applications this way.18

imds — v1 vs v2 credential retrievalbash
# IMDSv1 — a single unauthenticated GET (what SSRF abuses)
$ curl http://169.254.169.254/latest/meta-data/iam/security-credentials/
# → returns the role name, then its AccessKeyId / SecretAccessKey / Token

# IMDSv2 — session-oriented: first PUT a token (hop limit blocks proxies)
$ TOKEN=$(curl -s -X PUT "http://169.254.169.254/latest/api/token" \
    -H "X-aws-ec2-metadata-token-ttl-seconds: 21600")
$ curl -s -H "X-aws-ec2-metadata-token: $TOKEN" \
    http://169.254.169.254/latest/meta-data/iam/security-credentials/

AWS introduced IMDSv2 as the mitigation. It requires a session token obtained via a PUT request, and that request carries a hop limit that, by default, prevents the token call from traversing the proxies that most SSRF attacks rely on.19 The defense is real but partial: SSRF that follows HTTP redirects can still pivot to the metadata IP, and an attacker who already has code execution on the instance can retrieve credentials regardless of which version is enforced.18 The hardened posture is HttpTokens=required with a hop limit of 1 (or 2 for container tasks), which blocks the request-forgery class outright.19 Session tokens never persist server-side, and a session is bound to the instance that created it.2

Incident Dossier · 2019

Capital One: one SSRF, 106 million records

The breach that turned IMDS exploitation into a boardroom topic followed the exact chain above. An attacker — a former AWS engineer — found a server-side request forgery flaw in a misconfigured open-source ModSecurity web-application firewall running on an EC2 instance.20 Through it, they queried IMDSv1 and retrieved temporary credentials for the WAF's attached role, named ISRM-WAF-Role, which carried far more permission than it needed — enough to list and read S3.20

With those credentials, the attacker enumerated and synced the bank's storage buckets, exfiltrating personal data tied to roughly 100 million applicants in the United States and 6 million in Canada — names, addresses, credit data, Social Security and bank-account numbers.21 The intrusion occurred in March 2019 but was not discovered until July, after a tip-off; the attacker was arrested shortly after public disclosure.21 No single exotic vulnerability was involved. It was a chain of two ordinary misconfigurations — an exploitable SSRF and an over-permissioned role — meeting an unprotected metadata endpoint.21

Working an AWS account with Pacu

Once a foothold yields credentials, Pacu — Rhino Security Labs' open-source AWS exploitation framework — structures the post-exploitation phase. After importing keys, a tester enumerates with iam__enum_permissions and the users/roles/policies modules, hunts data with s3__bucket_finder, scans for escalation with iam__privesc_scan, and can manipulate CloudTrail logging to study detection gaps.17 Pacu's design mirrors a real adversary's workflow: enumeration, privilege escalation, persistence, data exfiltration, and log tampering, all driven through the same APIs an administrator uses.15 Beyond IMDS and IAM, the richest AWS targets are the places secrets accumulate — EC2 user-data scripts, the SSM Parameter Store, environment variables, and KMS-encrypted material that an over-broad role can decrypt.

Role-chaining and the AWS lateral surface

The Security Token Service is the engine of AWS lateral movement. Once a tester holds any credential, the operative question is which roles it can assume, and an attacker walks the resulting graph by calling sts:AssumeRole from one identity to the next — role-chaining through trust relationships until they reach an identity with the permissions they want.6 Each hop produces fresh temporary credentials and, unless trust policies are tightly scoped, the chain can cross account boundaries inside an organization, which is why mature engagements map the full assume-role graph rather than stopping at the first set of keys.13 Enumerating that graph blind is impractical by hand, so testers lean on credential-report and policy-simulation calls to learn what the current identity can reach before spending a single noisy action.2

Systems Manager is the other great AWS pivot. An identity with the right SSM permissions can run commands on any managed instance through SendCommand, or open an interactive shell via Session Manager — executing code on a host without SSH, without an open inbound port, and without ever touching the network layer the defender is watching.13 Because Session Manager activity is mediated entirely by the AWS API, it both blends into legitimate operations traffic and routes around perimeter controls, making it a favoured route from a compromised identity to code execution on production instances. Pacu packages much of this — enumerating SSM-managed hosts, harvesting Parameter Store secrets, and chaining the results back into its escalation modules — so a single imported key can be expanded into account-wide reach through nothing but documented control-plane calls.17

Detection-evasion shapes every step. A practised operator paces API calls to avoid the volumetric patterns GuardDuty flags, prefers read and assume actions that resemble routine automation, and treats CloudTrail as something to study and, where permissions allow, to disrupt — the same logging an investigator depends on after the fact.6 The defender's countermeasure is the inverse: organization-wide trails that the workload roles themselves cannot stop, and anomaly detection tuned to the assume-role and Session-Manager sequences that legitimate users rarely produce.13

06 — Azure

Microsoft Azure

Azure's identity boundary is the tenant, governed by Entra ID (formerly Azure AD). The defining characteristic for an attacker is that Entra is internet-facing by default — meaning the tenant can be probed, enumerated, and in many cases breached with no prior foothold inside the network at all.12 The toolkit that has grown up around this surface is PowerShell-first, which is why fluency with the Az PowerShell module and Az CLI is non-negotiable for Azure work.

Unauthenticated reconnaissance

Several tools map a tenant before any credential exists. AADInternals enumerates tenant metadata — verified domains, authentication and federation type, single-sign-on configuration — from the outside. NetSPI's MicroBurst, a PowerShell toolkit for assessing Azure, discovers resources registered under an organization's namespace and identifies publicly accessible Blob containers, while ROADtools collects directory data through the Microsoft Graph API.22 MicroBurst's Invoke-EnumerateAzureSubDomains reveals storage accounts, web apps, and tenant subdomains, and Invoke-EnumerateAzureBlobs fuzzes common naming patterns to surface exposed containers — a recurring source of leaked backups, configuration, and tokens.23

Initial access and token abuse

Password spraying remains one of the most effective entry techniques: because Entra is reachable without a foothold, a tool like MSOLSpray can test one or two common passwords across a curated user list, frequently yielding valid credentials where multi-factor authentication is not uniformly enforced.12 A more modern technique is device-code authentication abuse — luring a target to activate an attacker-generated device code grants a valid access token for Microsoft Graph and the Azure management APIs without ever touching the user's password.12 Legacy authentication protocols, which bypass modern conditional-access policies, give attackers another path around MFA.22

Operator tooling is itself a target. When an administrator uses Az PowerShell or Az CLI and fails to terminate the session cleanly, access tokens and authentication data are left cached in a local file; an attacker who compromises that machine can lift the token and inherit a persistent, MFA-bypassing session.22 This is the cloud analogue of credential theft from a workstation, and it is devastating precisely because the stolen artifact is a live, pre-authenticated token rather than a password.

Managed identities and escalation

Many Azure services authenticate to other resources using a managed identity. If an application carrying one is compromised — through remote code execution, say — the attacker can steal the application's access token and wield its privileges to reach other resources, turning a single application flaw into a much broader compromise.22 The token is fetched from Azure's metadata endpoint, which requires a Metadata: true header and is subject to a hop limit much like AWS IMDSv2.19 From there, escalation routes run through over-permissive role assignments (a principal able to run az role assignment create with the Owner role is, effectively, an owner), Azure Automation accounts and runbooks that execute under highly privileged identities, and hybrid-joined virtual machines that bridge the cloud tenant to an on-premises Active Directory.22 The standard Azure red-team toolkit therefore extends well beyond MicroBurst to AADInternals, ROADtools, GraphRunner, PowerZure, AzureHound, and the multi-cloud auditor ScoutSuite.22

Two control planes, one tenant

An Azure assessment is really an assessment of two distinct planes that a single identity may straddle. Azure Resource Manager governs subscriptions, resource groups, and the infrastructure within them; Entra ID and Microsoft Graph govern the directory itself — users, groups, applications, and the role assignments that bind them.22 A compromise often begins in one plane and is only catastrophic once it crosses into the other: a managed identity with Owner over a subscription can dominate its resources, but a principal that gains the Global Administrator or Privileged Role Administrator role in Entra can rewrite the directory's trust fabric for every subscription beneath it. Testers therefore enumerate both — Azure role assignments through ARM, and directory roles and application permissions through Graph — because the most damaging escalation is frequently the quiet ARM-to-Entra crossing that a resource-only review never sees.12

The Primary Refresh Token is Azure's highest-value credential artifact. Issued to a device when it joins Entra ID, the PRT is a long-lived token that brokers single sign-on across the tenant's cloud applications; an attacker who compromises an Entra-joined endpoint and extracts the PRT inherits the user's authenticated sessions wholesale, sidestepping both the password and, in many configurations, the multi-factor prompt that protects it.22 This is the cloud-native successor to pass-the-hash: the stolen object is not a password to crack but a live token to replay, and it is exactly why conditional-access policies that bind sessions to compliant, managed devices — rather than to a one-time factor at sign-in — are the control that meaningfully raises the cost of a tenant breach.12 Legacy authentication protocols undercut all of it, because a single application that still speaks an old protocol offers a path that conditional access was never able to inspect.22

07 — GCP

Google Cloud Platform

Google Cloud's identity model rewards a different instinct than AWS or Azure. Where AWS attackers obsess over policies attached to users and roles, GCP escalation is overwhelmingly a story about service accounts — non-human identities that both authenticate workloads and serve as the resources other principals are granted permission to act upon. The pivotal permission is iam.serviceAccounts.actAs, the GCP analogue of AWS iam:PassRole: it lets a principal operate as a service account, and any identity that can attach a more-privileged service account to a resource it controls can inherit that account's privileges.24 Granting the Service Account User role at the project level — a common convenience — hands a principal the ability to act as every service account in the project, default accounts included.24

That default matters because Google's legacy default Compute Engine service account is granted the project-wide Editor role, and unless an organization has explicitly disabled this behavior, any workload that runs as it — a GCE instance, a Cloud Function, a Cloud Build job — carries broad write access to the entire project.25 Compromise such a workload and you do not need a clever escalation chain; you already hold Editor.

The escalation catalogue

Rhino Security Labs and Praetorian have catalogued the standalone permissions that each grant a path to full project control. Several recur in real assessments:

  • iam.serviceAccountKeys.create — mint a new private key for a more-privileged service account and authenticate directly as it, no further interaction required.25
  • iam.serviceAccounts.getAccessToken / signJwt / signBlob — generate short-lived tokens or signed assertions for a target service account, impersonating it without ever touching a key file.24
  • iam.roles.update — if you can edit a custom role you are already assigned, append arbitrary permissions to it and escalate in place.25
  • iam.serviceAccounts.implicitDelegation — chain through one service account to obtain tokens for another, walking a delegation graph toward higher privilege.24
  • cloudfunctions.functions.create + actAs — deploy a function bound to a privileged service account and exfiltrate its token from inside the execution environment, mirroring the AWS Lambda escalation path.26

The most elegant GCP-native escalation runs through Deployment Manager. The Google APIs service agent that Deployment Manager uses to create resources runs with the Editor role, so a principal with deploymentmanager.deployments.create — but without direct administrative rights — can submit a deployment that creates resources or binds policy on their behalf, borrowing the agent's Editor privileges to escalate.24 It is a textbook confused-deputy problem baked into a first-party service.

In GCP the question is rarely “what can this identity do?” It is “which other identity can this one become?” — and how short is the walk from there to Editor.

Credentialed enumeration

Because GCP rarely exposes pre-authentication attack surface, engagements are predominantly credentialed grey-box exercises that begin from a provided service-account key or user identity and map reachable impersonation paths outward.8 The tooling reflects this. ScoutSuite's gcp provider audits project and organization configuration; Google's own gcp_scanner walks the resources and permissions reachable from a given credential; and NetSPI's gcpwn, alongside the older gcp_enum and Hacking the Cloud's privilege-escalation scripts, automates the search for the actAs edges and over-broad bindings that turn a foothold into project ownership.8 Workspace-integrated tenants add a further dimension: domain-wide delegation, where a service account is authorized to impersonate human users across Google Workspace, can convert a cloud compromise into mailbox and document access across the organization.8

Two GCP-specific mechanics reward attention. The first is the interaction between metadata-served tokens and OAuth scopes: a Compute Engine instance obtains its service account's access token from the same metadata endpoint pattern seen elsewhere, but that token is additionally bounded by the scopes configured on the instance, so a tester who pulls a token must check both the underlying IAM grants and the scope ceiling before assuming what it can do.25 In practice the default scope on many instances is broad enough that the IAM policy, not the scope, is the real limit — but the two-layer model is a recurring source of confusion that a careful enumeration resolves rather than guesses.26 The second is Cloud Build, whose default service account has historically carried expansive project permissions; a principal able to submit a build can run arbitrary steps under that identity, making the CI service itself an escalation path in the same family as AWS Lambda execution-role abuse.25

The defensive counterweight unique to Google Cloud is the organization policy service, which applies constraints across an entire resource hierarchy independent of IAM. Constraints that disable service-account key creation, restrict which identities may be granted roles, and forbid default-network creation close off whole categories of the escalation paths catalogued above before any individual project misconfigures itself — and because they are inherited down the folder and project tree, they scale in a way per-project review never can.8 For the tester, confirming whether these constraints are set is as informative as any single permission check: their absence is what makes the impersonation graph so reliably traversable in the first place.26

08 — Storage

Storage exposure

Object storage is the most reliably profitable target in cloud security because the failure mode is so simple: a bucket meant for one audience is reachable by another. Amazon S3, Azure Blob Storage, and Google Cloud Storage all expose the same essential risk through different access-control vocabularies, and the testing pattern is identical across them — enumerate candidate names, probe each for anonymous or over-broad access, and characterise what a reachable container leaks.3

Enumeration and access

Bucket namespaces are global and guessable, which makes them fuzzable. Attackers generate candidate names from the target's brand, products, and environments — acme-prod-backups, acme-dev-assets — and test each against the storage endpoint. On Azure this is exactly what MicroBurst's Invoke-EnumerateAzureBlobs automates, fuzzing common container patterns to surface publicly readable Blobs full of backups, configuration, and tokens.23 The misconfigurations that turn a discovered bucket into a breach are well understood: legacy S3 ACLs that grant read or write to AuthenticatedUsers (meaning any AWS account, not your account) or to AllUsers (the anonymous internet), and resource policies whose Principal is the wildcard *.3 A bucket open for listing leaks an inventory of every object; a bucket open for writing invites tampering and malware seeding; a bucket open for reading exposes whatever sits inside.

Permissive storage is rarely the whole breach — it is the second act. In the Capital One intrusion, stolen role credentials were used to run a list operation against the bank's S3 buckets and then sync their contents out, converting credential theft into the exfiltration of more than a hundred million records.20 The lesson testers carry into every engagement is that storage findings compound with identity findings: the impact of an over-permissive role is bounded by the data the role can reach, and object storage is usually where the data lives.

High FND-STO-02 Publicly listable object store with sensitive contents

A storage bucket grants list and read access to an over-broad principal — anonymous users, all authenticated cloud accounts, or a wildcard resource policy — exposing object inventories and their contents to unauthorised retrieval.

Defensively, the controls that close the exposure are blunt and effective. AWS S3 Block Public Access overrides any bucket-level ACL or policy that would otherwise make an object public, and is the single highest-leverage S3 setting an organisation can enable account-wide; Azure and GCP offer equivalent tenant- and project-level switches.3 Tellingly, the AWS penetration-testing rules of engagement explicitly prohibit S3 bucket takeover as out of scope — a reminder that the boundary between assessing your own exposed buckets and abusing the shared namespace against others is one testers must respect.5

09 — Kubernetes

Kubernetes & container attacks

Managed Kubernetes — EKS, AKS, GKE — folds container orchestration into the cloud identity plane, and the attacker's objective is almost always the same: escape the boundary of a single pod and pivot toward control of a node, and from there the cluster. The methodology runs from external reconnaissance of exposed control-plane and kubelet endpoints, through in-cluster enumeration of permissions, to container escape and lateral movement toward the master node.27

Exposed control-plane surface

A cluster exposes several network services that should never face the internet but frequently do. The kube-apiserver on 6443 (or 8080 in dangerously misconfigured legacy setups) is the front door; the kubelet exposes a read-write API on 10250 and, historically, an unauthenticated read-only port on 10255; and etcd on 2379, if reachable, hands over the cluster's entire state, secrets and all.28 An exposed kubelet API is particularly dangerous: where it permits anonymous access, an attacker can list pods and invoke the /exec endpoint to run commands inside running containers directly, bypassing the API server's authorization entirely.29 Tools such as kube-hunter probe for these exposures from outside and within, while kube-bench measures a cluster against the CIS Kubernetes Benchmark.28

Service-account tokens and RBAC

Every pod, by default, mounts a service-account token at a well-known path inside the container, and that token is a valid credential for the Kubernetes API. An attacker who achieves code execution in a pod immediately reads the token and queries the API to learn what it can do — the in-cluster equivalent of whoami being answered by kubectl auth can-i --list.29 Over-permissive Role-Based Access Control is what turns that token into a cluster takeover: a service account bound to rights it does not need — the ability to create pods, read secrets cluster-wide, or worst of all the cluster-admin role — lets an attacker escalate from a single compromised container to the entire cluster.30 A service account that can create pods, for instance, can schedule a malicious pod that mounts a privileged configuration and seizes the node it lands on.30

Kubernetes hardened this default in v1.24, which stopped auto-generating non-expiring secret-based tokens for service accounts in favour of the short-lived, audience-bound tokens issued by the TokenRequest API — narrowing the window during which a stolen token is useful.28 Setting automountServiceAccountToken: false on pods that do not call the API removes the credential from the blast radius entirely.29

Container escape

The decisive move is breaking out of the container onto the host. Three misconfigurations make this routine. A container run with the --privileged flag (or an equivalently permissive securityContext) is granted host-level capabilities that let a process manipulate the node's devices and kernel namespaces to reach the host filesystem. A hostPath volume mounting the node's root directory at / into the pod exposes the host's filesystem — including the kubelet's own credentials — directly to container processes. And hostNetwork or hostPID sharing collapses the isolation between container and node.28 Any one of these can be parlayed into code execution on the node; combined with a privileged service-account token, node compromise becomes cluster compromise.30

Attack Chain · Kubernetes

From application pod to cluster-admin

The canonical cluster takeover chains five ordinary weaknesses. A vulnerable web workload yields remote code execution inside a pod; the attacker reads the mounted service-account token and runs kubectl auth can-i --list to inventory its rights; a --privileged or hostPath pod mounts the node's root filesystem to escape onto the host; the kubelet's credentials are stolen and used to schedule a malicious pod on every node; and an over-broad RBAC binding finally grants cluster-admin — at which point cluster-wide secret theft, workload tampering, and durable persistence are all in reach.

Throughout, kubectl is the tester's primary instrument — authenticating with a discovered kubeconfig or token, enumerating permissions, inspecting secrets, and launching the pods that effect escape and lateral movement. In managed clusters the final twist is that the node's identity is itself a cloud principal: a compromised EKS node carries an IAM role, an AKS node a managed identity, so cluster escape flows straight back into the cloud-IAM attack surface the rest of this analysis has mapped.27

10 — Serverless

Serverless security

Functions-as-a-Service — AWS Lambda, Azure Functions, Google Cloud Functions — dissolve the server the defender used to patch and monitor, leaving the code, its event sources, and its execution-role permissions as the entire attack surface. The OWASP community codified the resulting risk shift in its Serverless Top 10, which re-maps familiar web weaknesses onto an architecture where every function is triggered by events drawn from a wide variety of sources.31 That diversity of triggers is the crux: a function may be invoked not only by an HTTP request but by an object landing in a bucket, a message on a queue, a database stream, or an email — and each of those event sources is a potential injection vector that conventional input validation, tuned for HTTP, may never inspect.32

Event injection and untrusted input

Event-data injection is the signature serverless flaw. Because event payloads arrive in many formats from many services, developers frequently trust them implicitly, passing fields straight into shell commands, database queries, or deserialization routines; an attacker who can influence any upstream event source can smuggle a payload that the function executes.33 The defensive instinct — treat every element of every event as untrusted, regardless of which service delivered it — is harder to apply than in a monolith precisely because the entry points are so numerous and so well disguised as internal plumbing.32

Over-privileged execution roles

The second dominant risk is the function's own identity. Every Lambda runs with an execution role, and the path of least resistance during development is to attach a broad managed policy and move on. When such a function is compromised, its role becomes the attacker's role — and the credentials are trivially available, because the runtime injects them into the environment as the AWS_ACCESS_KEY_ID, AWS_SECRET_ACCESS_KEY, and mandatory AWS_SESSION_TOKEN variables that any code execution inside the function can read.33 Granting each function only the specific permissions it needs is the controlling mitigation, because it bounds what a successful injection can ultimately reach.34 This over-privilege is also what makes Lambda a privilege-escalation pivot from the IAM direction: a principal able to pass a privileged role and create or update a function can run arbitrary code under that role's identity, exactly the escalation path catalogued earlier.14

Three further properties shape serverless testing. Execution environments are reused across invocations, so the writable /tmp directory and process memory can retain data — or an attacker's implant — between calls, and sensitive material left behind by one invocation may be readable by the next.34 Per-function billing introduces denial-of-wallet: rather than knocking a service offline, an attacker drives up invocation volume to inflict runaway cost.32 And the ecosystem provides deliberately vulnerable applications — OWASP's ServerlessGoat and the Damn Vulnerable Serverless Application — for practising these techniques against Lambda in a controlled account.31

Practitioner note
Map the trigger graph first. For each function, enumerate every event source that can invoke it and every resource its execution role can reach; the product of those two sets is the function's true blast radius, and it is almost always larger than the team that wrote the function believes.
11 — Toolkit

The cloud pentester's toolkit

Cloud assessment tooling divides cleanly into two families: auditors that read configuration to surface misconfiguration at breadth, and exploitation frameworks that act on a foothold to demonstrate impact. A disciplined engagement runs the auditors first to build a map, then reaches for the offensive tools to prove which findings are exploitable.13

Auditors and posture scanners

ScoutSuite, maintained by NCC Group, is the canonical multi-cloud auditor. Pointed at AWS, Azure, GCP, or others through read-only credentials, it queries the provider's configuration APIs and emits a static HTML report ranking misconfigurations by risk — a fast, non-intrusive first pass that needs only an account with a read-only posture-audit policy (on AWS, the managed SecurityAudit policy) to run.35 Prowler occupies the same niche with a compliance emphasis: an open-source tool covering AWS, Azure, GCP, and Kubernetes, it ships hundreds of checks mapped to dozens of frameworks — CIS, PCI DSS, HIPAA, GDPR, NIST and more — and integrates with AWS Security Hub for continuous monitoring.36 Created by Toni de la Fuente, Prowler is frequently the tool that translates a pile of raw findings into the language of an audit an organisation actually has to pass.36

For identity specifically, PMapper (Principal Mapper) models an account's IAM as a directed graph and computes who can reach administrative privilege through the escalation edges that manual review misses, complementing the auditors' breadth with a focused answer to the question that matters most.13

Exploitation frameworks

Pacu, from Rhino Security Labs, is to AWS what Metasploit is to a network: a modular post-exploitation framework whose modules enumerate permissions, scan for and execute privilege escalation, hunt and exfiltrate data, establish persistence, and tamper with CloudTrail logging — all driven through the same APIs an administrator uses, so the activity blends into normal control-plane traffic.17 On the Azure side, NetSPI's MicroBurst is the PowerShell-first counterpart for enumeration and exploitation, discovering namespace resources, fuzzing for exposed Blob containers, and dumping automation-account credentials, with Az PowerShell and the Az CLI underpinning all interactive work against a tenant.23 For Kubernetes, kubectl remains the primary exploitation instrument, paired with kube-hunter for discovery and kube-bench for benchmark measurement, and the picture is complete: a read-only auditor for breadth, a graph tool for identity, and a provider-native exploitation framework to convert findings into demonstrated, reportable risk.13

12 — Standards

Standards, benchmarks & regulation

A cloud penetration test exists inside a lattice of standards that tell the tester what "secure" is supposed to mean and tell the client which boxes a clean report lets them tick. The most operationally useful of these are the CIS Benchmarks — consensus-developed, prescriptive configuration baselines maintained by the Center for Internet Security for virtually every cloud platform and service in common use.37 There are Foundations Benchmarks for AWS, Azure, and GCP, plus dedicated benchmarks for Kubernetes and managed offerings such as Amazon EKS, and each control is graded as Level 1 (a baseline every organisation should meet without breaking functionality) or Level 2 (defence-in-depth for high-security environments at some cost to convenience).36 Because tools like Prowler and kube-bench check directly against these benchmarks, CIS is where abstract policy meets a concrete pass/fail an assessor can run.36

The CIS AWS Foundations Benchmark illustrates the texture: its controls span identity and access management, logging, monitoring, and networking, and AWS Security Hub operationalises them as automated checks reported against the standard.38 An organisation that passes the relevant Level 1 controls has closed most of the misconfigurations that the earlier sections of this analysis showed an attacker exploiting first — public buckets, unrotated keys, absent logging, over-broad security groups.38

Frameworks and regulation

Above the benchmarks sit the broader frameworks and the regulations that compel them. NIST contributes the control catalogue SP 800-53 and the cloud-relevant testing guidance SP 800-115, while the Cloud Security Alliance maintains the Cloud Controls Matrix — a cloud-specific control framework — and the STAR registry through which providers attest their posture against it.39 The ISO/IEC 27000 family extends the general 27001 information-security management system into the cloud through 27017 (cloud security controls) and 27018 (protection of personal data in public clouds).40 Layered on top are the compliance regimes a finding can implicate directly: PCI DSS for payment-card data, HIPAA for health information, SOC 2 for service-organisation controls, and GDPR for the personal data of EU residents.40 These regimes are why a single exposed bucket is rarely "just" a technical finding — depending on what it holds, it can constitute a reportable breach under one or more legal frameworks, and the penetration-test report becomes evidence in that determination.39

The synthesising layer most clients invoke is the cloud provider's own Well-Architected Framework, whose security pillar restates much of the above as actionable architectural guidance. For the tester, the practical value of this lattice is shared vocabulary: a finding expressed as a failed CIS control, mapped to a MITRE ATT&CK technique and a CVSS score, lands with both the engineers who must fix it and the auditors and regulators who must sign off on it.39

13 — Defense

Best practices & defensive hardening

The value of an offensive engagement is the defensive program it informs. Every attack path mapped in this analysis terminates in a control that would have broken it, and the highest-leverage controls cluster around three themes: shrink identity privilege, lock the metadata service, and make activity visible.41

Identity is the new perimeter

The recurring lesson of cloud breaches is that compromise rarely begins with a kernel exploit; it begins with an over-permissive identity. Least privilege, rigorously applied, is therefore the master control — grant each principal only the permissions it demonstrably needs, prefer narrowly scoped policies over managed administrative ones, and eliminate the standing wildcards (Action: *, Resource: *, Principal: *) that convert any foothold into a catastrophe.14 Enforce multi-factor authentication universally, because the password spray that opens an Azure tenant fails the moment a second factor is required; rotate and, better, eliminate long-lived access keys in favour of short-lived role credentials; and use organisation-wide guardrails — AWS Service Control Policies and their Azure and GCP equivalents — to set hard ceilings that no individual account can exceed.41 On the GCP side specifically, disabling the default Editor grant on the Compute Engine service account removes one of the most common escalation shortcuts before it can ever be used.14

Close the metadata and storage gaps

Because the metadata endpoint is the hinge of so many cloud intrusions, enforcing IMDSv2 — requiring session tokens, setting a hop limit of one, and disabling IMDSv1 outright — is among the single most effective hardening steps an AWS estate can take, neutralising the SSRF-to-credential-theft chain that defined the Capital One breach.19 In parallel, enabling account-wide public-access blocking on object storage closes the exposure class that leaks data most often, overriding any errant bucket policy an engineer might introduce later.41

Control DEF-IMDS-01 Enforce IMDSv2 and a hop limit of one

Require token-backed metadata requests, set the response hop limit to 1 so a containerised or proxied SSRF cannot reach the endpoint, and disable IMDSv1 across the fleet — converting the most reliable cloud credential-theft primitive into a dead end.

Make the invisible visible

An attacker operating through legitimate APIs is invisible only if no one is watching the control plane. Comprehensive, tamper-resistant logging — AWS CloudTrail across all regions, Azure Activity and sign-in logs, GCP Cloud Audit Logs — is the substrate on which every detection depends, which is precisely why Pacu includes modules to disrupt it.41 Layered on top, behavioural threat-detection services such as Amazon GuardDuty surface the anomalies that signature rules miss: credentials used from an unfamiliar location, reconnaissance API patterns, the tell-tale call sequence of an escalation attempt.41 The defensive end-state is a feedback loop — least privilege shrinks what an attacker can do, a hardened metadata service and storage posture removes the easy primitives, and pervasive logging plus anomaly detection ensures that whatever remains is seen and answered.41

14 — Learn

Certifications, labs & training

Cloud penetration testing is learned in vulnerable accounts, not slide decks, but a structured path through certifications, hands-on ranges, and community resources accelerates the climb from competent network tester to credible cloud specialist.42

Certifications

The vendor-neutral offensive credential most associated with the discipline is GIAC's GCPN (GIAC Cloud Penetration Tester), paired with SANS course SEC588; the exam spans assessment of both AWS and Azure environments and the cloud-native services, identity systems, and microservice architectures that define them.43 SANS positions SEC588 as its dedicated cloud penetration-testing course, covering the techniques for attacking cloud infrastructure, Kubernetes, and the modern application stack.44 Beyond it, a well-trodden ladder runs through Altered Security's CARTP (Certified Azure Red Team Professional) for Azure depth, the HackTricks training certifications (ARTE for AWS, AzRTE for Azure, GRTE for GCP), CyberWarFare Labs and the SecOps Group's certified cloud-pentest tracks, and the defensive-leaning vendor exams — AWS Certified Security Specialty and Microsoft's AZ-500 — that teach the controls from the inside.42 For breadth, ISC2's CCSP and EC-Council's CEH round out a résumé, though neither is a substitute for demonstrated offensive skill.45

Hands-on ranges

The deliberately vulnerable lab is the heart of cloud-security practice, and the canonical environments are open source and free to deploy in a sandbox account:

  • CloudGoat — Rhino Security Labs' "vulnerable by design" AWS deployment tool, which provisions scenario-based environments each centred on a specific escalation or exploitation path.46
  • AWSGoat and CloudFoxable — scenario ranges that chain web, IAM, and service misconfigurations into realistic AWS attack paths.47
  • IAM Vulnerable — a Terraform project from Bishop Fox that stands up an account riddled with the privilege-escalation paths catalogued by the research community, the ideal range for practising Pacu's iam__privesc_scan against live targets.47
  • flaws.cloud and flaws2.cloud — Scott Piper's browser-based, progressively harder AWS challenges that introduce IMDS, S3, and IAM mistakes one lesson at a time.47
  • ServerlessGoat and the Damn Vulnerable Serverless Application — Lambda-focused ranges for the event-injection and over-privileged-role techniques of serverless testing.31

Community and continuous learning

The fast-moving knowledge lives in community references and platforms. HackTricks Cloud is the field's most comprehensive open reference, with provider-by-provider methodology and a deep catalogue of GCP, AWS, and Azure escalation techniques; Hacking the Cloud collects exploitation write-ups; and Pwned Labs and CloudBreach offer guided, hands-on cloud-attack training.42 Bug-bounty platforms — HackerOne and Bugcrowd — provide legal targets and a feedback loop against real production environments for those ready to test live systems within a defined scope.45 For ongoing study, practitioners follow the research output and conference talks of the firms whose tools and findings recur throughout this field — Rhino Security Labs, NCC Group, NetSPI, Bishop Fox, and Wiz among them — whose blogs, GitHub repositories, and recorded presentations are where each season's new techniques first appear.47

A suggested path
Start in flaws.cloud and CloudGoat to internalise the core AWS primitives, graduate to IAM Vulnerable with Pacu to drill privilege escalation, add Azure through CARTP and a MicroBurst-driven lab, then validate with GCPN/SEC588 — and keep a live bug-bounty scope open throughout to stay honest against production reality.
15 — Closing

The control plane is the battlefield

What unites every technique in this analysis is a single shift in where the fight happens. The on-premises attacker fought for packets and processes; the cloud attacker fights for API calls. Credentials harvested from a metadata endpoint, a service account impersonated through a single permission, a managed identity lifted from a compromised function, a service-account token read from a pod — each is a key to the control plane, and the control plane is where data lives, moves, and leaks. This is why identity, not the network, is the perimeter that matters, and why the most consequential findings on a cloud engagement are almost never memory-corruption bugs but configuration and permission mistakes that let legitimate APIs be turned against their owner.

For the practitioner, the discipline that follows is twofold. First, operate within the boundary: honour the provider's rules of engagement, stay inside the agreed scope, and remember that the same namespace you assess is shared with tenants you may not touch. Second, translate relentlessly — a finding is only as valuable as the fix it provokes, so every exploited path should leave the client with a least-privilege policy to write, a metadata service to harden, a bucket to lock, or a logging gap to close. The Capital One breach, which recurs throughout this analysis, was not the work of a novel exploit; it was an SSRF reaching an over-permissive role through a metadata service that a single configuration flag would have protected. The cloud's attack surface is vast, but its defensive levers are precise. Pull them in the right order, and the control plane stops being the adversary's battlefield and becomes the defender's.
