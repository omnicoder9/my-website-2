Metaethics · A Survey

Neither Bedrock
nor Abyss

The foundations of morality have been declared bankrupt many times over — under Hume's fork, Moore's question, Mackie's queerness, Darwin's long shadow. This is a map of what survives the collapse, and an argument that the loss of bedrock is not the loss of truth.

In twelve parts · with eight figures · Vancouver references

§ I · The terrain

The question beneath the questions

Ask someone whether it is wrong to set a cat alight for entertainment, and the answer comes back fast and hard. Ask what they did in giving it — reported a fact, vented a feeling, or issued a command binding on anyone who understands it — and the speed drains away. That second question is the whole business of metaethics.

Normative ethics asks which things are good and which acts are right; applied ethics asks what those verdicts demand in a clinic, a slaughterhouse, or a war. Metaethics stands a storey above both and asks what we are doing when we moralize at all.1 Its questions come in four kinds. Semantic: what do moral sentences mean — do they state something that can be true or false? Metaphysical: are there moral facts, and if so, what in the world are they made of? Epistemological: if such facts exist, how could a creature like us ever come to know them? And psychological: why does a moral judgement seem to grip the will, so that sincerely calling an act vile already disinclines you to do it, when a weather report never could?

The oldest version of the puzzle is still the sharpest. In Plato's Euthyphro, Socrates asks whether the holy is loved by the gods because it is holy, or holy because they love it.2 Strip out the theology and a permanent dilemma survives for every theory that grounds morality in some authority — God, a culture, a sentiment, a choice. Either actions are right because the authority favours them, in which case morality looks arbitrary and might have gone the other way on a whim; or the authority favours them because they are already right, in which case the authority is not the source, and we must still ask what is. Twenty-four centuries of metaethics are, in a sense, the search for a passage between those horns.

Because the candidate answers multiply quickly, it helps to carry a map. Figure 1 lays the major positions out along the two axes that will organize this essay.

Read left to right, the horizontal axis is the semantic question: do moral sentences express beliefs that aim at truth (cognitivism), or do they express desires, attitudes, and endorsements that do not (non-cognitivism)? Read bottom to top, the vertical axis is the metaphysical one: granting that a moral claim can be true, is it made true by facts that hold independently of what any of us thinks (realism), or not? The tour that follows runs roughly in the order the modern debate built itself (Figure 2), and it ends where the opening example was always heading: if moral conviction is threaded through feeling, and feeling rests on nothing unshakeable, does morality retain any claim to be true?

§ II · Sentiment and definition

Hume's guillotine and Moore's question

Two arguments, a century and a half apart, set the agenda for everything after. The first is David Hume's. Folded into the Treatise is the observation that writers on morality slide, without remark, from strings of premises about what is the case to a conclusion about what ought to be — and that this transition, being of an entirely new kind, needs an explanation none of them has supplied.3 No accumulation of purely descriptive facts, however vast, appears to entail a normative conclusion by logic alone (Figure 3). The point is now called Hume's guillotine, or the is–ought gap.

Hume paired the logical point with a psychological one. Reason, he held, discerns relations and matters of fact, but it is inert: it maps the terrain and calculates the route without ever supplying the motive to travel. The impetus to act comes from passion, from sentiment; morality is “more properly felt than judg'd of.” His notorious slogan makes reason the “slave of the passions.”3 Here, already, is the entanglement of belief and feeling this essay must eventually reckon with: for Hume, to draw a moral distinction just is to feel a certain approbation or disgust, refined and made general.

The second argument is G. E. Moore's, in 1903. Suppose someone proposes that “good” simply means some natural property — say, “pleasant,” or “what we desire to desire.” Moore's open-question argument replies that for any such property it always remains an intelligible, open question whether things possessing it are good. “This is pleasant, but is it good?” is not the empty tautology it would be if the two were identical. Since the question stays open, “good” cannot be identical to any natural property; to think otherwise is to commit what Moore named the naturalistic fallacy.4 Good, he concluded, is a real but simple, unanalysable, non-natural quality, apprehended by a kind of intuition.

The irony is that Moore, a realist who believed in objective goodness, handed the next generation the very tool with which to deny it. If “good” names no natural fact — nothing you could see, measure, or reduce — then perhaps, they reasoned, it names no fact at all.

If “good” names no natural fact, perhaps it names no fact at all. That inference, drawn from a realist's premise, launched a century of anti-realism.

§ III · The emotive turn

Morality as attitude

The inference was drawn by the logical positivists. On their verification principle, a declarative sentence has factual meaning only if it is either true by definition or checkable, in principle, against experience. Moral sentences are neither. A. J. Ayer took the blunt path: to say “stealing is wrong” adds no factual content whatever to “stealing.” It expresses the speaker's disapproval and seeks to kindle the same in the listener — the view later caricatured, not altogether unfairly, as the “boo/hurrah” theory of ethics.5 Moral disagreement, on this picture, is not disagreement about any fact; it is a collision of attitudes wearing the grammar of assertion.

Charles Stevenson made the position subtler. Moral terms, he argued, carry emotive meaning and are put to a dynamic use: their office is to influence attitudes, not to describe. When two people dispute whether a war was just, they may agree on every fact and still disagree — a “disagreement in attitude” — which is why marshalling further facts need not settle it.6 R. M. Hare then recast non-cognitivism as prescriptivism: moral judgements are a species of prescription, akin to commands, but universalizable ones. To hold that you ought not to lie commits you to holding that no one relevantly similar may lie, yourself included; the logic of “ought” is the logic of universal imperatives.7 Hare also pressed the feature every later theory would have to honour — supervenience: two things cannot differ morally without differing in some natural respect.8

Non-cognitivism thus advanced two claims: a semantic one, that moral utterances are not in the business of stating facts, and a psychological one, that they express conative states — desires, endorsements, feelings — rather than beliefs.8 Its appeal was considerable. It explained, with no metaphysical extravagance, why moral judgement moves us (it just is a pro- or con-attitude), why moral disagreement runs so deep, and why “good” had resisted every naturalistic definition. For a while it looked as though the Euthyphro dilemma had been dissolved by denying that moral claims were ever fact-stating to begin with.

§ IV · The logic of moral talk

The Frege–Geach problem

Then Peter Geach, developing a point of Frege's, produced the objection that has shadowed non-cognitivism ever since. Moral sentences do not only get asserted; they get embedded — in conditionals, questions, disjunctions — and they figure in valid inferences. Take a plain piece of reasoning: lying is wrong; if lying is wrong, then getting your little brother to lie is wrong; therefore getting your little brother to lie is wrong.9

The argument is valid by modus ponens. But look at the clause “lying is wrong.” In the first premise it is asserted; in the antecedent of the second it is not — the speaker is not there voicing disapproval of lying, only supposing it for the sake of the conditional. If the emotivist is right that the meaning of “lying is wrong” just is the act of venting disapproval, then the clause means something in the premise and nothing in the antecedent, and the inference should equivocate. It plainly does not. Naive non-cognitivism could not explain how moral sentences keep a stable meaning across asserted and unasserted contexts — how they behave, logically, exactly like ordinary truth-apt claims. The embedding problem drove the field back toward cognitivism, and set its more sophisticated defenders the task, decades later, of building a logic for attitudes.

§ V · The realist revival

Realisms, natural and non-natural

If moral sentences express beliefs apt for truth, and some of those beliefs are true, then there are moral facts — and one is a moral realist. The late twentieth century saw realism return in two very different forms, distinguished by what they take moral facts to be made of.

Non-naturalists hold, with Moore, that moral properties are real but sui generis — not identical to, or reducible to, any natural or physical property, though they depend upon them. The view was revived with new rigour by Russell Shafer-Landau10 and defended as “robust realism” by David Enoch, who argues that irreducibly normative truths are indispensable to deliberation and must simply be taken seriously as a basic feature of reality.11 Derek Parfit's monumental late work belongs here too, defending non-natural normative truths that are, he held, as real as mathematical ones though they exist in no place and no time.12 The standing objection is epistemic and metaphysical at once: how do such facts fit into the natural world described by science, and by what faculty could we possibly detect them?

Naturalists answer by collapsing the gap. Moral facts, they hold, just are natural facts — complex ones, perhaps, about what serves human flourishing, or about what a suitably idealized agent would want — and are known the way other natural facts are, a posteriori, by their explanatory fruit. On the “Cornell realist” version, moral terms latch onto natural properties by the same causal mechanisms that let “water” refer to H₂O, with no need for a definition.13 Peter Railton built a “reforming naturalism” that identifies a person's good with what their fully informed self would want them to want,14 and David Brink assembled a systematic defence of moral realism on naturalist and coherentist foundations.15

The sharpest challenge to any realism was Gilbert Harman's. Moral facts, he argued, seem to do no explanatory work: when you round a corner, see hoodlums setting a cat alight, and judge the act wrong, the best explanation of your judgement cites your upbringing and moral sensibility, not any wrongness resident in the scene — unlike a physicist's observation, which really does require the particle to explain it.16 Nicholas Sturgeon replied that moral facts earn their keep after all: a tyrant's depravity figures in the best explanation of what he does, and the injustice of a social order helps explain the unrest it breeds; moral facts are no more dispensable than many respectable posits of the special sciences.17 Whatever one concludes, all parties came to treat supervenience as a fixed point: no two situations can differ in their moral properties without differing in some natural property.8

§ VI · The case for the abyss

Error, queerness, debunking

The most uncompromising anti-realism grants the realist almost everything about how moral language works — and then declares the whole of it a systematic mistake. This is J. L. Mackie's error theory. Ordinary moral thought, Mackie agrees, is cognitivist and realist to the core: when we call an act wrong we mean to be describing an objective feature of it, one that would give anyone a reason to refrain quite apart from their desires. That is exactly the pretension he thinks fails. His book opens by nailing its thesis to the door: “There are no objective values.”18 If our moral claims one and all presuppose such values, and there are none, then every positive moral claim is, strictly, false — as talk of witches was false.

Mackie gave two arguments. The argument from relativity observes that the sheer breadth and stubbornness of moral variation across cultures is better explained by supposing that moral codes reflect ways of life than by supposing that most people, most of the time, are misperceiving the same objective values. The argument from queerness cuts deeper: objective values, were there any, would be entities or qualities utterly unlike anything else in the universe — built-in prescriptions, facts that motivate all on their own — and knowing them would demand a special faculty unlike any ordinary mode of perception. Rather than admit such queer things, we should conclude they are not there.18 Note that this is not subjectivism, which keeps moral truth and merely relocates it inside us; it is nihilism about objective value, after which Mackie proposes that we carry on and consciously invent a morality to serve the purposes morality serves.

A modern descendant sharpens the worry with biology. Sharon Street's “Darwinian dilemma” points out that our basic evaluative tendencies were shaped by natural selection because they enhanced reproductive fitness, not because they tracked any mind-independent moral truth. The realist then faces a fork: either deny any relation between the two — in which case it would be sheer luck that our fitness-driven attitudes ever landed on the moral truth, and we should be sceptics — or claim that selection tracked the truth, which is a scientifically hopeless bet. Anti-realism slips between the tines by positing no attitude-independent truths for evolution to have missed.19 Richard Joyce presses a parallel “evolutionary debunking” argument: once we see that we would hold our moral beliefs whether or not they were true, their justification is undercut.20 This is the case for the abyss in its strongest contemporary form, and it is the case the remainder of this essay must answer.

If the sentiments underwriting morality are evolutionary happenstance, what could possibly make any moral claim true?

§ VII · Belief, desire, and the will

The motivation problem

To answer it we have to look harder at the knot the opening example exposed — the way moral conviction seems made of judgement and feeling together. The tradition analyses this through the psychology of motivation, and here a tidy puzzle organizes the ground. Three claims, each independently attractive, cannot all be true. Michael Smith calls their conjunction the moral problem (Figure 5).21

The first claim is cognitivism: moral judgements are beliefs, apt for truth. The second is motivational internalism: there is a necessary, if defeasible, link between judging an act right and being motivated to perform it — sincere moral judgement is intrinsically practical, which is why we grow suspicious of the person who placidly agrees that cruelty is wrong and feels not the faintest pull against it. The third is the Humean theory of motivation: beliefs and desires are, in Hume's phrase, “distinct existences”; a belief can represent the world truly but cannot, on its own, move anyone to act — motivation always needs a desire as well.3,21 Each pair is consistent; the trio is not. If moral judgements are beliefs, and beliefs alone cannot motivate, then moral judgements cannot be intrinsically motivating — which contradicts internalism.

Each family of theories is a different escape. Non-cognitivists deny the first claim: a moral judgement just is a desire-like state, so of course it motivates — no puzzle remains. Externalists such as Brink and Railton deny the second: the tie between judgement and motivation is real but contingent, forged by a further desire to be moral, and the coherent “amoralist” who judges truly yet stays unmoved is a genuine possibility.15 Smith's own solution keeps cognitivism and internalism and revises the Humean claim through rationalism: to judge that an act is right is to believe that one would desire to do it if one were fully rational and informed; an agent who holds that belief but lacks the corresponding desire is not merely odd but practically irrational, out of step with their own reasons.21 This descends from a Kantian line, defended by Thomas Nagel, on which some motivations are requirements of reason itself rather than brute desires we happen to find ourselves with.22 Bernard Williams pushed hard from the opposite side, holding that all genuine reasons must connect to an agent's existing motivations, and casting suspicion on the whole peculiar institution of “morality” as a distinct, obligation-driven system.23

Now carry the puzzle back to the messy case. Consider a person who sincerely avows that all people are equal but who still feels a flash of prejudice — the professed egalitarian in the grip of an inherited revulsion. A crude subjectivism, seeing only two attitudes, must say there is nothing to choose between them: the principle and the prejudice are alike just things the person feels. But that is not what we say, and not what the person says on reflection. We say the disgust is a bad inheritance and the principle ought to govern it. That verdict is the crucial datum (Figure 6).

The relationship between principle and feeling is indeed a loop, and it runs both ways: our articulated commitments slowly retrain our reactions, and our reactions press back on our commitments. But the very act of judging that the prejudice, and not the principle, is the part that should give way appeals to something beyond either — a second-order standard of consistency, of fit with the evidence, of answerability to reasons the person themselves endorses. We do not merely have attitudes; we assess them, and rank some as better grounded than others. That capacity for internal criticism is exactly what a picture of morality as nothing but the play of sentiment leaves out. It does not, on its own, prove that there are mind-independent moral facts. What it does is sever the inference from “moral judgement is entangled with feeling” to “moral judgement answers to nothing” — the inference on which both subjectivism and nihilism silently lean.

§ VIII · Earning the right to truth

Quasi-realism

The most ingenious response to that whole predicament refuses to choose between the attitude-based starting point and the realist-sounding conclusions. Simon Blackburn's quasi-realism begins where Hume did — with sentiments we project onto a world that does not, in itself, contain values; we “gild and stain” nature with colours borrowed from our own feelings. The programme is then to show, step by patient step, that a projectivist is entitled to talk exactly as a realist does: to speak of moral claims as true or false, of moral knowledge and error, of facts we can be wrong about, of standards that hold independently of what any one person happens to feel — and to do all this without ever positing Mackie's queer objective values.24,25 The realist-sounding surface of moral language is earned from below rather than read off a special realm above; hence “quasi.” Blackburn extends the account into a full theory of practical reasoning.26

Allan Gibbard supplied the formal machinery the Frege–Geach problem had demanded. To make a normative judgement, on his view, is to accept a system of norms — or, in his later work, to settle on a plan for how to live, ruling out some options across the range of circumstances one might face.27,28 Embedded moral clauses are then handled as constraints of consistency among plans: to reason with “if lying is wrong, then …” is to explore what someone who rules out lying is thereby committed to ruling out. This finally gives non-cognitivism the compositional logic it had lacked, letting attitudes feature in inferences without ever being asserted.8

The upshot is striking, and the standard reference literature records it plainly: once expressivism grows sophisticated enough to earn talk of truth, objectivity, and logical structure, and once realism accepts that moral facts are tightly constrained by our responses and by supervenience, it becomes a delicate, almost terminological matter which label a refined theory deserves.8 For the argument of this essay that convergence is the decisive move. It shows that granting the affective, human, evolved base of moral thought does not cost us the right to speak of moral truth, correctness, and error. Anti-realism about queer metaphysical facts need not be nihilism about moral discourse.

§ IX · Coherence without bedrock

Reflective equilibrium

But if there is no bedrock of self-evident moral axioms, and no special realm of facts to read off, what makes one set of moral views better than another? Here the decisive contribution is a change in moral epistemology. The foundationalist picture — the picture the nihilist assumes and then declares bankrupt — demands a base of certain, self-justifying first principles from which all else is deduced. The persistent failure to locate such principles is taken, by the nihilist, as proof that the enterprise is hollow.

The coherentist alternative was carried into ethics by John Rawls, who adapted it from Nelson Goodman's account of how we justify the very rules of logical inference. Goodman's insight was that a rule of inference and a particular inference we accept justify each other: we amend a rule that licenses an inference we cannot stomach, and we give up an inference that no acceptable rule permits, working back and forth until rules and judgements agree.29 Rawls called the moral analogue reflective equilibrium. We begin from our considered judgements — the moral convictions we hold confidently and under good conditions, free of obvious distortion, such as that chattel slavery is unjust. We frame principles that would systematize them. And we adjust in both directions: a principle that entails a monstrous verdict is revised or discarded, and a “gut” conviction that no defensible principle can support is treated as a prejudice to be discounted — until judgements and principles are brought into alignment.30

Norman Daniels widened the method. In wide reflective equilibrium the pool of beliefs to be reconciled includes not only judgements and principles but background theories — of human nature, of the person, of society, of the role of morality itself — many of them informed by the sciences.31 This is what lets empirical discovery bear on moral principle: findings about the depth of animal sentience, or the mechanics of implicit bias, are exactly the sort of background theory that can dislodge a considered judgement and force a principle to be rewritten. Justification, on this model, is holistic — a moral claim is warranted not by descent from an indubitable axiom but by its fit within a wide, mutually supporting web of judgements, principles, and theories (Figure 7).

Two features of the method matter for the larger argument. It is not subjectivism: a lone feeling that coheres with nothing else one believes, that survives no scrutiny and fits no defensible principle, earns no standing at all. And it is not an anything-goes “coherence,” since the considered judgements are disciplined by consistency, by counterexample, and by shared experience. The standard treatment notes exactly this double exclusion — reflective equilibrium is built to rule out moral scepticism and nihilism on the one side and reductive foundationalism on the other.32 It is the working machinery by which the messy loop of principle and feeling becomes a structure that can bear an argument.

§ X · From mine to ours

Constructivism and the intersubjective

Coherence answers how we revise, but it invites a further question: what makes a reflective equilibrium correct rather than merely stable — comfortable to the one who reaches it? One influential answer transforms the notion of objectivity itself. On constructivist views, moral truths are not descriptions of a realm that exists prior to and apart from us; they are the outputs of an idealized procedure of practical reasoning. What makes a principle correct is that it would be the result of the right kind of reasoning under the right conditions.

The lineage is Kantian. Kant held that the moral law is not discovered in the world but legislated by rational agents for themselves — that autonomy, the giving of universalizable principles to oneself, is the source of obligation.33 Rawls's own constructivism gave this a procedural shape: the principles of justice are those that free and equal persons would choose from behind a “veil of ignorance,” unaware of their place in society and so unable to rig the rules in their own favour.30 Christine Korsgaard located the source of normativity in the reflective structure of agency: a self-conscious creature cannot act at all without endorsing some principle as its reason, and that endorsement, made from the standpoint of one's practical identity, is what confers obligation.34 T. M. Scanlon's contractualism gave the intersubjective idea its sharpest formula: an act is wrong if it would be disallowed by any principle that no one could reasonably reject as a basis for general, informed agreement.35

What unites these views — and what matters most for this essay's thesis — is that they reconstrue objectivity as validity for all reasoners rather than correspondence to a thing. This is the passage from the merely subjective to the genuinely intersubjective. “It is true because I feel it” is subjectivism, and worthless. “It is warranted because it would survive the reasoned scrutiny of everyone it affects” is something else entirely — a standard that any individual, oneself included, can fail to meet. Morality becomes the domain of what we can justify to one another.

Ludwig Wittgenstein supplies the deep reason this is no retreat into private caprice. Meaning and rule-following, he argued, are not solitary feats: a standard that only one person could ever consult, with no possible check, is no standard at all. Our concepts live within shared “forms of life,” and moral concepts in particular are anchored in the vulnerabilities we hold in common — to pain, to loss, to dependence, to death.36 Because those are shared, even interlocutors who begin from very different values can recognize one another's suffering, follow one another's reasons, and be moved by the facts about what their principles actually do to people. The dispute between the libertarian and the communitarian is not a bottomless clash of tastes; both are answerable to what their favoured arrangements produce in the lives of real human beings, and that shared answerability is what makes the argument between them a genuine argument rather than two soliloquies. Convergence is slow, partial, and never guaranteed — but the uneven, contested, and real spread of a shared vocabulary of human rights across very different societies is what partial convergence looks like from the inside.

§ XI · Progress and its analogues

Convergence and the shape of inquiry

The boldest wager on convergence is Derek Parfit's. The best versions of the three great traditions in normative ethics — Kantian, contractualist, and consequentialist — do not, he argued, merely coexist as rival options; properly refined, they agree. They are, in his image, climbers ascending the same mountain by different faces, their apparent disagreements the artefacts of imperfect formulation, their shared summit a compact set of principles he called the Triple Theory (Figure 8).12 Parfit saw himself finishing a project Henry Sidgwick had left open a century before, when Sidgwick closed his great work confronting an unresolved “dualism of practical reason” between self-interest and the good of all.37

Parfit was a robust realist and would have rejected the coherentist and constructivist glosses offered here; the point is not that he endorses this essay's conclusion but that even his opponents can read his convergence claim as a reframing of moral disagreement — turning it from evidence of emptiness into the ordinary friction of a subject still being worked out. And that reframing licenses the essay's central analogy. The nihilist's demand is for an unmoved, emotionless, cosmic foundation — moral facts inscribed in the structure of reality, graspable with no contribution from us — and, not finding one, the nihilist pronounces the practice void. But that standard is met nowhere in serious human inquiry, and we do not, in any other case, conclude that the inquiry has no object.

Physics surrendered absolute simultaneity to relativity, and exact joint position-and-momentum to quantum mechanics; its deepest posits are revisable, probabilistic, and strange, and no experiment certifies the framework from wholly outside it. Mathematics learned from Gödel that any consistent formal system rich enough to express arithmetic contains truths it cannot prove and cannot establish its own consistency from within — there is no self-certifying foundation even there. We do not infer that physics is a matter of taste, or that mathematics is a mere convention. Both are self-correcting practices that make demonstrable progress without resting on unshakeable bedrock. Morality, the argument runs, can be — and largely is — another such practice: intuitions enter as data, reasons interrogate and sometimes overturn them, principles are revised when they are found to license atrocity, and the revisions are argued out, shared, resisted, and now and then vindicated across generations and cultures. The abolition of slavery and the slow widening of the circle of moral concern are not shifts of mere fashion; they are the visible track record of reflective equilibrium working under argument and evidence — moral progress, of the only kind a foundationless inquiry could ever exhibit.

We do not conclude that physics is a matter of taste, or mathematics a mere convention. The demand for moral bedrock is a standard met nowhere else in inquiry.

§ XII · Conclusion

The arena of the loop

Return, at the last, to the wound the beginning opened: belief and feeling form no clean firewall but a single loop, each shaping the other, resting on nothing that cannot be questioned. Three verdicts were on offer. Subjectivism says the loop is all there is, so a moral claim is only a report of what the speaker feels. Nihilism says that because the loop bottoms out in no emotionless foundation, morality is a sustained mistake. The survey assembled here points to a third reading, stitched from the strongest threads of the tradition. Quasi-realism shows that talk rooted in attitude can earn the full apparatus of truth and objectivity. Coherentism shows that justification requires fit within a demanding web, not descent from bedrock. Constructivism and the intersubjective turn show that correctness can be validity for all reasoners rather than correspondence to a queer fact. And the analogy with mathematics and physics shows that foundationlessness is the normal condition of every successful inquiry, not a sentence of death.

On this account, morality is a dynamic, self-correcting, intersubjective system — coherentist in its justification, constructivist or pragmatic in what it takes correctness to be, and thoroughly human in its materials. Its truths are not written in the stars; but neither are they written only in you. They are what survives when a creature that can feel, reason, and answer to others holds its sentiments up to its principles, its principles up to the evidence, and all of them up to what it must justify to everyone else. The one thing that cannot be done is to debunk the entire practice from the outside — because for a being that reasons, suffers, and speaks with others, there is no outside on which to stand. The feedback loop between feeling and principle is not the defeat of morality. It is the arena in which morality happens.

None of which settles the field. Robust realists still insist that the best explanation of moral progress is that we are tracking stance-independent truths, and that convergence under reflection is evidence of a mountain really there to be climbed;11,12 error theorists still insist that ordinary moral thought claims more than any such reconstruction can honestly redeem, and that intellectual honesty requires admitting the claim to objective value fails.18 Metaethics remains, after twenty-four centuries, an open inquiry — which is, if the argument of these pages is right, precisely what we should expect of a subject that has an object but no floor.
