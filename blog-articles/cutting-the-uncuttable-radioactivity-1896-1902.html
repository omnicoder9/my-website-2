Physics · Chemistry — 1896–1902

Uncuttable

In seven years, three radiations, two new elements, and one leaking shed in Paris dismantled the oldest certainty in the physical sciences — that the atom, átomos, “that which cannot be cut,” was eternal, immutable, and the final floor of matter. It was, instead, quietly coming apart on its own: transmuting one element into another and pouring out energy from a source no one could name.

Prologue · The Citadel

The last fixed point in nature

The word was a wager about the structure of reality. When Leucippus and Democritus proposed, in the fifth century before the common era, that matter cut finely enough must terminate in átomos — “uncuttable” — they were not describing an experiment. They were closing a logical regress. If you could divide a thing without limit, they argued, being itself would dissolve into nothing; there had to be a smallest, full, eternal unit beneath which the knife could not pass. Transmitted through Epicurus and, in its most enduring literary form, through Lucretius’ De rerum natura, the atom entered Western thought less as a particle than as a guarantee: the bedrock that made permanence possible in a world of flux.1

For two millennia the idea remained essentially philosophical. What turned it into the load-bearing principle of an entire science was John Dalton’s decision, in A New System of Chemical Philosophy (1808), to treat the atom as a measurable thing. Dalton’s atoms were not merely small; they were indivisible, indestructible, and identical within an element, combining only in fixed whole-number ratios. From these postulates flowed the entire grammar of modern chemistry — stoichiometry, the law of definite proportions, the conservation of mass through every reaction, and eventually Mendeleev’s periodic table of 1869, in which each element occupied its box by virtue of an atomic weight assumed to be a fixed and permanent property of a permanent kind of atom.2 Chemistry had, in effect, defined itself against the one thing the alchemists had wanted: it was the science in which lead could never become gold, because the lead atom was eternal and unchangeable.

By the late nineteenth century this was not a hypothesis so much as a settled mood. James Clerk Maxwell gave it its most famous expression in his 1873 lecture on molecules: the atoms of a given element were everywhere and always alike, unworn by collision and unaltered by time, bearing — in his striking phrase — the character of “a manufactured article.”3 They had not evolved; they would not decay; they had continued, as he put it, exactly as they were created. The atom was the universe’s one genuinely conservative institution. To suggest that it might come apart, change its identity, or release energy from inside itself would have seemed, in 1895, not so much wrong as category-confused — a violation of what the word atom meant.

What follows is the story of how that certainty was demolished in under a decade, not by a frontal assault but by a faint, ceaseless, uninvited glow. The instruments were a fogged photographic plate, a gold-leaf electrometer, and a few tons of Bohemian mining waste. The casualties were the eternity of the elements, the strict determinism of classical physics, and — very nearly — the law of conservation of energy itself.

8 Nov 1895 · Würzburg

An accidental overture in invisible light

The demolition began, as such things often do, somewhere else entirely. On the evening of 8 November 1895, Wilhelm Conrad Röntgen, working with a Crookes tube shrouded in black cardboard, noticed a barium-platinocyanide screen across the room fluorescing when by every rule of the dark it should not have. Within weeks he had characterised a new and deeply penetrating radiation, photographed the bones of his wife’s hand through her flesh, and published “Über eine neue Art von Strahlen” — on a new kind of rays.4 The X-rays were a sensation of a kind science rarely produces: instantly reproducible by any laboratory with a vacuum tube, and immediately, viscerally useful. Europe’s physicists dropped what they were doing.

For the prehistory of radioactivity, one feature of Röntgen’s tube mattered above all others. The X-rays appeared to stream from the spot on the glass wall where the cathode beam struck it — precisely the spot that fluoresced a brilliant yellow-green. To many observers this suggested a natural hypothesis: perhaps the penetrating rays and the visible glow were two faces of one phenomenon, and perhaps any substance made to fluoresce or phosphoresce might therefore emit its own penetrating radiation. When the Paris Academy of Sciences examined Röntgen’s photographs at its session of 20 January 1896, Henri Poincaré pressed exactly this point, and the suggestion travelled quickly to those best equipped to test it.1

No family on Earth was better equipped than the Becquerels. Henri Becquerel held the chair of physics at the Muséum d’Histoire Naturelle that his father and grandfather had held before him; the family specialty, across three generations, was luminescence — the absorption and re-emission of light by phosphorescent solids. Henri had at hand a cabinet of brilliantly phosphorescent uranium salts, in particular the double sulphate of uranium and potassium, which after exposure to sunlight glowed with a characteristic greenish light. If Poincaré’s conjecture were right, here was the ideal substance to prove it. As it turned out, Becquerel was about to confirm something far stranger than the conjecture, and to do so by being temporarily wrong about almost everything.

Feb–May 1896 · Paris

The cloudy week that broke the rule

Becquerel’s first experiments seemed only to vindicate the fluorescence hypothesis. He wrapped a photographic plate in two sheets of thick black paper, opaque to sunlight, laid a crust of the phosphorescent uranium salt on top, and set the assembly in the sun. After several hours he developed the plate and found the silhouette of the salt printed upon it: something emitted by the salt had passed through the opaque paper and acted on the emulsion. To sharpen the result he interposed a coin, and later a cut-out metal screen — including, famously, a copper Maltese cross — and obtained its crisp shadow. He reported this to the Academy on 24 February 1896, interpreting it, reasonably, as penetrating radiation excited in the salt by sunlight, an effect of the phosphorescence.5

Then the weather intervened. At the end of February Paris turned overcast for several days, and Becquerel, unable to charge his salts in the sun, returned the prepared plates and uranium crystals to a drawer to wait. On 1 March, expecting at most a feeble latent image from whatever residual phosphorescence remained, he developed the plates anyway. The silhouettes appeared with full, undiminished intensity. The radiation had not faded in the dark, had not required sunlight, had not depended on the phosphorescence being excited at all. Whatever it was, the uranium salt was emitting it continuously and spontaneously, of its own accord, in a closed drawer.6 Becquerel announced the result on 2 March, and the fluorescence hypothesis that had launched the investigation quietly collapsed.

The truly decisive controls came over the following weeks. Becquerel tested uranium compounds that do not phosphoresce, and they radiated. He tested a salt kept in the dark for months, and it radiated as strongly as ever. In May he obtained pure, non-phosphorescent uranium metal and found it more active than any of its salts.8 Phosphorescence was now wholly beside the point. The radiation tracked one thing and one thing only: the presence and quantity of the element uranium. He further established that, like Röntgen’s rays, the new radiation could discharge an electrified body — it rendered the surrounding air a temporary conductor of electricity7 — a property that seemed a mere curiosity at the time and would, within two years, become the most powerful tool in the entire field.

Here, in the spring of 1896, the essential anomaly was already on the table, fully formed and almost entirely unappreciated. A lump of inert metal, sitting in a drawer at room temperature, untouched and unfed, was steadily emitting a penetrating radiation with no detectable energy source and no sign of running down. Nothing in the thermodynamics or chemistry of the period could account for it. And yet the discovery landed with a soft thud. The radiation was weak compared with a good X-ray tube; it seemed peculiar to a single obscure element; and it could not photograph a broken bone. For nearly two years “uranium rays” — or, as a few called them, “Becquerel rays” — were a minor curiosity at the edge of the X-ray excitement, awaiting someone who would take their quiet persistence seriously enough to ask where the energy came from.

1897–1898 · rue Lhomond

From the photograph to the current

The person who took them seriously was a thirty-year-old Polish émigrée looking for a doctoral subject. Marie Skłodowska Curie chose Becquerel’s rays for the most pragmatic of reasons: the field was nearly empty, which meant that an unknown researcher could do original work without first traversing a vast literature. Her decisive contribution, before any new element, was methodological, and it is impossible to overstate its importance. She abandoned the photographic plate.

The plate was qualitative, slow, and capricious; it told you that a substance radiated, not how much. Becquerel had noted in passing that the rays made air conduct electricity, and Curie seized on this as a measuring principle. The radiation ionises the air in a chamber; under an applied voltage those ions carry a minute current; the magnitude of that current is a direct, continuous measure of the radiation’s intensity. To measure currents of the order of 10−11 A she used an instrument of exquisite delicacy: the piezoelectric quartz electrometer that her husband Pierre and his brother Jacques had developed years earlier. In Curie’s null method, the ionisation current was balanced against the charge generated by a known weight straining a quartz crystal, and the operator timed the compensation with a stopwatch. The result was a quantitative ionometry of radioactivity — reproducible numbers, comparable across substances, sensitive to small differences.9 She had turned a photographic oddity into a physical quantity, and in doing so made the whole subject measurable.

The numbers spoke immediately, and what they said was profound. In her first communication to the Academy, presented on her behalf on 12 April 1898, Curie reported that the intensity of the radiation from a uranium compound was strictly proportional to the mass of uranium present, and was otherwise indifferent to everything: to the particular chemical compound, to whether the uranium was combined or pure, to temperature, to illumination, to the substance’s physical or chemical history.9 No chemical reaction behaves this way. Chemistry lives in the bonds between atoms, and is therefore exquisitely sensitive to compound and condition. A property that ignored all chemistry and depended only on how much of the element was present could not be a property of the molecule. It had to be a property of the atom itself. With that inference — radiation as an atomic property, a behaviour of the individual uranium atom irrespective of its chemical surroundings — Curie placed the source of the phenomenon inside the atom, where Dalton and Maxwell had promised nothing ever happened.

Two consequences followed in quick succession. First, treating radioactivity as an elemental property turned it into an analytical search problem: one could survey the whole table of elements with the electrometer and ask which others radiated. Curie did exactly this and found that compounds of thorium were active as well — a result reached independently and published a few weeks earlier by Gerhard Carl Schmidt in Germany.10 Radioactivity was not unique to uranium; it was a property shared by the heaviest elements. To name this newly general phenomenon, Curie coined a word — radioactivité — that would first appear in print that summer and has been the name of the thing ever since.

The second consequence was the one that changed history. When Curie measured the radioactivity of the natural uranium ore pitchblende, she found it three to four times more active than its uranium content could possibly explain — and the closely related mineral chalcolite told the same story. The discrepancy was not a rounding error; her instrument was far too precise for that. There was more radioactivity in the ore than there was known radioactive element to account for it. From this purely quantitative excess she drew a daring qualitative conclusion: the ore must contain an unknown element, present in minute quantity, but enormously more radioactive than uranium itself. It was a deduction made entirely through a physical property, before a single atom of the new substance had been seen, weighed, or chemically isolated. Pierre Curie found the inference compelling enough to set aside his own research on crystals and magnetism and join his wife in the hunt.

Jul–Dec 1898 · the shed

Polonium, radium, and a ton of Bohemian rock

The Curies’ method for tracking down the hidden element was itself a small revolution, and it inverted the usual relationship between chemistry and physics. They processed the ore by entirely conventional chemical means — dissolving, precipitating, fractionating — but at each step they decided what to keep not by chemical identity but by radioactivity. They followed the activity. Whichever fraction carried the intense radiation was the fraction worth pursuing, and they drove it down, again and again, into ever smaller volumes of ever greater concentration. Radioactivity had become an instrument of chemical analysis sensitive enough to detect quantities of matter no balance could weigh.

It worked twice. In July 1898 the Curies announced a new element concentrated in the bismuth fraction of the ore, far more radioactive than uranium, which they named polonium in honour of Marie’s partitioned homeland — and in whose announcement the word radio-active made its first published appearance.11 Five months later, in December, working now in the barium fraction, they reported a second and even more intensely active substance. They called it radium.12 Crucially, they did not rest the claim on radioactivity alone. The spectroscopist Eugène Demarçay examined their most active barium preparation and found a spectral line belonging to no known element — independent, optical proof that a genuinely new species of matter was present.13 Two new elements, found in a single year, by following a glow.

Announcing an element and possessing it, however, are different things, and the gap between them very nearly consumed the next four years of the Curies’ lives. Radium was present in pitchblende at a concentration of a few parts in ten million. To obtain a weighable, characterisable specimen, they had to process the ore on an industrial scale, and they did it by hand. The Austrian government, which mined pitchblende for uranium glaze-pigment at Joachimsthal in Bohemia, let them have the spent residue — from which the valuable uranium had already been extracted — and tons of this brown waste were shipped to Paris. There, in a converted shed on the rue Lhomond with a leaking glass roof and no proper ventilation, Marie Curie dissolved, boiled, precipitated, and recrystallised the material in batches, stirring cauldrons of it with an iron rod nearly her own height. The chemist Wilhelm Ostwald, visiting later, described the laboratory as something between “a stable and a potato-cellar,” and confessed that without the apparatus on the workbench he would have taken it for a practical joke.14

By 1902 the labour yielded its proof: from roughly a ton of residue, about a tenth of a gram of pure radium chloride, enough to determine an atomic weight of about 225 — placing radium firmly in the alkaline-earth column beneath barium, exactly where its chemistry said it belonged, and silencing any suggestion that it was an artefact or an impurity.15 Radium was real, weighable, and roughly a million times more radioactive than uranium. It was also, the Curies could not help but notice, beautiful. They had taken to keeping their concentrated preparations in the shed overnight, and Marie later remembered going in after dark to see the faintly luminous outlines of the vessels glowing on the shelves and benches, like — in her words — faint, fairy lights suspended in the blackness.15 The glow was lovely. It was also the visible signature of something pouring out of the matter without pause, and the question of where it came from could not be deferred much longer.

1899–1900 · Montreal & Paris

The anatomy of the rays

Before anyone could explain why atoms radiate, they had to learn what the radiation actually was — and the radiation turned out not to be one thing. The dissection was led by a young New Zealander, Ernest Rutherford, who had arrived at the Cavendish Laboratory under J. J. Thomson in 1895 and moved to McGill University in Montreal in 1898. Rutherford’s instinct was to probe the rays by absorption: interpose increasing thicknesses of matter and watch how the ionising power fell off. In 1899 he reported that uranium radiation was demonstrably complex — that it comprised at least two distinct components with utterly different penetrating powers. One was soft, absorbed almost completely by about two-hundredths of a millimetre of aluminium; the other was far more penetrating, passing through a hundred times as much. He named them, with deliberate neutrality, after the first two letters of the Greek alphabet: α and β.16 This was the first appearance in the literature of the alpha and beta rays.

Their identities came apart along the same lines. The beta rays, exposed to a magnetic field, swung aside in great arcs in the manner of cathode rays — and cathode rays had just, in 1897, been shown by Thomson to be streams of a subatomic corpuscle, the electron, with a charge-to-mass ratio thousands of times larger than any atom’s.17 When Becquerel measured the charge-to-mass ratio of the beta particles in 1900, it matched the electron’s. The conclusion was as direct as it was startling: a radioactive atom emits electrons — it throws out, at enormous speed, the very particles that Thomson had identified as building blocks smaller than the atom. (These beta electrons were, in fact, the fastest particles then known to science, approaching the speed of light; within a few years Walter Kaufmann would use radium’s beta rays to show that a particle’s effective mass grows with its velocity, handing an early experimental clue to the relativity that was coming.) The supposedly indivisible atom was visibly shedding pieces of itself.

A third component soon completed the picture. In 1900 Paul Villard, studying the radiation of radium in Paris, identified rays still more penetrating than beta and, unlike both alpha and beta, entirely undeflected by a magnetic field.18 These were electrically neutral and electromagnetic in nature — in effect, a still harder cousin of Röntgen’s X-rays. Rutherford would later christen them γ rays, extending the alphabet by one more letter. The alpha rays themselves proved the most stubborn to characterise: only in 1903 did Rutherford succeed in deflecting them in a very strong field, showing them to be positively charged and comparatively massive, and not until 1909 did he and Thomas Royds clinch the matter by collecting the gas that accumulates around an alpha source and reading, in its spectrum, the unmistakable lines of helium. The alpha particle was a helium atom stripped of its electrons — a helium nucleus, charge +2, ejected bodily from the interior of a far heavier atom.19

Fig. 1 — Three things, not one. The same radioactive source emits all three. In a magnetic field the heavy, doubly positive α (a helium nucleus) curves gently one way; the light, negative β (an electron) curves sharply the other; the neutral, electromagnetic γ passes straight through. Their penetrating powers span a factor of thousands — α is halted by skin, β by a few millimetres of aluminium, γ only by centimetres of lead. Each is a fragment, or a quantum of energy, leaving the atom’s interior.

Assemble the inventory and the implication is inescapable. A radioactive atom spontaneously emits electrons, helium nuclei, and hard electromagnetic radiation — that is, it ejects its own constituents and radiates energy, continuously, with no external prompting. Thomson’s electron of 1897 had already breached the atom from one direction, proving it contained smaller charged parts. Radioactivity breached it from the other, proving that it could expel those parts and survive as something else. Between the two results, the atom had acquired both internal structure and mutability — the two things the word atom had been coined precisely to deny. What remained was to say, out loud, what the atoms were turning into. That sentence, when it finally came, was the one nobody wanted to be caught saying.

1901–1902 · McGill, Montreal

Transmutation, and the word they dared not print

To say what the atoms were becoming, Rutherford needed a chemist, and in 1901 he acquired a formidable one: Frederick Soddy, a young Oxford-trained demonstrator at McGill. The partnership lasted barely eighteen months but produced the central idea of the field. Their way in was a puzzle in thorium. Thorium compounds gave off a radioactive emanation — a gas — and also yielded, on chemical separation, a powerfully radioactive substance Rutherford and Soddy labelled thorium X. The behaviour of these products over time was the thread that, pulled, unravelled the eternal atom.

The decisive experiment was almost embarrassingly simple, and its result was a pair of curves. When they chemically removed thorium X from a thorium preparation and set the two portions aside, the separated thorium X did not hold its activity: it decayed, falling off smoothly and losing half its strength in about four days. Meanwhile the thorium from which it had been stripped did not stay depleted: it recovered, regaining its lost activity over precisely the same timescale. Plotted together, decay and recovery were mirror images — and the time for thorium X to fall to half exactly matched the time for thorium to climb back halfway.20

Fig. 2 — The curves that revealed transmutation. Separated thorium X (now known as ²²⁴Ra) loses activity on the same exponential clock by which the stripped thorium regains it; the two meet at half strength after one half-life. The only reading that fits: the parent atom is continuously producing the daughter as fast as the daughter disintegrates, so their activities sum to a near-constant total. Radioactivity is one element manufacturing another, atom by atom, in real time.

Only one interpretation fit both halves of the picture. The thorium atom was not emitting radiation as some steady glandular secretion; it was spontaneously disintegrating into a new kind of atom — thorium X — which in turn disintegrated into the gaseous emanation, which disintegrated into something further, in a chain. The separated thorium X decayed because no new supply was reaching it; the depleted thorium recovered because it was steadily making fresh thorium X from its own substance. The “new elements” harvested from these ores — thorium X, the emanations, even radium and polonium themselves — were not pre-existing impurities. They were products: atoms being transmuted from other atoms before the experimenters’ eyes. Rutherford and Soddy set this out in their 1902 papers on the cause and nature of radioactivity and refined it the following year: radioactivity is sub-atomic chemical change, the transformation of one element into another, governed by a fixed statistical law.2122

They understood at once exactly what they had said, and exactly how dangerous it sounded. Soddy later recalled the moment of recognition — a flush of something past joy — and the words bursting out of him that this was transmutation. Rutherford’s response was not scientific but political: do not, he said, call it that, or “they’ll have our heads off as alchemists.”23 The reaction was not a joke. Transmutation was the defining fantasy of alchemy, the discredited pseudoscience against which respectable chemistry had spent a hundred years defining itself. To stand before the Chemical Society and announce that one element was turning into another, spontaneously, was to risk professional ruin. So in print they chose their words with care, preferring transformation and disintegration to the forbidden term, and Rutherford wrote privately to the chemist William Crookes that, while he believed a genuine process of disintegration or transmutation was steadily underway in the radioactive elements, it was inadvisable to put the case too bluntly to a chemical audience.24 The most revolutionary claim in the modern history of matter was introduced, deliberately, in a whisper.

For Mike’s sake, Soddy, don’t call it transmutation. They’ll have our heads off as alchemists. Ernest Rutherford to Frederick Soddy, McGill, 1901 (as Soddy recalled it)

The law that governed the process was as strange, in its way, as the transmutation itself. Each radioactive atom carries a fixed probability of disintegrating in any given second — a probability, the decay constant λ, that is utterly impervious to circumstance. Heating the sample white-hot does not change it; cooling it toward absolute zero does not change it; pressure, chemical combination, electric and magnetic fields, the age of the particular atom — none of it has the slightest effect. From this single premise the entire phenomenology follows.

The population of surviving atoms falls geometrically: a fixed fraction disappears each interval, so the time to halve — the half-life — is a constant of the element, ranging across the decay chains from microseconds to billions of years. The first quantitative hint of such a constant had come from the thorium emanation Rutherford studied with R. B. Owens, a gas that loses half its activity in under a minute (now identified as radon-220).

And here the discovery delivered its first deep philosophical wound — distinct from, and in some ways graver than, the loss of the atom’s permanence. The decay law is irreducibly statistical. It predicts, with great precision, what a vast population of atoms will do; it says nothing whatever about when any particular atom will disintegrate. Two atoms of radium, identical in every measurable respect, formed at the same instant, sitting side by side — one may explode in the next second and the other endure for ten thousand years, and there is no hidden variable, no internal clock, no precipitating cause that distinguishes them. The individual event appears to be spontaneous in the strict sense: uncaused, and predictable only as a probability. For a physics built on Laplace’s clockwork — on the principle that every effect has a sufficient cause and that the future is fixed by the present — this was an affront at the foundations. It would take quantum mechanics, a quarter-century later, to make peace with fundamental chance. Radioactive decay was the first phenomenon to demand it. The atom had not merely come apart; it had brought a-causality with it.

1903–1905 · Paris & Bern

The energy heresy

Transmutation answered the question of what was happening and immediately sharpened a second question that had haunted the field since Becquerel’s drawer: where does the energy come from? Each disintegration flings out an alpha or beta particle at a speed corresponding to real, measurable energy; radium emits these particles endlessly, day after day, with no fuel, no cooling, and no perceptible decline. In 1903 Pierre Curie and Albert Laborde pinned the problem to a single, unforgettable number. Using a calorimeter, they showed that radium salts continuously evolve heat — about a hundred small calories per gram per hour — and keep doing so indefinitely.25 A gram of radium, sealed and untouched, stays perceptibly warmer than its surroundings forever; it releases enough heat each hour to melt its own weight of ice, and over its lifetime perhaps a billion calories per gram — on the order of a million times the energy liberated, atom for atom, by the most violent chemical reaction known.

A gram of radium melts its own weight of ice every hour — and shows no sign, across a human lifetime, of ever running down.

To a physics that held the conservation of energy as its first commandment, this looked like a flat impossibility — a perpetual fountain of energy welling out of cold, inert matter. The community split over how to absorb it. One possibility, which Pierre Curie himself was willing to entertain, was that radioactive bodies absorbed energy from some unknown, all-pervading external radiation and merely re-emitted it. A second, darker possibility was that the sacred law of conservation simply failed in this new regime. The third possibility — the correct one — was the boldest: that the energy had been inside the atom all along, stored in quantities no chemistry had ever touched, and was being released as the atom disintegrated. Rutherford and Soddy argued precisely this. The energy of radioactive change, they estimated, was at least twenty thousand and perhaps a million times greater than that of any molecular or chemical change.26 The atom was not only divisible and mutable; it was a reservoir of colossal latent energy, a fact whose implications neither man underestimated. Soddy in particular began to speak, in lectures and popular essays, of a coming age in which the deliberate release of atomic energy might transform — or annihilate — human civilisation, prophecies that passed directly into H. G. Wells’s 1914 vision of “atomic bombs.”

The new energy source did not merely unsettle thermodynamics; it walked straight into the most famous quantitative standoff in nineteenth-century science. For decades Lord Kelvin — William Thomson, the era’s most authoritative physicist — had constrained the age of the Earth by treating it as a body cooling from an initially molten state. With no internal source of heat to replenish it, the planet’s measured temperature gradient implied an age of only some twenty to forty million years: far too short for the slow geology of Lyell or the gradual evolution of Darwin, and the cause of a long, bitter quarrel between physics on one side and geology and biology on the other. Radioactivity demolished Kelvin’s key assumption at a stroke. The radioactive elements dispersed through the Earth’s crust are precisely the missing internal furnace; the planet generates its own heat and cools far more slowly than Kelvin had supposed. His upper bound evaporated, and with it the conflict.

Rutherford relished the irony, and told the story for the rest of his life. Presenting these results at the Royal Institution in 1904, he spotted Kelvin himself in the darkened hall and braced for trouble at the part of his talk that contradicted the great man. To his relief Kelvin appeared to doze — until Rutherford reached the crucial point, whereupon the old physicist roused and fixed him with a sharp, baleful look. Rutherford saved himself with a diplomatic improvisation: Lord Kelvin, he observed, had limited the age of the Earth on the explicit condition that no new source of heat be found — and radium was exactly such a source. Kelvin, flattered, subsided. In print Rutherford put it plainly: the radioactive elements, by liberating enormous energy in their disintegration, lengthen the possible duration of life on the planet and restore the vast tracts of time that geologists and biologists had demanded.26 Within a few years, radioactive dating — pioneered by Bertram Boltwood and Rutherford himself — would put numbers on it, reading ages of hundreds of millions and then billions of years in uranium-bearing rocks, and vindicating the geologists beyond appeal.

Yet the deepest resolution of the energy paradox came from outside radioactivity altogether, from a Swiss patent clerk in 1905. In a three-page sequel to his paper on special relativity, Albert Einstein argued that the inertia of a body is a measure of its energy content — that mass and energy are the same currency, related by the soon-to-be-famous equivalence.28 Einstein noted that bodies whose energy content changes greatly, such as radium salts, might offer a test of the idea. The energy pouring out of radium was neither borrowed from the ether nor conjured from nothing. A minute fraction of the atom’s own mass — the mass defect between parent and products — was being converted directly into the kinetic energy of the emitted particles. Radioactivity was the first natural process to lay bare the equivalence of mass and energy; the “inexhaustible” fountain was simply matter turning quietly into energy, a few millionths of itself at a time. Conservation of energy was not broken. It had merely been shown to include a clause no one had read.

ca. 1902–1910 · the reckoning

Alchemy in the citadel

It is worth pausing on why all this provoked not just revision but genuine upset — a disturbance that ran well past the technical journals into philosophy, the press, religion, and the occult. Four distinct certainties had been breached, and each had its own constituency of the affronted.

The metaphysical breach came first. The atom had been the last genuinely fixed point in the natural order — the “manufactured article” that never wore, never changed, never died. Its mutability dissolved the bedrock not only of Daltonian chemistry but of the entire atomist tradition reaching back to Democritus. If atoms decay and transmute, then the elements are not eternal Platonic kinds but transient configurations; matter itself has a history, a lifecycle, even a mortality. Permanence, it turned out, was not a feature of the foundations of the world. It was a long-lived illusion produced by half-lives that happen to be longer than civilisations.

The stigma of alchemy made the breach socially explosive. Chemistry had professionalised itself, over the nineteenth century, precisely by repudiating the alchemical dream of transmutation; the immutability of the elements was, for a working chemist, almost a mark of respectability. Now physicists were calmly reporting that one element became another of its own accord. The popular press seized on “modern alchemy” with delight, and a remarkable wave of occult and mystical enthusiasm attached itself to radioactivity, which seemed to vindicate every hermetic intuition the Enlightenment had buried.30 The irony cut both ways, and cut deep. The alchemists had been right in their central conviction — that one element can become another — at the very moment chemistry had finished proving them charlatans. But they had been wrong about everything else: transmutation could not be coaxed by chemical art, could not be hastened or steered or harnessed to make gold; it proceeded on its own inhuman schedule, deep in the atom, indifferent to the adept and his furnace. The dream came true in a form that rendered the dreamer irrelevant.

The alchemists were right that one element can become another. They were wrong about every other thing they believed — including that anyone could make it happen.

The wound to causality was, for many physicists, the most disquieting of all, precisely because it was the quietest. That atoms change was astonishing; that they change for no assignable reason, on no schedule, by pure chance was, to a mind raised on classical determinism, almost unintelligible. The decay of a single atom had no cause that any experiment could find and no trigger that any intervention could supply. This was not a temporary gap in knowledge to be filled by a cleverer measurement; it was, as quantum mechanics would later confirm, a structural feature of reality. Radioactivity was the first crack in the Laplacean edifice of strict determinism — the opening wedge of the indeterminism that would define twentieth-century physics — and it arrived two decades before anyone had the conceptual apparatus to receive it.

The threat to conservation of energy rounded out the affront, and it had a single, eminent personification. Lord Kelvin never accepted that the energy of radium came from within the atom. To the end he sought alternative explanations — the absorption of energy from the surrounding ether, anything that preserved the older picture — and he died in 1907 still a holdout, the embodiment of a generation of physicists for whom the new physics was a bridge too far.1 The resistance was real, and the caution of Rutherford and Soddy before the chemical establishment was not paranoia. And yet the conquest was astonishingly swift. Within two or three years the disintegration theory had carried the field, persuading all but a few irreconcilable die-hards, and the institutions of science moved to ratify it with unusual speed.

The ratification took the form of Nobel Prizes, and the pattern of those prizes tells its own story. In 1903 the Physics prize was divided between Henri Becquerel and the Curies, honouring the joint research on the radiation phenomena Becquerel had discovered — and making Marie Curie the first woman to win a Nobel Prize.31 In 1908 the Chemistry prize went to Rutherford, for his investigations into the disintegration of the elements and the chemistry of radioactive substances32 — a delicious disciplinary irony, since Rutherford regarded himself as a physicist and was famous for quipping that all science was either physics or stamp-collecting. The man who had warned Soddy that they would be hounded as alchemists was crowned, by the chemists, for proving transmutation real. Marie Curie won a second Nobel, in Chemistry, in 1911, for the discovery of radium and polonium and the isolation of radium — the first person to win the prize twice. The alchemy that dared not speak its name in 1901 was, within a decade, the most decorated achievement in science.

A note on the word: the radioactive “emanation” that revealed transmutation was a chemically inert gas — a member of the noble-gas family Ramsay had only recently uncovered — which is why some retellings have Soddy crying that thorium was turning into argon. It was in fact a new noble gas, the element we now call radon, the heaviest of the family and itself fiercely radioactive.

There remained one further sense in which the discovery was an upset, the most literal of all: the rays that dismantled the eternal atom also burned the people who studied them. The danger announced itself, as so much else in this story did, by accident. Becquerel carried a sealed tube of the Curies’ radium preparation in his waistcoat pocket and developed, days later, a stubborn ulcer on the skin beneath. Pierre Curie, intrigued, deliberately bound a radium source to his own forearm and kept a clinical diary of the wound it produced — a reddening like a burn, then a lesion that scabbed, opened, and resisted healing for nearly two months. The two men published the observations together in 1901.29 From that grim self-experiment grew, almost at once, the deliberate medical use of radium against tumours — the therapy soon called Curiethérapie — the first glimpse of the benign face of a phenomenon whose malignant face was only beginning to be understood.

For the Curies themselves the cost was lifelong and, in Marie’s case, almost certainly fatal: the chronic ill-health, the ruined hands, the aplastic anaemia from which she died in 1934 are now read as the signature of decades of unshielded exposure. Her laboratory notebooks remain so radioactive that they are stored in lead-lined boxes and consulted only with precautions. And the wonder curdled, in the public sphere, into a craze whose tragedies belong to the following decades — radium tonics and radium cosmetics, the luminous-dial painters who pointed their brushes with their lips and died of necrosis and cancer. The substance that overturned the immutability of matter could not be made to respect the integrity of the human body. The discovery that broke the atom broke bodies too, and the same faint glow that the Curies had admired in their shed as fairy-light was, the whole time, the visible exhaust of a process that does not care what it passes through.

Epilogue · after the uncuttable

A fossil word for a thing we cut

Once breached, the atom came apart quickly. Thomson’s electron in 1897 and the Rutherford–Soddy transmutation of 1902 were the first two cuts; the rest followed within a generation, and — in a closing loop that ought to be more famous than it is — the instrument that made the next great cut was radioactivity itself. The alpha particle, identified as a helium nucleus and flung out by every heavy radioactive atom, was the perfect sub-atomic projectile, and Rutherford turned it into a probe. When he and his collaborators fired alpha particles at thin gold foil and found a small fraction rebounding through enormous angles, the only explanation was that the atom’s positive charge and nearly all its mass were concentrated in a minuscule central nucleus — the picture he published in 1911.34 Radioactivity had supplied both the mystery and the tool to solve it. The same identification was corroborated from the chemical side: William Ramsay and Soddy had shown in 1903 that helium gas steadily accumulates in a sealed radium sample, the literal exhalation of a transmuting element.27

The nucleus reorganised everything. Niels Bohr placed Thomson’s electrons in quantised orbits around it in 1913; in the same year Soddy resolved a lingering paradox of the decay chains by recognising that a single element could come in several varieties, chemically identical and inseparable but differing in atomic weight and radioactive behaviour. He named them isotopes — “same place,” because they share one box on the periodic table — and, with Kazimierz Fajans and others, formulated the displacement law that reads the chains directly: an alpha emission shifts an element two places down the table, a beta emission one place up.33 The periodic table’s boxes, it emerged, were not occupied by single eternal kinds of atom after all, but by families of related nuclides, some stable and some in slow transit toward stability.

The final vindication of the alchemists came in 1919, and it came from human hands. Bombarding nitrogen with alpha particles, Rutherford detected fast protons emerging and recognised that he had broken the nitrogen nucleus and built oxygen — the first deliberate transmutation of one element into another, performed on purpose in a laboratory.35 What radium did blindly and ceaselessly, a physicist had now done with intent. James Chadwick completed the nuclear cast of characters with the neutron in 1932,36 and within six years Otto Hahn and Fritz Strassmann, interpreted by Lise Meitner and Otto Frisch, found that the uranium nucleus could be split clean in two, releasing — exactly as Rutherford and Soddy had foretold three decades earlier — a portion of that vast internal reservoir of atomic energy. From Becquerel’s fogged plate the line runs unbroken to the reactor and the bomb: every one of those machines is an engineering of the spontaneous disintegration that a uranium salt first revealed in a closed Parisian drawer.37

The word, meanwhile, never changed. We still call it the atom — the uncuttable — a fossil term embedded in our language for a thing we now split routinely, in power stations and particle accelerators and warheads, and whose constituents we have catalogued in exhaustive detail. The persistence of the name is a small monument to how completely the old picture was overturned, and to how recently. But the deepest lesson of 1896–1902 was never simply that the atom has parts, or even that it can be made to come apart. It was that permanence itself was an illusion at the very foundation of matter. The elements are not the eternal, manufactured articles of Maxwell’s imagination; they are transient states in a slow, silent combustion that has been burning since the atoms were forged in stars, and that no temperature, no chemistry, and no human will can hurry or halt. Henri Becquerel left some uranium salts in a drawer during a cloudy week in Paris and, without intending anything of the kind, ended the reign of the changeless atom. The citadel of the uncuttable fell not to a hammer but to a faint, patient, uninvited glow — one that no one had ordered, and that no one could turn off.
