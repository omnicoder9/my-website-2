Campaign Organising

The Art and Science of the Pressure Campaign

From target selection and victory definitions to radical flanks and knowing when to quit — a comprehensive, evidence-based guide to running campaigns that actually move the needle for animals.

Strategy Corporate Campaigns Grassroots Organising

Somewhere between a petition that goes nowhere and a movement that transforms an industry lies the pressure campaign — one of the most powerful tools in the vegan activist's arsenal. At its best, a well-run campaign can compel a multinational corporation to rewrite its supply chain, move millions of animals out of cages, or shift public discourse in ways that no individual act of dietary defection ever could. At its worst, a campaign can exhaust volunteers, alienate potential allies, and hand the opposition easy material for counter-messaging. The difference between these outcomes is rarely passion. It is almost always strategy.

This guide attempts to synthesise what researchers, experienced advocates, and social movement theorists have learned about the mechanics of pressure campaigns in the animal advocacy context and beyond. It draws on peer-reviewed literature in sociology, behavioural science, and organisational studies, as well as empirical data from campaign organisations, to offer a practical and honest account of how to run campaigns that produce results.

1. A Taxonomy of Campaigns

Not all pressure campaigns are alike, and treating them as interchangeable is a common source of strategic error. Before committing resources, organisers should be clear about what kind of campaign they are running, because the type determines almost everything else: timeline, tactics, staffing, messaging, and the definition of success itself.

Animal advocacy campaigns typically cluster along two primary axes: the nature of the target (corporate versus legislative versus cultural) and the specificity of the ask (single, binary demand versus composite reform package).1 Corporate campaigns have historically dominated the vegan and animal protection movement because corporations are, in several important respects, easier to move than governments: they are responsive to reputational damage, accountable to shareholders and customers, and capable of making binding policy changes without electoral cycles.2

Campaign Typology: Key Dimensions

Target type
Corporate (supply chain, procurement, branding), legislative (ballot measure, municipal policy, national law), or cultural (shifting norms, media narrative, professional standards).
Ask complexity
Simple, binary asks (e.g., "go cage-free by 2025") are faster to win and easier to verify. Composite asks (e.g., the Better Chicken Commitment) offer larger animal welfare benefits but require longer timelines and more sophisticated accountability infrastructure.1
Timeline
Short (weeks–months, e.g., blocking a specific bill), medium (1–3 years, typical corporate campaign), or long (5–15 years, structural or cultural change).
Resources
Staff-led campaigns run by professional organisations with permanent campaign coordinators achieve more victories more consistently than volunteer-only efforts, though the latter remain essential for creating authentic local pressure.2
Confrontation level
Engagement-first (starting with friendly meetings, good-PR framing), escalation campaigns (carrot first, then public pressure), or adversarial-from-the-start (reserved for targets with demonstrated bad faith).

The National Animal Rights Association (NARA) has articulated a clear operating philosophy: focus on one issue at a time and deploy an array of tactics until the goal is achieved.3 This approach — singular focus combined with tactical flexibility — is consistent with what social movement research identifies as effective resource allocation in resource-constrained organisations.4

2. Target Selection

Target selection is probably the most consequential decision a campaign team makes, and it is also one of the least theorised in the popular literature on activism. Academic research offers a more structured framework.

A study from the University of Tennessee examined the conditions under which corporations are most likely to respond to activist pressure. The authors found that a firm's corporate social performance (CSP) — its publicly stated commitments to stakeholder interests and social responsibility — was a key variable: firms that had already invested in CSP signalling were more vulnerable to activist demands, not because they were bad actors, but because they had more reputational capital to protect.5 This suggests that targeting companies with strong brand identities and existing sustainability rhetoric is frequently more effective than targeting companies that have made no such claims, since the latter have little to lose.

Campaigns should target organisations where there is meaningful leverage — reputational, financial, or relational — and where a successful win can be used to create pressure on competitors.

Adapted from effective animal advocacy literature

Beyond reputational vulnerability, experienced campaign organisers recommend evaluating targets across several additional dimensions. First, market position: winning a commitment from a market leader creates demonstration effects that cascade through the industry, as competitors fear being outflanked on corporate responsibility. The Humane League (THL) used this logic repeatedly in its cage-free campaigns — each successive major win made the next one easier, because the holdout company increasingly appeared to be the outlier.6 Second, counterfactual impact: will this company change without a campaign? If so, campaign resources may be better spent elsewhere. THL's own cost-effectiveness analysis explicitly notes that their modelling treats advocacy as the decisive factor enabling change, not merely an acceleration of trends that would occur regardless.7

Third, consider the supply chain position of the target. Targeting institutional food service companies (catering operators, university dining, hospital chains) can produce outsized results because their policy commitments ripple through hundreds of downstream suppliers. Sodexo's cage-free pledge, secured in part because THL persuaded dozens of the company's campus clients to request it, illustrates this multiplier logic precisely.6

Avoiding the Bad Target

A bad target is one that is impervious to the specific pressure you can bring to bear, that has no supply chain or reputational leverage, or whose victory would be symbolic but strategically inconsequential. Smaller, privately-held firms in jurisdictions with limited protest rights, or companies so far outside the mainstream food system that public campaigns produce no media traction, are poor investments of campaign energy. Context matters enormously: research on cage-free campaigns in emerging markets has found that progress is significantly slower in regions facing weaker market concentration and legal restrictions on protests, even with capable campaign organisations working them.8

3. Defining Victory

A campaign without a clear, verifiable definition of success is a campaign that can never end — and one that, in practice, rarely produces anything. Victory criteria should be established before the campaign launches, not after pressure has been applied and the target begins to negotiate.

The most durable campaigns distinguish between three layers of success. The first is commitment acquisition — securing a public pledge. The second is implementation — verifying that the pledge is actually carried out. The third, and most transformative, is industry-wide shift — using individual victories to reset the sector standard. All three matter, and organisers who celebrate commitment acquisition without building accountability infrastructure often find themselves with a wall of unfulfilled pledges.

These numbers from The Humane League's own cost-effectiveness analysis are striking, but they also illustrate the importance of building fulfilment tracking into the campaign model from the beginning.7,9 The Open Wing Alliance (OWA) publishes annual cage-free fulfilment reports, holds public "Eggspose" campaigns against companies that have not met their deadlines, and stages targeted protests at specific outlets — Hardee's parent company CKE faced protests at franchise locations in October 2024 for failing to provide progress information.9

Defining success also requires being honest about scope. A cage-free commitment does not mean cruelty-free farming. The animal rights movement contains sincere disagreements about whether welfare reforms constitute meaningful progress or a distraction from the abolitionist goal. This article does not resolve that debate, but it does note that campaigns with ambiguous or unstated victory conditions are more likely to fracture their own coalitions than campaigns with explicit, shared criteria — even if those criteria are contested among allies.

4. Process and Execution

The procedural anatomy of an effective pressure campaign, drawing on both advocacy practice and social movement theory, breaks into four broad phases: research and preparation, engagement and escalation, public pressure, and accountability.

Research and Preparation

Before any outreach occurs, campaign teams should produce a power analysis of the target — identifying its key decision-makers, its pressure points (investors, major customers, brand partners, regulators), its public commitments and contradictions, and the routes through which external actors can create leverage.10 This is not optional background work. Campaigns that skip it routinely find themselves applying the wrong kind of pressure to the wrong people.

Campaign research also includes mapping the landscape of allies and opponents. Who else cares about this target? Are there investors, municipalities, or institutional clients whose concerns could be channelled into the campaign? Are there industry associations or trade bodies whose blessing the target values? What counter-narratives will the target deploy? The animal agriculture industry has historically labelled dietary change campaigns as "animal rights extremist campaigns," while simultaneously working to shape media coverage and control scientific narratives.11 Anticipating these counter-moves allows a campaign to pre-position its messaging.

Engagement and Escalation

The dominant model in effective animal advocacy begins with private engagement: requesting a meeting, presenting the ask, offering positive framing (good for the brand, aligns with stated values, ahead of regulatory trends), and giving the target a reasonable window to respond. This approach is not naive — it is strategic. It creates a record of good faith, which matters both for internal morale and for public framing if the campaign escalates. It also sometimes works. THL's experience includes multiple instances of companies agreeing to cage-free transitions on or before the day a campaign launch was announced.6

When private engagement fails, the campaign escalates — moving from private correspondence to public statements, social media activation, coalition letters from institutional stakeholders, and, where appropriate, street-level protest. The escalation should be pre-planned and incremental, with each step announced to the target before it is executed. This maintains credibility and gives the target ongoing off-ramps.

Effective campaigns are, as one analysis of issue advocacy puts it, "unrelenting when applying constant online and offline pressure to decision-makers."12 Consistency and persistence matter more than single high-drama events.

The Ask: Scale and Specificity

Animal Ask, a research organisation focused on corporate campaign design, has investigated a foundational uncertainty in campaign construction: how much should you ask for? Their analysis identifies a genuine tension. More ambitious asks — composite commitments addressing multiple welfare dimensions — produce larger potential benefits for animals if achieved, but take longer, are harder to verify, and may increase the probability of failure or partial compliance.1 Simpler, binary asks are faster to win but may leave significant welfare gains on the table.

The practical resolution seems to depend on target type and campaign stage. For initial campaigns against targets with no prior welfare commitments, a focused, binary ask is more likely to succeed and establishes the precedent of compliance. Follow-on campaigns can then raise the standard. For targets that have already made partial commitments, a composite ask may be appropriate. The key is that the ask must be clear, specific, measurable, and verifiable — vague commitments to "improve animal welfare" are worth little.

Execution Checklist
  • Conduct a full power analysis before outreach begins
  • Define the specific, verifiable ask and the non-negotiable minimum
  • Set a private engagement deadline before public escalation
  • Map the escalation ladder in advance — know each step before you take it
  • Assign ownership: who speaks to press, who runs digital, who manages relationships
  • Build in accountability mechanisms from day one (deadline tracking, public reporting)
  • Measure inputs and outputs throughout, not just at the end

5. Room for Innovation and Creativity

Tactical creativity is not merely decorative — in some cases it is the difference between a campaign that breaks through and one that generates no media coverage. Gene Sharp's enumeration of 198 methods of nonviolent action offers a starting point for expanding beyond the default repertoire of petition, picket, and press release.13 But vegan campaigns have also generated their own repertoire of distinctive interventions.

Open rescues — in which activists enter factory farms openly, document conditions, and sometimes remove individual animals — occupy a complex space. They generate powerful emotional content and can produce national media coverage, but experimental research published through Animal Charity Evaluators found that exposure to open rescue content produced mixed and sometimes negative attitudinal effects among general audiences.14 The research found that most combinations of disruptive protest types and messaging frames produced worse outcomes for pro-animal attitudes among U.K. participants, with blockade-style actions producing the most negative effects.

This does not mean creativity is counterproductive. It means that tactical creativity must be tested against evidence rather than deployed on the basis of intuition alone. Imagery research from the Humane League Labs has demonstrated that specific kinds of images — animals in confined spaces, mother-offspring pairs, signs of physical injury — produce measurably stronger behavioural intention changes than others.15 This is the kind of finding that can and should inform how creative content is developed for campaigns.

Institutional targeting is one underused area of innovation. Student organiser campaigns at universities — which led companies like Sodexo and Aramark to make early cage-free commitments — demonstrated that institutional clients can act as powerful proxies for direct corporate pressure, even when the target institution is not itself the primary animal user.16 This "supply-chain judo" logic can be extended to corporate caterers, hospital systems, and government food procurement bodies.

Digital organising tools have also expanded the tactical repertoire substantially. Coordinated social media pressure, email action systems, and online petition campaigns can produce thousands of individual contacts with a company's customer service channels in a short period — creating the appearance and reality of broad public concern in ways that were not possible for grassroots campaigns before the social media era.17

6. The Radical Flank: A Complicating Factor

Any serious discussion of campaign strategy must grapple with the radical flank question: what role do more confrontational organisations and tactics play within a broader movement, and do they help or harm campaigns running through conventional channels?

Social movement theory has long recognised that movements contain multiple factions with varying agendas and tactics, and that these factions may complement or impede one another in complex ways.18 The "radical flank effect" describes the phenomenon by which the presence of a radical faction within a movement alters public perception of more moderate factions — sometimes positively (making the moderate position seem more reasonable by contrast), sometimes negatively (tainting the entire movement with the radical's imagery).

A 2022 study in PNAS Nexus, which conducted two online experiments with 2,772 participants including a study specifically of the animal rights movement, provided causal evidence that the presence of a radical flank increases public support for a moderate faction within the same movement.18 Crucially, it was the use of radical tactics (property destruction, disruptive physical confrontation) rather than a radical agenda that drove this contrast effect — the moderate faction's own characteristics were held constant across conditions.

It is the use of radical tactics — not a radical agenda — that leads the public to view moderate factions as less extreme by contrast. Actions, it seems, speak louder than stated goals.

Adapted from Feinberg et al. (2022), PNAS Nexus18

Earlier qualitative research on the Stop Huntingdon Animal Cruelty (SHAC) campaign found that moderate activists within that campaign recognised the radical wing as responsible for much of its success, even when they personally disapproved of radical methods. Businesses disassociated from Huntingdon Life Sciences not primarily for ethical reasons, SHAC organisers reported, but out of fear of becoming targets of the radical wing themselves.19

The picture is not uniformly positive, however. Research on the UK animal rights movement found cases where media coverage did not distinguish between moderate and radical factions, effectively tarring all advocates with the same brush and reducing the movement's overall effectiveness.20 Earth First's environmental campaigns suffered a similar reputational problem when more radical animal liberation activists joined their networks.20

The strategic lesson for campaign organisers is nuanced: the existence of more confrontational actors in the broader movement can sometimes benefit a moderate campaign's positioning, but campaign leaders should not deliberately cultivate or coordinate with radical groups in ways that create legal, reputational, or ethical liability. The effect is most reliably positive when the moderate organisation clearly distinguishes its own methods and maintains organisational independence.

7. Building Support Beyond the Protest

Campaigns live or die not on the number of people who attend a single demonstration, but on the depth and variety of the support infrastructure they build. The most durable campaigns treat "showing up at a protest" as one small input into a much larger volunteer and supporter ecosystem.

Faunalytics, the research arm of the animal advocacy movement, maintains a library of over 6,000 studies and offers one-on-one research support to advocates and organisations — effectively acting as an intelligence agency for the movement, helping campaigners identify what actually works before they spend resources on it.21 Their research has highlighted that advocates often need more accessible evidence syntheses to make informed decisions, a gap that creates structural inefficiency across the sector.

For individual campaign teams, the most productive forms of non-attendance contribution include: digital action (coordinated social media, email campaigns, online petitions targeted at company contact channels); skills contribution (graphic design, video production, legal research, data analysis, translation); professional leverage (doctors, veterinarians, nutritionists, or public health researchers willing to co-sign campaign communications); financial support (funding staff time is consistently more impactful per dollar than most other forms of individual contribution); and institutional advocacy (persuading your university, employer, or professional association to formally request change from the target). Faunalytics data suggests that 73% of the public support using media to reach audiences with animal protection messages — there is a large pool of sympathisers who can be engaged as amplifiers without asking them to take more confrontational action.22

Coalition building is often undervalued by campaign teams that default to insular movement organising. Climate advocates, public health researchers, labour organisers, and food system reformers frequently share overlapping interests with vegan campaigns and can dramatically expand a campaign's reach and credibility when engaged as formal or informal partners.

8. Case Studies

Case Study 1

The Open Wing Alliance: The Global Cage-Free Campaign

Beginning in earnest around 2015, The Humane League's corporate cage-free campaigns have produced one of the most rigorously documented records of campaign success in the animal advocacy movement. The model is straightforward: a professional, permanent campaign team identifies corporate targets by market position, engages in private dialogue first, gives the target a specific deadline to announce a commitment, and launches a public campaign with full escalation if the deadline passes. The announcement of an imminent campaign launch has frequently been sufficient to produce an immediate commitment — THL staff describe multiple instances where targets pledged on the day a campaign was about to go public.6

The formation of the Open Wing Alliance (OWA) in 2016 added a multiplier dimension. By uniting over 90 organisations across six continents, the OWA enabled THL to leverage each successive corporate win against the target's international competitors. By 2024, 89% of companies with implementation deadlines of 2023 or earlier — over 1,200 organisations — had met their cage-free commitments, a fulfilment rate that exceeded most observers' expectations.6 THL's own analysis estimates that corporate cage-free campaigns have spared approximately two hens per dollar spent, a figure derived from historical data spanning 2015–2024.7

The campaign has not been without complications. Applying the model to broiler chicken welfare has proven more difficult than the cage-free work, partly because the foundational supply chain infrastructure (labelling systems, alternative suppliers) is less developed for broiler chickens than for eggs. Country context also matters significantly: markets with high concentration, established protest rights, and strong media ecosystems are far more tractable than fragmented, less regulated markets.6,8

Case Study 2

SHAC: The Radical Campaign and Its Lessons

The Stop Huntingdon Animal Cruelty (SHAC) campaign against the animal testing laboratory Huntingdon Life Sciences is one of the most intensively studied campaigns in the animal liberation movement's history. Running from the late 1990s through the 2000s, SHAC targeted not just HLS directly but its entire business network — suppliers, investors, insurers, cleaning contractors — using a combination of above-ground public pressure and, controversially, underground direct action by affiliated actors.

Academic analysis of SHAC's moderate wing reveals the paradox of radical flank dynamics in action: campaign organisers acknowledged that much of their leverage came from their association with radical actors, even as they publicly distanced themselves from those tactics. Businesses disassociated from HLS primarily out of fear of becoming targets themselves — a powerful demonstration of how a campaign can alter the cost-benefit calculus of third-party actors without directly targeting them.19

SHAC ultimately faced severe legal repression, with key organisers prosecuted under anti-terrorism statutes in both the UK and the US. Its trajectory illustrates the ceiling effect of radical tactics: they can produce short-term leverage, but they also invite state intervention that can destroy a campaign's organisational infrastructure. The legacy is contested but instructive — SHAC showed both the power and the limits of campaigns designed around fear rather than persuasion.

9. Key Factors That Can Make or Break a Campaign

Synthesising across the research and the case study record, several factors emerge as consistently decisive in determining campaign outcomes.

Make-or-Break Factor 1: Clarity and Verifiability of the Ask

Campaigns with vague or unverifiable demands produce vague or unverifiable outcomes. The ask must be specific, measurable, and associated with a concrete deadline. Without this, even a company that wishes to comply cannot do so in a way that satisfies the campaign.

Make-or-Break Factor 2: Permanent, Funded Staff

Research consistently finds that corporate campaigns run by professional, permanent staff achieve more victories more consistently than volunteer-led efforts.2 This is not a criticism of volunteers, whose role in generating authentic public pressure is irreplaceable. It is a recognition that the sustained relationship-management, intelligence-gathering, and decision-making that effective corporate campaigns require cannot be maintained by a rotating volunteer base alone.

Make-or-Break Factor 3: The Theory of Change

A theory of change — a clear, explicit account of how the campaign's activities will produce the desired outcome — is not a bureaucratic formality. It is the diagnostic tool that allows a team to identify when their strategy is failing and to adapt accordingly.10 Without it, campaigns tend to drift toward activity for its own sake, prioritising visible action over strategic effectiveness.

Make-or-Break Factor 4: Momentum and the Demonstration Effect

Campaign wins are not just outcomes — they are inputs into subsequent campaigns. Each commitment secured reshapes the competitive landscape for remaining targets, raises public expectations, and gives campaign organisations a track record that attracts both volunteers and funders. The OWA explicitly built this logic into its global strategy, using European wins to create pressure on the same companies' operations in Asia and Latin America.6

Make-or-Break Factor 5: Counter-Narrative Preparedness

The animal agriculture industry conducts extensive research on public opinion and has demonstrated a consistent willingness to label dietary change campaigns as extremist interventions, to engage public relations firms to shape media narratives, and to influence scientific and regulatory processes.11 Campaigns that have not prepared counter-narratives in advance, or that have not built relationships with sympathetic journalists before a campaign launches, will consistently lose the message war.

10. Open Questions: Where Research Is Needed

The evidence base for animal advocacy campaigns is growing but remains uneven. Several important questions lack robust empirical answers, and honest advocacy requires acknowledging these gaps rather than filling them with confident assertions.

The question of long-run behavioural impact is perhaps the most significant gap. Most studies of advocacy effectiveness measure immediate attitudinal or intentional change; very few track whether these translate into sustained dietary behaviour change months or years later. Faunalytics has identified longitudinal follow-up as a priority for its 2026 research agenda precisely because of this gap.21

The effectiveness of campaigns in different cultural and market contexts is also poorly understood. Models developed in the US, UK, and Australia may not transfer straightforwardly to markets in Asia, Latin America, or sub-Saharan Africa, where regulatory environments, media landscapes, consumer expectations, and protest traditions differ substantially. The OWA's experience suggests that the cage-free model faces meaningfully different barriers in markets with weaker concentration and restricted protest rights.8 Faunalytics has begun commissioning contextual research in India, Brazil, and China, but the field is in early stages.21

The optimal escalation ladder — when exactly to move from private engagement to public pressure, and through which specific stages — has not been rigorously studied. Campaign practitioners have developed tacit wisdom about this, but it has not been systematically tested. Similarly, the conditions under which radical flanks help versus harm a concurrent moderate campaign are understood at a general level but have not been mapped to specific campaign types, target sectors, or media environments.18,20

Finally, the question of message backfire deserves more attention. Experimental research has found that interventions encouraging meat reduction can backfire, particularly among individuals with antisocial personality traits, reducing their willingness to change.23 Understanding who is actually reachable — and who is being inadvertently hardened in their positions — would allow campaign resources to be allocated far more efficiently.

11. When to Quit

The question of when to abandon or pause a campaign is uncomfortable but essential. Campaigns that continue past the point of strategic viability waste resources, demoralise volunteers, and — in the worst cases — provide ongoing justification for retaliatory or repressive responses from targets or governments.

The most defensible grounds for ending a campaign are: the target has become genuinely impervious to the specific leverage mechanisms available (and there is no realistic path to developing new ones); the opportunity cost has become prohibitive (the resources would produce more impact elsewhere); or the strategic landscape has shifted so fundamentally that the original theory of change no longer holds.

Sociological research on social movements describes a common pathology: activists conflate strategic effectiveness with emotional engagement, prioritising high-intensity visible action (the "take-off" stage of a movement) and treating de-escalation or reform-oriented engagement as abandonment of the cause.24 This conflation leads to burned-out organiser communities and movements that cannot sustain long-run pressure. The Movement Action Plan (MAP) framework, developed by Bill Moyer, is a useful diagnostic tool for distinguishing genuine strategic failure from the uncomfortable but necessary transition between movement phases.

It is also worth distinguishing between ending a campaign against a specific target and ending work on a specific issue. Abandoning an impervious target is often the right call; abandoning the issue is rarely justifiable when animals continue to suffer under the practice in question. The energy should be redirected, not dissipated. When THL encountered resistance from a target like Subway that failed to honour its cage-free commitment by its 2025 deadline, the response was not to walk away from egg industry reform — it was to escalate with specific accountability tools, including a targeted advertising campaign in Subway's local market.9

Abandoning an impervious target is often the right call. Abandoning the issue is almost never justified. Redirect the energy; don't dissipate it.

Good campaign governance includes building in pre-specified review points at which the team honestly assesses progress against the theory of change, compares the trajectory against the opportunity cost of alternative interventions, and makes an explicit decision to continue, pivot, or close. These reviews work best when conducted with reference to pre-agreed success criteria rather than in the heat of ongoing action.

12. Resources for Campaign Organisers

The animal advocacy movement has developed a richer research and training infrastructure than many advocates realise. Accessing these resources before and during a campaign can substantially improve strategic decision-making.

Faunalytics (faunalytics.org) maintains the world's largest database of animal advocacy research — over 6,000 summarised studies — and offers free one-on-one research support through its virtual office hours programme.21 For any campaign team trying to identify what the evidence says about a specific tactic or message, Faunalytics is the first stop.

Animal Charity Evaluators (animalcharityevaluators.org) publishes "Better for Animals," a living evidence review covering approximately 30 intervention types, and maintains a Research Library updated with over 100 new articles monthly.25 Their charity reviews include detailed operational case studies that are valuable for campaign design regardless of whether your organisation is being evaluated.

Animal Ask (animalask.org) specialises in the technical question of campaign ask design — how ambitious to be, how to structure composite versus simple asks, and how to model expected impact across different commitment levels.1

The Commons Library (commonslibrary.org) offers free tools for theory of change development, power analysis, system mapping, and campaign strategy — not animal-specific, but drawn from broad social movement practice and fully applicable to vegan campaigns.10

Animal Advocacy Careers (animaladvocacycareers.org) provides training and career support for advocates looking to professionalise their campaign work, including one-on-one career advice calls. The Effective Animal Advocacy (EAA) Facebook community offers a monthly roundup of opportunities, research, and strategic discussions.26

Humane League Labs (thehumaneleague.org/labs) has conducted some of the earliest empirical studies on animal advocacy effectiveness, with data covering messaging experiments, protest type impacts, and image effectiveness.14,15 Their study materials, pre-registrations, and data are posted to the Animal Charity Evaluators Open Science repository.

Conclusion

The pressure campaign is, at its core, an act of organised leverage — the deliberate concentration of moral, reputational, financial, and social force at the point where change is most possible. Nothing about this is simple, and the evidence base makes clear that well-intentioned campaigns without rigorous strategy can at best be ineffective and at worst actively counterproductive.

What the evidence does support, however, is genuine optimism about what well-designed campaigns can accomplish. The cage-free movement secured over 100 million hens out of battery cages in the United States in roughly a decade — a change that would have seemed fantastical to advocates working in the early 2000s. It was achieved not through a single dramatic event but through the patient, iterative application of strategic pressure: clear targets, specific asks, escalating tactics, and rigorous accountability.

The animals who are suffering now do not have the luxury of waiting for a perfect campaign. But they are also not served by campaigns that burn out their organisers, antagonise potential allies, or fail to learn from what the evidence already tells us. The discipline to run evidence-based, strategically coherent campaigns is not a retreat from urgency. It is the fullest expression of it.
