The Half-Second Gap

A measurable brain signal climbs hundreds of milliseconds before a person reports deciding to move. For forty years, that gap has been read as evidence that the conscious self does not start its own actions — and resisted just as fiercely as it has been asserted.

Sometime in the early 1980s, in a laboratory at the University of California, San Francisco, a neurophysiologist named Benjamin Libet measured a number that has unsettled the idea of free will ever since: the brain begins preparing a voluntary movement several hundred milliseconds before the person feels they have decided to make it.

Stated plainly, the finding is almost banal — of course neurons fire before muscles move. The provocation lies in the sequence Libet claimed to time precisely. The electrical sign of motor preparation, the readiness potential, was already climbing while the conscious intention to act had not yet, by the subject's own report, arrived.1 If the brain sets the action in motion first, and awareness of "deciding" comes second, then the conscious will looks less like the author of our acts than like a spectator informed after the fact. That inference — contested at every link in its chain — is what turned a modest electrophysiology result into one of the most argued-over experiments in the cognitive sciences.2

Origins · 1964–1965

Before Libet: a voltage that precedes the will

The signal at the center of the story was not Libet's discovery. In the spring of 1964, two researchers at the University of Freiburg — Hans Helmut Kornhuber and his doctoral student Lüder Deecke — set out to study something the physiology of the era had largely ignored: the brain's self-initiated activity, the electrical run-up to a movement a person simply chooses to make.3

Conventional averaging could not see it, because there was no external stimulus to lock the recordings to. Their workaround was to record movements and EEG onto tape and then average the brain activity backward from the moment of movement — a reverse-averaging technique that exposed a slow, gradually rising negative potential beginning up to a second or more before a self-paced finger or foot movement. They named it the Bereitschaftspotential: the readiness potential.3 It is a low-amplitude shift on the order of ten to fifteen microvolts, largest over the midline and contralateral motor regions, and now attributed largely to the supplementary and pre-supplementary motor areas.

For nearly two decades the readiness potential was a tool of motor physiology, not a philosophical bomb. It demonstrated that voluntary action had a long, recordable cortical prologue. What it did not address was the question Libet would force upon it: where, in that prologue, does the conscious decision belong?

The Method · Brain, 1983

The 1983 experiment

Libet's design, published with Curtis Gleason, Elwood Wright, and Dennis Pearl in Brain, was an exercise in mental chronometry — the attempt to put two events, one neural and one subjective, onto the same clock.1 Six subjects were asked to perform a quick, abrupt flexion of the wrist or fingers whenever they felt the urge, with no preplanning of when. Three streams were recorded at once:

The three clocks
RP — EEG Scalp electrodes captured the readiness potential, the unconscious cortical buildup preceding the movement.
W — report Subjects watched a fast-revolving spot on an oscilloscope "clock" and afterward reported its position at the instant they first became aware of the wish or intention to move — the time Libet labeled W.
EMG — muscle Electromyography marked the onset of muscle activity — the movement itself, set as time zero.

The published averages told a consistent story. The readiness potential began roughly 550 ms before the muscle fired; the reported moment of conscious intention, W, fell only about 200 ms before it.1 Subtract the two and the brain's preparation led the conscious decision by something on the order of 350 ms — the half-second gap of the headline, give or take. (Libet distinguished movements with advance "preplanning," whose readiness potentials began earlier, from spontaneous ones; even the spontaneous "type II" series showed cerebral onset clearly preceding W.)1

The conclusion Libet drew was deliberately worded but unmistakable in import: the cerebral initiation of a freely voluntary act begins unconsciously, before the person has any reportable awareness of intending it.

Interpretation · 1985–1999

Libet's reading — and the veto

In a 1985 target article in Behavioral and Brain Sciences, Libet laid out the interpretation that would define the debate: if the readiness potential reliably precedes awareness, then "unconscious cerebral initiative" — not conscious will — gets voluntary action underway.4 The article drew dozens of commentaries and effectively opened the field of the empirical free-will debate.

Yet Libet himself was not a determinist about the conscious self, and he resisted the bleakest reading of his own data. He noticed that after W there remained roughly 150–200 ms before the muscle fired, and he proposed that consciousness might use that window not to start the act but to stop it. The will, on this view, retains a power of veto — a capacity Libet and others came to call "free won't."5

The brain begins the act unconsciously; the role left for conscious will may be to permit it — or to forbid it. Libet's "veto" hypothesis, 1985–1999

It was an elegant compromise, and a precarious one. Critics noted that a conscious veto, if it too were preceded by its own unconscious buildup, would simply relocate the problem rather than solve it — a worry Libet acknowledged, arguing the veto might be a control function not itself dictated by the preceding preparatory process.5 Whether the veto is real, and whether it escapes the regress, became its own line of experiments decades later.

The Stakes

The upset: agency, the law, the self

Why did a 350-millisecond lag provoke forty years of argument? Because of what rides on the order of those two events. Our ordinary self-image, and much of our moral and legal architecture, assumes that a conscious agent deliberates and then causes the corresponding action. If the causal work is already done before the agent is aware of deciding, conscious will starts to look like what philosophers call an epiphenomenon — a real experience that makes nothing happen, a readout rather than a cause.

That worry was sharpened by the social psychologist Daniel Wegner, whose 2002 book argued that the feeling of consciously willing an action is a constructed inference — the mind's after-the-fact story about behavior its conscious processes did not initiate.6 Libet's timing data became Exhibit A for that thesis, and for a broader claim that conscious agency is, at least in part, an illusion the brain authors about itself.

The implications reach into law. Anglo-American criminal responsibility leans on notions of voluntary action and culpable choice; if neuroscience shows choices to be settled by prior brain states outside awareness, does the framework of blame and desert still stand? In an influential analysis, Joshua Greene and Jonathan Cohen argued that, strictly speaking, neuroscience changes nothing for a law that already operates on compatibilist terms — but that, psychologically, vivid mechanistic findings may erode retributive intuitions and push punishment toward purely forward-looking, consequentialist justifications.7 "For the law," in their phrase, neuroscience changes nothing and everything at once.

Underneath the headlines sat a genuine conceptual question that the philosopher Adina Roskies pressed: what relationship, exactly, does a result about the timing of a finger-flick bear to the rich notion of volition that moral responsibility actually invokes?8 Holding that question in view turns out to matter a great deal.

Escalation · 2008–2011

The escalations: seconds, not milliseconds

For two decades Libet's effect was argued over but rarely surpassed. Then two studies appeared to push the brain's lead time from milliseconds to seconds — and from scalp averages to the activity of individual cells.

Soon et al., 2008 — decoding decisions from fMRI

Working in John-Dylan Haynes's group, Chun Siong Soon and colleagues used functional MRI and pattern-classification rather than a single averaged waveform. Subjects freely chose to press a left or right button while a stream of letters let them mark when they consciously decided. Patterns in the frontopolar cortex (Brodmann area 10) and precuneus/parietal cortex carried information about which button would be pressed up to about seven seconds before the conscious decision — and, accounting for the sluggishness of the blood-oxygen signal, perhaps ten seconds before it.9

A necessary caveat The decoding in Soon et al. was statistically reliable but modest — only somewhat above the 50% chance level, not a near-perfect prediction. The brain's early activity biased the upcoming choice; it did not announce it with certainty. The authors themselves stressed that their study did not settle the free-will question and that whether such a prepared decision could still be reversed remained open.9

Fried et al., 2011 — listening to single neurons

Itzhak Fried, Roy Mukamel, and Gabriel Kreiman gained a vantage no scalp electrode allows: they recorded from intracranial electrodes in epilepsy patients undergoing surgical monitoring. Across more than a thousand neurons, firing rates in the supplementary motor area progressively shifted in the roughly 1,500 ms before subjects reported deciding to move; a population of a few hundred SMA neurons predicted the impending decision with better than 80% accuracy some 700 ms before awareness.10 They modeled the result as volition emerging when internally generated activity crosses a threshold — a framing that, as it happened, pointed toward the very reinterpretation about to upend the field.

By this point the readiness potential and its successors had become a cornerstone of the neuroscience of voluntary action, treated in syntheses of the field as the canonical entry point to the biology of the will.11

Reinterpretation · 2012

The counter-revolution: an artifact of averaging

The most consequential challenge to Libet came not from philosophy but from a re-examination of what the readiness potential is. In 2012, Aaron Schurger, Jacobo Sitt, and Stanislas Dehaene proposed that the smooth pre-movement ramp might be an artifact of the very method that revealed it.12

Their model treats the decision of when to move, in a task with no external cue, as a leaky stochastic accumulator: ongoing neural activity drifts and fluctuates around a threshold, and a movement is triggered whenever spontaneous, autocorrelated noise happens to push it across. Crucially, the analysis averages epochs time-locked to the movement. When you align many threshold crossings and average backward, the random fluctuations that preceded each crossing sum into a gradual, exponential-looking rise — a readiness potential — even though no deterministic "decision to move" was building beforehand.12

Two predictions made the model testable, and the data bore them out: the same averaging should produce an RP-like buildup even before cued fast responses, and the shape of the ramp should track the distribution of waiting times — both of which the model reproduced.12 The reinterpretation does not prove free will exists. What it does is dissolve the specific inference at the heart of the Libet reading: if the pre-movement ramp is largely the signature of spontaneous fluctuation crossing a threshold, then it is not evidence that "the decision" was made and merely awaited conscious notification.13

Methodological Critique

What is W actually measuring?

Even setting the accumulator model aside, the inference from Libet's data leans on two assumptions that were challenged almost from the start: that the readiness potential is the cause of the action, and that W faithfully marks the true onset of conscious intention.2

The second assumption is fragile. W is a memory- and attention-dependent introspective judgment — a person mapping a fleeting inner event onto the position of a moving dot — and the act of timing one's own intention may distort the very thing being timed. Reported intention times shift with the method used to elicit them, which is hard to reconcile with W being a clean readout of a fixed neural milestone.

The first assumption fared no better under direct test. Jeff Trevena and Jeff Miller, using the lateralized readiness potential to distinguish preparation for a specific movement from general arousal, found brain activity that did not behave as an unconscious initiator of the particular act should — evidence, they argued, against the claim that the readiness potential reflects unconscious movement initiation rather than a more general readiness to act.14

There is also the matter of scope. Libet's subjects were instructed to flex without reason, deliberation, or stakes — to pick, not to choose. Whether anything learned about such arbitrary, contentless twitches generalizes to the deliberated decisions that responsibility cares about is precisely the inferential leap critics refuse to grant.

The Veto · 2016

The veto, rehabilitated

Libet's "free won't" lingered for decades as a hopeful conjecture without much direct evidence. In 2016, Matthias Schultze-Kraft and colleagues in Haynes's lab gave it an empirical test by staging a contest between a person and a machine.15

They built a brain–computer interface that monitored EEG in real time and tried to predict an impending movement from the rising readiness potential, issuing a stop signal the moment a movement looked imminent. Could subjects still cancel once their own preparatory signal was already underway? Largely, yes — participants could win the "duel" and abort the movement even after the readiness potential had begun. But not without limit: there was a point of no return, roughly 200 ms before muscle onset, past which cancellation became impossible.15

The result cuts both ways, which is why it endures. It vindicates Libet's intuition that an early brain signal does not lock in behavior — there is a genuine window for last-moment control. And it bounds that window sharply, confirming that beyond a certain instant the act is committed regardless of intention.

The Rejoinder

The philosophers' rejoinder

Running parallel to the empirical disputes was a philosophical objection that many regard as decisive: that Libet, and especially his popularizers, took aim at the wrong target.

Alfred Mele has argued at length that the experiments do not support the sweeping conclusions hung on them. The readiness potential may index an urge or a leaning toward action rather than a settled decision; W's reliability is doubtful; and proximal intentions to "move now" are not the distal, deliberated intentions — to take the job, to keep the promise — that anchor ordinary talk of free and responsible choice.16 On this reading, even granting every measurement, what Libet's paradigm cannot reach is the kind of willing that the free-will debate is actually about.17

Daniel Dennett pressed a complementary, compatibilist point. The "self" whose authorship the experiment seems to deny is a metaphysical fiction — an inner unmoved mover that must act before and independently of any brain process. But there is no such homunculus to defy. You are not separate from your unconscious neural preparation; that preparation is part of the deciding you do.16 A five-hundred-millisecond head start for the brain over the conscious report threatens free will only on an assumption — that real agency requires consciousness to be the first link in the chain — that compatibilists deny in the first place.

That conscious awareness lags the brain that produces it is a discovery about timing, not a refutation of agency. The compatibilist reading

None of this is a consensus that free will is safe; philosophers disagree as sharply as ever, and hard determinists read the same data as confirmation. What the rejoinder establishes is narrower and more durable: the leap from Libet's chronometry to "free will is an illusion" smuggles in contestable assumptions about what willing is — assumptions the experiment was never designed to test.

State of the Question

Where the question stands

Four decades on, the field has largely moved from "does the readiness potential refute free will?" to a more careful set of questions about what the signal is and which decisions it speaks to. A 2021 review by Schurger and colleagues reframes the readiness potential not as a stamped command awaiting execution but as a signal whose pre-movement rise reflects, in substantial part, the dynamics of spontaneous fluctuation and threshold-crossing — a reading that defuses the original metaphysical alarm while leaving the neuroscience richer.18

A second shift is about scope. The newer neuroscience of volition increasingly distinguishes arbitrary actions — Libet's contentless flexes — from deliberate ones made for reasons, and there is mounting reason to doubt that findings about the former transfer cleanly to the latter.19 If so, the experiments that launched the empirical free-will debate may have been probing a corner of human action least representative of the choices over which we hold one another responsible.

The lasting legacy of Libet's work is not the conclusion that the conscious self is a bystander; that conclusion is, at best, unproven and, on current evidence, overreaching. The legacy is the move itself — taking a question that had belonged to metaphysics for millennia and making it answerable, in part, with electrodes and a clock. The gap between brain and awareness is real and measurable. What it means is still, fittingly, something we are deciding.
