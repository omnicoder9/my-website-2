Vegan Activism & Market Strategy

Don't Ask Nicely. Win the Market.

The most powerful force for reducing animal suffering isn't moral persuasion — it's building better systems that make cruelty economically obsolete. A new generation of advocates is learning that lesson.

by Nicholas Knapp
April 23, 2026

Every January, a predictable cycle plays out. Well-meaning people go vegan for a month, post about it, and the industrial food machine — a system worth hundreds of billions of dollars, propped up by deep subsidies and centuries of infrastructure — continues humming. Advocacy organizations celebrate downloads of their starter kits. The chickens die by the billions anyway.

This is not a criticism of individual choice. Personal ethics matter. But it is a critique of a strategic paradigm that has dominated animal advocacy for decades: the belief that changing what people believe will change what the market does. The evidence suggests otherwise. And a growing cohort of economists, technologists, food entrepreneurs, and behavioral scientists are building a different model — one that doesn't ask the system to be better, but redesigns the incentive structure until being better is simply the most profitable option.

The thesis, put plainly: bad systems don't get morally rejected at scale. They get outcompeted, out-defaulted, and out-priced. The history of every major industry disruption — from fossil fuels to tobacco to leaded gasoline — teaches the same lesson. Moral arguments help. Market forces finish the job.

The Demand-Side Trap

The dominant model of vegan advocacy has been demand-side restraint: tell people to buy less of something harmful. It is the same logic as telling people to drive less rather than building transit systems, or asking them to use less plastic rather than banning single-use products. The strategy is not wrong, exactly — it's just operating at the wrong scale, and at the wrong point in the causal chain.

Markets are not fundamentally driven by individual moral resolve. They are shaped by price signals, defaults, institutional procurement, infrastructure, and regulation. When you change those things, you change mass behavior — not because people had a conversion experience, but because the easy choice became the right choice. Advocacy that ignores these levers will always struggle against the gravitational pull of a cheaper, more convenient, more familiar alternative.

The good news is that the movement is learning. Inspired by the work of organizations like the Good Food Institute (GFI), and informed by decades of behavioral economics research, a new generation of advocates is working on four interlocking strategies that operate on the supply side, the institutional side, and the policy side simultaneously.

I. Outcompete: Build Things That Win

The most durable way to displace a harmful product is to build a better one. This isn't rhetoric — it's the logic of every successful technology transition in history. Solar didn't beat coal because people felt guilty about carbon. It beat coal because the price per kilowatt-hour dropped below parity and the economics became irresistible.

The alternative protein sector is attempting exactly this transition. Market Signal Impossible Foods has cut its retail and foodservice prices multiple times, with reductions of 15–20% per cycle at their peak.[4] Beyond Meat famously set a target of achieving price parity with conventional meat by the mid-2020s. These are not marketing gestures — they are strategic maneuvers to reach the point where the moral calculus and the economic calculus converge.

The challenge is real. Conventional animal meat is cheap partly because its costs are hidden. The price at the grocery store does not include the greenhouse gas emissions, the water pollution, the antibiotic resistance risk, or the animal welfare externalities that the industry generates. Plant-based companies, trying to compete on a tilted field, face a catch-22: scale requires demand, and bringing prices down requires scale.[4]

Case Study

Impossible Foods & the Restaurant Channel Strategy

When Impossible Foods launched the Impossible Whopper at Burger King in 2019, it wasn't primarily targeting committed vegans. It was targeting the 95% of Americans who eat meat and would never walk into a vegan restaurant. By making plant-based protein available in the most recognizable fast food context in the world — at a comparable price point — it demonstrated something that moral advocacy rarely can: that the switch can be nearly invisible.[4]

The lesson is structural. You do not need to change what people believe. You need to change what they are offered. When Impossible is on the default menu alongside beef, it competes on taste and price rather than on ethics. That is a much more winnable fight.

The investment ecosystem is beginning to understand this. In 2024, fermentation-enabled protein companies attracted the largest share of alternative protein investment globally, with $651 million flowing into the sector — a 43% increase year-over-year.[3] Fermentation is particularly promising because it can produce protein with far greater efficiency than animal agriculture, and companies like Meati Foods (mycelium protein) and Formo (precision fermentation dairy) are targeting taste and texture parity with the products they aim to replace. Europe's alternative protein companies overall raised nearly $509 million in 2024, a 23% increase from the prior year.[5]

Perhaps most significant is the emergence of the Bezos Earth Fund's Centers for Sustainable Protein — launched at North Carolina State University, Imperial College London, and the National University of Singapore — which injected $400 million in public research dollars into the global alternative protein ecosystem in 2024 alone.[6] When philanthropic capital and academic infrastructure align with commercial ambition, the path to cost-competitive alternatives shortens dramatically.

If the world is to achieve our climate, biodiversity, public health, and food security goals, reimagining the way meat is made will be as essential as the global transition to renewable energy.

— Caroline Bushnell, SVP, Good Food Institute, April 2024

II. Shift Defaults: Make Good the Easy Choice

Behavioral economics has produced one of the most counterintuitive and powerful findings of the past thirty years: for the vast majority of decisions, the option that is presented as the default wins. People do not carefully deliberate every choice. They take the path of least resistance. This is not a flaw of human character — it is a feature of how cognition works under conditions of abundance and complexity.

For food systems, the implication is enormous. If a cafeteria serves chicken by default and offers a vegetarian option on request, most people eat chicken. If it serves a vegetarian meal by default and offers meat on request, most people eat vegetables. The food is different. The ethics are irrelevant to the decision. The architecture did all the work.

A randomized controlled trial at six university events across four U.S. academic institutions found that changing the default meal option from meat to plant-based increased plant-based selection by 43 percentage points, while reducing harmful environmental impacts by 45%.[7] Critically, participant choice was fully preserved — nothing was banned or removed. The number of people who opted out was small. The environmental gain was enormous.

Case Study

Greener by Default & the Campus Procurement Play

Greener by Default is a nonprofit that works directly with institutional food service providers — universities, hospitals, corporate campuses — to implement plant-based default policies in dining settings. Their evidence base is robust. A Dutch restaurant study found that implementing a menu-based default nudge for a bean-based alternative increased its selection from 8.6% to 80.0%.[8] A seaweed-based alternative went from 16.1% to 58.3%.[8]

At the French university cafeteria scale: simply doubling the proportion of vegetarian main meals on offer — with no other changes — doubled the rate of vegetarian meal selection, and student satisfaction improved slightly. Only 6% of students even noticed the change.[9]

An analysis of plant-based defaults in New York City hospitals found the intervention saved approximately 59 cents per meal by using whole-food plant-based ingredients.[10] Institutional plant-based defaults are, in other words, simultaneously better for animal welfare, better for the climate, and cheaper for the institution. They require no ideological conversion from anyone.

The research also illuminates which framing works and which doesn't. Taste-focused labels — describing flavor, texture, and provenance — increased vegetable selection by 29% compared to health-focused labels in a cafeteria setting.[9] The word "vegan" is actively counterproductive for most audiences outside the natural channel. "Plant-based," "direct protein," or simply evocative culinary descriptions outperform it consistently.[10]

The scale of the opportunity is staggering. If every college student in the United States attended just one plant-based default catered event per year, back-of-envelope calculations suggest conservation of up to 27 million kilograms of CO₂ — the equivalent of emissions from roughly 3 million gallons of gasoline.[7] That's from a single institutional nudge, applied once, without asking anyone to become vegan.

III. Price In the Harm: Make Externalities Visible

Animal agriculture is, at its economic core, a system in which private actors extract private profit while distributing costs onto the public: in the form of greenhouse gas emissions, water pollution, antibiotic resistance, deforestation, and biodiversity loss. This is the textbook definition of a negative externality. And the textbook response to a negative externality is a corrective tax or price signal.

Livestock production is responsible for approximately 14.5% of total global greenhouse gas emissions, according to the FAO.[11] Animal-based products account for 83% of all agricultural land use while providing only 18% of the world's calories.[12] The meat and dairy industries in California alone were found to account for 47% of the state's consumptive water use during the severe drought of the 2010s.[13] None of these costs appear on a price tag. They are, in the economic sense, free — borne by everyone except the entities that generated them.

The logic of a corrective meat tax is well-established in the academic literature. A 2022 review in the Review of Environmental Economics and Policy found that meat is significantly underpriced when environmental and health externalities are properly accounted for, and that a well-designed consumption tax could address multiple externalities simultaneously while improving public health outcomes.[16] Research from the Oxford Martin Programme on the Future of Food estimated that a meat tax could prevent more than 220,000 deaths annually and save over $40 billion in global healthcare costs.[15]

Policy is beginning to move. Denmark — a major pork and dairy exporter — will begin taxing livestock carbon emissions in 2030.[17] New Zealand legislated to measure and price farm emissions, and Spain and Switzerland have carbon pricing frameworks under development.[17] The FAIRR Initiative, a global network of investors managing over $20 trillion in assets, has built livestock carbon risk into financial modelling and calculates that emerging carbon taxes could cost the 40 largest meat companies up to $11.6 billion of EBITDA by 2050.[18]

Case Study

The Danish Ethics Council & the Political Anatomy of a Meat Tax

In 2016, Denmark's Ethics Council recommended a tax on red meat based on climate impact — a consumer-facing sales tax that would apply equally to domestic and imported products, preventing carbon leakage. The proposal sparked significant public debate about the relationship between food and climate, which advocates regarded as a win in its own right even as the Parliament rejected the measure.[13]

Mickey Gjerris, former Council chairman and Associate Professor of Bioethics at the University of Copenhagen, argued afterward that future proposals would be more viable if the tax revenue were explicitly earmarked to help livestock farmers transition toward sustainable alternatives — reframing the policy from punishment to managed transition.[13] The political lesson: corrective pricing works best when it is paired with investment in alternatives and protection for the most economically vulnerable.

The subsidy question is equally important — and perhaps more politically tractable. In the EU, livestock farmers currently receive 1,200 times more public funding than those producing plant-based or cultivated meat alternatives. In the United States, animal farmers benefit from 800 times more public funding than their plant-based counterparts.[14] From 2014 to 2020, combined spending on plant-based alternatives in both regions amounted to just $42 million — 0.1% of the $44 billion spent on meat and dairy support over the same period.[14] Rebalancing that subsidy structure would shift the competitive landscape dramatically, without requiring a single consumer to change their values.

IV. Create New Markets: Invent What Doesn't Exist Yet

The most ambitious version of this strategy doesn't try to reform the existing system at all. It builds a new one. Cultivated meat — real animal muscle tissue grown directly from cells, without slaughter — represents the most radical attempt to create a new market that obsoletes the old one by producing an identical product through a fundamentally different process.

The category is still pre-commercial at meaningful scale. In 2024, cultivated meat and seafood companies raised $139 million globally — down sharply from the peak years — and the number of consumers who have ever purchased cultivated meat remains in the thousands.[19] Regulatory barriers are real: while Upside Foods and Good Meat received FDA approval in 2023, and Hong Kong and Israel have both approved products for sale, multiple U.S. states have preemptively moved to ban cultivated meat entirely.[19] The industry is navigating both the science of scale-up and the politics of incumbents who understand what disruption means for their business.

Yet the infrastructure is being built. In 2024 alone, at least four commercial-scale production facilities opened globally, including Multus' serum-free growth media facility in the UK, Nutreco's food-grade cell feed plant in the Netherlands, and BLUU Seafood's pilot facility in Germany.[19] In 2025, plant-based alternative protein investment rose 39% to $450 million, signaling recalibration toward commercial traction over speculative scale-up.[20]

Case Study

The Good Food Institute: Think Tank as Market Architect

Founded by Bruce Friedrich in 2016, GFI occupies an unusual position: it functions simultaneously as a scientific research funder, a policy advocate, a corporate engagement partner, and an industry analyst. Its explicit theory of change is that animal agriculture will be displaced not through moral rejection but through market competition — and that the role of a nonprofit is to accelerate the conditions for that competition by funding the open-source science, the regulatory pathways, and the investor education that no single company could afford to produce alone.[6]

GFI has published open-access research on key technical barriers — cell cultivation media costs, fermentation scale-up, protein ingredient functionality — creating a knowledge commons that lowers R&D costs across the entire sector. This is a deliberate market-creation strategy: by treating foundational research as a public good, GFI accelerates the entire industry rather than any single player, making disruption of animal agriculture more likely regardless of which companies ultimately dominate.[6]

The fermentation pathway may prove most commercially viable in the near term. Mycoprotein companies like Quorn have demonstrated that fungal protein can achieve consumer acceptance at scale. Newer entrants like Meati Foods (mycelium) and Formo (precision-fermented dairy proteins) are targeting the premium segment first, using quality and novelty to build margins that fund the cost reductions needed for mass-market entry — precisely the strategy Tesla used in the EV market.

Bad systems don't get morally rejected at scale. They get outcompeted, out-defaulted, and out-priced. The history of every major industry disruption teaches the same lesson.

The Honest Accounting: What Still Doesn't Work

Any honest assessment of this new paradigm has to grapple with some uncomfortable data. The U.S. retail plant-based food market contracted in both dollar and unit sales in 2025, with plant-based meat and seafood declining for a third consecutive year.[2] The products still carry significant price premiums over conventional counterparts, and consumer research consistently identifies price and taste gaps as the primary barriers to adoption.[2] The cultivated meat category, despite years of investment and high-profile regulatory approvals, remains almost entirely pre-revenue.[19]

These are not arguments against the market strategy. They are arguments for humility about timelines and against triumphalism. The solar industry experienced a decade of skepticism and contraction before the cost curves broke decisively in its favor. The EV industry fought years of consumer indifference before reaching the tipping point of mass adoption. Industrial food systems are among the most entrenched in human civilization — they are subsidized, politically protected, culturally embedded, and optimized over generations. Displacing them will take longer than any of us would prefer.

What the evidence does suggest clearly is that the behavioral default strategy works right now, at scale, with existing products, at low cost. The externality pricing strategy is politically difficult but economically compelling and gaining momentum. The competitive technology strategy is longer-term but increasingly well-funded. And all three converge toward the same outcome: a food system where choosing the plant-based option is not a sacrifice, not a statement, not an act of discipline — but simply the obvious thing to do.

Who Is Building This Future

The intellectual and organizational landscape of market-oriented food system change is rich and growing. Caroline Bushnell and Bruce Friedrich at GFI are perhaps the most prominent voices for the alt-protein market-creation approach. Philosopher and author Peter Singer, whose 1975 book Animal Liberation laid the moral foundation for the modern movement, has been explicit in his support for technological and market solutions as the most scalable path to reducing animal suffering. Economist Matthew Lusk has contributed foundational work on the economics of meat taxation. The FAIRR Initiative, led by Jeremy Coller, is building the investor constituency for agricultural carbon pricing from within the financial system rather than against it.

Organizations like Greener by Default and the Better Food Foundation are implementing default-nudge programs at institutions across North America and the UK, building the evidence base and the institutional relationships needed for policy adoption. World Resources Institute's Better Buying Lab has tested renaming and relabeling strategies in foodservice settings, finding that evocative culinary language consistently outperforms health or sustainability framing.[9]

And in university labs from Tufts to Imperial College to the National University of Singapore, a generation of food scientists and bioengineers is working on the technical problems — cheaper growth media, better texturization, more efficient fermentation — that stand between the current state of alternative proteins and the moment of price parity that changes everything.

The Endgame

The goal is a world where the default protein on every menu, in every cafeteria, at every catered event, is produced without animal suffering. Where the price of a beef burger accurately reflects its cost to the climate and to public health. Where cultivated and fermented proteins have achieved the economies of scale that make them cheaper per gram of nutrition than anything produced by industrial animal agriculture. Where the vegan choice is not the hard choice, or the expensive choice, or the preachy choice — but simply the normal one.

Getting there does not require converting everyone to veganism. It requires changing enough defaults, pricing enough externalities, and building enough competitive alternatives that the system tips. History suggests that once tipping points arrive, they arrive fast. Kerosene lamps disappeared within a generation of electric light. Typewriters vanished within a decade of the PC. The question for animal advocacy is not whether market disruption is the right strategy. It demonstrably is. The question is whether the movement can build the coalitions — across food science, behavioral economics, public policy, and patient capital — needed to make it happen.

The pamphlet and the protest have their place. But the future of animal welfare will be written in price curves and procurement contracts, in default settings and subsidy reform, in bioreactors and fermenters and the slow, relentless mathematics of scale. That is where the fight is now. And increasingly, that is where the movement is going.
