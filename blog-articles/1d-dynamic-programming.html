Along a Single Axis

Dynamic programming has a reputation it does not deserve. To a newcomer it looks like a bag of tricks, each problem a separate act of invention. In practice the opposite is true: a small number of structural shapes account for the overwhelming majority of contest problems, and the first place to learn to see those shapes is in one dimension, where the entire state of a subproblem is captured by a single index.

The technique was named by Richard Bellman in the 1950s, and his framing remains the most useful one we have. An optimal solution is built from optimal solutions to subproblems — the principle of optimality — so that decisions taken in sequence never need to be revisited.[1] The two conditions that license the method are optimal substructure (an optimal answer contains optimal answers to its parts) and overlapping subproblems (the same parts recur, so they are worth remembering).[2] When both hold, dynamic programming is nothing more exotic than a careful, memoized brute force: we enumerate every relevant subproblem exactly once and store the answer.

Why begin in one dimension? Because the hardest part of DP is not the arithmetic of the recurrence but the act of defining what a subproblem is. A 1-D problem forces that definition into its simplest possible form — "the answer for the prefix ending at index i", or "the answer for target value i" — and once you can read that signature on sight, the two-dimensional and tree-shaped problems that follow stop feeling like new species. Competitive programmers internalize a catalogue of these one-dimensional shapes precisely because they reappear, lightly disguised, in problem after problem.[3]

This guide walks the canonical set — Climbing Stairs, House Robber, Coin Change, Longest Increasing Subsequence, Word Break — and a tour of the other essentials, treating each not as an isolated puzzle but as a representative of a pattern. Every core algorithm is given as a working reference implementation in C++, Go, Java, Rust, C#, and TypeScript; use the selector at the top of the page to read all of them in your language of choice. Smaller variations are shown in C++, the lingua franca of the contest scene, with the translation to other languages left as the mechanical exercise it is.

Master the subproblem definition and the recurrence writes itself; master the recurrence and the code is just transcription.
§ 01 · The Method

The anatomy of a 1-D DP

Every one-dimensional dynamic program can be specified by answering five questions in order. Skipping any of them is the usual source of bugs, and answering them out of order is the usual source of confusion. Treat the list as a checklist you run on every new problem.[4]

  1. State. What does dp[i] mean, in one precise sentence? This is the only creative step. A good definition is unambiguous about whether the index is inclusive ("the best answer using the first i elements") and about what quantity is stored (a count, a length, a maximum, a boolean).
  2. Transition. How is dp[i] expressed in terms of strictly earlier states? This is the recurrence, and it is usually a statement about the last decision: what is the final move that produces state i, and what earlier state did it come from?
  3. Base cases. Which states are known without recursion? Get these exactly right — an off-by-one in dp[0] propagates silently through the whole table.
  4. Order of evaluation. In what order must states be filled so that every dependency is already computed? For 1-D problems this is almost always strictly increasing index, but the direction of the inner loop matters enormously in knapsack-style problems, as we will see.
  5. Answer. Where does the final result live — dp[n], the maximum over the whole table, the last entry?

Three implementations of the same idea

Any recurrence admits three standard codings, and it is worth being fluent in moving between them because each has a place in a contest.

Top-down (memoized recursion) transcribes the recurrence almost verbatim and caches results. It is the fastest to write under time pressure and computes only the states it actually needs, but it pays the cost of recursion and risks stack overflow on long inputs.[5] A memoized Climbing Stairs looks like the recurrence itself:

Bottom-up (tabulation) fills an array in dependency order with an explicit loop. It removes recursion entirely, makes the time complexity obvious by inspection, and is what you reach for when inputs are large.

Space-optimized (rolling state) notices that the table reads only a constant number of recent entries and replaces the whole array with a handful of scalars. The pattern recurs so often it is worth recognizing as its own move: if dp[i] depends only on dp[i-1] and dp[i-2], you never need more than two variables.

Pattern · The rolling-array reduction

The space a 1-D DP truly needs is set by its dependency depth — how far back the recurrence reaches. A fixed look-back of k means O(k) space regardless of n. This is mechanical and lossless, and most "optimal space" follow-ups in interviews are asking for exactly this transformation. The one thing it sacrifices is the ability to reconstruct the chosen solution after the fact — for that you must keep the full table or store parent pointers.

Where the complexity comes from

The running time of a tabulated DP is, to a first approximation, the product of two numbers: the number of states and the cost of one transition.[2] A problem with n states and O(1) transitions is linear; n states each scanning all earlier states is O(n²); n states each scanning a set of m coins is O(nm). Keeping this product in view tells you immediately whether a formulation will pass the constraints, and often suggests where to look for a speed-up — usually by replacing a linear inner scan with a binary search or a monotonic structure, a move we will make explicit in the Longest Increasing Subsequence section.

With the method in hand, the rest of this guide is a gallery. Each problem introduces a shape; the variations show how far the shape stretches; and the taxonomy at the end collects the shapes into a small, memorable set.

§ 02 · Linear Recurrence

Climbing Stairs

You are climbing a staircase of n steps and may ascend either one or two steps at a time. In how many distinct ways can you reach the top?[6] This is the first DP everyone meets, and for good reason: its recurrence is the purest possible illustration of "the last decision."

Ask what the final move onto step n could have been. Either it was a single step from n−1, or a double step from n−2. Those two cases are disjoint and exhaustive, so the number of ways to reach n is the sum of the ways to reach each predecessor.

dp[n] = dp[n−1] + dp[n−2] base: dp[0] = 1 (the empty climb), dp[1] = 1

That is the Fibonacci recurrence wearing a different hat, which immediately tells you the answer grows exponentially in n and that closed forms and fast-doubling methods exist.[7] Because each value depends only on the two before it, the rolling-array reduction applies and we never need an array at all — two scalars suffice for an O(n)-time, O(1)-space solution.

The shape here — a value defined as a fixed-weight combination of a constant number of recent values — is the linear recurrence, and it is the backbone of an entire family of problems.

Pattern · Fixed-window linear recurrence

Signature: dp[i] combines a constant number of immediately preceding entries with constant work. Cost: O(n) time, O(1) space after rolling. Tell-tale: the problem counts paths, tilings, or arrangements that are built up one position at a time with a small fixed set of choices at each step. Decode Ways and Tribonacci live here too.

Variations

Steps of size 1…k

If you may climb any number from 1 to k at a time, the last move came from any of the previous k steps, so dp[i] = dp[i-1] + … + dp[i-k]. Maintaining a running window sum keeps each transition O(1) rather than O(k), preserving linear time.

Minimum Cost Climbing Stairs

A close relative attaches a cost to each step and asks for the cheapest ascent rather than a count.[6] The recurrence flips from a sum to a minimum — dp[i] = min(dp[i-1] + cost[i-1], dp[i-2] + cost[i-2]) — but the skeleton is identical, which is exactly the point of recognizing the pattern.

Astronomically large n

When n is enormous (say up to 1018) the linear scan is too slow, but a linear recurrence can be advanced by raising a transition matrix to the n-th power, giving an O(log n) solution via fast exponentiation.[2] This is the standard escape hatch for any fixed-window recurrence under a huge bound.

§ 03 · Decision DP

House Robber

Houses sit in a row, each holding some amount of money, and you may not rob two adjacent houses or an alarm sounds. Maximize the loot.[6] Where Climbing Stairs counted, House Robber chooses — and the choice at each position is binary.

Define dp[i] as the maximum money obtainable from the first i houses. Consider the last house. Either you skip it, inheriting dp[i-1], or you rob it, collecting nums[i] plus the best you could do up to i-2 (the adjacency rule forbids i-1). Take the better branch.

dp[i] = max( skip: dp[i−1] , rob: dp[i−2] + nums[i] ) base: dp[−1] = 0, dp[0] = nums[0] — handled cleanly by two rolling scalars both starting at 0

As with Climbing Stairs the recurrence reaches back exactly two positions, so two variables replace the array. This is the same problem as weighted interval scheduling on a line of unit intervals, and the take-or-skip formulation generalizes far beyond houses.[5]

Pattern · Binary decision under an adjacency constraint

Signature: at each element you either take it or leave it, and taking it constrains what you may take next. State: the best achievable considering a prefix. Transition: max(skip, take + dp[i − gap]), where the gap encodes the constraint. Subset-sum, paint-fence, and "delete and earn" problems are all this pattern with a different gap or a different objective.

Variations

House Robber II — houses in a circle

Arrange the houses in a ring so that the first and last are now adjacent.[6] The elegant reduction: in any valid plan you rob at most one of house 0 and house n−1, so the optimum is the better of two linear sub-instances — one that excludes the last house, one that excludes the first — each solved by the original algorithm.

Delete and Earn

A problem about deleting array values and earning their sum, with the rule that deleting value v destroys all copies of v−1 and v+1, reduces exactly to House Robber once you bucket earnings by value and lay them out on the number line: adjacent values now play the role of adjacent houses. Recognizing this disguise is worth more than memorizing either problem.

The tree-shaped cousin

House Robber III places the houses at the nodes of a binary tree, forbidding the robbery of a parent and child together. The take-or-skip logic survives verbatim, but the state now indexes a subtree rather than a prefix — it is no longer one-dimensional, and it marks the natural bridge from linear DP into tree DP.

§ 04 · Knapsack on a Line

Coin Change

Coin Change is really two problems sharing a name, and conflating them is one of the most common DP mistakes. Both take a set of coin denominations and a target amount; the first asks for the fewest coins that sum to the amount, the second for the number of distinct ways to make it.[6] Crucially, in both you may use each denomination unlimited times — this is the unbounded knapsack, and the index of the DP is now the target value rather than a position in an array.[8]

Minimum coins

Let dp[a] be the fewest coins summing to amount a. The last coin used was some denomination c ≤ a; remove it and you are left with the optimal solution for a − c. Minimize over every coin that fits.

dp[a] = min over coins c ≤ a of ( dp[a − c] + 1 ) base: dp[0] = 0; unreachable amounts stay at a large sentinel and report −1

The sentinel deserves care: initialize unreachable states to a value so large that dp[a-c] + 1 can never beat a real answer, then read it back as "impossible." Using amount + 1 works because no real answer ever uses more than amount coins.

Number of ways

Now dp[a] counts the combinations of coins summing to a, and here the loop structure carries the entire meaning of the problem. To count combinations — where {1,2} and {2,1} are the same — you must put the coins on the outer loop and amounts on the inner. Doing so introduces each denomination once and for all, so a coin can only be added "after" the coins already considered, which forbids counting the same multiset in two orders.

for each coin c:   for a = c … amount:   dp[a] += dp[a − c] base: dp[0] = 1 (one way to make nothing). Outer loop over coins ⇒ combinations.
Pitfall · Combinations vs. permutations is loop order

Swap the loops — amounts outer, coins inner — and the very same body counts ordered sequences instead, because at each amount you reconsider every coin as a possible last step. That is the right answer for "Combination Sum IV", which despite its name counts permutations, and the wrong answer for Coin Change II. There is no flag and no warning; only the loop nesting distinguishes them, and getting it backwards yields a plausible-but-wrong number that passes small hand-checks.

Pattern · Unbounded knapsack

Signature: a target is assembled from an unlimited supply of items. State: indexed by the remaining (or accumulated) target value. Inner loop ascending over the target lets each item be reused, which is exactly what "unbounded" means; a descending inner loop restricts each item to one use and gives the 0/1 knapsack instead. That single difference in loop direction is the whole distinction between the bounded and unbounded variants — a fact we exploit again in Partition Equal Subset Sum.

Variations & relatives

The same template answers the largest amount that cannot be formed (the Frobenius / "Chicken McNugget" flavor for small denominations), the minimum coins under a coin-count cap (add a second dimension), and the classic observation that the obvious greedy "take the largest coin that fits" is correct only for canonical coin systems and fails in general — which is the entire reason the DP exists.[8]

§ 05 · Subsequence DP

Longest Increasing Subsequence

Given a sequence, find the length of its longest strictly increasing subsequence — a subsequence need not be contiguous, only ordered.[6] LIS is the gateway to subsequence DP and, unusually for an introductory problem, it rewards a second look with a genuinely beautiful speed-up from quadratic to O(n log n).

The quadratic formulation

Define dp[i] as the length of the longest increasing subsequence that ends at index i. Anchoring the state to the final element is the characteristic move of subsequence DP: it makes the transition local. The subsequence ending at i extends some earlier subsequence ending at a j < i with a smaller value, so we take the best such predecessor and add one.[9]

dp[i] = 1 + max{ dp[j] : j < i and nums[j] < nums[i] }   (or 1 if no such j) answer: max over all i of dp[i]. States: n. Transition: scan all earlier ⇒ O(n²).

The O(n log n) leap

The quadratic version asks, for each element, "what is the best subsequence I can extend?" The faster method asks a subtly different question and maintains a single auxiliary array, often called tails: tails[k] is the smallest possible value that can end an increasing subsequence of length k+1 seen so far. This array is always sorted, and that is what unlocks binary search.[11]

For each incoming value x, binary-search for the first entry in tails that is not smaller than x. If none exists, x is larger than every tail and extends the longest run, so append it. Otherwise overwrite that entry with x, recording that a subsequence of that length can now end on a smaller value — which can only help future extensions. The length of tails at the end is the LIS length. The construction is the patience-sorting card game in disguise, and its connection to deep results in probability is a small piece of mathematical folklore.[10]

Pattern · Best-ending-here + a monotonic accelerator

Signature: the answer is the optimum over all subsequences, and a subsequence's value depends only on its last element. State: the best subsequence ending at each index. The quadratic scan over predecessors is the naïve transition; replacing it with a search over a sorted summary structure is the general route to O(n log n). This "maintain a monotonic array and binary-search it" idea recurs across the hardest subsequence and scheduling problems.[12]

Variations

Longest non-decreasing subsequence

Allow equal adjacent values and the only change is the search predicate: replace the "first entry ≥ x" search (a lower bound) with "first entry > x" (an upper bound). One comparison flips strict into non-strict — a perfect example of how a tiny edit re-targets a whole algorithm.[12]

Counting the number of LIS

To count how many longest increasing subsequences exist, augment the quadratic DP with a parallel count[i] array: when a strictly better predecessor is found, copy its count; when an equally good one is found, add it. The bookkeeping is fiddly but mechanical.

Longest bitonic subsequence

A bitonic sequence increases then decreases. Run LIS left-to-right and right-to-left, then for each index combine the two lengths — a clean demonstration that two independent 1-D passes can answer a question neither could alone.

Russian Doll Envelopes & chains

Two-dimensional "nesting" problems reduce to LIS by sorting on one coordinate (breaking ties in reverse on the second to prevent equal-width items from chaining) and then running LIS on the other coordinate. Maximum Length of Pair Chain is the same idea in one fewer dimension. The reduction is the real lesson: many problems become LIS once sorted correctly.[8]

§ 06 · Feasibility DP

Word Break

Given a string and a dictionary of words, decide whether the string can be cut into a sequence of dictionary words with no leftover characters.[6] Word Break is the canonical feasibility problem: the answer is a single yes/no, and the DP table records, for every prefix, whether that prefix is reachable as a clean concatenation of words. It is the boolean cousin of the counting and optimization DPs that came before.

State and transition

Let dp[i] be true when the first i characters of the string can be segmented entirely into dictionary words. The empty prefix is trivially segmentable, so dp[0] is true. A prefix of length i is segmentable exactly when there is some earlier cut point j such that the first j characters are themselves segmentable and the substring from j to i is a dictionary word.[4]

dp[i] = OR over j < i of ( dp[j] AND s[j..i) ∈ dictionary ) ,   dp[0] = true answer: dp[n]. States: n+1. Transition: scan cut points × substring test.

Storing the dictionary in a hash set makes the membership test expected constant time on the substring. The transition still scans every earlier cut point, giving O(n²) substring tests, each costing up to O(L) to hash, for O(n² L) overall. A standard and worthwhile pruning is to track the length of the longest dictionary word and only look back that far: a substring longer than every word can never be a single word, so the inner loop need not run to zero.[3]

Pattern · Prefix reachability

Signature: can a linear structure be tiled, segmented, or traversed to its end under local placement rules? State: a boolean per position — "is this point reachable?" Transition: a position is reachable if some earlier reachable position connects to it by a single legal piece. Jump Game, tiling counts, and string-segmentation problems all share this skeleton; only the legality predicate changes. When the question turns from "is it possible?" into "how many ways?" or "what is the best way?", the same indices carry a count or an optimum instead of a bit.

Variations

Word Break II — reconstructing every segmentation

When the task is to list all valid sentences rather than answer yes/no, the feasibility table becomes a pruning oracle for a backtracking search: only recurse into suffixes that the boolean DP has already certified as segmentable, and memoize the list of completions per starting index. The feasibility pass collapses an exponential search to only the branches that can succeed.[2]

Palindrome Partitioning

Swap the "is a dictionary word" predicate for "is a palindrome" and the identical skeleton answers whether — and in how few cuts — a string splits into palindromes. The minimum-cuts version layers an optimization DP on top: cuts[i] is the fewest cuts for the first i characters, relaxed across every palindromic final piece. Precomputing palindromicity in a separate table keeps the predicate constant-time.

Concatenated Words

To find every word in a list that is the concatenation of two or more other words from the same list, run Word Break on each candidate using the remaining words as the dictionary. The feasibility routine becomes a reusable subroutine — a recurring theme: once a 1-D DP is understood, it is rarely the whole program, but a component invoked inside a larger search.

§ 07 · A wider field

Other essential 1-D problems

The five problems above cover the dominant shapes, but a competitor's reflexes are built by recognizing a few more recurring forms. Each problem below earns its place by introducing a twist — a second running state, a validity guard, a greedy collapse, or a reachable-set encoding — that reappears throughout the catalogue.

Maximum subarray — Kadane's algorithm

Find the contiguous subarray with the largest sum. The DP insight is to fix the subarray's right end: let best[i] be the largest sum of a subarray ending exactly at index i. Such a subarray either extends the best one ending at i−1 or restarts at i alone — so it keeps the previous run only while that run is a net positive contribution.[13] Collapsed to a single rolling scalar, this is Kadane's algorithm, a one-pass O(n) classic and one of the cleanest examples of best-ending-here thinking.[14]

best[i] = nums[i] + max(best[i−1], 0)   ;   answer = max over i of best[i] one rolling value; O(n) time, O(1) space. Track a running best-so-far for the answer.

Maximum product subarray

Switch sum for product and the single-state trick breaks: a large negative times another negative becomes a large positive, so the smallest running product is as valuable as the largest. The fix is to carry two states at each step — the maximum and minimum products ending here — and swap them whenever the current value is negative. Two coupled rolling values, an early signal that the "right" state sometimes has more than one component.[9]

Decode Ways

Count how many ways a digit string decodes to letters under the mapping A=1 through Z=26. The structure is Fibonacci-shaped — each position may stand alone or pair with its predecessor — but guarded by validity: a lone digit counts only if it is not '0', and a two-digit pair counts only if it falls between 10 and 26. The guards are where careless solutions fail, so the zeros deserve deliberate handling.[6]

Jump Game and Jump Game II

Given maximum jump lengths at each index, the feasibility version asks whether the last index is reachable; the optimization version asks for the fewest jumps. Both admit pure-DP solutions, but each collapses to an O(n) greedy. Reachability tracks the farthest index attainable so far and fails the moment the current position outruns it. Minimum jumps sweeps the array as implicit breadth-first layers: maintain the current jump's reach and the farthest reachable next, incrementing the count when the current reach is exhausted.[8] It is a useful reminder that a DP framing and a greedy proof often coexist, and the greedy usually wins on constants.

Partition Equal Subset Sum — knapsack on a line

Decide whether an array splits into two groups of equal sum. If the total is odd the answer is immediately no; otherwise the question becomes whether some subset sums to exactly half the total — a 0/1 subset-sum, the boolean face of the knapsack family. A single boolean array over achievable sums suffices, and the crucial detail is iterating the sum dimension downward for each item so that each value is used at most once. Sweeping upward silently turns the 0/1 knapsack into the unbounded one — the precise mirror of the loop-direction lesson from Coin Change.[5]

State machines on a line

A final family worth naming carries a small fixed set of states forward rather than a single number. Best Time to Buy and Sell Stock with a cooldown or a transaction cap is the archetype: track the best balance while holding versus not holding a share, and relax both each day. Paint Fence and Tiling-with-constraints behave the same way — a constant number of "modes" advancing in lockstep. Because the state count is fixed, time stays O(n) and space drops to O(1); recognizing such problems is mostly a matter of asking "what minimal fact about the past does the next step actually need?"[2] The Tribonacci sequence, where each term sums the previous three, is the gentlest instance of all: a fixed-width window with no decision at all.

§ 08 · Synthesis

A taxonomy of 1-D patterns

Strip away the cover stories and the problems in this article fall into a small number of shapes. Memorizing solutions does not scale; recognizing shapes does. The table below names the recurring 1-D patterns, the surface signal that betrays each one, and the form its transition takes. Most contest problems are one of these wearing a costume — and the harder ones are two of them stacked.[3]

PatternSignal in the promptState & transitionCanonical problems
Linear recurrence (fixed window) "How many ways to reach step n"; counts that grow from a fixed look-back. dp[i] from a constant set of recent entries; O(1) rolling. Climbing Stairs, Tribonacci, Decode Ways
Take-or-skip decision Choose a subset under an adjacency or compatibility constraint to optimize a total. dp[i] = best of include i vs exclude i. House Robber, Delete and Earn
Knapsack on a line A target amount or capacity assembled from items, with or without reuse. Sweep items outer, amount inner; direction encodes reuse. Coin Change (both), Partition Equal Subset Sum
Best-ending-here / running optimum Optimal contiguous run or subsequence anchored at its last element. dp[i] extends or restarts at i; track a global best. Maximum Subarray, Max Product, LIS

Constant-state machine A few discrete modes (holding / idle, painted-same / painted-diff) advancing in time. Fixed set of states relaxed each step; O(1) space. Buy/Sell Stock variants, Paint Fence

The implementation ladder

Independent of pattern, every 1-D DP can be expressed at four rungs of the same ladder, and moving down it is mechanical once the recurrence is fixed. The recurrence is the idea; the rung is an engineering choice.[2]

  1. Brute-force recursion — transcribe the recurrence literally. Correct, exponential, and the reference the later rungs must match.
  2. Top-down memoization — cache each state on first computation. Same recursion, now polynomial; the gentlest rung when the transition is intricate or the reachable states are sparse.
  3. Bottom-up tabulation — fill an array in dependency order. Removes recursion overhead and stack-depth risk, and makes the iteration order explicit.
  4. Rolling state — keep only as many recent entries as the recurrence reads. The lossless space cut, at the cost of being unable to walk the table back for a solution.

The single most useful question across all six patterns is the one that fixes the state: what is the smallest summary of the past that the next decision actually needs? Get that right and the recurrence, the base cases, and the space bound all follow almost without further thought.[1]

§ 09 · Hard-won lessons

Pitfalls & debugging

Correct 1-D DPs fail in a handful of predictable ways. The recurrence is rarely the culprit; the boundaries, the arithmetic, and the iteration order are. The list below is roughly ordered by how often each costs a submission.[4]

  • Off-by-one in the base cases. The most common failure of all. Decide deliberately whether dp[i] means "using the first i items" (so the array has length n+1 and the answer is dp[n]) or "ending at index i" (length n, answer a maximum). Mixing the two conventions mid-solution corrupts every downstream entry silently.
  • Sentinel overflow. Initializing a minimization with a literal "infinity" and then writing dp[i] = dp[i−c] + 1 can overflow if dp[i−c] is still the sentinel. Use a safe sentinel such as amount + 1 that cannot overflow when incremented, and test against it rather than against the language's maximum integer.
  • Loop direction in knapsack. Iterating the capacity dimension upward permits reusing an item (unbounded knapsack); iterating downward forbids it (0/1). Choosing the wrong direction produces a confident, wrong answer with no crash. Coin Change II and Partition Equal Subset Sum sit on opposite sides of this single line.
  • Counting order — combinations vs permutations. In counting DPs, looping items outside the amount counts unordered combinations; looping amount outside items counts ordered sequences. Both compile and run; only one answers the question asked. Re-read the prompt to decide which.
  • Integer overflow in counting DPs. The number of ways grows exponentially and overflows 32-bit integers quickly. Use 64-bit accumulators, and when the statement says "modulo 10⁹+7", take the modulus at every addition, not only at the end.
  • Wrong initialization. A "number of ways" table seeds dp[0] = 1 (one way to make nothing — the empty selection); a "best value" table seeds the empty case to 0; a feasibility table seeds it to true. Copying the skeleton of one DP into another without re-checking the seed is a frequent, quiet bug.
  • Forgetting to reconstruct. If the problem wants the actual subsequence or coin set, not just its size, the rolling-state version has thrown away what you need. Keep the full table or store parent pointers, then walk backward from the answer.
  • Recursion stack depth. A top-down solution on an input of 10⁶ can overflow the call stack before it overflows time. Either convert to bottom-up or raise the stack limit; deep linear recursion is a runtime crash waiting in large tests.
Debugging technique · The brute-force oracle

The fastest way to trust an optimized DP is to keep the exponential brute force you started from and compare them on thousands of small random inputs. Any mismatch is a tiny, instantly reproducible counterexample — far cheaper to debug than a wrong answer on a hidden test of size 10⁵. This differential-testing habit catches base-case and order bugs that staring at the recurrence never will, and it costs only a few lines.[8]

§ 10 · Building the reflex

A practice ladder

Pattern recognition is trained, not read. The progression below moves from pure template to multi-pattern synthesis; the aim is to reach the point where the state definition arrives within seconds of reading a prompt. Solve each without reference, then re-derive the space-optimized form from memory.[6]

  1. Foundations. Climbing Stairs, Min Cost Climbing Stairs, Tribonacci — internalize fixed-window rolling state until it is automatic.
  2. The take-or-skip family. House Robber, then House Robber II, then Delete and Earn — watch one recurrence absorb a circular constraint and a value remapping.
  3. Knapsack on a line. Coin Change, Coin Change II, Partition Equal Subset Sum — drill the loop-direction distinction until it is reflexive.
  4. Running optima. Maximum Subarray, Maximum Product Subarray, then Best Time to Buy and Sell Stock — single-state, then twin-state, then a small state machine.
  5. Subsequence DP. Longest Increasing Subsequence at O(n²), then the O(n log n) rebuild, then Russian Doll Envelopes as a reduction.
  6. Feasibility and reconstruction. Word Break, Word Break II, Palindrome Partitioning — boolean DP, then the same table driving a search.
  7. Synthesis. Decode Ways and Jump Game II, where recognizing the pattern is most of the battle and the implementation is short once it is named.

The structured problem sets at CSES and the curated DP tracks on the major judges follow roughly this arc and are the most efficient way to convert the ideas here into instinct.[4] The goal is not to have seen every problem — it is to have seen every shape, so that the next unfamiliar prompt resolves into one you already know.
