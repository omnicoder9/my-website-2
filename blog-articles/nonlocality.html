§01 — The quarrel

A correlation that should not exist

In a letter to Max Born written on 3 March 1947, Albert Einstein set down the phrase that would haunt the foundations of physics for the next seventy years. Quantum mechanics, he conceded, was empirically formidable; but he could not bring himself to believe in a theory that demanded what he called spukhafte Fernwirkung — spooky action at a distance.1 The objection was not aesthetic squeamishness. It was a claim about the architecture of reality: that the world is built out of separate parts, each carrying its own definite properties, and that no part can be steered by an operation performed arbitrarily far away. Einstein was prepared to abandon almost anything in physics before he would abandon that.

The trouble had been seeded twelve years earlier. In 1935 Einstein, together with Boris Podolsky and Nathan Rosen, published a four-page argument with a title posed as a question — could the quantum-mechanical description of physical reality be considered complete?2 Their answer was no. By examining a pair of particles that had interacted and then separated, they constructed what looked like an airtight case that quantum mechanics omits real features of the world, and that a more complete theory must exist beneath it. Niels Bohr replied within months, in a paper bearing the identical title, insisting that the argument rested on an untenable idea of what it means for a property to be real.3 The exchange settled nothing. For three decades the dispute looked like exactly the kind of metaphysical standoff that working physicists are trained to ignore: two great minds disagreeing about the meaning of a formalism whose predictions neither of them doubted.

Then, in 1964, an Irish theorist at CERN named John Stewart Bell did something nobody had thought possible. He showed that the disagreement was not metaphysical at all — that the competing pictures of reality made different, measurable predictions.4 Any theory of the kind Einstein wanted, in which particles carry pre-existing values and influences travel no faster than light, must obey a precise arithmetic constraint. Quantum mechanics violates that constraint. The question of whether the universe is locally real had become an experimental question, answerable in a laboratory.

The experiments came, and they have been merciless. From the first crude atomic-cascade measurements of the early 1970s, through Alain Aspect's celebrated Orsay experiments, to the loophole-free tests of 2015 that finally sealed every conceivable escape, the verdict has only sharpened: nature violates Bell's constraint, and it does so by exactly the margin quantum mechanics predicts.5 The picture of the world Einstein fought to preserve — a collection of separately real systems, each minding its own local business — is not merely unconfirmed. It is excluded by experiment.

This article traces that long defeat, and asks what precisely was defeated. The phrase "spooky action at a distance" is a provocation, not a theorem, and a great deal of the enduring controversy lives in the gap between them. Entangled particles do not, in fact, signal to one another; relativity survives intact, and no information crosses the gap faster than light. Yet something Einstein held sacred did die. Untangling what died — locality, or realism, or the freedom of the experimenter to choose — is the work of the chapters that follow. The unease the result provokes is not a failure of education that better pedagogy could cure. It is a rational response to a genuinely strange world.

§02 — The 1935 provocation

EPR and the criterion of reality

The argument now known by its authors' initials was, by Einstein's own account, not the paper he would have written. Podolsky drafted the text, and Einstein complained afterward that the central point had been "buried in the formalism." The point he cared about was separability coupled with locality: that two systems far apart possess their own independent real states, and that an intervention on one cannot instantaneously alter the real state of the other.6 Everything in the EPR argument hangs on that pair of assumptions, which Einstein regarded as a precondition for doing physics at all.

EPR proceed from a deliberately modest sufficient condition for an element of reality. If, without disturbing a system in any way, one can predict with certainty the value of some physical quantity, then there exists an element of physical reality corresponding to that quantity.2 The criterion is meant to be hard to reject: it does not ask what reality "really" is, only that a quantity we can predict with perfect confidence, while keeping our hands off the system, must already have been out there to be predicted.

Now consider two particles prepared in a correlated state and allowed to fly apart. In the original formulation the correlation is between continuous variables — the positions and momenta of the two particles are entangled so that the total momentum and the relative position are both sharply defined. Measure the momentum of particle 1 and you can predict the momentum of particle 2 with certainty; measure instead the position of particle 1 and you can predict the position of particle 2 with certainty. Because the two particles are far apart, the locality assumption guarantees that whatever you choose to do to particle 1 cannot disturb particle 2. So both the position and the momentum of particle 2 satisfy the criterion of reality — each could be predicted with certainty without touching it.

The conclusion is fatal for completeness. Quantum mechanics forbids any state in which a particle simultaneously possesses sharp position and sharp momentum; the Heisenberg relations stand in the way. Yet the argument has just established that particle 2 must have both. Either the criterion of reality is wrong, or locality is wrong, or the quantum-mechanical wavefunction is an incomplete description of the particle's real state. EPR took the third horn. The wavefunction, they concluded, leaves out genuine elements of reality; a more complete theory — what later came to be called a hidden-variable theory — must underlie it.2

It is worth being precise about the logical shape of the argument, because Bell's theorem is built directly on it. EPR did not assert hidden variables as a hypothesis. They derived them, from two premises — locality and the perfect correlations that quantum mechanics itself predicts. Given those correlations, locality forces the conclusion that the outcomes were predetermined: particle 2 must "have known" what answer to give before it was measured, because nothing travelling from particle 1 could have told it in time. This is the move that matters. EPR converts locality, applied to perfectly correlated pairs, into the existence of pre-existing values. Hold that thought; in 1964 Bell will show that pre-existing values of exactly this kind are mathematically impossible.

§03 — Bohr and Schrödinger

A riposte nobody could quite restate, and a word that stuck

Bohr's reply is one of the most cited and least understood papers in the literature. He did not dispute the quantum predictions, and he explicitly conceded that there is no question of any mechanical disturbance of the second system. His objection was to the criterion of reality itself, and specifically to the phrase "without in any way disturbing a system." The choice of what to measure on particle 1, Bohr argued, fixes the conditions under which any statement about particle 2 can be given meaning — it constitutes "an influence on the very conditions which define the possible types of predictions" about the far particle.3 One cannot, on this view, speak of particle 2's position and momentum as jointly real, because the experimental arrangements that would define them are mutually exclusive. This is complementarity applied at a distance: reality is not carved up into separate local pieces with all their properties settled, but is constituted relationally by entire experimental contexts.

The difficulty is that Bohr's argument is so compressed as to be nearly oracular, and even sympathetic readers have struggled to reconstruct it into a sharp claim. Bohr himself revisited the wording for years. What the reply established, in practice, was a stance rather than a refutation: the Copenhagen school would simply decline to grant the EPR premise that distant systems carry separate, fully specified real states. Whether this is a profound insight or an elegant evasion is a question that the experiments of later decades would force back into the open — because, as we shall see, the issue is not whether one finds Bohr's prose persuasive, but whether any theory honoring Einstein's separability can reproduce what laboratories actually measure.

The third major voice of 1935 belonged to Erwin Schrödinger, and his contribution outlasted the others as vocabulary. Responding to EPR in a paper communicated to the Cambridge Philosophical Society, Schrödinger named the phenomenon at the heart of the puzzle. He called it Verschränkung — entanglement — and he did not regard it as a curiosity. Entanglement, he wrote, is not one but rather the characteristic trait of quantum mechanics, the feature that enforces its entire departure from classical lines of thought.7 When two systems interact and then separate, their joint state can no longer be factored into a state for each; the best possible knowledge of the whole does not include the best possible knowledge of the parts. That is the formal core of everything in this article.

|ψ−⟩  =  1√2 ( |↑⟩A|↓⟩B  −  |↓⟩A|↑⟩B )
The spin singlet: no factorization |·⟩A⊗|·⟩B reproduces it. The whole is sharply defined; neither part is.

Schrödinger pressed further, into territory that disturbed him more than the cat paradox he introduced in the same trilogy of papers. He observed that by choosing which quantity to measure on the near particle, an experimenter can project the far particle into one or another set of states — he can steer the remote system, selecting at will the ensemble into which it is forced, despite having no physical access to it.8 Schrödinger found it genuinely uncomfortable that the theory should permit a system to be piloted into whichever type of state the distant experimenter pleased. Steering is a weaker cousin of the nonlocality Bell would later pin down, and it has since become a precisely defined resource in quantum information; but in 1935 it was simply one more reason to suspect that the formalism was telling a story about the world that no one yet knew how to read.

§04 — Bohm makes it concrete

From momenta to spins: the version that could be tested

For sixteen years the EPR argument remained trapped in the continuous variables in which Podolsky had cast it — positions and momenta, with their unbounded spectra and their delicate improper eigenstates. The decisive simplification came from David Bohm, who in his 1951 textbook Quantum Theory recast the entire scenario in terms of a two-valued discrete observable: the spin of a spin-½ particle.9 The translation looks at first like a mere pedagogical convenience. It was, in fact, the move that made the dispute decidable. Every Bell test ever performed is a descendant of Bohm's reformulation.

The setup is now standard. A spin-0 source emits two spin-½ particles in opposite directions, their joint spin state being the rotationally invariant singlet introduced above. The defining property of that state is anticorrelation along every axis: if Alice and Bob each orient a Stern–Gerlach analyzer along the same direction n̂, and each records a binary outcome — spin up or spin down, which we label +1 and −1 — then their results are always opposite, with perfect reliability, no matter which common direction they pick. Up here is matched by down there; down here by up there; and this holds for vertical, horizontal, and every orientation between.

It is hard to overstate how provocative that simple fact is once locality is taken seriously. Because the analyzers can be arbitrarily far apart, and because Alice may choose her axis after the particles have separated, the singlet's perfect anticorrelation is precisely the situation EPR analyzed — now stripped to its bones. Run the EPR argument on it: since Alice can predict Bob's outcome with certainty (it is simply the opposite of her own) without going anywhere near his particle, locality demands that Bob's outcome along that axis was a settled matter all along. But Alice could have chosen any axis, and the same reasoning applies to each. So Bob's particle must carry a definite predetermined answer for every possible measurement direction simultaneously — a complete book of instructions, fixed at the source, specifying what it will do for each orientation Alice might select.

This is the hidden-variable picture in its sharpest form, and Bohm's discrete language is what makes it sharp. In the continuous case one could wave a hand and speak vaguely of incompatible position and momentum values. In the spin case the demand is concrete and combinatorial: a definite ±1 outcome assigned to every direction on the sphere, for each particle, fixed before measurement and merely revealed by it. Such an assignment is exactly what a local realist must posit to explain the perfect anticorrelations without invoking action at a distance. The quantum formalism, by contrast, assigns no such values; it furnishes only the joint state and the statistics it generates. The two pictures agree perfectly on the perfect correlations — measurements along identical axes — and for thirteen years no one saw how they could ever be made to disagree. The achievement of John Bell was to ask the one question that pries them apart: what happens when Alice and Bob choose different axes?

§05 — Bell's theorem

The arithmetic of local realism

The path to Bell's theorem ran straight through a famous error. In 1932 John von Neumann had published, inside his magisterial Mathematische Grundlagen der Quantenmechanik, what was widely received as a proof that no hidden-variable theory could ever reproduce the quantum predictions.10 For two decades the proof functioned as a closed door; promising lines of inquiry were abandoned because von Neumann was understood to have shown they led nowhere. The door was not as solid as it looked.

In 1952 David Bohm did the thing von Neumann's theorem appeared to forbid. He constructed an explicit, fully deterministic hidden-variable theory — a revival and completion of de Broglie's pilot-wave idea — that reproduces every statistical prediction of non-relativistic quantum mechanics exactly.11 Particles in Bohm's theory have definite positions at all times, guided by the wavefunction; the theory is avowedly nonlocal, but it works. Its mere existence was a logical scandal: a working counterexample to a celebrated impossibility proof. Something in von Neumann's argument had to be wrong.

It fell to Bell to say exactly what. In a paper written in 1964 but, through an editorial mishap, not published until 1966, Bell dismantled the impossibility proof with surgical care.12 Von Neumann had required that the expectation value of a sum of observables equal the sum of their expectation values — and had imposed this additivity even on non-commuting observables, even at the level of the hypothetical dispersion-free states. For quantum states the relation holds as a theorem; for the underlying hidden states there is no reason whatever to demand it, since non-commuting observables are measured in mutually exclusive arrangements. The condition was an assumption smuggled in as an axiom. Strip it away and von Neumann's wall simply falls down. (The same flaw had been identified by the mathematician Grete Hermann as early as 1935, in a critique that the physics community comprehensively ignored.) Hidden variables were viable after all — and the real question, Bell saw, was not whether they could exist but what properties they were forced to have.

This is where Bell's genius announced itself. Rather than ask whether hidden variables exist, he asked whether local ones can match the quantum statistics, and he turned the question into arithmetic.4 Consider again the EPR–Bohm pairs. Let Alice's measurement, with her analyzer set to direction a, yield an outcome A = ±1, and Bob's, with setting b, yield B = ±1. A local hidden-variable theory says these outcomes are fixed by the settings together with some shared variable λ — anything at all carried from the source, of any nature or dimension — distributed according to some density ρ(λ). The crucial assumption is locality, expressed as a factorization: Alice's outcome A(a, λ) does not depend on Bob's distant setting b, and Bob's outcome B(b, λ) does not depend on Alice's setting a. Each particle responds only to the analyzer it actually encounters and to the baggage it brought from the source.

From these innocuous-looking assumptions a constraint follows. The measured correlation is the average of the product of outcomes, E(a, b) = ∫ A(a, λ) B(b, λ) ρ(λ) dλ. The perfect anticorrelation of the singlet at equal settings forces A(a, λ) = −B(a, λ), so one function suffices to describe both wings. A few lines of algebra on three different settings a, b, c — using only that each outcome is ±1 — then yield Bell's original inequality, a bound that every local hidden-variable theory in the universe must respect:

Bell 1964| E(a, b) − E(a, c) |  ≤  1 + E(b, c)
A theorem of pure arithmetic for any theory in which outcomes are ±1, predetermined by λ, and locally separable. No quantum mechanics has entered yet.

Now let quantum mechanics enter. For the singlet, the correlation between two analyzers separated by angle θ is E(a, b) = −cos θab — a smooth cosine, not the straight lines a local model is forced toward. Feed it into the inequality with a deliberately chosen geometry: place the three settings so that θab = 60°, θbc = 60°, and θac = 120°. The left side becomes |−cos 60° + cos 120°| = |−½ − ½| = 1. The right side becomes 1 − cos 60° = ½. The inequality demands 1 ≤ ½. It is violated, flatly and unambiguously, by the predictions of quantum mechanics. No local theory of pre-existing values can produce the cosine; the geometry of entangled correlations is too strong for it.

Bell spent years trying to make the result intuitive, and his most enduring attempt is a parable about a colleague's laundry. Reinhold Bertlmann, a real physicist at Vienna, was known for always wearing socks of two different colours, chosen unpredictably each day. Catch sight of one foot and find the sock pink, and you know at once — instantly, at any distance — that the other sock is not pink.13 No one is tempted to call this spooky: the socks had their colours all along, and you merely learned about a correlation established in advance, in the laundry. The whole content of Bell's theorem is that the quantum correlations are demonstrably not of Bertlmann's-socks type. If the particles carried their answers from the source the way the socks carry their colours, the inequality would hold. It does not hold. Whatever explains the correlations, it is not a list of properties packed at the source and carried passively outward.

§06 — CHSH and the Tsirelson bound

An inequality the laboratory can actually use

Bell's 1964 inequality is a theorem, not a protocol. It assumes perfect anticorrelation at identical settings — outcomes that are exactly opposite, every time — and no real apparatus delivers that. Detectors miss particles, polarizers leak, beams wander; the idealized equality A(a, λ) = −B(a, λ) that let Bell collapse two functions into one is precisely what an experiment cannot guarantee. To drag the theorem into the laboratory one needs an inequality that assumes none of this. In 1969 John Clauser, Michael Horne, Abner Shimony, and Richard Holt supplied it.14 Their reformulation — universally abbreviated CHSH — uses two settings on each side and makes no reference to perfect correlations, perfect efficiency, or any other idealization. It is the inequality that essentially every real Bell test has measured.

The construction assembles four correlations from Alice's two possible settings, a and a′, and Bob's two, b and b′, into a single quantity:

CHSH 1969S  =  E(a, b) − E(a, b′) + E(a′, b) + E(a′, b′)

| S |  ≤  2    (every local hidden-variable theory)
Each outcome is ±1, so each of the four correlations lies in [−1, +1]. For predetermined local values the four cannot be independently extremized: fixing the ± assignments shows the combination is bounded by 2.

The bound of 2 is, once again, pure arithmetic: if every outcome is a predetermined ±1 depending only on the local setting and the shared λ, the four correlations are algebraically chained, and their particular combination can never exceed 2 in magnitude. Now insert the quantum prediction E(a, b) = −cos θab and choose the settings shrewdly. Take Alice's analyzers at 0° and 90° and Bob's at 45° and 135°. Three of the four correlations equal −cos 45° = −0.707 and one equals −cos 135° = +0.707; the combination evaluates to −2√2 ≈ −2.828. Its magnitude, 2√2, exceeds the local bound of 2 by a wide and experimentally comfortable margin. The gap between 2 and 2.828 is the entire empirical content of nonlocality, rendered as a number a detector can chase.

That number, 2√2, is not an accident of the chosen angles. In 1980 Boris Tsirelson proved that it is the ceiling: for any quantum state, with any observables whatsoever, the CHSH quantity cannot exceed 2√2.15 Quantum mechanics violates the local bound, but only so far and no farther. This is the Tsirelson bound, and experiments find nature pressed right against it — saturating the quantum maximum, neither falling short of it nor, as we will see, exceeding it. The comprehensive theoretical and experimental status of the whole program was consolidated by Clauser and Shimony in an influential 1978 review that set the agenda for the tests to follow.16

A subtler question now surfaces, and it has occupied foundational physics ever since. The algebraic maximum of the CHSH quantity is 4 — that is what you get if all four correlations reach their extreme values with the right signs. Quantum mechanics stops at 2√2, well short of 4. One might hope that relativity supplies the missing constraint: surely the prohibition on faster-than-light signaling is what holds nonlocality in check. It does not. In 1994 Sandu Popescu and Daniel Rohrlich exhibited a hypothetical device — now called a PR box — that respects the no-signaling principle perfectly, leaking no usable information from one wing to the other, yet achieves the full algebraic value S = 4.17 Such a box is more nonlocal than anything quantum mechanics allows, and still it never lets Alice send Bob a message. Relativity, then, is not what caps nonlocality at the Tsirelson bound; no-signaling alone would tolerate a far wilder world.

So why does nature stop where it does? The search for a physical principle that singles out 2√2 — that explains why the universe is exactly this nonlocal and no more — has become a program of its own. One of its most striking results is the principle of information causality, advanced by Marcin Pawłowski and collaborators in 2009: the idea that the information one party can gain about another's distant data cannot exceed the number of classical bits actually transmitted.18 Ordinary no-signaling is the special case of zero transmitted bits; information causality is strictly stronger. Remarkably, it is satisfied by every correlation up to S = 2√2 and violated by every stronger one — the principle reproduces the Tsirelson bound from an information-theoretic axiom rather than from the quantum formalism. It is not the last word; other candidate principles exist, and none has been shown to pin down the full structure of quantum correlations uniquely. But the very fact that the question is live tells us something about how strange Bell's discovery was. We are no longer asking whether the world is nonlocal. We are asking why it is nonlocal to precisely the degree it is.

§07 — From thought experiment to photons

Clauser, Aspect, and the first verdicts

For all its conceptual force, Bell's theorem sat for eight years as a result about an experiment nobody had done. Translating it into hardware meant finding a real source of entangled pairs, real analyzers, and real detectors, and accepting the compromises each imposed. The first to manage it were John Clauser and Stuart Freedman, who in 1972 at Berkeley measured the polarization correlations of photon pairs emitted in an atomic cascade of calcium.19 An excited calcium atom dropping through two successive levels emits two photons whose polarizations are entangled; Freedman and Clauser sent them to oriented polarizers and counted coincidences as they varied the angle. The data violated a Bell-type inequality — recast by Freedman into a form suited to the apparatus — by approximately six standard deviations. It was the first experimental sign that nature takes Bell's quantum side against Einstein's, and it was promptly underappreciated.

Part of the reason was that the early experiments leaned on auxiliary assumptions, and the theorists knew it. Clauser and Michael Horne addressed this in 1974 with a more carefully hedged inequality — the CH74 — built to rest on a weaker and more defensible premise about how undetected events behave, rather than on the optimistic supposition that detected pairs fairly represent all pairs.20 The CH74 framework would become the template for honest accounting in Bell tests: state your assumptions about the unseen events explicitly, and design the inequality so that a local-realist adversary cannot hide in them. The history of the field from this point forward is largely the history of removing such assumptions one by one.

The experiments that fixed Bell tests in the physics imagination were performed by Alain Aspect and his collaborators at Orsay between 1981 and 1982. The first refined the source and detection enough to produce a clean, high-statistics violation.21 The second introduced two-channel polarizers — analyzers with two output ports, so that every photon yields a genuine binary ±1 result rather than a detected-or-not asymmetry — and delivered the canonical number of the era: a CHSH value of S = 2.697 ± 0.015, violating the local bound of 2 by more than forty standard deviations and agreeing with the quantum prediction to within experimental error.22 For a generation of physicists this was the result that settled the matter: local hidden-variable theories were not a live option.

The third Orsay experiment was the most conceptually ambitious, because it went after locality directly.23 In every earlier test the analyzer orientations were fixed while the data were taken, which leaves open an uncomfortable possibility: if the setting on Alice's side is established long before a photon arrives, some slow influence could in principle have informed Bob's particle of it. Aspect, Jean Dalibard, and Gérard Roger broke this by switching the analyzer orientations while the photons were in flight. Acousto-optic deflectors redirected each photon toward one of two differently oriented polarizers, toggling every ten nanoseconds or so — faster than the roughly forty nanoseconds light needs to cross the thirteen metres between the two stations. The choice of what to measure was thus made too late for any light-speed signal to carry it to the far particle before that particle was measured. The violation survived. It was not a fully airtight closure of the locality loophole — the switching was periodic rather than truly random, a gap later critics would press — but it was the first time the timing of the experiment, not merely its statistics, was turned against Einstein's picture.

§08 — The loopholes

How a determined local realist could still escape

A Bell violation refutes local realism only if the experiment genuinely meets the theorem's premises, and real experiments meet them imperfectly. Each gap between the idealized assumptions and the actual apparatus is a loophole — a logical aperture through which a sufficiently stubborn local hidden-variable theory could still slip and reproduce the data. Mapping these apertures, and then sealing them, occupied experimentalists for three decades after Aspect. Two loopholes dominated the worry.

The first is the locality or communication loophole: if the choice of setting on one side, and the registration of the outcome, are not space-like separated from the events on the other side, then nothing in principle forbids a sub-luminal influence from coordinating the two wings. Closing it demands that the settings be selected by fast, unpredictable processes and that the whole measurement — choice and detection — be completed on each side before any light-speed signal could arrive from the other. The second is the detection or fair-sampling loophole, and it is more insidious. Real detectors register only a fraction of the pairs emitted. If one assumes that the detected sample faithfully represents the whole, the inequalities are violated; but that assumption is exactly what a clever local model can attack, arranging for the hidden variable to influence which pairs get detected so that the lucky subsample shows correlations the full ensemble does not.

The detection loophole has a precise threshold, and a crucial insight about it came from Philippe Eberhard in 1993. The naive expectation was that defeating the loophole required catching better than about 83% of all pairs — a punishing demand for photons. Eberhard showed that the threshold is not fixed: by using non-maximally entangled states, the required detection efficiency could be pushed down to two-thirds.24 Counterintuitively, a less perfectly entangled pair tolerates more inefficient detection. This result reshaped the experimental strategy for photonic tests, and the optimized non-maximal states it recommended would prove decisive two decades later. The full taxonomy of loopholes — locality, detection, freedom-of-choice, coincidence-time, memory, and several more exotic relatives — was eventually catalogued in a thorough review by Jan-Åke Larsson in 2014, by which point the list had become a checklist that any claim of a definitive test was expected to satisfy.25

Through the late 1990s and early 2000s the two principal loopholes were closed — but separately, in different systems, never at once. In 1998 Gregor Weihs and collaborators in Anton Zeilinger's Innsbruck group closed the locality loophole rigorously.26 They placed their two stations 400 metres apart, used genuinely fast random-number generators to set the analyzers, and timed everything so that each setting choice and detection lay outside the light cone of the other — a strict spatial enforcement of locality, with random rather than periodic switching. The CHSH violation held at thirty standard deviations. Three years later, in 2001, David Wineland's group at NIST closed the other door.27 Using two trapped beryllium ions and reading out their internal states with essentially perfect efficiency, they performed a Bell test in which every pair was detected — no fair-sampling assumption required. But the ions sat micrometres apart, so the locality loophole was wide open. The two experiments were mirror images: each sealed exactly the loophole the other left exposed. A local realist could concede both and still, with a straight face, maintain that no single experiment had closed everything at once — that the universe might exploit detection inefficiency precisely when the measurements were space-like separated, and spooky coordination precisely when detection was complete.

§09 — Loophole-free, and cosmic

2015 and the closing of the last doors

The holdout position — that nature might exploit whichever loophole a given experiment leaves open — was finally extinguished in 2015, when three groups, by three different routes, closed the locality and detection loopholes in a single experiment. The first to report was a team led by Ronald Hanson in Delft, and its design was the most elegant.28 Rather than send fragile entangled particles across the lab, they entangled the electron spins of two nitrogen-vacancy centres in separate diamonds, 1.3 kilometres apart, by an event-ready scheme: each spin first emitted a photon, the two photons were brought together and jointly measured, and a successful joint detection heralded — announced in advance of the spin measurements — that the distant spins were now entangled. Because the spins themselves were read out with near-perfect efficiency, the detection loophole was closed; because 1.3 kilometres bought ample time for space-like separation, the locality loophole was closed too. The price was a punishing data rate: a few hundred trials over weeks. The result, S = 2.42 ± 0.20, violated the local bound with a p-value of about 0.039 — modest significance, but for the first time genuinely loophole-free.

Within months two photonic experiments, exploiting precisely the high-efficiency superconducting detectors and Eberhard-optimized non-maximal states that the theory had been pointing toward, drove the significance to crushing levels. A Vienna-led collaboration reported a violation with a p-value near 10−31; a NIST-led group reported one around 10−7.2930 Where Hensen's experiment closed the loopholes with heroic difficulty and thin statistics, these closed them with overwhelming numbers. Together the three made the conclusion inescapable, and Alain Aspect — who had waited more than thirty years for exactly this — marked the moment in a commentary whose title needed no elaboration: the door on the Einstein–Bohr debate had been closed.31

The picture of the world Einstein fought to preserve was not merely unconfirmed. It was now closed off by experiment, with every escape route sealed.

— the verdict of 2015

One loophole remained, and it is the strangest of the three. The freedom-of-choice loophole asks whether the experimenters' setting choices are themselves truly independent of the hidden variables. If some common cause in the past influenced both the particle properties at the source and the "random" choices of how to measure them, the correlations could be reproduced by a local model — a position known as superdeterminism. No experiment can refute superdeterminism outright; the most one can do is push the required conspiracy further and further back in time, making it ever more contrived. The cosmic Bell tests were built to do exactly that. In 2017 Johannes Handsteiner and colleagues let the colours of photons arriving from two Milky Way stars, emitted some six hundred years ago, determine the analyzer settings — so any conspiracy would have had to be arranged centuries before the experiment was conceived.32 In 2018 the same group pushed the determination out to quasars billions of light-years away, with light emitted roughly eight and twelve billion years ago, demanding that any local-realist coordination be set in place when the universe was a fraction of its present age.33 The same year, a complementary project took the opposite tack: the BIG Bell Test recruited some one hundred thousand people worldwide to generate unpredictable bits by playing an online game, supplying human free choice as the source of randomness across thirteen laboratories on a single day in late 2016.34 Cosmic history and human caprice converged on the same conclusion the photons had already announced.

In 2022 the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics to John Clauser, Alain Aspect, and Anton Zeilinger for the experimental work that turned Bell's theorem from a philosophical curiosity into established physics and laid the foundation of quantum information science.35 The recognition was, in a sense, the institutional acknowledgement of what the experiments had shown: the question Einstein posed in 1935 had received a definitive empirical answer, and it was not the answer he wanted.

§10 — Beyond inequalities

All-or-nothing contradictions, and the fall of nonlocal realism

Bell inequalities are statistical instruments. They are violated only on average, over many runs, and a single measurement can never by itself convict a local theory. In the late 1980s Daniel Greenberger, Michael Horne, Abner Shimony, and Anton Zeilinger found a way to sharpen the contradiction into something absolute — a clash between local realism and quantum mechanics visible, in principle, in a single experimental run.36 The trick is to use three particles rather than two, prepared in what is now called a GHZ state. For suitable choices of spin component on each particle, quantum mechanics predicts a set of perfectly correlated outcomes with certainty — no probabilities, no averaging.

The contradiction is then a matter of elementary algebra. Suppose each particle carries predetermined values, ±1, for both of the components it might be asked about. Quantum mechanics fixes four of the joint products with certainty:

GHZ / MerminX₁ Y₂ Y₃ = +1    Y₁ X₂ Y₃ = +1    Y₁ Y₂ X₃ = +1    X₁ X₂ X₃ = −1
Multiply the first three: each Y appears twice, so Yᵢ² = 1, leaving X₁X₂X₃ = (+1)(+1)(+1) = +1. Quantum mechanics demands −1. No assignment of predetermined ±1 values can satisfy all four.

Take the product of the first three relations. Each Y value appears exactly twice, and since each is ±1 its square is 1; the Y's cancel completely, leaving X₁X₂X₃ = +1. But the fourth relation, equally a prediction of quantum mechanics, says X₁X₂X₃ = −1. A local-realist assignment of definite values is not merely improbable here; it is logically impossible, contradicted with certainty by a single suitable run. The GHZ argument turns Bell's statistical tension into a flat algebraic impossibility, and it has been confirmed in the laboratory with three-photon states.

A two-particle version of the same inequality-free spirit was found by Lucien Hardy in 1993.37 Using a cleverly chosen non-maximally entangled state, Hardy identified a specific chain of measurement outcomes that local realism declares possible but quantum mechanics forbids — and showed that for a definite fraction of runs the forbidden event nonetheless occurs. The effect appears in only a minority of trials, but where it appears it is unconditional, requiring no inequality at all. David Mermin called it the simplest and most penetrating version of Bell's theorem; it is nonlocality exhibited as a logical contradiction rather than a statistical bias.

All of this still targets local realism. A natural retreat for the realist is to keep definite properties and abandon only locality — to suppose that each particle really does carry a definite polarization, while permitting some nonlocal influence to coordinate the pair. Could such a nonlocal-realist theory escape every constraint? In 2003 Anthony Leggett showed that it could not.38 He constructed a broad class of models in which the measured particles possess definite polarizations — realism preserved — but nonlocal signals are allowed to act, and he derived an inequality that any theory in this class must satisfy, distinct from Bell's. Four years later Simon Gröblacher and collaborators tested it.39 The Leggett inequality was violated, and the quantum predictions confirmed, excluding the entire family of nonlocal-realist models Leggett had described. The lesson is severe: it is not enough to give up locality while clinging to the idea that measurements merely reveal pre-existing definite values. A wide and natural class of such pictures — definite polarizations plus nonlocal influence — is itself refuted by experiment. Whatever survives Bell, it cannot be the comfortable view that the photon "really had" a polarization all along and the apparatus simply read it off.

§11 — What exactly died?

Locality, realism, or the freedom to choose

Experiment has established that the conjunction of Bell's premises is false. Logic alone cannot tell us which premise to surrender — only that at least one must go. This is where the genuine controversy lives, and it is a controversy about interpretation, not about data. The premises at stake are three: locality, the absence of any superluminal causal influence; realism, here meaning that measurements reveal pre-existing definite values; and measurement independence or free choice, the assumption that the settings can be selected independently of the hidden variables. A Bell violation forces the abandonment of one or more of these, and different schools abandon different ones.

The version that reached the popular imagination is that realism died — that quantum mechanics proves there is no definite reality until it is observed. David Mermin gave this reading its enduring slogan in 1985, asking whether the moon is there when nobody looks, and using Bell's result to dramatize the suggestion that unobserved properties may simply not exist.40 On this telling the lesson of Bell is metaphysical modesty about the reality of unmeasured quantities, and locality is preserved at the cost of realism.

Bell himself rejected this framing, firmly and repeatedly, and his own analysis points the other way.41 The decisive observation is that EPR and Bell form a two-step argument, and realism is not an independent premise of it but a conclusion of the first step. Tim Maudlin has reconstructed the logic with particular clarity.42 Step one is EPR: given the perfect correlations of the singlet, locality alone forces the existence of predetermined values — if Alice can predict Bob's outcome without disturbing it, and no influence travels between them, the outcome must have been fixed in advance. Realism, in other words, is derived from locality plus the quantum correlations, not assumed alongside it. Step two is Bell's theorem: those predetermined values are mathematically impossible, because they would force the inequality that experiment violates. Run the two steps together and the structure is a clean reductio: locality implies predetermined values; predetermined values are impossible; therefore locality is false. On this reading there is no separate "realism assumption" left to jettison — it was only ever a consequence of locality — and what the experiments refute is locality itself. Maudlin presses the point to its conclusion: quantum mechanics involves genuine nonlocality, a real dependence of distant outcomes on local choices, even though that dependence can never be used to send a signal.

The dispute is partly terminological, and recognizing this dissolves some of the heat. Travis Norsen has argued that the "realism" so often nominated for sacrifice is rarely given a precise definition, and that once one is careful, the honest conclusion of the EPR–Bell argument is nonlocality rather than anti-realism.43 Howard Wiseman and Eric Cavalcanti have gone further, untangling what they identify as two genuinely distinct theorems that the literature persistently conflates: an 1964-style theorem that assumes the EPR/determinism step and concludes nonlocality, and a later operational theorem that assumes a single combined premise of "local causality" and concludes only that this combined premise fails.44 Much of the apparent disagreement about "what Bell proved" turns out to be disagreement about which of these theorems one has in mind.

And yet the choice is not purely verbal, because different interpretations of quantum mechanics make it differently and mean it. A pilot-wave theorist keeps definite values and accepts forthright nonlocality. A many-worlds theorist denies that measurements have single definite outcomes at all, and thereby claims to preserve strict locality — there is nothing nonlocal to explain if Alice's measurement merely branches her, with no fact about which outcome Bob will "find" until the branches meet. A superdeterminist keeps both locality and realism and surrenders measurement independence, paying for it with the cosmic conspiracies the Bell tests have made so baroque. Each of these is a coherent response; none is forced. What is forced — the irreducible content of the experiments — is that the intuitive classical package of separable systems carrying definite local properties, choosable and measurable independently, cannot survive intact. Some cherished piece of it must be given up. The fights are over which piece, and the answer one gives is, in the end, a choice of which strangeness one finds easier to live with.

§12 — Peaceful coexistence

Why relativity survives a nonlocal world

If distant outcomes are genuinely correlated in a way no local mechanism can explain, the immediate fear is that relativity has been broken — that entanglement is a hidden telegraph capable of sending messages faster than light. It is not, and the reason is a theorem as important as Bell's own. Quantum mechanics permits the nonlocal correlations and simultaneously forbids their use for communication. These two facts are both true, and holding them together is the central conceptual achievement of the subject.

The prohibition on signaling is precise. GianCarlo Ghirardi, Alberto Rimini, and Tullio Weber established in 1980 what is now called the no-signaling theorem: in any quantum experiment on an entangled pair, the statistics Alice observes on her side are completely independent of what Bob chooses to do on his — independent of his setting, and indeed of whether he measures at all.45 Formally, Alice's local description — her reduced density operator — is untouched by any operation Bob performs. Whatever Bob does, Alice sees the same local randomness: a featureless sequence of plus and minus results with no discernible pattern. Bob cannot modulate that sequence to encode a bit, because he cannot influence her marginal statistics at all. The correlation between their records is real, but it is invisible from either side alone. It materializes only when the two records are physically brought together and compared — and that comparison requires carrying one record to the other through an ordinary, light-speed channel.

Abner Shimony captured the resulting settlement in a phrase that has stuck: a peaceful coexistence between quantum mechanics and relativity.46 The peace rests on a distinction he drew sharply. There is a kind of nonlocality that would violate relativity — controllable nonlocality, in which an experimenter could steer a distant outcome at will and thereby signal. Quantum mechanics does not exhibit it. And there is a kind that it does exhibit — an uncontrollable nonlocality, a correlation that genuinely links the wings but that neither party can command, because neither can govern their own local result. Shimony sometimes called this passion-at-a-distance, as opposed to action-at-a-distance: something happens, the outcomes are bound together, but no one acts to make a chosen thing happen far away. Relativity prohibits only the controllable variety, and that variety is precisely what the no-signaling theorem rules out.

It would be a mistake to read this peace as cheap or automatic. The no-signaling constraint is, in fact, weaker than the full structure of quantum mechanics — and the PR box is the proof. A Popescu–Rohrlich box, recall, saturates the algebraic limit of nonlocal correlation while still forbidding any signal; it respects no-signaling completely and yet is far more nonlocal than quantum theory permits.17 No-signaling, in other words, does not by itself force the Tsirelson bound; a world could in principle be more nonlocal than ours and still keep relativity's letter. That quantum mechanics sits below the no-signaling ceiling, exactly at 2√2, is a structural feature requiring its own explanation — the very puzzle that information causality and its rivals attempt to resolve. The coexistence of nonlocality and relativity is therefore real but genuinely subtle: relativity is not merely tolerated by accident, yet neither is it the thing that fixes how nonlocal the world is allowed to be.

What remains uneasy in the peace is the question of order. The correlated outcomes invite the question of which measurement happened first — which one "established" the joint result that the other then conformed to. Relativity denies that this question has a frame-independent answer: for space-like separated measurements, different inertial observers disagree about their time order, and none is privileged. The correlation is a fact all observers agree on; the story of which event brought it about is not. Most physicists conclude that there is simply no such story to tell — that the correlation is a joint property of the pair rather than the result of one outcome propagating to the other. The bookkeeping works perfectly; no contradiction with relativity can be exhibited. But the absence of any consistent account of how the correlation comes to be, in a world without absolute simultaneity, is exactly the residue of strangeness that keeps the foundations of the subject alive.

§13 — From paradox to resource

Nonlocality put to work

The deepest reversal in this story is not conceptual but practical. The nonlocal correlations that Einstein offered as a reason to reject quantum mechanics turned out, half a century later, to be a resource — something one can spend, certify, and build technologies upon. A Bell violation is no longer only a verdict against local realism; it is an operational guarantee. Because the correlations cannot be reproduced by any local mechanism, their presence certifies that a system holds something genuinely quantum that no classical adversary can have forged. An entire field grew from taking that guarantee seriously.

The pivot was Artur Ekert's 1991 protocol for quantum key distribution.47 Two parties share a stream of entangled pairs and divide them to two purposes: some pairs are measured to test a Bell inequality, the rest to build a shared secret key. The security argument is Bell's theorem turned into cryptography. If an eavesdropper had intercepted the particles, or if the correlations were secretly predetermined — the local-realist scenario — then the measured pairs would fail to violate the inequality. A genuine violation certifies that the key shares the very property local realism lacks: it cannot have been copied or pre-set by anyone outside. The impossibility that Bell proved becomes the guarantee that the channel is private.

Two years later came a use of entanglement that reads almost like the science fiction the phrase "spooky action" always invited. Charles Bennett and collaborators showed in 1993 that an unknown quantum state can be transferred from one location to another by consuming a shared entangled pair and sending two classical bits — quantum teleportation.48 The state vanishes at the source and reappears at the destination, never traversing the space between. Crucially, nothing here outruns light: the two classical bits are indispensable, and they travel by ordinary means, so the protocol respects relativity exactly while exploiting nonlocality fully. The entanglement is the fuel; the classical message is the ignition. Teleportation made vivid that entanglement is a quantifiable commodity — created, distributed, and used up like any other resource.

The most radical development took the certifying power of a Bell violation to its logical extreme. In ordinary cryptography one must trust that the devices do what their manufacturer claims. Device-independent protocols dispense with that trust entirely. Antonio Acín and collaborators showed in 2007 that quantum key distribution can be made secure on the strength of an observed Bell violation alone, with no assumptions whatever about the internal workings of the apparatus — even if the boxes were built by the adversary.49 The logic is airtight in a way that ordinary security is not: a true violation of the inequality is, by the theorem, impossible to fake with any local mechanism, so the statistics themselves — not the engineering — certify security. The same principle yields certified randomness. Stefano Pironio and collaborators demonstrated in 2010 that a Bell violation can guarantee that a string of bits is genuinely random, not merely apparently so, again without trusting the device that produced them.50 Since a violation rules out predetermined values, the outcomes must carry irreducible unpredictability; randomness becomes a quantity one can certify by experiment rather than assume. Random numbers guaranteed by the failure of local realism are now generated in working laboratories.

The arc is worth pausing on. EPR was a reductio: surely, Einstein reasoned, a theory implying such correlations must be incomplete, because the correlations themselves are intolerable. Bell made the reductio testable; the experiments made it fail; and then the very feature that was supposed to be intolerable became the basis of cryptography, communication, and certified randomness. The strangeness was not a defect to be explained away. It was a previously unimagined kind of physical resource, and recognizing it as such reorganized a substantial part of physics and computer science around it. What Einstein took as grounds to doubt the theory, the next generation took as something to plug in and use.

§14 — The persistence of unease

What it means to compute an answer you cannot picture

The matter is, as physics, closed. The inequalities are violated; the loopholes are sealed; the framework is textbook material, surveyed in comprehensive reviews and taught to graduate students as settled fact.5 The 2022 Nobel Prize ratified the experimental program as foundational rather than fringe.35 By every operational measure, the question Einstein raised in 1935 has been answered, and the answer is that the world is not locally real. And yet the unease the result provokes has not dissipated, and it is worth being clear that this is not a failure of understanding that further study would remedy. It is the appropriate response to what was found.

The peculiar character of the situation is that we understand the phenomenon completely in one sense and not at all in another. We can write down the state, compute the correlations to as many decimal places as anyone cares to measure, predict every experimental result in advance, and engineer the effect into working cryptographic hardware. What we cannot do is fit any of it into a picture of the world as a collection of separate things, each carrying its own properties, interacting only through their immediate surroundings. That picture — call it separability — is the one our intuitions were built for, refined over a lifetime of experience at the classical middle scale, and it is precisely the picture the experiments exclude. We are left able to calculate the answer and unable to visualize the world that produces it.

John Bell, who did more than anyone to bring the question to this point, never made peace with the orthodox complacency about it.41 He regarded the existence of the pilot-wave theory as proof that the bafflement was not mandatory — that one could, at the price of explicit nonlocality, have a clear picture of what happens — and he thought the usual refusal to ask such questions was an evasion dressed up as sophistication. His insistence was always that physics should say what the world is like, not merely what the instruments will read. The discomfort he kept alive is the discomfort of taking that demand seriously in the face of a result that frustrates it.

The spookiness was never in any action crossing the gap. It is in the correlation itself — in a world that refuses to come apart into separate pieces.

— on the phrase that named the problem

The phrase Einstein chose to dismiss the theory has, with time, become a reasonably good name for what the theory describes — provided one reads it carefully. There is no action at a distance, in the literal sense: nothing crosses the gap, no signal is sent, no outcome on one side reaches out to disturb the other. Relativity is untouched and causal order, in the operational sense that matters for sending messages, survives. What does not survive is the assumption underneath the worry — that the two particles were ever really two separate systems with their own independent states. The entangled pair is, in a sense our language is poorly equipped to express, a single indivisible thing spread across space, and the correlations are simply what that single thing looks like when you interrogate its two ends. The spookiness lives in the holism, not in any hotline between the halves.

Einstein lost the argument and asked the right question. He was wrong that the correlations betray an incomplete theory; the theory is, as far as anyone can tell, exactly complete enough. But the question he was really pressing — what must the world be like, for these correlations to be facts? — was the right question, and it remains open in the only sense that now matters. We can compute the answer to arbitrary precision. We have not learned how to picture it. After ninety years, the gap between those two achievements is the residue of the quarrel, and there is no present sign that it will close.
