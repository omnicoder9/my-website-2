The Long Game: How Culture Becomes the Most Powerful Engine of Vegan Change

Films, philosophy, language, and identity are quietly rewriting what billions of people consider normal — and the science shows this is no accident.

Every great social transformation arrives in two phases. First comes argument — the pamphlets, the protests, the evidence marshaled in formal debate. Then comes something quieter and more durable: the slow revision of what a society considers obvious. Slavery was once economic common sense. Smoking was once medicine. Industrial animal agriculture is, right now, in the middle of its own uncomfortable reckoning — not primarily in legislatures or laboratories, but in living rooms, movie theaters, restaurant menus, Oscar acceptance speeches, and the ancient texts of the world's oldest philosophical traditions. This is the cultural front of vegan activism, and the science increasingly suggests it may be the most powerful front of all.

Unlike policy reform or product innovation, which operate within existing systems of incentive and constraint, cultural change rewires the systems themselves. It shifts the invisible architecture of what people assume, value, and feel comfortable doing without thinking. This article surveys the major vectors of that shift: documentary film and narrative media, celebrity advocacy and public discourse, the science of linguistic framing, the psychology of identity and social norms, and the ancient religious traditions that are finding unexpected resonance with contemporary veganism. The picture that emerges is one of a movement that understands, even if implicitly, what strategists have long known: to change behavior at scale, you must first change what people believe is normal.

Section 01  /  Screen

The Documentary as Catalyst

There is a particular power in the feature-length documentary: it holds a viewer captive for ninety minutes, constructing an emotional and evidentiary arc that is difficult to dismiss or scroll past. The vegan movement has exploited this medium with unusual strategic sophistication, producing a series of films that have collectively reached tens of millions of viewers and, in measurable ways, shifted how people search, think, and eat.

The landmark study in this space, published in Nature Food in 2025 by Thomas, Hope, and Mathur, analyzed the relationship between the popularity of six major plant-based documentaries and actual shifts in search behavior and food purchasing across the United States.1 The results were striking in their specificity. What the Health, The Game Changers, and You Are What You Eat: A Twin Experiment each generated significant spikes in plant-based dietary searches — not just general curiosity about veganism, but active behavioral searches such as "easy vegetarian recipes" and "best plant-based restaurants near me." In the U.S., popularity for What the Health, The Game Changers, and You Are What You Eat was associated with behavioral plant-based search increases of 43%, 11%, and 11% respectively.1

Case Study 01

Cowspiracy (2014) — The Film That Made Environmentalists Uncomfortable

Produced by Leonardo DiCaprio and directed by Kip Andersen,9 Cowspiracy did something few environmental documentaries had dared: it placed animal agriculture — not fossil fuels — at the center of the ecological crisis. The film exposed the silence of major environmental organizations on livestock's contribution to greenhouse gas emissions, deforestation, and water depletion.

Its cultural impact is empirically documented. A 2020 preliminary study published in Frontiers in Communication showed participants either Cowspiracy or an episode of Planet Earth as a control.11 Among participants who watched Cowspiracy, both attitude toward meat reduction and intention to eat less meat increased significantly, while neither measure changed in the Planet Earth group. A global survey by vegan dating platform Veggly found that more than one in five respondents chose Cowspiracy as the most effective documentary to show new vegans — ranking it first overall.9

Case Study 02

The Game Changers (2018–2019) — Rewriting the Protein Myth for Men

Perhaps no documentary in the modern vegan canon was more strategically targeted than The Game Changers. Produced by James Cameron and Arnold Schwarzenegger and directed by Academy Award-winner Louie Psihoyos,10 it was designed with a specific psychological obstacle in mind: the deep cultural association between meat and masculinity.

The film has been credited with influencing viewers to shift their dietary habits toward more plant-based options — an impact described in popular commentary as the "Game Changers effect."10 Actor Dolph Lundgren and the CEO of Greggs each cited the documentary as the reason they adopted plant-based eating.10 The film became the best-selling documentary of all time on iTunes within a week of release and has been recognized by the American College of Lifestyle Medicine, which offers continuing medical education credits for watching it.10

The film attracted criticism from nutrition scientists for cherry-picking data and prioritizing emotional impact over scientific rigor — a genuine tension within activist documentary-making.10 Yet its cultural function, researchers argue, may be separable from its scientific precision. Its aim was to demolish a psychological barrier, and by that measure, it reached an audience no peer-reviewed paper ever could.

The broader research literature offers an important qualification: while documentary popularity was associated with significant increases in dietary search behavior, analysis of national food sales data did not reveal a clear corresponding effect on aggregate meat or plant-based product purchases.1 This gap between intent and sustained behavior change is consistent with what social scientists call the "attitude-behavior gap" — a persistent finding in environmental and dietary psychology. Cultural tools like documentaries appear to be extraordinarily effective at generating awareness and initial motivation; converting that motivation into durable habit change requires additional reinforcement. The implication for activists is not that films are ineffective, but that they are entry points, not endpoints.

Section 02  /  Public Figures

Celebrity Advocacy: Visibility, Normalization, and the Limits of the Pedestal

The role of celebrity in social movements has always been double-edged. A famous face can project a marginal idea into the mainstream overnight; it can also reduce that idea to lifestyle accessory, divorcing it from the ethical foundations that give it force. The vegan movement's engagement with celebrity culture is a study in both possibilities.

Research on the direct persuasive power of celebrity endorsement in dietary change is sobering. A 2020 experiment that showed participants Instagram posts from celebrities (Ben Affleck and Elliot Page) versus regular influencers found no difference in attitudes toward veganism or likelihood of dietary change — the person behind the post did not seem to matter.13 In a 2022 study identifying why people reduced animal product consumption, celebrity endorsements ranked 16th out of 16 tactics — dead last.13

But this picture, as researchers note, is incomplete. Celebrities like Billie Eilish and Joaquin Phoenix have achieved concrete institutional wins by convincing venues like stadiums and award shows to adopt plant-based catering — changes that affect hundreds of thousands of people regardless of their dietary philosophy.13 The mechanism here is structural rather than inspirational: instead of persuading individuals, celebrity leverage shifts the default choices available to everyone.

"We go into the natural world and we plunder it for its resources. We feel entitled to artificially inseminate a cow and when she gives birth, we steal her baby, even though her cries of anguish are unmistakeable."
— Joaquin Phoenix, 92nd Academy Awards acceptance speech, February 202012

Joaquin Phoenix's 2020 Oscar speech represents perhaps the most analytically rich celebrity intervention in recent vegan history. A peer-reviewed study published in Celebrity Studies analyzed the critical reception of the speech across 37 U.S. and U.K. publications.12 The authors found that Phoenix's advocacy complicated dominant norms around both celebrity and masculinity — his star persona as a "deformist" figure who dismantles conventions of manhood made his veganism harder to dismiss as feminine or peripheral. The research also highlighted a broader cultural phenomenon: the emergence of the "hegan" identity — an ultra-masculine vegan, often an elite athlete, whose plant-based choices are framed in terms of strength, performance, and sexual prowess rather than moral obligation.12

The cultural politics of celebrity veganism also extend to questions of inclusion. Scholarship on Beyoncé's public engagement with plant-based eating acknowledges significant tensions but also identifies genuine value: the endorsement of plant-based eating by a prominent Black public figure has the potential to open these conversations to people who might have previously felt excluded or alienated from a movement perceived as predominantly white and middle-class.14

The emerging scholarly consensus on celebrity's most productive function aligns with what researchers call "complex social contagion" theory — the idea that diet change requires multiple exposures from multiple sources before most people act.13 Celebrities spread ideas through weak ties, while actual behavior change tends to come through the strong ties of family, friends, and close community. Celebrity thus functions best as a cultural amplifier that makes veganism visible and imaginable across a wider population — softening the ground for the deeper community-level change that actually converts.

Section 03  /  Semiotics

The Word Is the World: Language Shifts and the Architecture of Choice

Among the most practically consequential findings in vegan advocacy research is also the most surprising: the words used to describe plant-based food have a measurable, sometimes dramatic effect on whether people choose it. The debate over "vegan" versus "plant-based" versus sensory-experiential descriptors is not semantic vanity — it is strategy backed by field experiments with hundreds of thousands of real-world consumer decisions.

The "Plant-Based" Rebranding: What the Data Actually Shows

The conventional wisdom in vegan marketing for roughly a decade has held that the word "vegan" carries stigma — it signals moral judgment, identity politics, and deprivation — while "plant-based" is neutral, approachable, and health-adjacent. The Good Food Institute, among others, has recommended that food brands and restaurants adopt "plant-based" as their preferred descriptor, citing consumer research showing greater purchasing intent.4 Studies using "plant-based" and "plant protein" as category descriptors found both more appealing to mainstream consumers than alternatives like "vegan," "meat-free," or "meatless."4

Yet a major field experiment published in the Journal of Environmental Psychology in 2022 complicates this picture considerably. Tracking 167,637 real consumer decisions at a restaurant over ten weeks, researchers randomly varied whether menu items were described as "vegetarian" and "vegan" versus "plant-based."5 Items marketed as vegetarian or vegan were 24% more likely to sell than when the same items were marketed as plant-based — a finding that inverts the dominant assumption.5

The reconciliation of these seemingly contradictory findings is found in context and audience. The restaurant field experiment likely attracted a self-selected population already open to vegetarian options, for whom the clarity of "vegan" or "vegetarian" was a feature, not a barrier.5 In mass-market product contexts targeting skeptical mainstream consumers, "plant-based" may still outperform in purchasing intent.8 The strategic lesson is not that one label dominates, but that language must be calibrated to audience and context — a more sophisticated prescription than any single-term solution.

The Sensory Turn: Describing Pleasure, Not Ideology

Across multiple research programs, the most consistent finding is not about the vegan/plant-based distinction but about a deeper principle: language that invokes the pleasure of eating systematically outperforms language that describes nutritional, ethical, or environmental attributes.

76%
The Power of Evocative Naming

Research cited in the Proceedings of the Nutrition Society found that attractive, sensory-rich descriptions of plant-based dishes — such as "Cumberland Spiced Veggie Sausages and Mash" — increased sales by up to 76% compared to plain descriptions of the same items. Appealing dish names also increased the amount of food taken per plate by nearly 44% in additional studies.7

Research published in the Proceedings of the Nutrition Society found that while vegans think about plant-based foods in terms of enjoyable eating experiences, omnivores think about them in terms of health, vegan identity, and other abstract information that does not motivate consumption in the moment.6 This cognitive asymmetry has direct implications for communication strategy: activist-facing language prioritizes ethics and environment, but the language most likely to move mainstream behavior must anchor itself in sensory anticipation — texture, warmth, flavor, satisfaction.

Further, a study published in Appetite found that figurative rather than literal language in environmental messaging about plant-based foods was more effective — allowing people to visualize the impact of their choices in ways that motivated behavior change.3 The implication extends beyond menu design into all forms of advocacy communication: story and metaphor move people in ways that statistics alone cannot.

A Globe Scan report found that while 68 percent of people worldwide express interest in consuming more plant-based foods, only 20 percent do so regularly — down from 23 percent in 2023.8 That gap between stated aspiration and actual behavior is precisely the territory that language architecture is designed to close. The question is not whether someone believes plant-based eating is good; it is whether, in the moment of decision at a restaurant counter or grocery aisle, the option feels appetizing, familiar, and culturally available.

Section 04  /  Social Psychology

Identity, Norms, and the Politics of Normal

The deepest mechanism of cultural change is normative: the gradual reclassification of behaviors from deviant to acceptable to unremarkable. For veganism, this means transforming the act of not eating animals from an eccentricity into an unremarkable choice available to ordinary people. The social psychology of this transformation is now a substantial research field, and its findings have direct implications for how advocates should frame their work.

The Norm Intervention: Promise and Complexity

Social norms research distinguishes between two types of influence: descriptive norms (what people actually do) and injunctive norms (what people think one ought to do). For meat consumption, a landmark study found that while people in high-meat-consuming countries (U.S., U.K., Australia) viewed meat consumption as a strong descriptive norm, they did not view it as a clear injunctive norm — meaning eating meat was perceived as common, but not necessarily morally required.15 This is a significant gap that advocates can exploit: the behavior is entrenched through perceived ubiquity, not through deep conviction that it is right.

Norm messaging that highlights the growing number of people eating plant-based can shift this perception. In one café study, customers who saw information about a growing number of Americans trying to eat less meat were twice as likely to order a vegetarian lunch compared to a control group.16,17 These "dynamic norms" — emphasizing that behavior is actively changing — appear more effective than static descriptive norms in motivating dietary change.17

Identity Research

Research published in the Journal of Social Psychology (2022) found that vegans with a strong, centralized vegan identity — where dietary choice is core to their self-concept — are significantly more resilient to social pressure and less likely to experience dietary lapses, even in difficult social contexts.24

Social Contagion

A longitudinal study of Dutch university students found that dietary behavior is socially contagious within close friendship networks — students' animal product consumption converged with that of their friends over time, suggesting that normalized plant-based eating among peer groups propagates further without deliberate advocacy.25

Children's Workshops

A study of 155 school children who participated in vegan cooking workshops found that shared identity, pro-vegan norms, attitudes, and dietary intentions all increased significantly after the workshop — demonstrating that brief, community-based experiences can shift normative frameworks even in young people.23

National Identity Risk

People who strongly identified with their (high-meat-consuming) national identity showed lower intentions to eat vegetarian meals — suggesting that veganism's reframing away from "un-American" or culturally alien is as important as positive messaging about plant-based eating itself.15

"Eating meat is still perceived as the norm — and recommended in most national dietary guidelines. To change behavior at scale, you must change what is considered obvious."
— Papies et al., Proceedings of the Nutrition Society, 2023

Memes, Storytelling, and the Grammar of Belonging

Digital culture has added a new register to the vocabulary of normative change: the meme. Often dismissed as trivial, the viral image-text format is, in structural terms, one of the most effective norm-signaling tools ever developed. It circulates within networks, accumulates cultural weight through repetition, and — crucially — signals in-group membership. For veganism, the meme ecosystem has evolved from the preachy and moralistic toward humor, irony, and the gentle ridicule of outdated assumptions about diet. Whether satirizing the "but where do you get your protein?" conversation or rendering industrial farming in the aesthetic register of horror, memes compress complex arguments into emotionally potent single frames.

More formally, narrative framing research has established that stories built around character and transformation are more persuasive than argument-centric advocacy. Research on restaurant diners found that health information was most effective when conveyed through gain-framed messages with benefit-based descriptions, whereas environmental information worked better through loss-framed messages with attribute-based descriptions — suggesting that the optimal narrative frame depends on what aspect of plant-based eating is being emphasized.2 The insight is not that one story fits all, but that advocates should match their narrative architecture to their specific argument.

Section 05  /  Tradition

Ancient Roots: Religious and Philosophical Integration

Among the most underexplored vectors of cultural change in vegan advocacy is one of the oldest: the integration of plant-based ethics into established religious and philosophical traditions. This is not a new argument manufactured for contemporary audiences — it is the excavation of a genuine and ancient convergence between the ethics of non-violence, the sacredness of life, and the daily practice of what one eats.

The Ahimsa Traditions: Jainism, Buddhism, and Hinduism

The principle of ahimsa — non-violence or non-harming toward all sentient beings — is the closest thing to a shared ethical foundation across the major traditions of the Indian subcontinent, and its resonance with contemporary veganism is neither coincidence nor co-optation. It is convergence.

In Jainism, vegetarianism is mandatory for all adherents; in Hinduism and Mahayana Buddhism, it is promoted by scriptures and religious authorities though not universally required.18 Many Hindus traditionally follow a vegetarian diet, particularly in India, where an estimated 40% of the population is vegetarian, often due to religious beliefs about reincarnation and the sacredness of all life.19 A growing number of contemporary Jains, influenced by scholars examining Jain sutras through the lens of modern animal agriculture, now adopt fully plant-based diets as the most philosophically coherent expression of ahimsa — acknowledging that dairy farming involves the same harms that vegetarianism was meant to avoid.21

Case Study 03

Gandhi's Ahimsa and the Political Theology of Food

Mahatma Gandhi — whose influence on both Indian independence and global nonviolent movements is incalculable — was among the most prominent modern advocates for the connection between non-violence and diet. In My Experiments with Truth, Gandhi vehemently criticized the violence and suffering meted out to animals, emphasizing vegetarianism and abstinence from milk as expressions of ahimsa.20

Gandhi's framing is instructive for contemporary advocacy because it embedded dietary ethics within a larger spiritual and political project. Food was not a private lifestyle choice but a public moral commitment — a daily practice of the values one claimed to hold. This integration of diet into a broader philosophical framework of non-violence, simplicity, and care for the suffering of others offers a template for how plant-based advocacy might be embedded in value systems that already command deep loyalty, rather than competing with them as an alien ideology.

Buddhism brought a related emphasis on compassion and the reduction of suffering. The Lankavatara Sutra, one of the core Mahayana texts, contains explicit arguments against meat consumption on grounds of compassion.21 The Dalai Lama has spoken about the benefits of vegetarian diet, emphasizing compassion toward all sentient beings, though Buddhist dietary practice varies considerably by tradition and geography.22

Abrahamic Traditions: The Stewardship Argument

The Abrahamic faiths — Christianity, Judaism, and Islam — present a more complex and contested relationship with plant-based ethics, but one with significant untapped potential. The dominant historical interpretation has placed humans as legitimate stewards and consumers of animals, a reading supported by scriptural passages across all three traditions. But a growing body of theological scholarship and community practice argues that the stewardship mandate itself, rightly understood, points toward minimizing unnecessary harm to creation — a reading congenial to veganism even if not identical with it.

Some Christians interpret Genesis 1:29 — describing a plant-based diet as God's original intention for humanity — as theological support for veganism. Christian organizations like the Christian Vegetarian Association actively promote plant-based living on Biblical grounds.22 Within Judaism, the concept of tza'ar ba'alei chaim — compassion toward animals — has been interpreted by some communities as requiring engagement with the conditions of industrial animal agriculture.22 Within Islam, the values of mercy and compassion encoded in the tradition are increasingly cited by a growing number of Muslims as reasons to examine and reduce animal product consumption.19

The strategic value of religious integration is not primarily doctrinal but social. Religious communities are among the most durable and socially dense institutions in human life. They provide the repeated exposure, peer reinforcement, and identity embedding that research consistently identifies as necessary for sustained behavior change. When a religious community adopts plant-based eating as an expression of its own deepest values — rather than as an imposition from outside — it creates the conditions for the kind of complex social contagion that produces lasting norm change.

Section 06  /  Synthesis

The Architecture of Slow Change

What unites the documentary that reaches forty million households, the Oscar speech that trends globally, the menu description that increases plant-based sales by 76%, and the Jain scholar who reinterprets ancient texts through the lens of modern animal agriculture? Each is an intervention into the same underlying target: the invisible consensus about what is normal, natural, and acceptable. Each is operating on a different timescale and through different mechanisms, but all are engaged in the same fundamental project of cultural renovation.

The research literature on dietary behavior change is consistent on one fundamental point that has strategic implications for the vegan movement: the three core motivations for food choice are taste, cost, and convenience — and a food option must first meet perceived needs for tastiness, affordability, and ease before most consumers will incorporate their higher values into the decision.4 Cultural tools are most effective when they work with this grain rather than against it — making plant-based food feel desirable and ordinary rather than challenging and exceptional.

This does not mean abandoning ethical arguments. It means sequencing them. The documentary creates awareness and opens emotional engagement. The sensory menu description and the celebrity's visible enjoyment make plant-based food feel appetizing and normal. The norm intervention signals that "everyone is doing it." The identity framing allows people to incorporate reduced animal consumption into their self-concept without threatening their other values. The religious integration gives the change moral depth and community reinforcement. Together, these tools form a multi-layer system that addresses the full arc of how humans actually change — not through single rational decisions but through accumulated exposure, social permission, and the slow renegotiation of identity.

68%
The Aspiration Gap

Of people worldwide who express interest in consuming more plant-based foods, only 20% do so regularly. The 48-point gap between aspiration and action is the territory that cultural change strategy exists to close — not through argument, but through the patient rewriting of defaults, norms, and identities.8

The great caution in cultural strategy is the time horizon. Norm change is deep but slow — it operates across years and decades, not news cycles. Advocates who measure success only in immediate behavioral outcomes will consistently underestimate the value of what they are building. The films that shift search behavior but not purchasing patterns today are laying the cognitive groundwork for the purchasing patterns of five years from now. The religious communities that begin discussing ahimsa in relation to dairy farming in 2025 are creating the normative environment in which the next generation's dietary defaults will be formed.

The history of every successful social movement offers the same lesson: the visible victories — the laws passed, the products launched, the numbers who identified as vegan in any given survey year — are the trailing edge of a cultural transformation that began decades earlier in language, story, art, and the patient redefinition of what a good person eats.

The Verdict

Cultural and narrative change is the slowest lever in vegan advocacy — and the most powerful. It operates beneath the threshold of conscious persuasion, rewriting the assumptions people bring to every meal, every restaurant menu, every conversation about food. Films shift what millions consider worth questioning. Language shapes whether a plant-based option feels appetizing or ascetic. Celebrities make veganism visible in contexts — elite sports, awards ceremonies, blockbuster documentaries — that reach audiences impervious to formal advocacy. Religious and philosophical integration roots the ethics of non-violence in communities that have practiced related values for millennia. And social norm science shows that once plant-based eating becomes common enough to feel unremarkable, it generates its own momentum through the simple human instinct to do what those around us do. This is the long game. It is the only game that permanently wins.
