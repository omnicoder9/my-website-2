The iron triangle of the air

Every wireless device ever built obeys the same unforgiving arithmetic. You can move data quickly, you can move it far, or you can move it on almost no power — but you cannot have all three at once. A phone streaming 4K video drains its battery in hours and reaches a tower a few kilometres away. A soil-moisture probe buried in a field talks to a gateway fifteen kilometres distant and runs for a decade on a coin cell, but it sends only a dribble of bytes a day. The distance between those two devices is not a matter of engineering polish. It is a matter of physics, and no amount of cleverness repeals it.

This is the central tension of machine-to-machine communication, and it explains why the Internet of Things did not converge on a single triumphant protocol the way the web converged on HTTP. Instead it fractured into a bestiary of incompatible radios — Wi-Fi, Bluetooth Low Energy, Zigbee, Z-Wave, Thread, LoRaWAN, Sigfox, LTE-M, NB-IoT, 5G — each of which is the right answer to a different corner of the same problem. To understand IoT connectivity is to understand why that fragmentation is not a failure of standardisation but a direct consequence of the trade-offs, and how a competent engineer navigates them.

The three constraints in tension are throughput (how many bits per second the link carries), range (how far the signal usefully travels), and energy (how much power the radio burns to do it). They are bound together by information theory and by the way electromagnetic energy spreads through space. Shannon's capacity theorem fixes the maximum error-free bit rate of a channel as a function of its bandwidth and its signal-to-noise ratio; the Friis equation describes how received power falls with the square of distance. Push on any one corner of the triangle and the other two push back. Want more range at fixed power? Slow the data rate down and spend more time per bit, so the receiver can integrate enough energy to dig the signal out of the noise. Want more throughput? Widen the channel and raise the transmit power, and watch the battery evaporate. Want to sip power? Then you must transmit rarely, briefly, and slowly.

The fragmentation of IoT radios is not a failure of standardisation. It is the trade-off space made visible.

The technologies in this article are best understood not as competitors but as coordinates in that space. Short-range standards such as Wi-Fi 6 and Bluetooth trade range for enormous throughput and low latency. Low-power wide-area networks (LPWANs) such as LoRaWAN and Sigfox trade throughput for staggering range and battery life measured in years 1. Cellular IoT — LTE-M, NB-IoT, and increasingly 5G — buys ubiquitous coverage and seamless mobility at the price of licensed spectrum and network complexity. There is no free lunch, only a menu, and the engineer's job is to read it correctly.

By some estimates the number of connected IoT devices has already passed the tens of billions and continues to climb, with cellular IoT connections alone numbered in the billions and forecast to keep growing through the decade 2. That scale is precisely why the trade-offs matter: a wrong radio choice, multiplied across a million field devices, becomes a wrong decision no firmware update can undo. This article maps the terrain corner by corner — the history that produced it, the physics that governs it, the protocols that inhabit it, the code that drives them, the attacks that exploit them, and the framework a practitioner uses to choose.

How we got here

The phrase "Internet of Things" has a specific author and a specific year. Kevin Ashton, then working on supply-chain problems at Procter & Gamble, coined it in 1999 as the title of a presentation about putting radio-frequency identification (RFID) tags on products so that computers could track physical goods without human data entry. Ashton later recalled that he chose the phrase to link the then-fashionable "Internet" to the unglamorous world of tagged pallets, arguing that if computers could sense the physical world for themselves, they could count and track everything, cutting waste and cost dramatically 3. The idea predated the name — networked vending machines and industrial telemetry go back decades — but Ashton gave the field its banner.

For most of the 2000s the research conversation was dominated by wireless sensor networks (WSNs): dense clusters of low-cost nodes cooperating to monitor an environment, routing data multi-hop back to a sink. The academic literature of this period is preoccupied with energy-efficient routing, medium-access scheduling, and the harsh reality that battery-powered radios spend most of their energy simply listening. The IEEE 802.15.4 standard, first published in 2003 and revised repeatedly since, gave this world a common physical and link layer: a low-rate, low-power radio operating chiefly in the 2.4 GHz band with a raw rate of 250 kbit/s and carrier-sense multiple access 4. On top of 802.15.4 the industry stacked application frameworks, most prominently Zigbee, whose first full specification arrived in 2006 and which brought mesh networking to home automation, lighting, and building control.

The first great survey papers crystallised the field. Atzori, Iera and Morabito's 2010 review gave the phrase "Internet of Things" its canonical academic framing, cataloguing the visions, enabling technologies, and open problems that would define a decade of research 5. Gubbi and colleagues followed in 2013 with a cloud-centric architecture that anticipated how sensor data would flow to elastic back-end compute 6. By 2015 Al-Fuqaha and co-authors could write a comprehensive taxonomy of enabling technologies and application protocols, by which point the outlines of the modern stack were visible 7.

Then came the two developments that reshaped the map. First, around 2010, a cluster of French start-ups attacked a problem the short-range world had ignored: how to reach a battery device many kilometres away for a decade on unlicensed spectrum. Sigfox, founded in 2010, built an ultra-narrowband network and a business model to match. Cycleo, a Grenoble company, invented a chirp-based modulation it called LoRa; Semtech acquired Cycleo in 2012 and the LoRa Alliance formed in 2015 to standardise the network layer above it. These low-power wide-area networks opened a corner of the trade-off space that had been effectively empty 8.

Second, the cellular industry noticed. The 3rd Generation Partnership Project (3GPP), the body that standardises mobile networks, completed a set of machine-type communication features in its Release 13 in 2016, introducing LTE-M (formally LTE-MTC, category M1) and Narrowband IoT (NB-IoT) alongside enhancements to GSM for IoT 9. For the first time, licensed-spectrum operators had radios purpose-built for cheap, low-power, deep-coverage sensors rather than smartphones. The arrival of 5G, whose New Radio (NR) specification landed in 3GPP Release 15, then promised to fold the extremes of the IoT world — ultra-reliable low-latency control and massive machine-type density — into a single framework 10.

Why no single winner?

The web could standardise on HTTP because every browser and server shared roughly the same constraints: mains power, a fast link, a screen. IoT devices share almost nothing. A door sensor, a pallet tag, an insulin pump, a parking meter, and an autonomous forklift have wildly different power budgets, data rates, latency tolerances, and mobility needs. A protocol optimal for one is disastrous for another. Fragmentation is the rational response to genuinely heterogeneous requirements.

The result is the layered, plural ecosystem we have today: a physical-and-link layer chosen from a dozen radios according to the range/power/throughput trade-off, and an application layer chosen from a smaller set of messaging protocols according to how the data must be delivered. The rest of this article takes those two layers in turn, but first it helps to have a single picture of the whole landscape.

Reading the landscape

The masthead figure above plots the major protocol families along a single axis: how far their signal reaches, from the ten-metre intimacy of Bluetooth to the tens-of-kilometres reach of LoRaWAN and NB-IoT. Range alone, though, tells only one dimension of the story. To choose a radio you need to see range and throughput together, because the two are inversely coupled, and to understand why, you need to see how the whole field distributes itself across that plane.

Figure 1 arranges the technologies on logarithmic axes of range and data rate, with bubble size encoding typical transmit current. The eye is drawn to a diagonal frontier running from the bottom-right (short range, high rate: Wi-Fi) to the top-left (long range, low rate: Sigfox and NB-IoT). The upper-right quadrant — high throughput and long range — is conspicuously empty. No dot lives there because no device can. Reaching far while sending fast would demand transmit power and channel bandwidth that no battery and no unlicensed band can supply. That empty quadrant is the iron triangle rendered as a picture.

The second axis that governs radio choice is spectrum: which slice of the electromagnetic spectrum a technology uses, and whether that slice is licensed or free to use. Spectrum determines propagation (lower frequencies travel farther and penetrate walls better), available bandwidth (higher frequencies have more room for fast data), and regulatory constraints (unlicensed bands impose duty-cycle and power limits to keep them shared and civil).

Figure 2 lays out these bands. The sub-gigahertz industrial, scientific and medical (ISM) allocations near 868 MHz in Europe and 915 MHz in North America are the natural home of LPWAN: lower frequencies mean lower path loss and better wall penetration, buying range for free. The 2.4 GHz band is a crowded commons shared by Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, Zigbee and microwave ovens, offering more bandwidth but more interference and shorter reach. The 5 and 6 GHz bands, opened to Wi-Fi 6E, trade range for spectrum: gigahertz of clean bandwidth for multi-gigabit throughput, but signals that struggle to cross a single wall. Cellular technologies operate in licensed bands the operator has paid for, which is precisely what guarantees their quality of service.

Sitting above the radio is the protocol stack — the layered set of agreements that turn a modulated waveform into an addressable, deliverable message. It is worth separating two ideas that casual usage conflates: connectivity technologies, which live at the physical and link layers and answer "how do bits cross the air?", and communication protocols, which live at the transport and application layers and answer "how is a meaningful message delivered end to end?"

Figure 3 makes the separation concrete. At the bottom sit the radios — Wi-Fi's OFDM physical layer, 802.15.4's offset-QPSK, LoRa's chirp modulation, cellular's NR waveform. Above them sit medium-access-control (MAC) layers that arbitrate who talks when. Above those, an adaptation and network layer handles addressing and, in constrained networks, the compression that lets full IPv6 packets ride over tiny 802.15.4 frames — the job of 6LoWPAN, defined in RFC 6282 11. Only at the top do the messaging protocols live: MQTT, CoAP, and their kin, which we treat in depth later. The power of the layered model is exactly this mix-and-match freedom: the same MQTT application can run over Wi-Fi in a building or over LTE-M in a vehicle without changing a line of application code.

The final structural choice is topology: how devices, infrastructure, and the wider network are wired together logically. Three patterns dominate IoT, and each aligns with particular radios.

Figure 4 contrasts them. The star is the simplest: every device is one hop from an access point or base station. Wi-Fi, cellular, and Sigfox are stars. Stars are easy to reason about and cheap on device energy — a device need only reach the hub — but the hub is a single point of failure and coverage is bounded by one radio's reach. The mesh distributes routing: each node relays packets for its neighbours, so the network can cover a large area with short hops and heal itself when a node dies, rerouting around the break. Zigbee, Thread, and Bluetooth mesh work this way. The price is complexity and energy: relay nodes must stay awake to forward, and routing state must be maintained. LoRaWAN uses a distinctive star-of-stars: end devices simply broadcast, every gateway that hears a packet forwards it to a central network server, and the server discards duplicates. This pushes all intelligence into the network and keeps the end device breathtakingly simple — it need not even know which gateway, if any, received it 8.

With these four lenses — range-versus-rate, spectrum, layered stack, and topology — the individual technologies stop looking like an arbitrary alphabet soup and start looking like considered points in a design space. We now walk the space corner by corner.

Short range, high bandwidth: the near field

The near-field corner of the trade-off space is defined by abundance. Where power is available or range is short, an engineer can spend freely on throughput and responsiveness. This is the domain of Wi-Fi and Bluetooth on one hand, and the low-rate mesh standards of the smart home — Zigbee, Z-Wave, Thread — on the other.

Wi-Fi 6 and 6E: engineering for density

Wi-Fi's relevance to IoT is easy to underrate because Wi-Fi is so familiar, but the sixth generation — IEEE 802.11ax, certified as Wi-Fi 6 in 2019 — was explicitly re-engineered for environments packed with devices, which is exactly what a smart building is. Its headline mechanism is orthogonal frequency-division multiple access (OFDMA), borrowed from cellular. Earlier Wi-Fi handed the entire channel to one station at a time; if a smart plug needed to send two bytes, it seized the whole 20 MHz channel to do it, and everyone else waited.

Figure 5 shows why OFDMA matters. The 802.11ax channel is subdivided into resource units (RUs), and the access point can assign different RUs to different stations within a single transmit opportunity, so a dozen sensors can be served at once instead of in a slow queue 12. For IoT traffic — many devices each sending small, infrequent packets — this is transformative, cutting the airtime overhead that dominates small transfers. Alongside OFDMA, 802.11ax brought 1024-QAM for higher peak rates, BSS colouring to reduce interference between overlapping networks, and, crucially for battery devices, Target Wake Time (TWT), which lets an access point and a station negotiate scheduled wake windows so the station can keep its radio asleep the rest of the time 13. TWT is the feature that makes battery-powered Wi-Fi sensors plausible, narrowing Wi-Fi's traditional power disadvantage. Wi-Fi 6E extends all of this into the newly opened 6 GHz band, roughly 1200 MHz of additional, uncongested spectrum, mandating WPA3 security in the process 14. The trade-off is unchanged, however: even Wi-Fi 6 remains a mains-or-large-battery technology reaching tens of metres, not a coin-cell technology reaching kilometres.

Bluetooth Low Energy: the personal-area workhorse

Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE), introduced with Bluetooth 4.0 in 2010, is arguably the most widely deployed IoT radio on Earth, sitting in every fitness band, wireless earbud, beacon, and medical wearable. BLE is a deliberate reinvention of classic Bluetooth for devices that must run for months on a tiny battery. It achieves this by keeping the radio off almost all the time and waking only briefly to advertise its presence or exchange a small burst of data, structured through the Generic Attribute Profile (GATT) as a hierarchy of services and characteristics 15. A heart-rate monitor exposes a heart-rate service; a client reads or subscribes to its characteristics. This attribute model is simple, discoverable, and cheap.

Classic Bluetooth (BR/EDR) still has a role where a sustained higher-rate stream is needed — wireless audio being the obvious case — but BLE dominates sensor and control applications. Later revisions added coded PHYs for longer range, higher throughput modes, direction finding, and Bluetooth mesh, which layers a many-to-many flooding mesh over BLE radios for building-scale lighting and sensor networks 16. BLE's niche is the short-range, low-power, moderate-data personal-area link, and it fills that niche so completely that competitors rarely bother to contest it.

The smart-home mesh: Zigbee, Z-Wave, Thread, and Matter

The home-automation world grew up around low-rate mesh radios distinct from both Wi-Fi and BLE. Zigbee, built on IEEE 802.15.4, offers 250 kbit/s in the 2.4 GHz band, forms self-organising meshes, and has long powered smart bulbs, sensors, and hubs; its specification and trademark are stewarded by the Connectivity Standards Alliance 4. Z-Wave took a different tack, operating in sub-gigahertz bands (around 908 MHz in North America) to gain range and avoid 2.4 GHz congestion, supporting up to 232 devices in a mesh with AES-128 encryption, at the cost of lower data rates and a historically more closed, single-silicon-vendor ecosystem.

The most consequential recent development is the rise of IP-native meshes. Thread, introduced in 2014, runs IPv6 over 6LoWPAN over 802.15.4, producing a self-healing mesh with no single hub and no protocol translation gateway — every Thread device is an IP endpoint 17. Thread solves the networking layer but deliberately says nothing about application semantics. That gap is filled by Matter, launched in November 2022 by the Connectivity Standards Alliance (the former Zigbee Alliance) after beginning life as Project Connected Home over IP. Matter is an application layer that runs over IPv6 on Thread, Wi-Fi, or Ethernet, uses BLE only for initial commissioning, and is backed by Apple, Google, Amazon, and Samsung with a royalty-free licence 18. Its ambition is to end the smart-home interoperability wars: a Matter device should work with any Matter controller regardless of brand. Whether it fully delivers on that promise is still being litigated in the market, but it represents the clearest attempt yet to impose an application-layer standard on the fragmented near field.

Thread solves the network layer; Matter solves the semantics. Together they are the near field's bid for the coherence the web took for granted.

The through-line of the near field is that power and range are cheap enough to spend on throughput, responsiveness, and rich mesh behaviour. The moment those resources become scarce — the moment a device must run for years or reach for kilometres — the design philosophy inverts completely. That inversion is the low-power wide-area network.

Long range, low power: the LPWAN inversion

Low-power wide-area networks answer a question the near field cannot: how do you talk to a device fifteen kilometres away, buried in a basement or a field, that must run for ten years on a battery you will never change? The answer is a wholesale inversion of priorities. Throughput, which the near field maximises, LPWAN sacrifices almost entirely. Range and battery life, which the near field treats as afterthoughts, LPWAN maximises to extremes. The result is a class of networks that can cover a city with a handful of gateways and support devices that transmit a few dozen bytes a few times an hour for a decade 1.

The physics that makes this possible is the trade of data rate for receiver sensitivity. A receiver can detect a weaker signal — and therefore one that has travelled farther — if it spends more time and processing on each bit. Slow the data rate down, spread each symbol over a longer duration, and the receiver can integrate enough energy to pull the signal out from beneath the noise floor. LPWAN takes this principle to its limit. There are two dominant approaches, and they represent almost opposite bets on how to spend the sensitivity budget.

LoRa and LoRaWAN: chirp spread spectrum

LoRa — the physical layer — uses chirp spread spectrum (CSS). Instead of encoding data by shifting a carrier's amplitude or phase at a fixed frequency, LoRa sweeps the carrier linearly across the whole channel bandwidth, producing a "chirp" like the rising whistle of a swept tone. Data is encoded in the starting frequency of each chirp: a symbol is a chirp that begins at a particular offset and wraps around when it hits the band edge 19.

Figure 6 shows the modulation directly, computed from the chirp equations rather than sketched. The key parameter is the spreading factor (SF), which ranges from 7 to 12 and sets how many chip periods make up one symbol: SF7 spreads a symbol over 2^7 = 128 chips, SF12 over 2^12 = 4096. Each step up in SF doubles the symbol duration, roughly halving the data rate but dramatically improving sensitivity and range, because the receiver integrates over twice as long. The nominal bit rate follows a compact relation,

Rb = SF · (BW / 2SF) bit/s (before coding),

so for a 125 kHz channel, SF7 yields on the order of several kbit/s while SF12 drops to a few hundred bit/s 20. The remarkable consequence is that a LoRa receiver can decode signals roughly 20 dB below the noise floor — a signal weaker than the background noise — which is what buys the kilometres, a property established in the earliest analyses of the technology 21. A forward error-correction code rate (CR) of 4/5 through 4/8 trades a little more rate for robustness. Because different spreading factors are quasi-orthogonal, a gateway can decode several simultaneous transmissions at different SFs, and an adaptive data rate (ADR) scheme lets the network dial each device to the lowest SF that still reaches a gateway, conserving both airtime and device energy.

LoRaWAN is the MAC and network layer standardised by the LoRa Alliance above the LoRa PHY. It defines the star-of-stars topology described earlier, three device classes trading downlink latency for power (Class A devices, the lowest power, open only two brief receive windows after each uplink; Class C devices listen continuously at higher power; Class B adds scheduled receive slots), and end-to-end AES-128 encryption using separate network and application session keys 22. Crucially, LoRaWAN operates in unlicensed sub-gigahertz ISM bands, which subjects it to regulatory duty-cycle limits — in the European 868 MHz band, a device may occupy the channel no more than 1 % of the time under ETSI EN 300 220 23. That single regulatory fact shapes the entire application space: LoRaWAN is for infrequent, small messages, not for anything resembling a stream.

Sigfox: the ultra-narrowband bet

Sigfox makes the opposite wager. Where LoRa spreads each symbol across a wide band, Sigfox squeezes it into an ultra-narrowband (UNB) channel just 100 Hz wide, using differential binary phase-shift keying on the uplink 24. An extremely narrow channel concentrates all the transmit energy into a tiny slice of spectrum, which raises the signal-to-noise ratio within that slice and yields link budgets on the order of 160 dB — comparable to LoRa's reach. The trade-off is severe and deliberate: a Sigfox device may send at most 140 messages per day, each carrying a 12-byte payload, with an 8-byte downlink; typical rates are on the order of 100 bit/s 24. This is enough for a daily meter reading, a periodic tank level, a lost-parcel alert — and nothing more.

Sigfox's second distinguishing choice was commercial rather than technical: it operated as a single global network operator rather than licensing its technology for anyone to deploy, which gave it reach but concentrated business risk. That model struggled; the company entered receivership in 2022 and its assets were acquired by the Singapore-based firm UnaBiz, which continues to operate the network. The technical lesson survives its corporate troubles: for the very lowest-throughput, lowest-cost, longest-life applications, ultra-narrowband is a coherent design point, and the comparison between LoRa and NB-IoT and Sigfox is one of the most-studied trade-offs in the LPWAN literature 25 26.

The duty-cycle tax

Unlicensed spectrum is a commons, and commons require rules to prevent a tragedy. Duty-cycle limits — the 1 % ceiling in the EU 868 MHz band, for instance — force every device to stay quiet most of the time so the band remains usable for everyone. This regulatory constraint, not merely the physics, is why LPWANs are structurally incapable of high throughput on unlicensed spectrum. It is also the strongest argument for licensed-band cellular IoT, where the operator's exclusive licence removes the duty-cycle tax entirely.

Both LoRaWAN and Sigfox live in unlicensed spectrum, which makes them cheap to deploy and free of carrier subscriptions but subject to duty-cycle limits and, in dense deployments, to interference they cannot control. The alternative is to pay for spectrum — and that is precisely the proposition of cellular IoT.

Cellular IoT: coverage, mobility, and the price of spectrum

Cellular IoT occupies a distinct corner of the trade-off space defined by three advantages the unlicensed technologies cannot match: near-ubiquitous existing coverage, seamless mobility as a device moves between cells, and the guaranteed quality of service that comes from licensed, interference-controlled spectrum. The price is complexity and cost: cellular modules are more expensive, and connectivity requires a carrier subscription. The 3GPP standardised two low-power variants in Release 13 (2016) that dominate the space, plus a 5G evolution now unfolding 9.

LTE-M and NB-IoT: two answers in one release

LTE-M (LTE-MTC, category M1) is the higher-capability option. It uses a 1.4 MHz channel — six LTE resource blocks — supports peak rates around 1 Mbit/s, and, critically, preserves mobility and voice support, so it can hand over between cells as a device moves and even carry VoLTE. This makes LTE-M the natural choice for asset trackers, wearables, and anything that moves or needs moderate throughput and lower latency, on the order of 10–20 ms 27. Its maximum coupling loss — the total path loss the link can tolerate and still function, a standard measure of coverage — is specified around 155.7 dB, a substantial improvement over ordinary LTE.

NB-IoT (category NB1) is the deeper, cheaper, more constrained option. It occupies just 180 kHz — a single LTE resource block, or a repurposed GSM channel — and can be deployed standalone, in-band within an LTE carrier, or in the guard band between carriers 28. It sacrifices mobility (no handover; a device re-attaches if it moves) and throughput (tens of kbit/s) to achieve extreme coverage and cost. Its maximum coupling loss reaches roughly 164 dB, some 20 dB beyond legacy GSM, which translates into penetration deep inside buildings and underground — the basements and utility vaults where water and gas meters live 27. NB-IoT is the technology of the static, deeply buried, ultra-low-cost sensor.

Both variants achieve their coverage through coverage enhancement techniques, chiefly the repetition of transmissions: sending the same data many times so the receiver can combine the copies and recover a message that any single transmission could not deliver. This is the cellular analogue of LoRa's spreading — spending time and redundancy to buy sensitivity — and it is why both LTE-M and NB-IoT can reach places ordinary LTE cannot.

Sleeping deeply: PSM and eDRX

The feature that makes cellular IoT competitive with LPWAN on battery life is aggressive sleep. Two mechanisms, introduced for machine-type communication, let a device power down its radio for extended periods while remaining registered on the network. Power Saving Mode (PSM) lets a device tell the network it is going to sleep and will be unreachable until it next initiates contact; the device shuts its radio almost entirely, drawing microamps, while the network holds its registration so it need not perform a costly re-attach on waking. Extended Discontinuous Reception (eDRX) stretches the interval between the moments a device wakes to check for incoming messages, trading downlink responsiveness for power, with idle-mode cycles that can extend to many minutes 27.

Figure 7 makes the arithmetic concrete with a simple but honest energy budget. A device that draws 200 mA for 1.2 seconds each hour to attach, transmit and receive, then falls back to a 3 µA sleep floor, averages only about 70 µA over the full hour once the charge is integrated and spread across the cycle. On a 5000 mAh cell that is on the order of eight years of life — before self-discharge and temperature are accounted for. The lesson the figure teaches is general: in a well-designed low-power device, battery life is dominated almost entirely by the sleep-state current and the duty cycle, not by how much power the radio burns while transmitting. Shaving the active burst matters far less than deepening the sleep.

5G: folding in the extremes

Where LTE-M and NB-IoT are the low-power corner of cellular, 5G aims to span the entire IoT space, from trivial sensors to real-time control. The International Telecommunication Union framed the 5G vision (IMT-2020) around three usage scenarios, and two of them are squarely about machines 10. Enhanced mobile broadband (eMBB) targets peak rates of 20 Gbit/s downlink for humans. Massive machine-type communication (mMTC) targets a connection density of one million devices per square kilometre — the dense-sensor future. And ultra-reliable low-latency communication (URLLC) targets the extraordinary combination of 1 ms latency at 99.999 % reliability, quantified by the ITU as delivering a small layer-2 packet within 1 ms with a failure probability no worse than one in a hundred thousand 29.

URLLC is the enabler for applications the older technologies cannot touch: motion control in factories, coordination between autonomous vehicles, remote operation of machinery, where a late packet is not an inconvenience but a hazard. Meeting a one-millisecond, five-nines target requires rethinking the radio frame, the scheduling, and the reliability mechanisms, work that began in 3GPP Release 15 and expanded substantially in Release 16. 5G also brings network slicing, the ability to carve a single physical network into logically isolated virtual networks each with its own guaranteed characteristics, so a URLLC slice for factory control can coexist with an mMTC slice for metering on the same infrastructure.

Recognising that full 5G NR is overkill for mid-tier IoT, 3GPP added Reduced Capability (RedCap, or NR-Light) in Release 17: a deliberately trimmed 5G device profile using up to 20 MHz of bandwidth in FR1, fewer antennas, and relaxed processing, delivering perhaps 85–150 Mbit/s downlink at far lower cost and power than full NR — a fit for industrial sensors, wearables, and video surveillance that need more than NB-IoT but less than a smartphone 30. A further-reduced eRedCap arrived in Release 18. RedCap is, in effect, 5G's acknowledgement that the middle of the IoT trade-off space needs its own answer, and that the extremes cannot swallow everything.

Cellular's coverage and mobility are only half of any real deployment, though. Once bits reach the network, they must be delivered to an application as meaningful, reliable messages — and that is the job of a wholly separate layer.

The application layer: getting the message across

The radios and link layers of the previous sections answer only the question of how bits cross the air. They say nothing about how an application on a device and an application in the cloud agree on the meaning, ordering, and reliability of the messages they exchange. That is the province of application-layer messaging protocols, and for constrained IoT the field narrows to a small, well-characterised set. The dominant division is architectural: publish/subscribe versus request/response.

MQTT: publish, subscribe, and the broker

MQTT is the most widely used IoT messaging protocol, and its origin story is instructive. It was created in 1999 by Andy Stanford-Clark of IBM and Arlen Nipper, then at Arcom, to monitor an oil pipeline over an expensive, unreliable satellite link — a context that forced the protocol to be frugal with bandwidth and tolerant of intermittent connectivity from the very first line 31. Those constraints produced a design that turned out to be ideal for IoT at large.

MQTT is built on a publish/subscribe model mediated by a central broker. Devices do not talk to each other directly; instead, a publisher sends a message tagged with a topic (a hierarchical string such as building/floor3/room12/temp) to the broker, and any client that has subscribed to that topic — or a wildcard pattern over it — receives the message. This decoupling is powerful: publishers and subscribers need not know of one another's existence, need not be online at the same time, and can be added or removed without reconfiguring anyone else. MQTT runs over TCP, which gives it reliable, ordered delivery, and layers three quality-of-service levels on top: QoS 0 delivers at most once (fire and forget), QoS 1 at least once (acknowledged, possibly duplicated), and QoS 2 exactly once (a four-way handshake) 32. IBM released MQTT royalty-free, and it was standardised by OASIS as version 3.1.1 in 2014 and substantially extended as version 5.0 in 2019, which added features such as message expiry, shared subscriptions, and richer error reporting; it is also published as ISO/IEC 20922 33.

CoAP: REST for the constrained

The Constrained Application Protocol (CoAP) takes the opposite architectural tack. Standardised by the IETF as RFC 7252 in 2014, CoAP brings the familiar request/response model of HTTP — GET, POST, PUT, DELETE on resources identified by URIs — to devices too constrained for HTTP's verbosity and TCP's overhead 34. Instead of TCP, CoAP runs over UDP, which eliminates connection setup and per-connection state, and it defines its own lightweight reliability layer: a message can be marked confirmable, prompting the receiver to return a short acknowledgement, or non-confirmable for fire-and-forget telemetry. CoAP messages are binary and compact, with a four-byte fixed header, a stark contrast to HTTP's kilobytes of text.

CoAP shines where a device should look like a little web server — where the natural interaction is "read this resource" or "actuate this endpoint" — and where the request/response idiom fits better than a message bus. It maps cleanly onto HTTP for gateways bridging to the web, supports observing resources for change notifications, and secures its traffic with Datagram TLS. Choosing a messaging protocol

MQTT and CoAP are the poles, but they are not the only options. AMQP, the Advanced Message Queuing Protocol standardised by OASIS, offers richer enterprise messaging semantics — queues, exchanges, transactions — at higher overhead, suiting back-end integration more than constrained devices. HTTP itself remains common where devices are unconstrained and web-native simplicity matters. DDS, the Data Distribution Service from the Object Management Group, targets real-time, high-reliability machine-to-machine data sharing in systems such as robotics and defence. Systematic comparisons weigh these protocols on message overhead, reliability, latency, and security, and the consistent finding is that there is no universally best choice: MQTT's broker-mediated pub/sub suits telemetry fan-out and unreliable links, while CoAP's brokerless request/response suits resource-constrained, RESTful interactions 35. The right selection follows from the traffic pattern, not from fashion.

Underpinning all of these is the reality that IoT endpoints are genuinely constrained machines, a fact the IETF took seriously enough to standardise a vocabulary for it. RFC 7228 classifies constrained nodes by memory: Class 0 devices, with well under 10 KiB of RAM, cannot secure a general Internet connection unaided and lean on gateways; Class 1, around 10 KiB, can run protocols such as CoAP but not the full web stack; Class 2, around 50 KiB, can do more but still benefit from lightweight design 36. These classes are a reminder that IoT protocol design is not premature optimisation — it is working within hard, physical memory and energy limits that a server engineer never confronts.

Security: the lesson of Mirai

On 20 September 2016 the security journalist Brian Krebs's website was hit by one of the largest distributed denial-of-service attacks then recorded, a flood exceeding 600 gigabits per second that briefly drove his site offline 37. Weeks later, on 21 October, a related attack struck Dyn, a major DNS provider, disrupting access to Twitter, Spotify, Netflix, GitHub, Reddit, PayPal, and much of the American internet for hours. The weapon in both cases was the same: a botnet called Mirai, assembled almost entirely from compromised IoT devices — home routers, IP cameras, and digital video recorders.

Mirai's method was humiliating in its simplicity. It scanned the internet for devices with open Telnet ports and tried to log in using a hard-coded table of roughly sixty default username and password pairs — admin/admin, root/12345, and the like — that manufacturers had shipped and users had never changed 38. Any device that answered was enrolled into the botnet and set scanning for more. At its peak the botnet commanded on the order of hundreds of thousands of devices. The authors, later identified as three young men who pleaded guilty, released the source code publicly in October 2016, which guaranteed a long tail of copycat variants that persists to this day 38. The United States government's computer-emergency authorities issued formal alerts on the threat, and it became the canonical case study in IoT insecurity 39.

Mirai did not break any cryptography. It logged in with the passwords the devices were shipped with.

The enduring lessons of Mirai structure how IoT security is now approached. The first is that default credentials are indefensible: devices must ship with unique per-device credentials or force a change on first use, and unauthenticated management interfaces must never face the open internet. The second is that the sheer number and long field life of IoT devices make them a uniquely dangerous attack surface — a vulnerability multiplied across millions of units that will never be patched becomes a standing weapon. The third is that security must be designed in at every layer, because the constrained nature of these devices makes bolt-on security impractical.

That layered approach maps onto the stack of Figure 3. At the link layer, the radio standards themselves provide encryption: Zigbee and Z-Wave use AES-128, LoRaWAN encrypts payloads end to end with separate network and application session keys so that neither the network operator nor an eavesdropper can read application data, and cellular inherits the robust SIM-based authentication and ciphering of the mobile network 22. At the transport and application layers, the messaging protocols secure their traffic with the same cryptography that protects the web: MQTT runs over TLS, and CoAP over Datagram TLS, providing confidentiality, integrity, and server (and optionally client) authentication 34. The constrained-device profiles of these protocols exist precisely so that this protection fits within the memory and energy budgets that RFC 7228 enumerates 36.

The harder problems are the ones cryptography alone cannot solve: securely provisioning identity onto millions of devices at manufacture, distributing and rotating keys over a decade-long field life, delivering firmware updates to devices that sleep 99 % of the time and may never be physically reachable again, and ensuring that a device compromised in year seven cannot be trivially weaponised. These are open engineering challenges, and they are why security now features as a first-class dimension in any serious survey of IoT protocols rather than an afterthought 7.

Projects, networks, and tools

The abstractions of the preceding sections are made real by a substantial ecosystem of open networks, infrastructure, and developer tools. The most influential community project is The Things Network (TTN), a global, collaborative LoRaWAN network founded on the premise that anyone can deploy a gateway and contribute coverage, and anyone can connect a device for free. TTN provides the network server, the deduplication, the key management, and a console for registering devices, and its documentation has become a de facto tutorial for the whole LoRaWAN ecosystem, including the modulation and data-rate relationships discussed earlier 20. A related, incentive-driven model appeared with networks that reward gateway operators for providing coverage, extending the crowd-sourced approach with economic incentives.

Building a LoRaWAN application means confronting the reality of tiny payloads. Because a device may send only a few bytes a few times an hour under duty-cycle limits, engineers hand-pack binary payloads rather than sending JSON, and the network server runs a payload formatter to decode them. The wider toolchain spans the layers. For MQTT, the Eclipse Mosquitto broker and its command-line clients (mosquitto_pub, mosquitto_sub) are the standard proving ground, and the Eclipse Paho libraries provide clients in every major language. For CoAP, the aiocoap Python library and the coap-client from libcoap serve the same role. For radio-level debugging, software-defined radios paired with tools such as GNU Radio let engineers observe LoRa chirps or 802.15.4 frames directly on the spectrum. For cellular IoT, module vendors expose AT-command interfaces and increasingly higher-level SDKs, while operators provide device-management platforms for provisioning and monitoring SIMs at fleet scale 40. The consistent theme is that each layer of the stack has matured its own instrumentation, and competence means fluency across several of them.

Standards bodies are themselves a resource. The IETF's RFCs define CoAP, 6LoWPAN, and constrained-node terminology in freely available detail 34; OASIS publishes the MQTT specifications 33; the IEEE defines the 802.11 and 802.15.4 radios 4; the 3GPP publishes the cellular specifications; and the ITU-R sets the IMT-2020 performance requirements that define what "5G" means 29. For an engineer, the ability to read a specification directly — to check the actual coupling loss figure, the actual duty-cycle limit, the actual QoS semantics — is the difference between folklore and knowledge.

Choosing a connectivity technology

Everything in this article converges on a single practical decision: given an application, which radio and which messaging protocol should carry it? The trade-off space is navigable with a disciplined sequence of questions, each of which eliminates part of the field.

How much data, how often? This is the first and most decisive question. If the answer is "a few dozen bytes a few times an hour," the entire near field is overkill and the choice is LPWAN or NB-IoT. If it is "megabits, continuously," only Wi-Fi, wired, or full cellular will do. Data volume and frequency, filtered through duty-cycle limits, often settle the matter before any other consideration.

How far, and through what? Range in free space is one thing; range through concrete, soil, or steel is another. A device in a basement or buried in a field needs the deep-penetration coverage of NB-IoT or the sensitivity of LoRa; a device in the same room as its hub can use BLE or Zigbee. Sub-gigahertz technologies penetrate better than 2.4 GHz, which penetrates better than 5/6 GHz.

On what power budget? A mains-powered device can use Wi-Fi freely. A device that must run for years on a coin cell is constrained to BLE, LPWAN, or a deeply sleeping NB-IoT node, and its battery life will be dominated by sleep-state current, as Figure 7 showed. A device recharged daily, like a wearable, sits in between.

Does it move, and must it roam? Mobility across coverage areas is a cellular strength: LTE-M hands over between cells; NB-IoT and the unlicensed technologies do not. An asset tracker on a truck wants LTE-M; a fixed meter does not care.

Licensed or unlicensed, and who operates it? Unlicensed LPWAN is cheap to deploy and free of subscriptions but subject to duty-cycle limits and uncontrolled interference, and it requires someone to own the gateways. Licensed cellular carries a per-device subscription but delivers guaranteed service and needs no private infrastructure. This is as much a business-model question as a technical one, and Sigfox's trajectory is a cautionary tale about how the operator model can dominate the outcome.

How reliable, how fast a response? Most IoT tolerates seconds of latency and occasional loss. A few applications — industrial motion control, vehicular coordination — require milliseconds and five-nines reliability, which today means 5G URLLC and nothing else 29. Matching the reliability requirement to the technology prevents both under- and over-engineering.

Convergence, and the shape of what is next

The fragmentation that opened this article is real and rooted in physics, but the field is not static, and two counter-currents are worth watching. The first is convergence at the application layer even amid divergence at the radio layer. Matter is the clearest instance: it accepts that Thread, Wi-Fi, and Ethernet will coexist as transports and instead standardises the layer above, so that interoperability is achieved not by winning the radio war but by rising above it 18. The layered model that Figure 3 depicts is precisely what makes this possible — an application need not care which radio carries it — and the industry is increasingly exploiting that freedom rather than fighting over the physical layer.

The second current is the filling-in of the trade-off space by new standards aimed at its previously empty regions. RedCap is 5G's move into the mid-tier that NB-IoT was too slow for and full NR too expensive for, and its further-reduced successor pushes the point lower still 30. Non-terrestrial networks — satellites integrated into the cellular and LoRaWAN ecosystems — promise to extend coverage to the oceans, deserts, and poles that no terrestrial gateway reaches, closing the last gaps in the range dimension. And research into ambient and backscatter communication, where devices harvest energy from ambient radio waves and communicate by reflecting them rather than generating their own signal, points toward battery-free sensors that would occupy a corner of the power dimension currently thought unreachable.

The iron triangle does not soften. But we keep discovering new ways to sit inside it.

None of this repeals the iron triangle. A satellite-connected sensor still trades throughput for its extraordinary range; a backscatter tag still sends only a trickle of data for its miraculous freedom from batteries; a RedCap device still sits at a defined point between NB-IoT and full 5G, buying its middling throughput with middling cost and power. What advances is not the physics but our ingenuity in populating the trade-off space, finding coherent, useful design points in regions once thought empty or forbidding. The empty upper-right quadrant of Figure 1 — long range and high throughput at once, on a battery — remains empty, and short of a revolution in physics it will stay that way.

For the practitioner, the enduring skill is not memorising the current alphabet of protocols, which will change, but internalising the trade-off space that generates them, which will not. Know where your application sits in the range–throughput–power volume, understand the spectrum and topology that follow from that position, choose the radio that matches, and layer the right messaging protocol on top. Do that, and the bestiary of incompatible standards resolves into what it always was: a set of rational, specialised answers to a single, beautifully constrained question. The Internet of Things did not fail to converge. It converged on the only thing it could — a division of labour dictated by the physics of talking through the air.
