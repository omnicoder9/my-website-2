01 Vehicle systems

Anatomy of a rolling network

In July 2015 two researchers sat in a borrowed kitchen while a journalist drove a Jeep Cherokee down a St. Louis highway. From ten miles away they cut the transmission, leaving the vehicle crawling in traffic with the accelerator dead. They had not touched the car. They had reached it over the cellular network, climbed from the entertainment head unit onto the vehicle's internal bus, and rewritten the firmware of a controller until it would relay their commands to the brakes, steering and engine.[1] The manufacturer recalled 1.4 million vehicles.[2]

That demonstration ended an old assumption — that a car's computers were too obscure, too fragmented, and too physically sealed to be worth attacking — and it framed the discipline that this reference is about. A modern vehicle is not a machine with some electronics bolted on. It is a distributed computer network that happens to have wheels, and in that network a security failure is not an inconvenience. It is a safety failure that travels at highway speed.

The scale is easy to underestimate. A contemporary car carries dozens of small computers — well over a hundred in a premium model — each a self-contained microcontroller running its own firmware and talking to its neighbours over shared digital buses.[3] They run the engine and transmission, the anti-lock brakes and stability control, the airbags, the door locks and windows, the instrument cluster, the climate system, the lighting, the parking sensors, the lane-keeping cameras, the cellular modem and the touchscreen. The instruction to unlock a door and the instruction to apply a brake travel, in many older designs, over the very same wires.

The electrical and electronic architecture

Engineers call the whole arrangement the E/E architecture — the electrical and electronic system — and standards now treat it as the unit of analysis for cybersecurity.[4] Its building blocks are the electronic control unit (ECU), the network segments that connect ECUs, and the gateways that route between segments. Different functions sit on different networks chosen for cost and capability:

  • CAN and CAN FD — the Controller Area Network, the durable two-wire workhorse that carries powertrain, chassis and body traffic. It is the bus most associated with vehicle hacking, and it is the subject of the next section.
  • LIN — a cheap, slow single-wire bus for undemanding body electronics such as window motors, mirrors and rain sensors.
  • FlexRay — a faster, deterministic bus historically used for safety-critical control such as active suspension and some brake-by-wire systems.
  • Automotive Ethernet — high-bandwidth links (100 Mbit/s to multi-gigabit) that carry camera, radar and ADAS data and increasingly form the vehicle's backbone.

How these segments are wired together has changed as cars have absorbed more software. The earliest designs were essentially flat: a handful of CAN buses with everything hanging off them. The dominant pattern for the last decade has been the domain architecture, in which ECUs are grouped by function — powertrain, chassis, body, infotainment, advanced driver assistance — each domain managed by a more powerful controller, with a central gateway routing and filtering traffic between them. The newest vehicles are moving to a zonal architecture: a few high-performance compute units linked by an Ethernet backbone, with small zone controllers gathering the local sensors and actuators in each physical region of the car. This is the hardware substrate of the software-defined vehicle, in which features arrive and change through software long after the car leaves the factory.

For a defender, the gateway is the most consequential component in that picture. Done well, it is the enforcement point that keeps a compromised infotainment system from issuing brake commands. Done poorly, it is a single hinge on which the trustworthiness of the whole vehicle turns — which is exactly the hinge the Jeep researchers exploited.

Why the attack surface keeps growing

Every interface a car exposes to the outside world is a way in, and the list has only lengthened: the OBD-II diagnostic port under the dash, USB and Bluetooth, Wi-Fi hotspots, the cellular modem, the keyless-entry radio, tyre-pressure sensors, the infotainment browser, EV charging connectors, and emerging vehicle-to-everything (V2X) radios. The first systematic mapping of this surface, published in 2011, showed that remote compromise was feasible through a startling range of these channels — from a malformed audio file on a CD, to the Bluetooth stack, to the cellular link — long before the Jeep made it front-page news.[5]

The centre of gravity has since shifted. Where the canonical early attacks needed physical access or a direct radio link, the modern incident is overwhelmingly remote: in an analysis of 494 publicly reported events from 2025, 92 percent of attacks were carried out remotely, and most required no physical proximity to the vehicle at all.[6] The buses inside the car still matter enormously — they are where physical control ultimately lives — but the way an attacker reaches them now usually runs through a phone app, a backend API, or a telematics server. The sections that follow trace that path from the wire outward.

02 The wire

The CAN bus

If automotive security has a heart, it is the Controller Area Network. Designed by Robert Bosch and introduced in the mid-1980s, CAN solved a real problem of its era — replacing bulky point-to-point wiring harnesses with a single shared bus that any number of controllers could share — and it did so with such elegance that it became the standard, formalised as ISO 11898 and Bosch's CAN 2.0 specification.[7][8] It is still everywhere. It is also, by any modern measure, profoundly insecure — not because it was built badly, but because it was built for a world that no longer exists.

How CAN actually works

CAN is a two-wire differential bus. The pair, CANH and CANL, is driven in opposite directions and read as the voltage difference between them, so that electrical noise picked up equally on both wires cancels out — which is why CAN survives the brutal electromagnetic environment of an engine bay. A bit is either recessive (the wires at rest, roughly equal) or dominant (driven apart). The waveform at the top of this page is exactly that: CANH rising while CANL falls, mirror images around a 2.5-volt centre line.

Three properties of CAN matter more than any other for security, and all three follow from its 1980s design goals:

  • It is a broadcast medium. Every controller on a bus segment sees every frame. There is no point-to-point delivery; a message is simply put on the wire and read by all. The mental model is UDP on a shared cable — and like that cable, the bus carries no record of who sent what.[3]
  • It is message-addressed, not node-addressed. A CAN frame is labelled with an identifier describing the message — "engine RPM," say — not the sender or recipient. Identifiers also set priority: when two controllers transmit at once, the one with the numerically lower identifier wins through nondestructive bitwise arbitration, and the loser backs off and retries.
  • It has no security whatsoever. There is no authentication, no integrity protection beyond an error-detecting checksum, and no encryption. Nothing in the protocol lets a receiver verify that a frame came from the controller that is supposed to send it. As one practitioner reference puts it plainly, because any device on the bus can both see and transmit any frame, it is trivial for one device to impersonate any other.[3]

The data field — at most eight bytes in classic CAN — is where commands and sensor values live, encoded in a manufacturer-specific layout that is not published. Working out what each byte means is the central craft of CAN reverse engineering, and the reason a researcher's first move is almost always to capture traffic and correlate it with physical actions: press the brake, watch which frame changes.

The CAN bus assumes every device on it is honest. Connectivity quietly converted that assumption into the vehicle's largest vulnerability.

From the bus, control

Because there is no notion of a trusted sender, an attacker who can place frames on a CAN segment can do anything any ECU on that segment can do. This is the mechanism behind nearly every dramatic vehicle hack. The 2010 academic study that opened the field demonstrated, with a laptop wired to the bus, that researchers could lock and unlock the doors, run the wipers, blast the horn, falsify the speedometer, kill the engine, and — most alarmingly — engage and disable the brakes, all by injecting crafted CAN frames.[9] Five years later, the only thing the Jeep attack fundamentally added was a remote path to that same bus; the final, physical step was still CAN injection.[1]

Attack primitiveCAN frame injection & bus denial

Injection / spoofing. The attacker transmits frames bearing a legitimate ECU's identifier. Receivers have no way to tell the spoofed frame from the real one and act on whichever they see; a high enough injection rate simply drowns out the genuine sender. This is how false sensor readings and unauthorised actuator commands are introduced.[9]

Replay and fuzzing. Captured frames can be replayed verbatim to repeat an action, and the eight-byte data space is small enough to fuzz — sweeping values to discover undocumented commands and crash conditions.

Denial of service via the protocol itself. Continuously transmitting the highest-priority identifier monopolises arbitration and starves every other controller. More subtly, CAN's own fault-confinement rules can be weaponised: by deliberately corrupting a target's transmissions to provoke error frames, an attacker can drive that ECU into the bus-off state, silencing it completely — turning the network's safety feature into an off switch.[10]

CAN FD, and the limits of a patch

The successor in wide deployment, CAN FD (flexible data rate), raises the payload to 64 bytes and speeds up the data phase, which helps with the bandwidth pressure of modern features. What it does not do is add security: CAN FD frames are still unauthenticated broadcasts. The roomier payload is, however, precisely what makes a retrofit defence practical — there is now space to append a message authentication code and a freshness counter to each frame, which is the basis of the AUTOSAR Secure Onboard Communication mechanism discussed in section 08.[11] The lesson that runs through this whole reference is visible already here: you cannot encrypt your way out of an architecture, and the durable fixes for CAN's weaknesses live above the protocol — in authentication layered on top, in gateways that segment the network, and in monitoring that notices when a controller starts saying things it never says.

03 The nodes

Electronic control units

An electronic control unit is the basic citizen of the vehicle network: a self-contained embedded computer dedicated to a job. Strip away the automotive packaging and you find a microcontroller — commonly an automotive-grade part from Infineon, Renesas or NXP — with flash memory holding its firmware, a little RAM, and a set of peripherals that bind software to the physical world: CAN and Ethernet controllers, analogue inputs reading sensors, and outputs driving injectors, valves, motors and relays. Most run hard real-time code, frequently on the AUTOSAR Classic platform. There are scores of them in a car, and each is simultaneously a thing to be protected and, if compromised, a weapon.

Firmware and the bootloader

An ECU's behaviour is its firmware, and how that firmware can be replaced is a first-order security question. Two pieces of boot software usually sit beneath the application. A small primary bootloader, ideally immutable and held in on-chip ROM, runs first and is the natural place to anchor a root of trust. Above it, a flash bootloader handles reprogramming — receiving a new image and writing it to flash. That flash bootloader is the gate through which all firmware updates pass, which makes it one of the most valuable targets in the entire vehicle: an attacker who can persuade it to accept an unauthorised image owns the ECU outright, and persistently.

Diagnostics: the standardised way in

Every ECU also speaks a diagnostic protocol, and here the attack surface is unusually well documented because regulators required it to be. The OBD-II port mandated for emissions inspection exposes a standardised set of parameter identifiers, and on top of that minimal layer sits the far richer Unified Diagnostic Services (UDS, ISO 14229), carried over CAN by a transport protocol that fragments long messages across multiple frames. UDS is how a workshop tool reads fault codes, but it is also how it reads and writes ECU memory, enters a programming session, and reflashes firmware — capabilities never meant for an adversary's hands.[3]

Front doorUDS security access · service 0x27

Sensitive UDS operations are gated by a challenge–response scheme known as security access: the ECU returns a random seed, and the tester must reply with the correct key, computed from the seed by a secret algorithm. In principle this keeps unauthorised tools out. In practice the scheme has been a recurring weak point — the key algorithms are often recoverable by reverse-engineering dealer diagnostic software or the ECU firmware itself, and short seed spaces invite brute force. The seed–key "front door" is frequently easier to walk through than its designers assumed.[3]

The reflash, and the pivot

The deepest ECU attack is to rewrite its firmware. This is not theoretical: the decisive move in the Jeep compromise was reprogramming a particular controller — one that bridged the infotainment system to the vehicle's CAN networks — with a modified image so that it would relay arbitrary CAN frames on the attacker's behalf. The controller's firmware could be updated without any meaningful authentication, and that single gap converted a hacked radio into full control of the car's safety systems.[1]

That story illustrates the property that makes ECUs so consequential as a class: a compromised ECU is a pivot. The components most exposed to the outside world — the telematics unit and the infotainment head unit — are also, by necessity, the ones connected to multiple internal networks. An attacker rarely lands directly on the brakes. They land on something internet-facing, and then use its position on the bus to reach the systems that matter. Every defence discussed later in this reference is, in some sense, an attempt to break that chain: secure boot so an ECU refuses to run unsigned firmware, hardware-protected keys so reflashing cannot be forged, locked debug ports so the chip cannot simply be read out and rewritten on the bench, authenticated diagnostics, and gateways that ensure a compromised head unit cannot talk to the powertrain bus even if it tries.

04 The remote surface

Telematics & the cloud

The reason a stranger can now reach your car is the same reason you can start it from your phone. Wrapped around the embedded world of buses and ECUs is a second system entirely: a telematics control unit carrying a cellular modem and GPS, an infotainment head unit with Wi-Fi, Bluetooth and apps, a companion mobile application, and behind all of it the manufacturer's cloud, stitched together by web APIs. From a security standpoint this stack is a web and cloud target as much as an automotive one — and it is where the most scalable attacks now happen.

The shift is stark in the numbers. Of publicly reported incidents in 2025, two-thirds involved telematics and cloud systems as the attack vector, and API-centred attacks have climbed to roughly a sixth of all incidents — ahead of infotainment as an entry point.[6][12] The logic is simple and unforgiving: an attacker who finds one flaw in a backend that serves a million cars has, in a sense, found a flaw in a million cars.

When the car is a web app

The clearest demonstration of how thoroughly the surface has moved came in January 2023, when a group of researchers led by Sam Curry published the results of a months-long campaign against automotive backends. They reported systemic access-control failures across the telematics and API systems of sixteen carmakers — broken single sign-on, insecure direct object references that exposed other customers' records, and leaked credentials — that allowed them to locate vehicles, unlock and lock them, and start and stop engines entirely over the web, with no presence near the car.[13][14]

Case studyWeb Hackers vs. the Auto Industry · 2023

Among the findings: a connected-vehicle service exposed cloud credentials with broad read/write access to its storage, putting source code and what appeared to be user databases within reach. A fleet-telematics platform could be driven to grant full administrative control, from which an attacker could issue arbitrary commands to roughly 15.5 million vehicles — police cars, ambulances and commercial trucks among them.[15] None of this required touching a CAN bus. It required the kind of authorization bug that plagues ordinary web applications, sitting in front of physical machines.

That an entirely different research lineage — web bug-hunters rather than embedded reverse engineers — could reach vehicle commands is the single most important development in the field since the Jeep. It means automotive security is now also application security, identity and access management, and cloud configuration, and that the discipline's centre of mass has moved out of the garage and into the data centre.

The dongle and the head unit

Two older entry points still deserve respect because they bridge the internet directly to the bus. The first is the aftermarket telematics dongle — the small cellular device that insurers and fleets plug into the OBD-II port. In 2015 researchers showed that a popular such dongle could be reached remotely, over the cellular network, and made to inject traffic onto the vehicle's CAN bus: a thumb-sized gadget turning a diagnostic socket into an internet-facing on-ramp to the powertrain.[16] The lesson generalises to any device that connects the diagnostic port to a wider network.

The second is the infotainment head unit, a full computer — often Linux-based — exposed through browsers, media parsers and wireless stacks, any of which can harbour memory-corruption bugs. The Jeep chain began with the head unit reachable over cellular; a contemporaneous Tesla research effort began by exploiting the in-car browser.[17] In both cases the head unit was not the prize but the beachhead, used to pivot toward the controllers that move the car.

Ransomware reaches the vehicle

The most recent turn is financial. As organised, well-resourced groups have moved into the sector, ransomware became the fastest-growing category in 2025, accounting for almost half of all reported incidents. It is no longer confined to enterprise IT: in one mid-2025 case attackers reached a vehicle command-and-control system through the companion app, locked owners out of their cars, took control of functions such as ignition and door locks, and demanded payment to restore access.[6] At the enterprise scale, a 2025 attack on a major manufacturer crippled its IT systems and halted vehicle production for the better part of forty days, a vivid reminder that automotive cyber risk now spans the whole value chain, not just the car.[18]

The defensive implications are mostly familiar from the web world, applied with unusual stakes. Backend APIs must enforce authorization on every object rather than trusting an identifier in a request; the link between a telematics unit and the cloud should rest on strong, per-device identity and mutual authentication so that one extracted secret does not unlock the fleet; credentials belong in managed secret stores, not in source. And above all, the path from the cloud down to the bus must stay gated — a backend compromise should never translate directly into an actuator command, which is the whole point of the secure-boot and segmentation architecture in section 08.

05 Autonomous systems

Attacking the senses

Everything so far has assumed an attacker reaching for the bus — through the diagnostic port, the modem, the cloud. Driver-assistance and automated-driving systems introduce a category of attack that needs none of that, because they introduce something no earlier car had: machines that perceive. A lane-keeping camera, a radar, a laser scanner and a satellite receiver are all sensors that translate the physical world into data the car acts on. An attacker who can manipulate that physical world — a sticker on the road, a pattern of light, a forged radio signal — can feed the car false beliefs without ever touching its wiring.

The modern advanced driver-assistance system (ADAS) and its more capable automated-driving descendants rest on a stack of complementary sensors, each with different strengths and different ways of being deceived. Cameras read lane markings, traffic signs, signals and obstacles, but interpret them through machine-learning models that can be fooled. Radar measures the range and closing speed of other vehicles and works in fog and darkness, but resolves fine detail poorly. LiDAR builds a precise three-dimensional point cloud by timing reflected laser pulses, and anchors many automated-driving stacks, but is comparatively expensive and optically exposed. Ultrasonic sensors handle parking distances. A global navigation satellite system (GNSS) receiver, fused with an inertial measurement unit, supplies position. The job of sensor fusion is to weld these noisy, partial views into one coherent model of the world — and that model is precisely what an attacker wants to corrupt.

Fooling the cameras

Because a camera's understanding lives inside a neural network, the relevant attacks are adversarial examples: physical patterns crafted so that a model confidently sees the wrong thing. The most cited automotive demonstration came from Tencent's Keen Security Lab, whose researchers studied a production Tesla Autopilot and showed two distinct camera deceptions. They induced the automatic wipers to activate on a clear day by presenting an adversarial image to the forward camera, and — more alarmingly — they placed small, unobtrusive stickers on the road surface that nudged the lane-recognition system into treating an adjacent lane's boundary as its own, steering the car toward oncoming traffic.[17] The markings were innocuous to a human driver; their entire effect was on the model.

The principle generalises beyond one vendor. Any safety function that depends on classifying camera pixels — reading a speed-limit sign, distinguishing a shadow from an obstacle, finding a lane edge — inherits the brittleness of the underlying model, and an adversary who can place a small perturbation in the camera's field of view can sometimes flip the decision. This is why camera evidence is increasingly treated as one vote among several rather than ground truth.

Spoofing the laser

LiDAR feels more trustworthy than a camera because it measures geometry directly, but it too can be deceived by an attacker who controls light. The foundational work, presented in 2017, demonstrated relay and spoofing attacks against automotive LiDAR: by capturing the unit's outgoing pulses and re-emitting crafted pulses back at it, an attacker could inject spurious points and create the illusion of objects that were not there, or saturate the sensor outright.[19] The attack exploited the simple fact that the sensor trusts any returning light of the right wavelength and timing.

Later research sharpened the threat from raw spoofing into perception manipulation. A 2019 study showed that carefully timed injected points, though few in number, could be shaped to survive the processing pipeline and cause the perception system to register a phantom obstacle directly ahead — enough, in principle, to trigger emergency braking.[20] If making the car see a thing that is not there is dangerous, the inverse is worse: a 2023 study demonstrated physical removal attacks, in which laser interference deletes the genuine returns from a real obstacle, so that a pedestrian or vehicle in front simply vanishes from the point cloud and the automated-driving framework fails to react.[21] Spoofing an object in and erasing an object out are the two halves of fully controlling what the laser believes.

Threat patternPerception-layer attacks

What unites the camera and LiDAR attacks is that they bypass every cryptographic and network control on the vehicle. Secure boot, message authentication and a hardened gateway are irrelevant when the sensor data entering the system is faithfully transmitted but describes a reality the attacker has staged. The defence cannot live only in the network; it has to live in the perception logic itself — cross-checking sensors against one another, testing whether a claimed object is physically plausible, and refusing to let any single modality dictate a safety-critical action.

Lies to the other sensors

The remaining sensors have their own failure modes. Anti-lock braking and stability control depend on wheel-speed sensors that are typically magnetic; in 2013 researchers showed a non-invasive spoofing attack in which an external electromagnetic actuator placed near a wheel-speed sensor injected a false signal, deceiving the ABS into reading a wheel speed that did not match reality — a manipulation with obvious safety consequences during braking.[22] The attack required no electrical connection to the vehicle, only proximity.

Position is a similarly soft target. A GNSS receiver believes whatever signals reach its antenna, and satellite signals are faint and unauthenticated, so a local transmitter broadcasting counterfeit signals can pull a receiver's computed position or time away from the truth — GNSS spoofing — which matters acutely for any function that trusts the map, from automated navigation to geofenced features. Radar can likewise be jammed with noise or spoofed with crafted returns that fabricate a closing vehicle. None of these attacks touches a single wire of the car; all of them poison the inputs the car reasons from.

Why fusion is not a cure, and V2X widens the field

Sensor fusion is the natural defence — if the LiDAR reports an obstacle the camera and radar do not, the system has reason for doubt — and good fusion does raise the bar, since an attacker must now deceive several modalities at once and consistently. But fusion is not a panacea: research has repeatedly shown single-sensor attacks producing unsafe behaviour, and an adversary who understands the fusion logic can target its assumptions directly. Robust perception therefore layers plausibility and physics checks, temporal consistency, and redundancy on top of fusion, treating no sensor as authoritative on its own.

Cooperative driving extends the perimeter further still. Vehicle-to-everything (V2X) communication lets cars exchange position and intent with one another and with roadside infrastructure, which promises real safety benefits but also creates a channel in which a single malicious participant can broadcast falsified messages — a non-existent stopped vehicle, a spoofed identity — to influence the behaviour of others. The countermeasures here are a security ecosystem in their own right: a public-key infrastructure that signs every message so recipients can verify the sender is an authorised participant, together with misbehaviour-detection systems that flag implausible reports even when they are correctly signed. As with the sensors, authenticating the channel is necessary but not sufficient; the receiver must still judge whether the message describes a possible world.

06 Vehicle network attacks

A taxonomy of the break-in

The catalogue of vehicle attacks can look like a zoo of unrelated tricks — a relayed key fob here, a spoofed sensor there, a cellular exploit somewhere else — but it organises cleanly along one axis: how close the attacker must be. At one end are physical attacks that need hands on the car; in the middle are proximity attacks over short-range wireless; at the far end are remote attacks mounted from anywhere on the internet. The intellectual history of the discipline is essentially a steady march along that axis, from the workbench to the other side of the planet, and the danger of each class scales with the number of cars one attacker can reach.

That history has three landmark steps, and they map onto the taxonomy almost exactly. In 2010 a team from the University of Washington and the University of California, San Diego, performed the first rigorous security analysis of a modern car with direct access to its internal networks, and reached a sobering conclusion: once an adversary could inject traffic onto the in-vehicle bus, they could control essentially every computerised function — disabling the brakes, selectively braking individual wheels, stopping the engine — because the network simply trusted the messages it carried.[9] The result established that the consequences of bus access were total; the open question was whether such access could be obtained without opening the car.

The same researchers answered it the following year. Their 2011 study was a comprehensive analysis of the vehicle's external attack surface, and it demonstrated that the internal networks could be reached through a remarkable variety of channels without any physical contact — including short-range wireless interfaces and a long-range cellular path that allowed remote compromise.[5] Four years later, Miller and Valasek turned that academic possibility into a public, weaponised reality on a specific production vehicle, driving the Jeep remotely over the cellular network until the manufacturer recalled 1.4 million cars.[1][2] Physical, proximity, remote: the field walked the whole axis in five years.

Physical and direct attacks

Direct attacks assume the adversary can reach the car's interior or its wiring — most obviously through the OBD-II port, which by design grants access to the diagnostic and control networks. This is the most powerful position an attacker can hold, because, as the 2010 work showed, traffic injected onto the bus is honoured without question.[9] It is also the least scalable: it does not, by itself, threaten a fleet, because it does not compose into a remote campaign. Its real significance is twofold — it is the threat model for anyone who can briefly access a vehicle (rental, valet, workshop, a compromised aftermarket dongle in the port), and it is the foundation every remote attack is ultimately trying to reach, since the goal of a remote chain is precisely to obtain this bus-level control from a distance.

Proximity attacks

Proximity attacks exploit the short-range radios a car exposes to its immediate surroundings, and two examples have become emblematic. The first is the relay attack against passive keyless entry and start systems: the car unlocks and starts when it senses the owner's fob nearby, so two attackers with relay devices can extend that conversation across distance — one standing near the owner's key (in a house or pocket), the other by the car — tricking the vehicle into believing the key is present and driving away with it. The attack defeats the convenience feature by forwarding its radio signals rather than breaking any cryptography. The second is the tyre-pressure monitoring system (TPMS): each wheel transmits its pressure over an unauthenticated short-range link, which can be passively sniffed to track a vehicle by its sensor identifiers, or actively spoofed to trigger false low-pressure warnings. Both attacks, along with the broader landscape of keyless-entry and immobiliser weaknesses, are treated at length in the practitioner literature.[3] Their hallmark is that the attacker must be near the car or its owner, which bounds the threat geographically even when it cannot be cryptographically prevented.

Remote attacks

Remote attacks are the modern centre of gravity, and the reason the field now overlaps so heavily with conventional network and application security. The attacker reaches the vehicle through some internet-facing service — the cellular modem, the infotainment system's wireless stacks, a companion mobile app, or a backend telematics API — and from there attempts to pivot inward toward the bus, exactly as the Jeep chain did from head unit to controller.[1] The appeal is leverage: a single remote flaw can be exploited against every vehicle that shares the vulnerable component, turning one bug into a fleet-wide exposure. The data confirms the shift decisively. In an analysis of 494 publicly reported automotive cyber incidents during 2025, ninety-two percent were carried out remotely, with the majority requiring no physical proximity to the target at all.[6]

The taxonomy is more than a filing system; it is a guide to defence. Physical attacks argue for hardening the diagnostic port and authenticating what may sit behind it. Proximity attacks argue for authenticated short-range protocols and distance-bounding so that a relayed signal can be told apart from a present one. Remote attacks — the dominant and fastest-growing class — argue for everything the rest of this reference describes: secure interfaces, segmentation that keeps a compromised modem away from the brakes, authenticated messages on the bus, and a gateway that refuses to let the outside reach the inside. The closer an attack can be pushed back toward requiring physical access, the smaller the fleet any one adversary can threaten.

07 Firmware analysis

Reading the mind of the ECU

An ECU's behaviour is not in its silicon; it is in its firmware — the compiled program held in flash memory that decides how the controller answers every message and drives every output. Firmware analysis is the craft of getting that program off the chip and reading it, and it sits at the centre of the field for a simple reason: it is what both sides do. The attacker reverse-engineers firmware to find the flaw and, in the worst case, to forge a malicious update; the defender reverse-engineers it to audit what a supplier actually shipped. The Jeep attack did not end at the gateway — it ended with the researchers rewriting a controller's firmware so it would relay their commands onto the bus, which is what turned a foothold into physical control.[1]

Getting the firmware off the chip

Acquisition is the first and often hardest step, and practitioners reach for a ladder of techniques from least to most invasive.[3] The easiest, when it exists, is to extract firmware from an update package: if the manufacturer distributes software updates as downloadable files, the image can frequently be pulled apart without ever touching the car. Failing that, a software route may work — reading the image out through the diagnostic protocol or the bootloader, which succeeds only if the ECU's read protection has not been enabled. When software paths are closed, analysts turn to hardware debug interfaces: the JTAG and SWD ports that engineers use to program and debug microcontrollers can, if left accessible, dump the entire flash. A UART serial console, often present on infotainment boards, may expose a shell or bootloader prompt. The most invasive option is chip-off: desoldering the flash memory and reading it directly in an external programmer, which defeats most on-chip access controls at the cost of destroying the board's operation. Each rung trades effort and risk for a higher chance of success against a locked target.

Reading the binary

A raw firmware image is an undifferentiated block of bytes, and turning it into understanding relies on a now-standard toolkit. Binwalk scans a blob to identify and carve out embedded structures — compressed sections, file signatures and whole filesystems — which is especially productive against the Linux-based images that run modern infotainment units. For the program code itself, analysts use a disassembler and decompiler: Ghidra, the reverse-engineering suite released by the United States National Security Agency, is freely available and widely taught, while IDA Pro is the long-established commercial standard; both translate machine code back into assembly and approximate C across the many processor architectures ECUs use. A specialised corner of the field deals with engine-management calibration, where tools such as WinOLS are used to read and edit the tuning maps held in powertrain firmware.

The bare-metal microcontroller firmware typical of powertrain and chassis ECUs is the hardest to read, because it has no operating system or symbols to lean on. The reverse engineer must identify the processor architecture, determine the address at which the image loads, and locate the reset and interrupt vectors that reveal where execution begins — then patiently map how the code touches the chip's peripherals to infer what each routine does. Recovered strings, lookup tables and recognisable cryptographic constants are the breadcrumbs that make an opaque binary legible.[3]

What the analyst is hunting

The point of all this effort is to find a small number of high-value things. Hardcoded secrets — keys, certificates, credentials — are common and damaging, because a secret baked into firmware is a secret shared by every car running it. The seed-key algorithm behind diagnostic security access is a frequent target: recovering it from the bootloader, as discussed in the ECU section, can unlock the very reflashing routines an attacker wants. Cryptographic implementations are scrutinised for weak algorithms or flawed use that undermine whatever protection they were meant to provide. And the single most consequential prize is the update-verification logic itself: if an analyst can locate the routine that checks an update's signature and find a way to bypass or satisfy it, the door to forging accepted firmware swings open — which is exactly why that logic must be anchored in hardware, the subject of the next section.

Patching, and the defences that stop it

Once firmware is understood, modifying it is mechanical: change the relevant bytes, repair any checksums the ECU validates so the image still looks intact, and write it back through whichever acquisition path allowed access. This is the step that converts a research finding into persistent, real-world control, and it is the step defenders work hardest to deny.

The countermeasures form a layered barrier. Readout protection — the flash-protection fuses and lock bits offered by microcontrollers — disables debug access and prevents the firmware from being read out at all, and disabling or locking JTAG and other debug ports in production hardware closes the most convenient acquisition routes. Secure (authenticated) debug keeps a debug interface usable for legitimate engineering while requiring cryptographic proof of authorisation to unlock it. Encrypted firmware images mean that even a successful extraction yields ciphertext rather than readable code. None of these, however, stops a patched image from running; only a hardware-anchored boot process can do that, by refusing to execute code whose signature does not verify. Firmware analysis and secure boot are therefore two sides of one contest — one side trying to read and rewrite the program, the other trying to ensure that only the genuine program ever executes.

08 Secure boot & in-vehicle defence

Anchoring trust in hardware

The attacks in the preceding sections share a single root cause: misplaced trust. The bus trusts any message that appears on it; the processor trusts any code held in its flash. Software cannot fix this on its own, because software is exactly what a capable attacker rewrites. The defensive architecture of the modern vehicle therefore begins below software, in hardware that cannot be altered, and builds outward in layers — a hardware root of trust, a verified boot that refuses to run unsigned code, authenticated messages on the bus, segmentation that contains a breach, and monitoring that notices one in progress.

The hardware root of trust

Security has to be bootstrapped from something the attacker cannot reach, and in a vehicle that something is a dedicated secure hardware element built into or alongside the main processor. It stores cryptographic keys in tamper-resistant memory and performs cryptographic operations internally, so that the keys never appear in the application CPU's address space where a software compromise could steal them. Three overlapping lineages define what this hardware must do. The Secure Hardware Extension (SHE) is a lightweight specification originating in the automotive industry's HIS consortium, defining a minimal on-chip module — secure key storage and symmetric (AES) cryptography — deliberately cheap enough to place in ordinary ECUs. The European EVITA project defined a richer family of Hardware Security Modules in three tiers matched to need: a Full HSM with asymmetric cryptography and the strongest protection for the most exposed units such as gateways and V2X, a Medium HSM for general control units, and a Light HSM for the simplest sensors and actuators — protection scaled to the criticality and cost budget of each node.[23] Consolidating these expectations, the SAE standard J3101 specifies the requirements for hardware-protected security in ground vehicles, giving manufacturers a common target for what on-board security hardware must provide.[24]

Secure boot and the chain of trust

Given a hardware root of trust, an ECU can verify its own software before allowing it to run. At power-on, a small piece of immutable boot code anchored in the secure hardware checks the cryptographic signature of the first software stage; only if the signature is valid does it transfer control. That stage in turn verifies the next, and so on — a chain of trust extending from an unforgeable hardware root up through the bootloader to the application firmware, in which any broken link halts the boot.[24] Measured boot strengthens the scheme by recording a hash of each stage as it loads, producing evidence of the software's integrity that the vehicle can later attest. Anti-rollback protection closes a subtle gap: by tracking the minimum acceptable version, it prevents an attacker from installing an older image that is genuinely signed but known to be vulnerable. The combined effect directly defeats the firmware patching of the previous section — an image whose bytes have been altered no longer carries a valid signature, so secure boot simply refuses to execute it.

Authenticating the messages: SecOC

Secure boot protects code at rest but does nothing for traffic in motion, and the CAN bus still, by default, trusts whatever it carries. The standardised remedy is Secure Onboard Communication (SecOC), defined within AUTOSAR, which adds a message authentication code computed with a shared secret key to safety-relevant messages, together with a freshness value — a counter or timestamp — so that each message is provably both authentic and recent.[11] A receiving ECU verifies the MAC and checks the freshness before acting, which defeats precisely the injection and replay attacks described earlier: a forged frame lacks a valid MAC, and a captured-and-resent frame fails the freshness check.

ControlSecOC · authenticated CAN FD

Two conditions make SecOC effective. The shared keys must live inside the hardware security module so a compromised application core cannot extract them and forge valid MACs — which is why secure communication depends on the hardware root of trust rather than standing alone. And the bus must have room to carry the authentication data: classic CAN's eight-byte payload left little space for a MAC and counter, whereas CAN FD's larger frames make per-message authentication practical, one of the quiet reasons the industry's migration to CAN FD matters for security as much as for bandwidth.

Segmentation and the gateway, revisited

Layered defence assumes that some control will eventually fail, and asks what then contains the damage. The structural answer is network segmentation: grouping ECUs into separate domains so that a foothold in one does not grant the run of the whole vehicle. The central gateway — and, in newer designs, the zonal controllers — act as the enforcement points that decide which messages may cross between segments, so that a compromised infotainment unit is structurally prevented from emitting a braking command into the chassis domain. This is exactly the control whose weakness the Jeep researchers exploited when they crossed from the entertainment network to the controllers that move the car, and strengthening it is among the highest-leverage defensive investments a manufacturer can make.

Watching at runtime: intrusion detection

The final in-vehicle layer is observation. An automotive intrusion detection system monitors bus traffic for anomalies — frames that violate the known timing, frequency or content of legitimate communication — and raises an alert, increasingly forwarded to a back-end vehicle security operations centre for fleet-wide analysis. This approach is unusually well suited to the car, because legitimate CAN traffic is highly periodic and predictable, so deviations from its normal rhythm stand out sharply. The error-handling and injection attacks of the CAN section perturb exactly those regularities — abnormal message timing, unexpected frame rates, controllers behaving out of character — which makes timing-aware and statistical anomaly detection a practical complement to the cryptographic defences.[10]

Secure communication beyond the car: ISO 15118

Authenticated communication does not stop at the body panels. For electric vehicles, the standard ISO 15118 defines the digital interface between the car and the charging station, including the Plug and Charge capability in which the vehicle and charger use certificates and a public-key infrastructure to mutually authenticate and authorise billing automatically over the charging cable.[25] It is a useful illustration of where the discipline is heading: even the charging inlet, once a passive power connector, becomes an authenticated digital interface that must be designed, like everything else on the car, on the assumption that the other end might be hostile.

09 OTA update security

The update channel as battleground

Over-the-air updating is the field's sharpest double edge. Connecting a vehicle to the cloud so its software can be updated remotely is the only practical way to patch a vulnerability across a fleet of millions — the alternative, a dealer visit per car, does not scale to a problem discovered in the field. Yet a mechanism capable of writing new firmware to every ECU in every car is, if an attacker subverts it, the most powerful weapon imaginable: the same pipe that delivers the fix can deliver the payload. The entire discipline of OTA security exists to make that channel trustworthy enough that connecting it is a decisive net gain for safety rather than a single point of catastrophic failure.

Why OTA is no longer optional

The software-defined vehicle has made remote updating a structural necessity. Features now arrive and change as software long after a car leaves the factory, and — more urgently — a security flaw found in deployed vehicles must be remediable without recalling them physically, or it remains open for the life of the fleet. Regulators have recognised this shift and now treat the management of software updates as a formal obligation rather than a convenience, a development taken up in detail in the standards section. The practical consequence is that OTA capability and OTA security have become baseline requirements for shipping a connected car at all.

The threats unique to updates

An update pipeline faces dangers a running ECU does not. An attacker who can forge an update installs malicious firmware at fleet scale in one stroke. One who can roll back or replay reinstates an older image whose vulnerabilities are already known and weaponised. One who can tamper with an image in transit can corrupt an ECU outright. A compromised back-end repository might sign and distribute a malicious image with the manufacturer's own authority. And the act of installation must itself be fail-safe: an update interrupted by a power loss or a dropped connection must never leave a safety-critical controller bricked. A serious OTA design has to answer every one of these, and ideally to remain safe even when part of its own infrastructure has been breached.

Uptane: a framework designed to be compromised

The dominant response in the automotive industry is Uptane, a framework that adapts The Update Framework (TUF) to the constraints and threat model of vehicles. It was introduced by Kuppusamy and colleagues in 2016 and subsequently formalised as a public standard, with version 1.0 published through the IEEE/ISTO in 2019; it is now maintained as an open community project under the Linux Foundation.[26][27] Its central design conviction is unusual and instructive: rather than assuming compromise can be prevented, Uptane assumes it cannot, and engineers the system so that no single breach yields arbitrary, fleet-wide firmware control.

That resilience comes from separating authority across two repositories. The Image repository holds the firmware images and metadata signed with keys kept offline, so the most powerful signing keys are difficult to steal and rarely exposed. The Director repository operates online, deciding in real time which ECU in a given vehicle should receive which image and generating metadata to direct the installation. The two are deliberately complementary: because an installation must satisfy metadata from both, compromising the always-online Director alone is not sufficient to push malicious firmware — the images must still match what the offline-key-protected Image repository has attested. An attacker has to defeat both an exposed online service and a guarded offline process, which is a far higher bar than breaching any one server.

ControlUptane · primary/secondary & the vehicle manifest

Uptane also accommodates the reality that many ECUs are too small to perform full verification. A more capable Primary ECU downloads and verifies the metadata and images, then distributes them to the resource-constrained Secondary ECUs, so even the humblest controller benefits from the full set of checks. The vehicle reports the software each ECU is running in a signed Vehicle Version Manifest, giving the Director an accurate picture of fleet state and a means to detect rollback attempts or mismatched installations. The result is a verification scheme that degrades gracefully under partial compromise instead of failing catastrophically.

The properties every OTA system needs

Whatever framework a manufacturer adopts, a sound update system must provide the same handful of guarantees. Code signing ensures an image is cryptographically verified before it is flashed, and crucially the check at download should align with the signature the ECU itself enforces at boot — so that secure boot and OTA validate against the same root of trust rather than against two separable mechanisms. Anti-rollback version control prevents the reinstatement of older, vulnerable images. End-to-end integrity — transport protection combined with image signing — ensures that corruption or tampering in transit is detected rather than installed. Fail-safe installation, commonly using dual (A/B) firmware banks that validate the new image before switching to it and can fall back if an update is interrupted, prevents a half-written update from disabling a controller. Together these turn the update channel from a liability into the mechanism that keeps a fleet patched.

RXSWIN: tracking what is certified

Because updates change the very software regulators have approved, the framework needs a way to track which software is present for the purposes of type approval. The Regulatory Software Identification Number (RXSWIN) serves this role, identifying the software relevant to a regulated property of the vehicle so that an update's effect on regulatory compliance can be recorded and demonstrated. It is the thread that ties the technical act of updating to the regulatory regime governing connected vehicles — the subject of the next section.

10 Standards & regulations

From research to rulebook

For most of its history, automotive security had no rulebook. Researchers demonstrated attacks faster than anyone wrote requirements, and whether a manufacturer secured its vehicles was a matter of conscience and reputation rather than law. That changed around the turn of the decade, when an engineering standard and a binding regulation arrived almost together and, between them, converted cybersecurity from a voluntary good practice into a precondition for selling a car across much of the world. Understanding the modern field means understanding these two instruments and the constellation of standards around them.

ISO/SAE 21434: the engineering standard

The central standard for the discipline is ISO/SAE 21434, published in 2021 as a joint effort of the International Organization for Standardization and SAE International, and titled for road-vehicle cybersecurity engineering. Its defining choice is to mandate a process rather than a checklist of controls: it requires manufacturers and their suppliers to manage cybersecurity across the entire vehicle lifecycle — from concept and development through production, operation, maintenance and decommissioning.[4] At its heart is Threat Analysis and Risk Assessment (TARA), a structured method for identifying assets, enumerating threats against them and evaluating the resulting risk, which in turn drives a Cybersecurity Assurance Level (CAL) that scales the rigour of the engineering effort to the severity of the risk. The standard superseded the earlier guidance of SAE J3061 from 2016, and emerged from broad international consensus across many companies and many nations, which is part of why it has become the common language of the field.[28]

UN R155 and R156: the binding regulation

Where 21434 describes how to engineer security well, the United Nations regulations make doing so compulsory. Adopted by the World Forum for Harmonization of Vehicle Regulations (UNECE WP.29), UN Regulation No. 155 requires vehicle manufacturers to establish a certified Cyber Security Management System (CSMS) governing the vehicle throughout its lifecycle, and UN Regulation No. 156 requires a Software Update Management System (SUMS) to ensure that updates, including those delivered over the air, are carried out safely and traceably.[29][30] The regulations were adopted in June 2020 and entered into force in January 2021. In the European Union they became mandatory for all new vehicle types from July 2022 and for all newly produced vehicles from July 2024, and they bind the many contracting parties to the relevant UN agreement — among them the EU, the United Kingdom, Japan and South Korea.[31][32] The United States is not a party to these regulations, regulating vehicle safety instead through its own Federal Motor Vehicle Safety Standards and the guidance described below. It is through R156 that the RXSWIN introduced in the previous section acquires its force, as the identifier by which a regulated vehicle's software is tracked through updates.

The practical effect of this pairing is hard to overstate: in the markets that have adopted R155 and R156, a manufacturer cannot obtain type approval — cannot legally sell a new vehicle — without demonstrating the management systems these regulations require, and ISO/SAE 21434 has become the de facto means of demonstrating them. For the update-management obligations of R156 in particular, the corresponding engineering practices are laid out in ISO 24089, published in 2023 as the software-update-engineering companion standard.[33] Regulation supplies the obligation; the standard supplies the method.

These instruments sit within a broader body of standards that together govern the security and safety of the software-defined vehicle.

The safety standards next door

Automotive cybersecurity does not stand apart from the older discipline of functional safety; it interlocks with it. ISO 26262 governs functional safety — the hazards that arise when a system malfunctions — and was the established safety framework long before security became a formal concern.[34] ISO 21448, known as SOTIF (Safety of the Intended Functionality), addresses a subtler class of hazard: those arising not from a fault but from the limitations of a system performing exactly as designed — a perception system that behaves correctly yet is fooled by conditions outside its competence, which is precisely the territory of the sensor and perception attacks discussed earlier.[35] Because a security breach can directly cause a safety hazard, ISO/SAE 21434 was written to complement ISO 26262 rather than to replace it; in a vehicle, security and safety are two views of the same obligation.

Disclosure, sharing, and United States guidance

Three further pillars round out the governance picture. ISO/IEC 29147 standardises vulnerability disclosure — how an organisation should receive reports of weaknesses and act on them — which matters acutely in an industry where independent researchers have repeatedly found the most serious flaws.[36] In the United States, the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration published an updated edition of its Cybersecurity Best Practices for the Safety of Modern Vehicles in 2022, organised around layered, defence-in-depth protection and offering dozens of general and technical recommendations as non-binding guidance that revises its 2016 predecessor.[37] And the industry coordinates through the Automotive Information Sharing and Analysis Center (Auto-ISAC), a member body for exchanging threat intelligence and publishing best-practice guides across its many participating organisations.[38] Standard, regulation, safety framework, disclosure process and shared intelligence together form the scaffolding on which a modern vehicle-security programme is built.

11 Best practices

Principles that make it add up

The mechanisms in the preceding sections — secure boot, authenticated messaging, segmentation, signed updates, monitoring — are only as good as the principles that organise them into a coherent programme. None of those principles is unique to vehicles; they are the accumulated doctrine of security engineering. What is unique is the stakes, because here a lapse does not leak data, it endangers a person travelling at speed. A handful of ideas, applied consistently, separate a secure vehicle programme from a collection of disconnected features.

Defence in depth is the foundation. No single control should be load-bearing, because every control can fail, and the United States guidance frames the entire approach around layered protection for exactly this reason.[37] Secure boot may be bypassed on some unit; SecOC keys may leak from some supplier; a gateway rule may be misconfigured. A sound design assumes each of these will happen somewhere and ensures that no one of them is sufficient for an attacker to reach the actuators — the same compromise-resilient instinct that animates Uptane's update model.

Risk should drive effort. Finite security budgets are best spent where the consequences are gravest, which is the logic of the Threat Analysis and Risk Assessment at the core of ISO/SAE 21434: enumerate assets and threats, assess risk, and scale the rigour of protection to it, so that a brake controller and a cup-holder light are not defended as though they were equally important.[4] Least privilege and segmentation follow naturally — every component should reach only what it must, and the gateway between the connected, exposed domains and the safety-critical ones is the single most important boundary in the vehicle, the hinge whose failure defined the Jeep attack.

Security must be built in, not bolted on. The lifecycle orientation of 21434 is a deliberate rejection of late-stage security: threat modelling belongs in the concept phase, secure coding and review throughout development, and security validation before production, because a flaw designed into an architecture is far costlier to remove than one caught early.[4] Once vehicles are in the field, monitoring and response take over — intrusion detection feeding a vehicle security operations centre, fleet-wide telemetry to spot emerging campaigns, and the OTA capability to push a fix when one is found, closing the loop between detection and remediation.

Invite the researchers in. The field's most serious vulnerabilities have repeatedly been found by independent researchers rather than by manufacturers, which makes a coordinated vulnerability disclosure process — the subject of ISO/IEC 29147 — a practical necessity rather than a courtesy: a clear channel to receive reports and a commitment to act on them turns the research community from an adversary into an early-warning system.[36] Finally, security extends down the supply chain. A modern vehicle is integrated from components built by dozens of suppliers, and a weakness in any of them becomes the manufacturer's problem; this is why UN R155's management-system obligation is not satisfied at the vehicle maker's boundary but must flow through the tiers of suppliers whose software ends up in the car.[29] Taken together, these principles describe a posture rather than a product: layered, risk-driven, built-in, watched, openly reported and enforced end to end.

12 Getting started

Building your own bench

Automotive security has an intimidating reputation, but the barrier to a first working lab is far lower than the subject suggests. You do not need a car, a garage or expensive equipment to begin — a simulator, a handful of open-source tools and a cheap adapter put a functioning CAN environment on an ordinary laptop, and the foundational research is unusually accessible. What follows is a concrete starting point: a book, a bench, the credentials the industry recognises, and the communities where the work actually happens.

The book to start with

The standard entry point is The Car Hacker's Handbook by Craig Smith, published by No Starch Press in 2016, which walks a newcomer from the fundamentals of in-vehicle networks through ECUs, diagnostic protocols, tooling and concrete attack techniques, and remains the most-recommended single text in the field.[3] It pairs naturally with the primary research cited throughout this reference — the Koscher, Checkoway and Miller–Valasek papers are readable and repay the effort of going to the source.

A lab on your laptop

A practical bench can be assembled almost entirely from free software running on Linux's SocketCAN stack, which lets you practise capturing and injecting CAN traffic against a simulator before ever touching a vehicle.

Communities and where the work happens

The most useful learning is communal. The DEF CON Car Hacking Village is the premier hands-on venue, where newcomers can sit down with real hardware and experienced practitioners; the OpenGarages collective is the open-research community behind ICSim and much of the field's accessible-tooling ethos; and the Automotive Information Sharing and Analysis Center (Auto-ISAC) is the industry body through which manufacturers and suppliers share threat intelligence.[38] The original research continues to appear at the major security conferences — DEF CON and Black Hat on the practitioner side, and the academic venues (USENIX Security, IEEE Symposium on Security and Privacy, ACM CCS, and CHES for hardware) that produced nearly every primary result cited here. For ongoing study, conference talks and village workshops are a far richer resource than any single channel, and they have the advantage of being where the discipline's findings are first disclosed.

The road ahead

The trajectory is unambiguous. The software-defined vehicle adds code, connectivity and attack surface with every model year, and the adversaries have changed character — from individual researchers proving a point to organised, well-resourced groups pursuing profit, with ransomware now among the fastest-growing categories of incident. Artificial intelligence has entered the contest on both sides, and recent industry analysis attributes a sharp increase in the volume of automotive cyberattacks to its use by attackers, even as defenders deploy it for detection.[18] Against the backdrop of hundreds of publicly reported incidents a year, overwhelmingly remote, the gap between attacker capability and fleet-wide defence is a genuine and present concern.[6]

What has changed for the better is that the scaffolding now exists. A decade ago there was no standard, no regulation and little shared doctrine; today there is an engineering standard with real adoption, binding regulation across major markets, a maturing toolkit of hardware roots of trust and authenticated communication, and a research community working in the open. The central lesson of the Jeep on that St. Louis highway has been thoroughly absorbed by the people who build cars: in a machine that carries human beings at speed, security is not a feature to be weighed against others. It is a precondition for safety, and it has to be engineered into the vehicle from the first line of code to the last update pushed over the air.
