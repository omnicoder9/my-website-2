1. What makes a robot “soft”?

Soft robotics is not simply the practice of putting foam on a conventional robot arm. It is a design philosophy in which deformation, elasticity, compliance, distributed actuation, and environmental contact are treated as functional resources rather than unavoidable errors. The canonical modern definition describes soft robots as machines composed substantially of compliant materials and designed to deform continuously, often inspired by biological structures such as octopus arms, elephant trunks, worms, fish fins, tongues, and plant tendrils.[1,2]

Rigid robots are usually modeled as serial chains of links and joints, with motors enforcing positions through high-stiffness transmissions. Soft robots often replace this architecture with bodies that bend, twist, stretch, buckle, wrinkle, inflate, or locally stiffen. Their “joints” may be regions of low stiffness, their “links” may be curved elastomeric chambers, and their “gearboxes” may be geometry, fiber reinforcement, anisotropic material layups, or embedded constraints.[1,3]

This shift changes the engineering trade space. Compliance can improve safety in physical human-robot interaction because contact forces can be spread over larger areas and partly absorbed by deformation; it can help grasp objects with uncertain shapes because the robot can conform passively; and it can enable locomotion through clutter because the body can squeeze, bend, and recover.[1,4] The same compliance also makes modeling and control harder because the robot has many more degrees of freedom than actuators, nonlinear material behavior, hysteresis, time-dependent viscoelasticity, and strong coupling between shape, load, and pressure or electrical input.[1,5]

Core idea: in soft robotics, the body is not just hardware that obeys the controller. The body performs computation, filtering, adaptation, and mechanical decision-making through its own geometry and material response.[6,7]

2. Historical roots and milestones

Soft robotics emerged from several older traditions rather than from a single invention. Pneumatic artificial muscles, including McKibben-type actuators, established the idea that pressurized flexible structures could behave like artificial muscles; compliant mechanism research showed that flexure and elastic deformation could replace bearings and pins; continuum robotics demonstrated that robots could be modeled as continuously bending backbones; and biomimetics supplied many of the biological examples that motivated robotic softness.[1,8,9]

In the early 2000s and 2010s, soft lithography, silicone casting, fiber-reinforced elastomers, and affordable digital fabrication made it practical for robotics labs to build repeatable soft devices. A prominent review by Rus and Tolley in 2015 framed modern soft robotics around the design, fabrication, and control of compliant machines, and helped consolidate the field’s vocabulary for soft bodies, soft actuators, and soft robot control.[1] Around the same period, Marchese, Katzschmann, and Rus published a practical “recipe” for soft fluidic elastomer robots, comparing ribbed, cylindrical, and pleated fluidic actuator morphologies and showing how casting processes could be used to make repeatable soft machines.[10]

Several milestone systems illustrate how the field matured. The 2014 autonomous soft robotic fish showed that fluidic elastomer actuators could produce fish-like escape maneuvers in an untethered underwater robot.[11] The 2016 Octobot demonstrated an entirely soft autonomous robot using soft lithography, embedded 3D printing, microfluidic logic, and chemical fuel, addressing the problem that many soft robots still depended on rigid pumps, valves, electronics, or batteries.[12] The 2018 SoFi fish combined a soft robotic body with acoustic communication and ocean testing, showing how soft robots could interact with marine life in more naturalistic settings.[13] Later work on deep-sea bioinspired soft robots showed that distributed electronics and soft bodies could survive extreme hydrostatic pressure, expanding the field from laboratory grippers to extreme-environment exploration.[14]

The discipline now includes pneumatic networks, hydraulic and fluidic elastomer actuators, dielectric elastomers, shape-memory alloys and polymers, ionic polymer-metal composites, magnetic soft robots, soft grippers, textile robots, wearable exosuits, soft surgical manipulators, ingestible and implantable devices, haptic interfaces, and robot skins. It is a broad field because “softness” can appear in materials, joints, transmissions, sensors, control loops, contact strategy, or manufacturing processes.[2,15,16]

3. Mechanics: compliance, continuum bodies, and morphology

Compliance is the inverse of stiffness. A compliant body deforms significantly under load, and that deformation can be harmful, useful, or both depending on design intent. In rigid robotics, compliance is often added through springs, torque sensors, impedance control, or safety skins; in soft robotics, compliance is frequently the primary structural principle.[1,8]

A useful way to classify soft robot mechanics is by where deformation is intentionally concentrated. Some systems use lumped compliance, such as flexure hinges or soft joints. Others use distributed compliance, where an entire arm, tentacle, finger, fin, or sheet bends continuously. Continuum robots sit between traditional mechanisms and fully soft bodies: they are often modeled as backbones or constant-curvature sections, but their motion is still continuous rather than joint-discrete.[1,17]

Soft materials are usually nonlinear. Silicone elastomers, hydrogels, textiles, foams, and elastomer composites do not behave like ideal linear springs across large strains. They can be hyperelastic, viscoelastic, rate-dependent, hysteretic, anisotropic, and sensitive to temperature or fluid pressure history.[2,5] This makes the design process less like sizing a motor for a rigid joint and more like co-designing a material, geometry, loading path, actuator, sensor, and controller.

Four mechanical ideas appear repeatedly in high-performing soft robots:

1. Anisotropy. Direction-dependent stiffness can convert inflation into bending, twisting, extension, or radial expansion. Fiber winding, fabric layers, embedded sheets, and local wall thickness changes are common ways to create anisotropy.[10,18]
2. Instability. Buckling, snap-through, wrinkling, and jamming can be liabilities or features. Designers use mechanical instabilities for rapid motion, passive gripping, reconfigurable stiffness, and mechanical memory.[2,19]
3. Contact-rich behavior. Soft robots often rely on sliding, wrapping, squeezing, rolling, or distributing load across the environment, so the environment becomes part of the mechanism.[1,4]
4. Shape-field actuation. Instead of commanding one joint angle, the designer may command a pressure, voltage, magnetic field, temperature, tendon displacement, or flow rate that produces a spatial deformation pattern.[15,20]

The “morphology” of a soft robot therefore includes the material, internal cavities, reinforcement pattern, cross-sectional geometry, tendon routing, surface texture, sensor placement, and boundary conditions. Good morphology reduces the burden on the controller by making the desired behavior mechanically natural.[6,7]

4. Underactuation and morphological computation

A robot is underactuated when it has fewer independently controlled actuators than degrees of freedom. Underactuation is common in soft robots because a soft body can deform in many possible ways while being driven by a small number of pressure lines, tendons, electrodes, magnetic fields, or thermal inputs.[1,6] In a rigid robot, underactuation may be treated as a control challenge. In soft robotics, it is often a deliberate design strategy.

The classic example is a soft gripper finger with one pressure input. When inflated, the finger bends. When it contacts an object, the local contact constraints change its curvature. The finger may wrap around a cylinder, flatten against a box, or distribute contact across a fruit without requiring a controller to compute every contact point. The robot has not solved grasp synthesis in software; it has embedded part of the solution in compliance, geometry, and contact mechanics.[4,10]

Underactuation produces several advantages. It reduces cost and weight because fewer motors, valves, wires, and controllers are needed. It improves robustness because the body can adapt to shape variations without precise sensing. It can also reduce grasp planning complexity because passive conformation handles object-level uncertainty.[4,21] However, it sacrifices direct controllability. If a single pneumatic input causes a finger to bend, extend, and slightly twist, the controller cannot independently command those three deformation modes unless the body is redesigned or extra actuation is added.[5,22]

4.1 Underactuation in soft grippers

Soft grippers use underactuation to capture objects over a wide range of sizes and shapes. Pneumatic fingers, tendon-driven elastomer fingers, granular-jamming grippers, suction-based soft grippers, and fin-ray inspired structures can all exploit passive deformation. The gripper’s value is often not maximum precision but tolerant, damage-reducing handling of irregular or delicate objects such as produce, textiles, biological samples, or warehouse items.[4,21,23]

Underactuated hands must still be engineered carefully. Too much compliance can reduce payload, repeatability, and positional accuracy; too little compliance reduces passive adaptation. Contact surfaces must balance friction, cleanability, wear, and object marking. For industrial use, the design must also address cycle time, air consumption, sterilization, lifetime, and integration with existing robot arms and end-of-arm tooling.[21,24]

4.2 Morphological computation

Morphological computation is the idea that the physical body contributes to computation by transforming inputs into useful dynamics before the controller acts. In soft robotics, the body can filter disturbances, distribute forces, store elastic energy, convert scalar pressure into complex motion, or impose natural constraints that make undesirable motions unlikely.[6,7]

For example, a fiber-reinforced pneumatic actuator may be designed so that pressure naturally produces bending toward the palm rather than ballooning outward. A fish-like soft tail may passively oscillate at a useful frequency when driven by alternating pressure. A soft wearable exosuit may route fabric forces along the body so that a small actuator produces assistance at a joint without rigid frames.[10,13,25] The computation is not digital, but it is real: geometry and material laws map inputs and contacts to behavior.

5. Compliant mechanisms

A compliant mechanism transfers or transforms motion, force, or energy through elastic deformation rather than primarily through rigid-body joints. Flexure hinges, bistable mechanisms, monolithic grippers, compliant parallel guides, buckling beams, and origami-inspired fold patterns are examples.[8,19] The field predates modern soft robotics, but it supplies essential design principles for robots that need predictable elastic motion.

Compliant mechanisms differ from “soft blobs” because they are often designed to deform in specific modes. A flexure hinge may allow rotation around one axis while resisting other motions; a compliant transmission may amplify a small actuator displacement; a bistable beam may snap between two stable states and hold position without continuous power.[8,19] This selectivity is valuable because fully soft structures can be too unconstrained for precision tasks.

Soft robots often combine compliant mechanisms with elastomeric bodies. A pneumatic bending actuator may include a strain-limiting layer that acts as a compliant constraint. A gripper may use a fin-ray structure, where angled ribs cause the finger to wrap around an object when pressed. A surgical soft manipulator may contain tendons, notches, or variable-stiffness sections that guide the bending mode.[4,10,17]

For designers, compliant mechanisms provide a middle ground: they retain the adaptability of soft materials while introducing preferred motion paths, repeatability, and mechanical logic.[8,19]

6. Soft actuators

Actuators turn stored or supplied energy into motion. In soft robotics, the actuator is often inseparable from the structure. A pressurized chamber is both actuator and body; a dielectric elastomer membrane is both muscle and skin; a shape-memory alloy wire may be actuator, spring, and structural reinforcement. This integration can simplify packaging but complicates replacement, control, and failure analysis.[15,16]

6.1 Pneumatic and hydraulic elastomer actuators

Fluidic elastomer actuators are among the most widely used soft actuators. They use internal chambers inside an elastomeric body; pressure causes expansion, and asymmetric geometry or reinforcement converts that expansion into bending, extension, contraction, twisting, or combined motion.[10,15] Pneumatic systems are popular because air is clean, compressible, and easy to route, while hydraulic systems can produce higher force density and less compressibility-driven lag at the cost of sealing and fluid-handling challenges.[10,26]

Pneumatic networks, often called PneuNets, use patterned channels and strain-limiting layers to create bending motion. Fiber-reinforced actuators use inextensible or less-extensible fibers to constrain radial or axial strain. Pleated structures can reduce the pressure needed for motion. These design families demonstrate a recurring soft robotics principle: pressure is a simple scalar input, but geometry converts it into complex motion.[10,15]

6.2 Tendon-driven soft actuators

Tendon-driven soft robots route cables through or along a compliant body. Pulling a tendon creates bending, twisting, contraction, or shape change depending on routing and anchor points. Tendon actuation is attractive because motors and gearboxes can be placed remotely, leaving the distal robot lightweight and potentially compact.[17,22] Continuum surgical robots, soft grippers, and wearable devices frequently use tendon or cable transmission because it can be more controllable than fluidic actuation and easier to integrate with commercial motors.[17,25]

The main drawbacks are friction, backlash, cable stretch, routing wear, and coupling between tendons. A tendon path that works in one posture may produce different torques or curvatures in another posture because the robot’s shape changes the internal geometry.[17,22]

6.3 Dielectric elastomer actuators

Dielectric elastomer actuators consist of a soft insulating elastomer sandwiched between compliant electrodes. Applying a high electric field creates Maxwell stress that compresses the elastomer thickness and expands its area, producing actuation. DEAs are attractive because they can be lightweight, fast, efficient, and muscle-like, but they often require high voltage, robust compliant electrodes, electrical insulation, and protection from dielectric breakdown.[27,28]

DEAs are used in soft grippers, artificial muscles, crawling robots, pumps, lenses, haptic devices, and underwater systems. Their performance depends on elastomer permittivity, breakdown strength, pre-strain, electrode compliance, geometry, and drive electronics.[27,28]

6.4 Ionic polymer-metal composites and other electroactive polymers

Ionic polymer-metal composites are electroactive materials in which ion migration under low voltage causes differential swelling and bending. They can be lightweight, flexible, quiet, and capable of operating at low voltages, which makes them useful for small biomimetic fins, underwater actuators, and soft sensors.[29,30] Their limitations include hydration dependence, force output constraints, nonlinear dynamics, and durability challenges.[29,30]

6.5 Shape-memory alloys and shape-memory polymers

Shape-memory alloys such as nickel-titanium can recover a programmed shape when heated, producing high force density in compact packages. Shape-memory polymers can change stiffness or shape across thermal transitions. These materials are useful for small grippers, medical devices, deployable structures, and variable-stiffness modules, but they are often limited by thermal response time, heat management, fatigue, and efficiency.[15,16]

6.6 Magnetic, thermal, chemical, and light-driven actuation

Magnetically responsive soft robots embed magnetic particles in elastomers or hydrogels and use external magnetic fields for wireless actuation. This is especially important for small-scale biomedical and minimally invasive systems because it can move robots without onboard motors or batteries.[31] Thermally responsive hydrogels, light-driven liquid crystal elastomers, combustion-powered soft robots, and chemically powered microfluidic devices broaden the actuation palette, but each introduces tradeoffs in controllability, energy supply, safety, response speed, and integration.[12,15,16]

The current frontier is not merely inventing more actuators; it is making actuators untethered, manufacturable, durable, controllable, sensorized, and safe in real application environments.[16]

7. Soft sensors and proprioception

Soft robots need sensing because their bodies deform in ways that cannot be inferred reliably from actuator commands alone. A pressure command does not uniquely determine shape when payload, contact, temperature, material aging, and hysteresis vary. Soft robots therefore require proprioception, tactile sensing, force estimation, curvature measurement, environmental perception, and sometimes distributed “robot skin.”[32]

Soft sensors are difficult because they must stretch, bend, twist, survive repeated deformation, and remain bonded to soft substrates without delaminating. They must also distinguish multiple stimuli that are mechanically coupled: strain, pressure, shear, temperature, hydration, and contact can all change the same electrical signal.[32,33]

7.1 Resistive and capacitive strain sensors

Resistive soft sensors change electrical resistance when stretched or compressed. They can be made from conductive elastomer composites, carbon-filled polymers, liquid metal channels, conductive textiles, or printed conductive inks.[32,34] Capacitive sensors use changes in electrode area, separation, or dielectric properties to infer deformation or pressure. Capacitive designs can be highly sensitive and compatible with large-area robot skins, but they can be affected by parasitic capacitance and electromagnetic noise.[32]

7.2 Liquid metal sensors

Liquid metal sensors often use eutectic gallium-indium or related alloys embedded in elastomer microchannels. Stretching changes the channel geometry and therefore resistance; channel patterns can be designed for strain, pressure, shear, curvature, or tactile response.[34] Liquid metal is attractive because it remains conductive under large deformation, but manufacturing repeatability, leakage, oxidation, and packaging remain engineering concerns.[34]

7.3 Optical, magnetic, and fluidic sensing

Optical soft sensors use light intensity, wavelength shift, or image-based deformation to estimate shape and contact. Fiber Bragg gratings can measure strain along flexible structures, while embedded cameras can observe internal marker motion in soft tactile sensors. Magnetic sensors can track embedded magnets, and fluidic sensors can infer deformation from pressure or flow changes in soft channels.[32,35]

7.4 Sensor fusion

No single sensor type solves soft robot state estimation. Practical systems often combine pressure sensors, motor encoders, strain sensors, tactile arrays, cameras, and learned estimators. The goal is not necessarily to reconstruct every material point in the body; it is to estimate task-relevant state such as fingertip pose, grasp stability, curvature, contact location, or applied force.[32,33]

8. Modeling and simulation

Soft robot modeling is hard because the robot’s body is a deformable continuum. A rigid arm with six joints can be modeled with six generalized coordinates; a soft arm may require a spatial deformation field. The engineer must choose a model that is accurate enough for the task but simple enough for design, control, or optimization.[1,5]

8.1 Constant-curvature and piecewise constant-curvature models

Many continuum robots are approximated as arcs of constant curvature. This simplifies kinematics and allows designers to map actuator inputs to curvature, section length, and bending plane. Piecewise constant-curvature models divide a robot into segments, each with its own curvature. These models are computationally efficient and useful for tendon-driven or pneumatic continuum arms, but they break down when external loads, distributed contacts, or complex deformation modes dominate.[17,22]

8.2 Cosserat rod models

Cosserat rod theory models slender deformable bodies with position and orientation fields along their centerline, capturing bending, twisting, shear, and extension. It provides a stronger mechanics foundation than constant curvature for many continuum robots and can include distributed loads and boundary conditions.[36] The tradeoff is increased mathematical and computational complexity.

8.3 Finite element analysis and fluid-structure interaction

Finite element analysis is useful for designing actuators, estimating stress, predicting buckling, evaluating strain concentrations, and comparing materials. Fluid-structure interaction modeling is especially important for pneumatic and hydraulic actuators because internal pressure, wall deformation, and flow dynamics are coupled.[26,37] FEA can be too slow for real-time control, but it is powerful for offline design and for generating synthetic data used by reduced-order or learned models.[5,37]

8.4 Reduced-order and learned models

Reduced-order models compress the deformation of a soft body into a smaller set of coordinates, such as modal amplitudes, curvature parameters, or latent variables learned from data. Machine learning methods can approximate forward dynamics, inverse kinematics, inverse dynamics, state estimators, and control policies when analytical models are incomplete.[5,38] The major risk is extrapolation: learned models may fail when the robot encounters loads, contacts, temperatures, or material states not represented in training data.[38]

9. Control strategies for soft robots

Control is the process of choosing actuator inputs so the robot performs a task despite uncertainty. For soft robots, uncertainty is not an edge case; it is built into the body. The same input can produce different shapes under different loads, and contact can dominate the robot’s dynamics.[1,5]

9.1 Open-loop control

Open-loop control commands pressure, voltage, tendon displacement, magnetic field, or temperature without measuring the resulting motion. It is common in demonstrations and simple grippers because it is easy to implement and can work when the mechanical design is robust.[10] Open-loop control is fragile when tasks require precision, when objects vary, or when material properties drift.

9.2 Feedback control

Feedback control uses measurements to correct error. A soft gripper might regulate chamber pressure, fingertip position, curvature, or contact force. A soft manipulator might use embedded strain sensors and cameras to estimate shape, then adjust tendons or pressure. Feedback is essential for repeatable behavior, but it requires reliable sensing and a control model that maps errors to inputs.[32,33]

9.3 Model-based control

Model-based controllers use analytical or numerical models to predict how inputs affect motion. Examples include inverse kinematics for continuum arms, pressure-curvature maps for pneumatic actuators, Cosserat-rod-based control, model predictive control, and finite-element-informed reduced-order controllers.[5,36] Model-based approaches are interpretable and can extrapolate better when the model captures the underlying physics, but they are expensive to derive and calibrate.

9.4 Learning-based control

Learning-based approaches use data to learn dynamics, inverse models, state estimators, or policies. Machine learning has been used to calibrate actuator models, map sensor signals to shape, learn grasp outcomes, and control soft manipulators where analytical models are incomplete.[38,39] Reinforcement learning can be attractive for contact-rich tasks, but physical data collection can be slow and damaging, while simulation-to-real transfer is difficult because soft materials and contact are hard to simulate accurately.[38,39]

9.5 Hybrid strategies

The most promising systems often combine morphology, simple feedback, physics models, and learning. A designer may build a gripper that passively conforms, use pressure feedback to avoid overloading objects, use a learned tactile classifier to detect slip, and use a robot-arm planner for gross positioning. This layered strategy matches the nature of soft robotics: let the body solve easy contact adaptation, use sensors for task-relevant variables, and reserve computation for decisions the body cannot make.[6,32,38]

Design pattern for a soft gripper controller

1. Use body morphology for passive shape adaptation.
2. Regulate actuator pressure or tendon displacement for safe force limits.
3. Estimate contact, curvature, or slip using embedded sensors.
4. Close the loop on task-level outcomes: stable grasp, no damage, desired placement.
5. Add learning only where physics models or hand-coded thresholds are insufficient.

10. Manufacturing and tooling

Soft robot performance depends strongly on manufacturing. Small changes in wall thickness, trapped bubbles, curing conditions, channel dimensions, fiber angle, electrode adhesion, or sensor placement can change behavior. Manufacturing is therefore not a downstream implementation detail; it is part of the design model.[18,24]

10.1 Casting and molding

Silicone casting remains a core technique because it is accessible, inexpensive, and compatible with complex internal cavities. Designers make molds using machining, laser cutting, or 3D printing; pour or inject elastomer; cure the part; remove sacrificial cores; and bond layers. Lost-wax, soluble-core, and retractable-pin methods are used to create internal channels.[10,24]

10.2 Additive manufacturing

3D printing enables rapid iteration, embedded channels, graded stiffness, multi-material structures, and complex geometries that are hard to mold. Additive manufacturing has been used for molds, sacrificial cores, direct printing of elastomers, embedded conductive traces, and multimaterial soft structures.[12,40] The challenges include limited material libraries, anisotropic print properties, surface finish, leakage, support removal, and long-term durability.[40]

10.3 Textile and wearable fabrication

Wearable soft robots often use textiles, cables, pneumatic bladders, laminates, and sewn or bonded reinforcements. Textiles are attractive because they are lightweight, breathable, conformal, scalable, and familiar in apparel manufacturing. Soft exosuits use fabric load paths and remote actuation to assist human motion without rigid frames, but fitting, comfort, donning, laundering, and alignment with anatomy are major design constraints.[25]

10.4 Integration remains the hard part

A lab actuator may be soft, but a fieldable robot needs pumps, valves, reservoirs, batteries, electronics, sensors, connectors, software, safety systems, and maintenance procedures. Tethering remains a recurring limitation: many soft robots are demonstrated with external compressors, power supplies, or computers. Untethered soft robotics is therefore a major research direction.[12,16]

11. Applications and examples

11.1 Industrial gripping and logistics

Soft grippers are attractive for handling products with uncertain shape, delicate surfaces, or variable pose. They are used or investigated for food handling, agriculture, e-commerce, bin picking, textiles, and collaborative workcells.[4,21] Their main advantage is forgiving contact; their main challenge is delivering industrial reliability, speed, cleanliness, and repeatability under thousands or millions of cycles.[21,24]

11.2 Medical and surgical robotics

Soft robots are well suited to medical contexts because human tissue is soft, delicate, and deformable. Soft catheters, continuum manipulators, wearable rehabilitation devices, implantable devices, and minimally invasive tools can reduce contact stress and navigate tortuous anatomy.[17,25,31] Medical deployment also raises strict constraints on sterilization, biocompatibility, imaging compatibility, failure modes, regulatory approval, and clinician workflow.

11.3 Wearable robots and exosuits

Soft wearable robots assist or augment human movement using textiles, pneumatic bladders, cables, and body-mounted actuators. Compared with rigid exoskeletons, exosuits can be lighter and more comfortable, but they must transmit useful forces through soft tissue and clothing without causing discomfort or misalignment.[25] Applications include gait assistance, rehabilitation, industrial ergonomics, hand assistance, and haptic feedback.

11.4 Marine and field robotics

Soft marine robots can interact with fragile organisms and cluttered underwater environments. The SoFi robotic fish demonstrated oceanic testing and three-dimensional swimming while recording marine life.[13] Deep-sea soft robots have explored pressure-tolerant designs inspired by animals that live in high-pressure environments.[14] Field systems must solve waterproofing, biofouling, communication, localization, energy storage, and robustness.

11.5 Search, rescue, and hazardous environments

Soft robots can squeeze through gaps, survive impacts, and distribute contact forces, making them appealing for inspection, disaster response, and hazardous environments.[1,16] Practical deployment still requires rugged electronics, autonomy, untethered power, and reliable locomotion over dust, rubble, fluids, and sharp debris.

11.6 Education and rapid prototyping

Soft robotics has become accessible to students because many devices can be fabricated using silicone, 3D printed molds, tubing, syringes, hobby pumps, microcontrollers, and open design documentation. The Soft Robotics Toolkit provides open resources for design, fabrication, modeling, characterization, and control, lowering the barrier to entry for labs and classrooms.[41]

12. Design workflow and evaluation metrics

A practical soft robot project should begin with the task rather than the material. A gripper for strawberries, a wearable ankle-assist exosuit, a soft endoscope, and a swimming robot need different definitions of success. The designer should specify the object or environment, payload, force limits, allowable deformation, response time, duty cycle, safety requirements, sensing needs, and failure consequences.[1,21,25]

12.1 A practical workflow

  1. Define the interaction. Identify what the robot touches, how uncertain the contact is, and what damage or safety limits apply.
  2. Choose the morphology. Decide which behavior should be passive and which should be actively controlled.
  3. Select actuation. Compare pressure, tendon, electrical, magnetic, thermal, or hybrid actuation against force, speed, energy, packaging, and safety requirements.
  4. Design sensing early. Sensor placement should be co-designed with expected strain fields and control variables.
  5. Prototype and characterize. Measure pressure-motion curves, blocked force, response time, hysteresis, fatigue, leakage, and failure modes.
  6. Close the loop. Start with pressure or displacement feedback, then add shape, tactile, or learned feedback as needed.
  7. Test in the real task. Soft robots often succeed in benchtop motion tests but fail under realistic contacts, contamination, aging, or integration constraints.

12.2 Evaluation metrics

Common metrics include range of motion, tip force, blocked force, force-to-weight ratio, bandwidth, response time, energy consumption, efficiency, maximum strain, payload, positional error, repeatability, hysteresis, cycle life, leakage rate, grasp success rate, object damage rate, and recovery after overload.[10,15,21] For wearable robots, comfort, metabolic cost, skin pressure, donning time, thermal comfort, and user adaptation are also important.[25]

Soft robot papers sometimes emphasize visual novelty, but fieldability depends on boring metrics: connectors that do not leak, sensors that do not drift, parts that survive cleaning, software that recovers from faults, and designs that can be manufactured repeatedly.[16,24]

13. Current frontiers and open problems

13.1 Untethered autonomy

Many soft robots still depend on rigid or external infrastructure. Untethered systems must integrate energy storage, pumps or alternative actuation, valves, computation, sensing, and communication without destroying the advantages of softness.[12,16] Research on soft batteries, compact pumps, chemical control, pneumatic logic, and distributed electronics is therefore central to the field’s future.

13.2 Variable stiffness and controllable rigidity

Pure softness is not always optimal. A robot may need to be soft during contact, stiff during load bearing, and rigid during precision positioning. Variable-stiffness methods include granular jamming, layer jamming, fiber jamming, low-melting-point alloys, shape-memory materials, antagonistic actuation, and inflatable structures.[2,15,19]

13.3 Integrated actuation and sensing

Biological muscles, tendons, and skin are deeply integrated sensory-motor systems. Soft robotics is moving toward materials and structures where actuation and sensing are co-located rather than added as separate components. Integrated designs can improve state estimation and reduce packaging complexity, but they require careful decoupling of actuation signals from sensing signals.[33]

13.4 Durability, aging, and repair

Elastomers tear, fatigue, creep, absorb fluids, delaminate, and change properties over time. Conductive traces crack; liquid metal channels leak; pneumatic systems puncture; textile interfaces fray. Self-healing materials and replaceable modules are active research directions, but reliability engineering remains one of the biggest barriers to deployment.[2,16,24]

13.5 Standardization and benchmarks

Soft robot designs are difficult to compare because geometry, materials, manufacturing, and task setup vary widely. The field needs better benchmarks for actuator performance, gripper damage, sensor drift, fatigue, environmental robustness, and safety.[15,21,24] Shared repositories and open toolkits help, but standardized testing remains less mature than in conventional robotics.

13.6 Simulation-to-real transfer

Soft robot simulation is improving, but accurately simulating contact, friction, fluid-structure interaction, viscoelasticity, and manufacturing variation remains difficult. Better differentiable simulators, physics-informed learning, reduced-order models, and automated material characterization could make design optimization and learning-based control more reliable.[5,37,38]

14. Resources for learning and building

For a strong conceptual introduction, Rus and Tolley’s 2015 review remains a foundational overview of soft robot design, fabrication, and control.[1] Yasa and colleagues’ overview provides a broad modern survey of soft robotics, including materials, sensing, modeling, and future directions.[2] For hands-on fabrication, Marchese, Katzschmann, and Rus provide a practical guide to fluidic elastomer robot morphologies and fabrication routes.[10] For open educational material, the Soft Robotics Toolkit collects design documentation, fabrication guidance, control resources, and examples for researchers and educators.[41]

Readers interested in compliant mechanisms should study Howell’s text on compliant mechanism analysis and design.[8] Readers focused on sensing should begin with recent reviews of soft robotics sensing and sensor-integrated soft robots.[32,33] Readers focused on control should study learning-based control reviews alongside model-based continuum robotics literature, because high-performance soft robots increasingly combine physics and data.[36,38,39]

15. Conclusion

Soft robotics changes the central question of robot design. Instead of asking only how to command a rigid mechanism to follow a trajectory, the designer asks how material, geometry, actuation, sensing, and control can share responsibility for useful behavior. Underactuation is not merely a limitation; it is a way to let the body adapt. Compliance is not merely error; it is a tool for safety, grasping, locomotion, and environmental interaction. Soft actuators are not just motors made of rubber; they are integrated material-structure-energy systems. Soft sensors are not optional add-ons; they are necessary for closing the loop on deformable bodies.

The field’s greatest promise lies in machines that can go where rigid robots struggle: around people, inside bodies, through clutter, underwater, on delicate objects, and in environments where contact is too complex to pre-plan. The field’s greatest challenge is turning beautiful laboratory demonstrations into durable, manufacturable, controllable, and maintainable systems. Progress will come from co-design: materials with mechanics, actuators with sensors, morphology with control, and prototypes with real tasks from the beginning.[1,2,16]
