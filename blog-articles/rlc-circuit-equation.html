The RLC Circuit
Equation

A single second-order differential equation governs resonance, damping, settling, and frequency response across every linear analog system ever built. This is the grammar — derived, solved, drawn, and put to work.

01Why this one equation matters

Almost every dynamic behavior you can name in an analog system — the ring after a switch closes, the tone a tuned radio plucks from the ether, the overshoot that fries a chip, the slow crawl of an over-engineered settling time — is the same mathematics wearing different clothes. That mathematics is the resistor–inductor–capacitor circuit, and it lives in a single line.

Three passive elements, three constitutive laws, one loop. From them falls a linear, constant-coefficient, second-order ordinary differential equation. It is the smallest circuit able to store energy in two distinct forms — magnetic in the inductor, electric in the capacitor — and that duality is exactly what makes it oscillate, decay, and resonate. Once you can read this equation, you can read filters, oscillators, power-electronic converters, transmission-line ringing, antenna bandwidth, and the mechanical mass–spring–damper it is mathematically identical to.

The series form, with a driving source \(v_s(t)\) and loop current \(i(t)\), is the equation in the masthead above. Its companions — the charge form and the parallel-circuit dual — are simply the same physics projected onto different variables. The rest of this piece derives it from first principles, solves it in all three damping regimes, draws the pictures that make it intuitive, then traces it out into the engineering and scientific world it quietly underwrites.

Two energy stores and one dissipator: that is the entire anatomy of resonance.

02The physical circuit and its three laws

A series RLC circuit is a single closed loop containing a resistor \(R\), an inductor \(L\), and a capacitor \(C\), driven by a voltage source \(v_s(t)\). The same current \(i(t)\) flows through every element because there is only one path. Each element relates its voltage and current through a constitutive law:

\[ v_R = R\,i, \qquad v_L = L\,\frac{di}{dt}, \qquad i = C\,\frac{dv_C}{dt}\;\Longleftrightarrow\; v_C = \frac{1}{C}\int i\,dt \]

The resistor obeys Ohm's law and dissipates energy as heat; it is the only lossy element. The inductor opposes changes in current, storing energy \(\tfrac{1}{2}Li^{2}\) in its magnetic field. The capacitor opposes changes in voltage, storing energy \(\tfrac{1}{2}Cv_C^{2}\) in its electric field. Because the inductor and capacitor exchange energy back and forth while the resistor bleeds it away, the loop behaves like a struck bell whose tone fades.

To stitch these elements together we need one structural rule. Kirchhoff's voltage law (KVL) — the directed sum of voltages around any closed loop is zero — was formulated by Gustav Kirchhoff in 1845, generalizing Ohm's work and preceding Maxwell's.1 Together with the constitutive laws above, KVL is the only ingredient the derivation requires.

03Deriving the governing equation

Walk around the loop and sum the voltage rises and drops. The source supplies \(v_s(t)\); the three passive elements drop \(v_R\), \(v_L\), and \(v_C\). KVL demands they balance:

\[ v_L + v_R + v_C = v_s(t) \]

Substitute the constitutive laws. This yields the integro-differential form, which carries the integral because the capacitor's voltage is the running accumulation of current:

\[ L\,\frac{di}{dt} + R\,i + \frac{1}{C}\int_{0}^{t} i\,d\tau \;=\; v_s(t) \]

Differentiating once with respect to time removes the integral and gives the clean second-order equation in the loop current — the current form:

\[ \boxed{\,L\,\frac{d^{2}i}{dt^{2}} + R\,\frac{di}{dt} + \frac{1}{C}\,i \;=\; \frac{dv_s}{dt}\,} \]

Often it is cleaner to work in the capacitor charge \(q(t)\), since current is its derivative, \(i = \dfrac{dq}{dt}\). Substituting collapses the integral naturally and produces the charge form, which needs no differentiation of the source:

\[ L\,\frac{d^{2}q}{dt^{2}} + R\,\frac{dq}{dt} + \frac{1}{C}\,q \;=\; v_s(t) \]
Why two forms? The charge (or equivalently capacitor-voltage) form keeps the source un-differentiated, which is convenient for step and DC inputs. The current form is natural when the excitation is itself a current or when you care directly about \(i(t)\). They are the same physics; pick the variable that makes your boundary conditions simplest.

The parallel dual

Swap the roles of voltage and current and the parallel RLC circuit obeys the mirror-image equation. Writing it in the common capacitor voltage \(v\) with a driving current source \(i_s(t)\):

\[ C\,\frac{d^{2}v}{dt^{2}} + \frac{1}{R}\,\frac{dv}{dt} + \frac{1}{L}\,v \;=\; \frac{di_s}{dt} \]

The structure is identical; only the coefficients trade places. This duality is why a single body of solution theory covers both topologies — and, as we will see, covers mechanical, acoustic, and thermal systems too.

04The canonical form and its parameters

To solve and compare circuits we normalize. Divide the source-free charge equation by \(L\) and it takes the universal second-order shape every controls and physics text shares:

\[ \frac{d^{2}q}{dt^{2}} + 2\alpha\,\frac{dq}{dt} + \omega_0^{2}\,q = 0 \qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad \frac{d^{2}q}{dt^{2}} + 2\zeta\omega_0\,\frac{dq}{dt} + \omega_0^{2}\,q = 0 \]

Three derived parameters carry all the physics. The undamped natural frequency \(\omega_0\) sets the pitch the circuit would ring at with no losses. The damping coefficient (or neper frequency) \(\alpha\) sets how fast the envelope decays. Their ratio is the dimensionless damping ratio \(\zeta\), the single number that decides the qualitative behavior.6

\[ \omega_0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}}, \qquad \alpha = \frac{R}{2L}, \qquad \zeta = \frac{\alpha}{\omega_0} = \frac{R}{2}\sqrt{\frac{C}{L}} \]

The resonant frequency \(f_0 = \dfrac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{LC}}\) is the same for series and parallel circuits and is the workhorse formula of tuned-circuit design.8 A fourth, deeply useful figure of merit is the quality factor \(Q\), measuring how many radians of oscillation occur per e-folding of decay — equivalently, the sharpness of resonance:

\[ Q = \frac{\omega_0}{2\alpha} = \frac{1}{2\zeta} = \frac{1}{R}\sqrt{\frac{L}{C}} \quad\text{(series)}, \qquad Q = R\sqrt{\frac{C}{L}} \quad\text{(parallel)} \]

High \(Q\) means low loss, a tall narrow resonance, and long ringing; low \(Q\) means a broad, heavily damped response.16 Notice the series and parallel quality factors are reciprocal in \(R\) — a large series resistance kills \(Q\), while a large parallel resistance preserves it. That single inversion is responsible for a great deal of practical circuit topology choice.

05Solving it: the three damping regimes

The natural (source-free) response is found by assuming \(q(t)=e^{st}\) and substituting into the canonical homogeneous equation. The exponentials cancel, leaving the characteristic equation:

\[ s^{2} + 2\alpha\,s + \omega_0^{2} = 0 \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad s_{1,2} = -\alpha \pm \sqrt{\alpha^{2}-\omega_0^{2}} \]

Everything hinges on the discriminant \(\alpha^{2}-\omega_0^{2}\) — equivalently, on whether \(\zeta\) is greater than, equal to, or less than one. The boundary \(\alpha=\omega_0\) translates into the famous critical condition \(R^{2}=4L/C\).5 Three qualitatively distinct worlds result.

Over-damped  (\(\zeta \gt 1,\ \alpha \gt \omega_0\))

Two real, distinct, negative roots. The response is a sum of two decaying exponentials — a sluggish, monotonic return to equilibrium with no overshoot and no oscillation. The system is over-braked.

\[ q(t) = A_1 e^{s_1 t} + A_2 e^{s_2 t}, \qquad s_{1,2}\in\mathbb{R}_{\lt 0} \]

Critically damped  (\(\zeta = 1,\ \alpha = \omega_0\))

A repeated real root \(s=-\alpha\). This is the knife-edge that returns to equilibrium in the shortest possible time without overshoot — the prize target for many control and instrument designs.

\[ q(t) = \left(A_1 + A_2\,t\right) e^{-\alpha t} \]

Under-damped  (\(\zeta \lt 1,\ \alpha \lt \omega_0\))

A complex-conjugate pair of roots, \(s_{1,2} = -\alpha \pm j\omega_d\). The response is a sinusoid at the damped natural frequency \(\omega_d=\sqrt{\omega_0^{2}-\alpha^{2}}\), riding inside an exponentially shrinking envelope. This is ringing — the regime of bells, tuned radios, and most overshoot trouble.

\[ q(t) = e^{-\alpha t}\Big(B_1\cos\omega_d t + B_2\sin\omega_d t\Big), \qquad \omega_d = \omega_0\sqrt{1-\zeta^{2}} \]

The total solution to a driven circuit is the sum of this natural response and a particular (forced) response that matches the source. For a DC step, the forced part is a constant; for a sinusoid, it is the steady-state phasor solution we turn to next. Two initial conditions — typically the initial inductor current and capacitor voltage — pin down the constants \(A_1,A_2\) or \(B_1,B_2\).7

06Reading the s-plane

The characteristic roots \(s_{1,2}\) are the circuit's poles. Plotting them on the complex \(s\)-plane turns the algebra into a picture: the horizontal axis is the decay rate (\(-\alpha\)), the vertical axis is the oscillation frequency (\(\pm\omega_d\)). The distance from the origin to a complex pole is always \(\omega_0\), so as you vary \(\zeta\) at fixed \(\omega_0\), the poles ride a circle of radius \(\omega_0\).

This single diagram encodes stability and dynamics at a glance. Real part negative? The response decays — the passive RLC circuit is unconditionally stable because \(R\), and therefore \(\alpha\), is positive. Poles near the imaginary axis (\(\zeta\to 0\)) mean lightly damped, long-ringing, high-\(Q\) behavior. Poles far out on the real axis mean fast, sluggish, over-damped decay. The pole picture is the same tool used to design control loops and filters, which is why the RLC circuit is the canonical teaching example for both.

07Resonance and frequency response

Drive the loop with a sinusoid and analyze it with phasors. Each element contributes an impedance; in series they add. The capacitor and inductor reactances have opposite signs, so they fight each other:

\[ Z(j\omega) = R + j\omega L + \frac{1}{j\omega C} = R + j\!\left(\omega L - \frac{1}{\omega C}\right) \]

At resonance the two reactances cancel exactly, the imaginary part vanishes, and the impedance collapses to its purely resistive minimum \(|Z|=R\). For a series circuit this maximizes the current and the voltage transferred to the resistor.9 Maxwell first calculated this maximum-response-at-resonance behavior for an inductive–capacitive circuit driven by alternating current in 1868.2 The cancellation occurs precisely when

\[ \omega L = \frac{1}{\omega C} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \omega_0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}} \]

The two frequencies where the output falls to \(1/\sqrt{2}\) of its peak — the half-power or \(-3\,\text{dB}\) points — bracket the bandwidth. For the series circuit the bandwidth (in rad/s) is simply \(R/L\), and it ties directly to the quality factor:10

\[ \text{BW} = \omega_2 - \omega_1 = \frac{R}{L} = \frac{\omega_0}{Q} \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad Q = \frac{\omega_0}{\text{BW}} = \frac{f_0}{\Delta f} \]

This is the engineering meaning of \(Q\): the center frequency divided by the bandwidth. A radio front-end with \(Q=100\) at \(100\,\text{MHz}\) admits a \(1\,\text{MHz}\) slice and rejects the rest — the entire premise of tuning. A high \(Q\) gives sharp selectivity; a low \(Q\) gives a wide, forgiving passband.16

Resonance is two reactances agreeing to disappear, leaving only resistance behind.

08A worked example, end to end

Take concrete values: \(L = 10\ \text{mH}\), \(C = 1\ \mu\text{F}\), and a resistor we will vary. First the fixed quantities:

\[ \omega_0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{(0.01)(10^{-6})}} = 10{,}000\ \text{rad/s} \;\Rightarrow\; f_0 \approx 1591.5\ \text{Hz}, \qquad R_{\text{crit}} = 2\sqrt{\tfrac{L}{C}} = 200\ \Omega \]

Now choose \(R = 20\ \Omega\). The damping coefficient and ratio follow immediately:

\[ \alpha = \frac{R}{2L} = \frac{20}{0.02} = 1000\ \text{s}^{-1}, \qquad \zeta = \frac{\alpha}{\omega_0} = 0.1, \qquad Q = \frac{1}{2\zeta} = 5 \]

Because \(\zeta = 0.1 \lt 1\), the circuit is firmly under-damped. It will ring at the damped frequency

\[ \omega_d = \omega_0\sqrt{1-\zeta^{2}} = 10{,}000\sqrt{0.99} \approx 9950\ \text{rad/s}\;(\approx 1583\ \text{Hz}) \]

with a bandwidth \(\text{BW} = R/L = 2000\ \text{rad/s}\) — consistent with \(Q = \omega_0/\text{BW} = 5\). The envelope decays as \(e^{-1000t}\), so the ringing is essentially gone after a few milliseconds (a \(2\%\) settling time of roughly \(4/\alpha = 4\ \text{ms}\)). Dial \(R\) up to \(200\ \Omega\) and the circuit sits exactly at critical damping; push past it to \(R = 400\ \Omega\) and the poles split to real values \(s_1 \approx -2680\), \(s_2 \approx -37{,}320\ \text{s}^{-1}\) — over-damped, no ringing at all.

10Where it shows up: applications

Because the RLC equation is the minimal model of a resonant, dissipative system, it recurs everywhere energy is stored in two forms and slowly lost. A non-exhaustive tour:

Radio, RF, and tuning

Series and parallel resonant circuits are the original frequency selectors. They let a receiver pluck one narrow band out of the entire radio spectrum and reject the rest — the function that made wireless telegraphy possible.16 The selectivity is set directly by \(Q\): higher \(Q\), narrower channel.

Filters and signal shaping

Low-pass, high-pass, band-pass, and notch filters are all RLC responses read off at different nodes. A band-pass filter is the series resonance curve of Figure 4; a notch filter is its parallel complement. Beyond filtering, RLC building blocks appear in oscillators, power management, signal shaping, and vibration analysis.10

Power electronics and the grid

Switching converters ring when parasitic inductance meets junction capacitance; designers add RLC snubbers to damp the resulting spikes. Resonant converters deliberately operate near \(\omega_0\) for soft switching. On the grid, capacitor banks and line inductance form RLC loops whose resonances must be avoided to prevent harmonic amplification.

Signal integrity

Every fast digital edge excites the parasitic RLC of its package, board trace, and via. The result is overshoot, undershoot, and ringing that the under-damped solution above describes exactly. Controlling these is the daily work of high-speed board design, and it is why critical damping — the fastest clean settle — is so prized.

Wireless power transfer

Modern inductive charging pairs two high-\(Q\) resonant LC tanks tuned to the same frequency so that energy crosses an air gap efficiently. A transmit coil and receive coil each behave as an RLC resonator, and raising the system \(Q\) raises transfer efficiency.12 The resonator coil in a magnetic-resonant WPT system is modeled directly as an equivalent RLC circuit whose \(Q\) governs efficiency.13

The mechanical (and universal) analogy

The deepest reason to learn the RLC equation is that it is not really about electricity. A mass on a spring with a dashpot obeys the identical mathematics:

The same equation, four domains
QuantitySeries RLCMechanicalRole
InertiaInductance \(L\)Mass \(m\)stores kinetic-type energy
StiffnessElastance \(1/C\)Spring \(k\)stores potential-type energy
LossResistance \(R\)Damper \(c\)dissipates energy

Set \(m\ddot{x} + c\dot{x} + kx = F(t)\) beside the charge form and they are line-for-line the same. Acoustic resonators, suspension systems, building sway, and PLL loop dynamics all collapse onto this template. Learn it once electrically and you have learned a large slice of classical engineering dynamics.

11Tools and source code

You rarely solve these by hand past the teaching stage. The dominant tool is SPICE (Simulation Program with Integrated Circuit Emphasis), created at UC Berkeley and first presented publicly in 1973 by Laurence Nagel and Donald Pederson.14 Released into the public domain from the start, SPICE became the worldwide standard circuit simulator and earned an IEEE Milestone in 2011.15 Its descendants — ngspice, LTspice, Qucs, and the SPICE engines inside every commercial EDA suite — all trace back to that Berkeley code.

For analysis and plotting, Python with NumPy and SciPy integrates the state-space form directly. The snippet below classifies the regime and simulates the step response of the very example worked above.

Python: simulate and classify

rlc_sim.py
import numpy as np
from scipy.integrate import odeint
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Series RLC component values
L = 10e-3      # henries
C = 1e-6       # farads
R = 20.0       # ohms  (try 20, 200, 400)

# Canonical parameters
w0    = 1/np.sqrt(L*C)     # undamped natural frequency
alpha = R/(2*L)            # damping (neper) frequency
zeta  = alpha/w0            # damping ratio
Q     = 1/(2*zeta)         # quality factor

if zeta < 1:
    regime = "under-damped"
elif np.isclose(zeta, 1):
    regime = "critically damped"
else:
    regime = "over-damped"

print(f"f0={w0/(2*np.pi):.1f} Hz | zeta={zeta:.3f} | Q={Q:.2f} -> {regime}")

# State-space form of  L q'' + R q' + q/C = v_s(t),  with x=[q, i], i=q'
def rlc(x, t, vs):
    q, i = x
    di = (vs - R*i - q/C) / L
    return [i, di]

t  = np.linspace(0, 0.02, 4000)
sol = odeint(rlc, [0.0, 0.0], t, args=(1.0,))   # 1 V step

plt.plot(t*1e3, sol[:, 0]/C)   # capacitor voltage q/C
plt.xlabel("time [ms]"); plt.ylabel("v_C(t) [V]")
plt.title(f"Series RLC step response — {regime}")
plt.grid(True); plt.show()
Closed-form check For a quick analytical pass without numerical integration, SciPy's scipy.signal.lti([1/(L*C)], [1, R/L, 1/(L*C)]) defines the second-order transfer function directly, and .step() returns the same curve — a useful cross-check against the integrated solution.

Other capable environments include MATLAB/Simulink (transfer-function and state-space toolboxes), Wolfram Mathematica for symbolic root analysis, and online resonance calculators for back-of-envelope component selection.8

12A short history

The story begins with storage. The capacitor was born in 1745–1746 as the Leyden jar, discovered independently by Ewald Georg von Kleist and Pieter van Musschenbroek of Leiden — the first device able to accumulate and hold a large electric charge for release at will.3 For a century it was a curiosity that delivered shocks; the dynamics inside it went unexamined.

The oscillation was glimpsed indirectly. After noticing that a Leyden-jar discharge magnetized needles in alternating layers, observers in the 1820s conjectured the discharge was not a single pulse but a train of oscillations; Joseph Henry reached the same conclusion independently in 1842.2 The decisive step was mathematical. In 1853 William Thomson — Lord Kelvin — proved that discharging a Leyden jar through an inductance must be oscillatory, and derived its resonant frequency, laying the foundation for the entire theory of electrical oscillation later built upon by Hertz and Lodge.24

Kelvin wrote down the ring before anyone could see it; Feddersen then photographed the proof.

Visible confirmation arrived when Berend Wilhelm Feddersen photographed the oscillating spark of a resonant Leyden-jar circuit in a rotating mirror in the late 1850s. In 1868 James Clerk Maxwell calculated the response of an inductive–capacitive circuit to alternating current and showed the response peaks at the resonant frequency — the resonance curve of Figure 4.2 The structural law that lets us assemble these elements had already been published in 1845 by Gustav Kirchhoff, generalizing Ohm.1 Oliver Heaviside's operational calculus in the following decades turned the differential equation into algebra, the precursor of the Laplace-transform methods every engineer now uses.

The final transformation was computational. In 1973, Nagel and Pederson's SPICE made numerical solution of arbitrarily complex RLC-and-beyond networks routine and free, and the hand-solved RLC equation became the conceptual core of a tool that simulates billions of transistors.1415

13The research frontier

A two-hundred-year-old equation is not finished. The relationship between quality factor and fractional bandwidth — taken for granted in textbooks — turns out to be subtle for radiating systems, and recent work shows that only certain definitions of \(Q\) remain strictly proportional to bandwidth for a resonant antenna modeled as an RLC circuit.11 Antenna miniaturization runs straight into this: the smaller the radiator, the higher its \(Q\) and the narrower its achievable bandwidth, a fundamental limit expressed in RLC terms.

Wireless power transfer is an active arena where the RLC model is the design surface. Pushing operating frequency into the tens of megahertz raises system \(Q\) and mid-range transfer efficiency, but at the cost of switching losses — a direct, quantitative trade governed by the resonator's RLC parameters.12 In conducting media, eddy-current loss raises the coil's effective resistance, lowers \(Q\), and shifts the optimal frequency, a result derived by treating each coil as an equivalent RLC resonator.13 Metamaterials, MEMS resonators, and superconducting microwave cavities all extend the same second-order template into regimes the Leyden-jar era could not have imagined.

14Key concepts at a glance

The vocabulary of second-order response
ConceptSymbol / formulaWhat it tells you
Natural frequency\(\omega_0 = 1/\sqrt{LC}\)the pitch of the undamped ring
Damping coefficient\(\alpha = R/2L\)how fast the envelope decays
Damping ratio\(\zeta = \alpha/\omega_0\)which regime you are in
Quality factor\(Q = 1/2\zeta\)resonance sharpness, ring length
Damped frequency\(\omega_d = \omega_0\sqrt{1-\zeta^2}\)the actual ringing frequency
Critical resistance\(R_{\text{crit}} = 2\sqrt{L/C}\)the no-overshoot boundary
Bandwidth\(\text{BW} = \omega_0/Q\)the passband width

If you remember only one thing: the discriminant of the characteristic equation — encoded in \(\zeta\) — decides everything. Less than one and the circuit rings; equal to one and it settles as fast as it can without overshoot; greater than one and it crawls home. Every filter, every snubber, every settling-time specification is a choice of where to put \(\zeta\).
