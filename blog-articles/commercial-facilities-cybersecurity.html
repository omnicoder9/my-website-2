01 Foundations

A sector defined by openness

Of the sixteen critical infrastructure sectors the United States recognises, the Commercial Facilities Sector is the one you walk through without thinking. It is the arena on game night, the mall at the holidays, the conference hotel, the casino floor, the studio backlot and the office tower. The federal definition is unusually plain: a diverse range of sites that draw large crowds of people for shopping, business, entertainment, or lodging.1

What makes the sector distinctive is not what it contains but how it operates. Facilities here run on the principle of open public access — the public moves freely, without the visible security barriers that gate a power plant or a chemical works — and the overwhelming majority are privately owned and operated, with minimal day-to-day interaction with the federal government.1 The Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA), within the Department of Homeland Security, serves as the Sector Risk Management Agency, but its instrument is persuasion, not regulation. The sector's strategy is explicitly built on voluntary, collaborative effort coordinated through the National Infrastructure Protection Plan, rather than the mandatory cybersecurity rules of the kind that bind the electricity grid.13

CISA organises the sector into eight subsectors, each with its own technology culture and its own attack surface:1 public assembly (arenas, stadiums, aquariums, zoos, museums, convention centres); sports leagues; gaming (casinos); lodging (hotels, motels, conference centres, campgrounds); outdoor events (theme parks, fairs, parades, marathons); entertainment and media (motion-picture studios, broadcast media); real estate (office and apartment buildings, mixed-use developments, self-storage); and retail (shopping centres and malls). The common thread is people at scale — and, increasingly, the dense lattice of connected systems required to move, count, charge, cool, film, light and admit them.

That lattice is the subject of this dossier. Within the broader taxonomy of critical infrastructure — sixteen sectors whose incapacitation would have a debilitating effect on national security, the economy, or public health and safety2 — commercial facilities occupy a peculiar position. They are rarely the most consequential single target. But they are the most numerous, the most accessible, the most heterogeneous in ownership, and the least uniformly defended. And because they sit at the intersection of physical crowds and connected control systems, a compromise here is rarely only a data problem.

On the partnership model

The sector's governance runs through two paired bodies: a Sector Coordinating Council (the self-organised voice of private owners and operators) and a Government Coordinating Council (the interagency counterpart chaired by the risk-management agency). Together they produced the Commercial Facilities Sector-Specific Plan and a sector-tailored guide to implementing the NIST Cybersecurity Framework.3 Sector Coordinating Councils are, by design, self-governed and voluntary — they encourage practices rather than impose them.4 This is the structural fact that shapes everything downstream: in this sector, security is a choice each owner makes, not a standard each owner meets.

02 Technology

The technology stack of a crowd

A modern commercial facility is not one network; it is a federation of them, often installed by different vendors at different times and bridged together by necessity. Understanding the sector's risk means first understanding the systems that crowds never see.

The operational technology beneath the floor

At the base sits a layer of operational technology (OT) — the building automation and control systems (BAS/BMS) that govern heating, ventilation and air conditioning, lighting, elevators and escalators, along with the physical access control systems (PACS) that drive badge readers and electronic locks, the video management systems behind the cameras, and fire and life-safety controls. This is not an analogy to industrial control; it is industrial control. When NIST rewrote its federal guidance on securing OT in 2023, it explicitly listed building automation systems and physical access control systems among the canonical examples of operational technology — alongside the industrial control systems that run factories and the grid.56 The same publication frames the central tension of the entire sector: OT carries unique performance, reliability and safety requirements, because these systems do not merely process data — they detect or cause direct change in the physical environment.5

The commercial and guest-facing layer

Above the OT layer runs the technology that actually transacts with the public, and it varies sharply by subsector. Retail and gaming live on point-of-sale (POS) systems and payment processing. Lodging adds property management systems (PMS) that hold reservations and guest identity, plus electronic key systems — magnetic-stripe or RFID room locks tied to the front desk. Casinos layer on gaming-management systems, slot-machine networks, loyalty databases and the famous overhead surveillance estate. Public assembly and sports leagues run ticketing platforms, mass-notification and public-address systems, and digital signage. Almost every facility now offers guest or customer Wi-Fi, and almost every facility has quietly accumulated an Internet of Things — smart thermostats, occupancy sensors, environmental monitors, networked appliances — each one a small computer with an IP address and, frequently, a forgotten default password.

The defining characteristic of this stack is not any single component but the convergence between them. The flat networks that once kept a vending sensor and a payment terminal comfortably separate have largely dissolved. As later sections will show in detail, the seam between these layers — between the cheap connected device at the edge and the valuable database at the core — is precisely where this sector keeps getting hurt.

03 Threats

The threat landscape & its case files

The sector's incidents fall into recognisable families: theft of personal and payment data; ransomware and extortion; compromise of the operational systems that touch the physical world; social engineering of the people who hold the keys; and the third-party supply chain that connects all of the above. The cleanest way to understand each family is to read the case file.

First, scale. The financial weather of the sector tracks the global picture, and the global picture is severe but uneven. The 2025 edition of IBM's long-running breach-cost study — drawn from 600 organisations across seventeen industries — put the global average cost of a data breach at USD 4.44 million, the first decline in five years, driven largely by faster AI-assisted detection.7 The United States diverged sharply in the other direction, reaching an all-time high of USD 10.22 million per breach, and — critically for this sector — entertainment, media, hospitality and retail were among the few industries whose costs rose against the global trend.8 Phishing was the single most common initial vector (16% of breaches), with supply-chain compromise close behind at roughly 15%.8 Where extortion was involved, the average incident still cost USD 5.08 million.9 Those two vectors — the phished employee and the trusted vendor — are the through-line of nearly every case below.

Family I — Data theft through the trusted vendor

No incident in the sector is more instructive than the 2013 breach of the retailer Target, because it connects all the pieces. The attackers did not begin at Target at all. They began at Fazio Mechanical Services, a refrigeration and HVAC contractor in Sharpsburg, Pennsylvania, whose network credentials for Target's vendor portal were harvested via a phishing email.10 From that foothold, the intruders moved laterally across a network that failed to segment a heating contractor's access from the payment estate, ultimately pushing custom malware onto point-of-sale devices across roughly 1,800 stores.1011 The toll — about 40 million payment cards and personal data on a further 70 million people — was compounded by a process failure: Target's own intrusion-detection tooling fired alerts that no one acted on. Total costs exceeded USD 200 million, and both the chief executive and chief information officer ultimately resigned.11

Lodging produced an even larger data loss, and a longer-running one. The breach of Starwood's guest-reservation database — inherited by Marriott when it acquired Starwood in 2016 — began around July 2014 and went undetected until September 2018, a four-year residency that touched, at its upper bound, some 383 million guest records.1213 Among the exposed data were 5.25 million unencrypted passport numbers.12 The U.S. Federal Trade Commission, which in October 2024 required Marriott and Starwood to stand up a comprehensive information-security programme certified annually for twenty years, characterised the episode as a failure to implement reasonable data security across three breaches between 2014 and 2020.12 Later reporting linked the intrusion to a nation-state actor, and noted that the absence of cybersecurity due diligence during the Starwood acquisition was itself a root cause — the buyer inherited a network the attackers were already inside.14

Family II — Ransomware, extortion and the help desk

If data theft is the sector's chronic condition, ransomware is its acute one — and the gaming subsector delivered the defining example in September 2023, when two of Las Vegas's largest operators were hit within days of each other by the same loose threat collective. The group, tracked as Scattered Spider (UNC3944) and deploying ransomware from the ALPHV/BlackCat affiliate ecosystem, did not exploit a software flaw. It made a phone call.15 Researching an MGM Resorts employee on LinkedIn, the attackers impersonated that person to MGM's IT help desk, talked their way into credentials, and escalated to administrative control of the company's Okta and Azure identity environments — a foothold that began with a roughly ten-minute conversation.16 What followed was the sector's nightmare made literal: as MGM took systems offline to contain the spread, the disruption rippled into reservation systems, digital room keys and slot machines on the casino floor, with ransomware ultimately deployed across more than a hundred hypervisors.16

A company valued in the tens of billions was, in effect, defeated by a phone call to its help desk.

On the MGM Resorts intrusion, September 202316

The financial contrast between the two victims became a case study in itself. MGM declined to pay, working with law enforcement while absorbing an estimated USD 100 million hit to its third quarter and losses approaching USD 8.4 million per day during the outage.1518 Caesars Entertainment, breached days earlier through the same social-engineering playbook against an IT vendor, paid a reported USD 15 million — and still had to disclose, in a securities filing, that attackers had acquired a copy of its loyalty-programme database including driver's-license and Social Security numbers for a significant number of members.17

The same crew, the same method, surfaced again in 2025 against British retail. In April and May, Scattered Spider struck Marks & Spencer, the Co-operative Group and Harrods; M&S was hit hardest, its systems breached after attackers tricked staff into resetting admin-level credentials at a third-party vendor. The resulting ransomware disrupted e-commerce across more than 1,400 stores and was estimated to have cost roughly £300 million in lost revenue while wiping around £1 billion off the company's market value.19

Family III — When the attack reaches the physical world

The sector's most unsettling incidents are the ones where the compromise stops being abstract. Consider the most-cited IoT cautionary tale in security: an unnamed North American casino whose high-roller database was exfiltrated through an internet-connected thermometer in a lobby fish tank. As Darktrace recounted the episode, attackers used the smart thermometer to gain a foothold, located the high-value database, and pulled it back out across the network through the same aquarium device — reportedly some ten gigabytes routed to an external destination.20 The fish tank is funny until you generalise it: the device was on the network because it was convenient, it was insecure because it was cheap, and it was a bridge because nothing said it shouldn't be.

Lodging supplied the cyber-physical lesson in its most tactile form. At the Romantik Seehotel Jägerwirt, a four-star hotel in the Austrian Alps, ransomware repeatedly struck the systems running the electronic key-card programming, the reservation desk and the cash desk. During one incident the front desk could not issue new room keys for arriving guests, and management paid roughly €1,500 in Bitcoin to restore operations.2122 A crucial detail, widely misreported at the time, is that no guest was ever locked inside a room — fire codes require electronic locks to open manually from the inside — but the operational paralysis was real enough that the hotel publicly mused about reverting to physical keys.21

The fullest demonstration of physical consequence came in October 2021, at an office building in Germany whose building-automation engineering firm suddenly lost contact with its own installation. An attacker had reached the BAS through a UDP port exposed to the public internet and turned the system's own security key against its operators, locking them out and bricking roughly three-quarters of several hundred devices — light switches, motion detectors, window-shutter controllers and more.2326 There was no ransom note and no clear motive; restoring the building took weeks of reverting to manual control, with engineers physically flipping circuit breakers to keep the lights on.23 The flaw — later dubbed KNXlock, after the KNX automation standard involved — prompted a vendor security bulletin and continued to affect buildings into 2023.24 It was not the first such event: a 2016 distributed-denial-of-service attack left two apartment buildings in Finland without heating for days.26

A note on the ransomware curve

These are not isolated curiosities. As a sector resilience analysis observed, CISA published a dedicated fact sheet on the ransomware threat to operational technology as early as June 2021, against a backdrop of a 150% rise in ransomware attacks in 2020 and a tripling of payouts — and the threat now ranks among the most significant facing the Commercial Facilities Sector specifically.25 The attacker economics are stark: the cost of compromising a building can be trivial relative to what an operator will pay to turn it back on.25

04 The OT problem

When the building is the vulnerability

The recurring theme of the case files is that the building's own control systems are the soft target. To understand why, you have to look at the protocols and the installed base — because the weakness is not incidental, it is architectural and historical.

Protocols that were never meant to be defended

Building automation runs on a small family of communication protocols — chiefly BACnet, Modbus, KNX and LonWorks — that were designed in an era of physically isolated networks, when an “air gap” was assumed and security was therefore an afterthought. The consequence is that these protocols, in their common forms, lack basic authentication and encryption; secure variants such as BACnet Secure Connect and KNX IP Secure exist but are optional and unevenly deployed.2630 BACnet alone, first introduced in 1995 and standardised by ISO in 2003, commands more than 60% of the building-automation market — which means the dominant language of smart buildings is, by default, an unauthenticated one.26 Academic reviews of the field map BAS architecture across three tiers — management, automation and field — and catalogue protocol-specific weaknesses across exactly these four dominant standards, noting that the resulting exposure can produce occupant discomfort, excessive energy use and unexpected equipment downtime.30

The installed base is old, and the internet found it anyway

Compounding the protocol problem is the building stock itself. Roughly three-quarters of U.S. commercial buildings were constructed before 2000, many still running decades-old controls; “retrofitting” them with smart technology typically means bridging legacy, insecure protocols onto modern IP networks — a process that quietly exposes the old systems to lateral movement and remote attack.29 And the exposure is not theoretical. Analysis of building-management platforms by the OT-security firm Claroty found that three-quarters contained vulnerabilities already known to have been exploited, and nearly seven in ten carried flaws previously used in ransomware campaigns; using the Shodan device-search engine, the same work identified more than 23,000 BACnet or KNX devices directly reachable from the public internet.28 Finding a vulnerable building, in other words, can be as simple as a search query.

There is also a cultural fault line that the technology obscures. Building-management systems, as one analysis put it, are commonly not on the radar of corporate cybersecurity teams at all — they are installed and maintained by staff who are building engineers first and IT personnel a distant second.28 The result is an ownership gap: the systems most capable of producing physical harm are frequently the ones least integrated into the security programme. Researchers have repeatedly demonstrated the stakes — vendors' HVAC web interfaces with broken authentication allowing privilege escalation, and test malware (including a self-propagating worm) exposing software flaws across thousands of BAS devices — underscoring that a compromised controller is both an operational hazard and a quiet backdoor into the corporate network it shares.2627

05 Interdependency

Blast radius: the cross-sector problem

A breach in this sector rarely stays in this sector. The reason is interdependency — commercial facilities both depend on other critical infrastructure to function, and can become the vector through which other sectors are harmed. The sixteen-sector model is not a filing system; it is a description of a single, coupled machine.2

Inbound dependency: what a facility needs to operate

Every large facility is a consumer of other sectors' outputs. It cannot run without the Energy sector's electricity, the Water and Wastewater sector's supply and drainage, the Communications and Information Technology sectors' connectivity and cloud, the Transportation sector's access, the Financial Services sector's payment rails, and the Emergency Services sector's response. A cyber disruption to any of those upstream sectors cascades into the mall, the stadium and the hotel as surely as a power cut. This is the ordinary, gravitational dependency that the National Infrastructure Protection Plan exists to manage.

Outbound contagion: the facility as a vector

The more interesting — and less appreciated — direction is outbound. The Target breach is the simplest illustration: tens of millions of live payment cards flowed directly from a retailer's compromised POS estate into the fraud economy of the Financial Services sector.10 But the most striking outbound path runs from the building's own machinery to the electricity grid. Princeton researchers Soltan, Mittal and Poor demonstrated, in their 2018 USENIX Security work on what they named “Manipulation of demand via IoT” (MadIoT), that a botnet of high-wattage connected devices — exactly the air conditioners and heaters that fill commercial buildings — gives an adversary a unique lever on the grid's supply-demand balance.31 By synchronously switching such loads on and off, an attacker can induce frequency instability, force line failures by redistributing demand geographically, and in the worst modelled cases trigger cascading outages.32 The scale is sobering: the researchers estimated that a Mirai-sized botnet of compromised electric water heaters could shift demand in an area by 3,000 megawatts almost instantly — comparable, they noted, to commandeering the largest nuclear plant then deployed.33

The same mechanism can be aimed at money rather than stability. A later line of research — Georgia Tech's “IoT Skimmer” — showed that a high-wattage IoT botnet can nudge electricity demand just enough to manipulate wholesale energy-market prices, for an attacker's financial profit or a competitor's loss, exploiting the day-ahead and real-time markets that clear grid supply.34 In both cases the insecure devices live overwhelmingly in commercial and residential buildings, while the victim is the energy and financial systems. As the MadIoT authors put it, their work illuminates the interdependency between the vulnerability of IoT and that of the power grid — a security problem that belongs to neither the systems-security community nor the power-engineering community alone.31

The shared-vendor amplifier

There is a third contagion path that needs no botnet. Commercial facilities share their building-automation, property-management and payment vendors with hospitals, government buildings, schools and energy control rooms. A single compromise of a widely-deployed BAS product or a common POS platform — the Target pattern, but at the vendor rather than the contractor — can therefore propagate across many sectors at once. This is why CISA's supply-chain emphasis, and the breach data showing supply-chain compromise as the second-most-common vector, matter so much here: the sector's defenders are only ever as secure as the least-secure product their landlords bought.8

Finally, there is a category of consequence unique to this sector because of what it physically is: a container for crowds. A cyber-physical attack that disabled mass-notification or evacuation systems during an emergency, or that manufactured one, would convert a network intrusion directly into a public-safety event — drawing in the Emergency Services and Healthcare and Public Health sectors. The open-public-access principle that defines the sector is, in the cyber-physical era, also its sharpest exposure.1

06 Mitigation

The defender's toolkit

Against this landscape, the sector is not without instruments. The mitigation story has four layers: the voluntary frameworks that structure a security programme; the OT-specific standards that govern control systems; the compliance regimes that bind payment and data; and the collaborative machinery that moves intelligence between owners. None is mandatory across the sector — but together they describe what good looks like.

The framework layer: NIST CSF 2.0

The foundational reference is the NIST Cybersecurity Framework, first published in 2014 and substantially revised as version 2.0 on 26 February 2024. CSF 2.0 retains the five original functions — Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond and Recover — and adds a sixth, Govern, which elevates leadership accountability, risk-management strategy and, notably, supply-chain risk to the centre of the model.35 The 2.0 revision also widened the framework's stated audience from critical-infrastructure operators to organisations of every size and sector, and reorganised its safeguards to reflect the new governance emphasis.36 For a sector this heterogeneous, the framework's value is precisely that it is not one-size-fits-all: it offers a common language and a maturity ladder that a corner shop and a stadium can both climb. CISA's sector-specific implementation guidance maps this framework onto commercial facilities directly.3

The OT layer: NIST SP 800-82r3 and IEC 62443

Because so much of the sector's risk lives in building controls, the OT-specific guidance is where the real work happens. NIST's Special Publication 800-82, Revision 3 (September 2023) is the federal guide to securing operational technology, and it is written precisely for the systems this sector runs — its scope was deliberately broadened from industrial control systems to all OT, explicitly including building automation and physical access control.56 Crucially, it adapts mainstream IT controls to OT realities, providing a tailored “OT overlay” for the SP 800-53 control catalogue and insisting that incident response in these environments prioritise safe operation above conventional confidentiality goals.5

The international counterpart is the ISA/IEC 62443 series, the globally preferred standard for industrial automation and control-system security. Its central architectural idea — partitioning a network into zones (groups of assets sharing security requirements) connected by tightly-controlled conduits — is the direct antidote to the flat, bridged networks that let a fish tank reach a database.40 Where information-security management standards such as ISO/IEC 27001 govern the policies and processes of protecting data, 62443 governs the segmentation and lifecycle of the control systems themselves — an essential distinction in environments where assets last twenty to thirty years and cannot simply be patched on a Tuesday.41

The compliance layer: PCI DSS

For the subsectors that take payment — retail, gaming and lodging above all — the binding regime is the Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard, maintained by the PCI Security Standards Council (formed in 2006 by the major card brands).39 The current version, 4.0.1, was published in June 2024 as a maintenance update to the substantial 4.0 rewrite, and its future-dated requirements became mandatory on 31 March 2025.3738 Several of its changes read as a direct response to this sector's incident history: multi-factor authentication is now required for all access to the cardholder-data environment rather than just administrators, and new requirements (6.4.3 and 11.6.1) specifically target the e-skimming and script-tampering attacks — Magecart-style code injection on checkout pages — that have plagued retail.38 PCI DSS applies to any organisation that stores, processes or transmits card data, validated across four tiers by transaction volume, with the largest merchants assessed annually.39

The systemic layer: Secure by Design

The frameworks above ask operators to defend insecure products. The more durable fix is to stop shipping them — the premise of CISA's Secure by Design initiative, published with the FBI, NSA and international partners in April 2023 and expanded that October.42 Its three principles place the burden where the sector's IoT problem actually originates: take ownership of customer security outcomes, embrace radical transparency and accountability, and lead from the top.42 In practice the guidance urges manufacturers to eliminate default passwords, make multi-factor authentication standard, segment internal controls, run vulnerability-disclosure programmes, and build on NIST's Secure Software Development Framework (SP 800-218) — with the pointed warning that a vendor failing to meet CISA's baseline Cybersecurity Performance Goals cannot be regarded as delivering secure-by-design products at all.43 For a sector drowning in cheap connected devices, this is the only mitigation that scales.

The collaboration layer: ISACs and the partnership model

Finally, intelligence has to move. The sector is served by purpose-built Information Sharing and Analysis Centers (ISACs). The Retail & Hospitality ISAC (RH-ISAC), formed in 2014, is the hub for sector-specific threat intelligence across retailers, restaurants, hotels, casinos, theme parks and sports leagues, pooling submissions from a community of more than 2,000 member analysts and publishing daily reports, bulletins and benchmarks.44 The Real Estate ISAC (RE-ISAC), managed by the Real Estate Roundtable, performs the parallel role for the real-estate subsector, sharing threat and incident information to protect buildings and their occupants.45 These sit atop the formal public-private structure described earlier — the Sector and Government Coordinating Councils — through which the sector and CISA coordinate strategy.34 Together with the universal hardening measures the case files keep demanding — network segmentation, rigorous third-party access control, asset inventory, monitoring and logging of the OT estate, and the elimination of internet-exposed building controls — they form the achievable core of a sector defence.

07 Open problems

Unsolved problems & open research

For all the available instruments, the gap between what is possible and what is practised remains wide — and several of the sector's hardest problems are not yet solved by any framework. They define the research and innovation agenda.

The voluntary-model ceiling

The first problem is structural. A sector that is privately owned, radically heterogeneous, and governed by voluntary guidance will always have a long tail of operators for whom security is deferred. The same openness and fragmentation that make the sector vibrant make uniform defence impossible to mandate — and the breach data shows the bill landing precisely on the consumer-facing industries whose costs rose even as the global average fell.8 Closing that tail without crushing small operators under compliance is an unsolved policy question.

IT/OT convergence and the patching paradox

The deepest technical problem is the collision of two incompatible lifecycles. Enterprise IT is patched continuously; the building's turbines, controllers and locks were designed to run for decades and often cannot be updated without halting the facility.41 Bridging legacy protocols onto IP networks during retrofits keeps re-creating exposure faster than it can be remediated.29 The research frontier here is in compensating controls — virtual patching, protocol filtering, and especially anomaly detection tuned to the rhythms of building automation, since most BAS installations today lack the centralised logging and monitoring that incident detection requires.30 Migrating the installed base to secure protocol variants such as BACnet/SC and KNX Secure is a decade-long undertaking that has barely begun.26

The human layer and the identity problem

The MGM and Caesars intrusions exposed a problem no protocol upgrade fixes: the help desk. When the most effective attack against a multi-billion-dollar operator is a convincing phone call, the research need shifts toward robust identity verification, phishing-resistant authentication, and help-desk procedures that survive social engineering.16 That need is intensifying, not easing — the 2025 breach data found attackers already using AI for phishing and deepfake impersonation in a meaningful share of incidents, even as defenders use AI to compress detection time.8 The arms race now runs on both sides of the same technology.

The interdependency blind spot

Perhaps the most important open question is the one the MadIoT research raised and left unanswered: the security of the grid and the security of the buildings that load it are entangled, yet they are studied, owned and defended separately.31 No single sector authority owns the risk that millions of insecure commercial-building devices pose to the electricity system or to energy markets.34 Closing that blind spot demands research that crosses the systems-security and power-engineering communities — and a governance model that can act on a risk no one sector controls.

08 Conclusion

The seam, and who owns it

The Commercial Facilities Sector is defined by a single, productive contradiction: it must remain open to be useful, and that openness is now expressed not only in unguarded doorways but in unguarded networks. The case files in this dossier — a retailer breached through its air-conditioning contractor, a hotel chain leaking passports for four years, a casino floor frozen by a phone call, a building bricked from a misconfigured port, a high-roller list smuggled out through a fish tank — are not a catalogue of bad luck. They are variations on one structural fact: the seam where the open public realm meets the connected industrial one is the soft target, and in this sector that seam is everywhere.

The instruments to defend it exist, and they are converging on the right answers — NIST CSF 2.0's new emphasis on governance and supply chain, SP 800-82r3's recognition that building controls are critical OT, 62443's zones and conduits, PCI DSS 4.0.1's hard line on multi-factor authentication and e-skimming, and Secure by Design's insistence that the device makers, not just the operators, carry the burden. What is missing is not knowledge but uniform will, in a sector where security remains a choice rather than a requirement.

The work ahead is therefore as much about ownership as about technology: integrating the building engineer and the security analyst, treating the vendor's product as part of one's own attack surface, and recognising that an intrusion into a place where crowds gather is never only a data breach. The fence the sector lacks was always going to be a network one. Building it — quietly, by default, into every controller and every help-desk script — is the task this dossier leaves open.
