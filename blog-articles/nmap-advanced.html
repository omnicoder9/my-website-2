The Anatomy
of a Scan

What Nmap is actually doing on the wire — from the TCP state machine and stack fingerprinting to the Lua engine, congestion-controlled probing, and reconnaissance at internet scale.

Abstract

Most practitioners can drive Nmap. Far fewer can explain why a SYN scan and a FIN scan disagree about a Windows host, how the timing engine borrows congestion control from TCP, or what the forty-odd response tests behind -O actually measure. This manual is for the second group — and for anyone who wants to join it.

We work outward from the packet: the flags and state transitions Nmap manipulates, the probe-and-match machinery of version and OS detection, the architecture of the scan engine itself, and finally the operational realities of scanning tens of thousands of hosts while a blue team watches the SIEM. Every nontrivial claim is cited to primary sources — the official Nmap Network Scanning reference, the relevant RFCs, and the original research.

Origins & Posture

A port scanner that became a platform

Nmap entered the world not as a product but as a magazine article. On 1 September 1997, in issue 51 of the hacker zine Phrack, a contributor writing as Fyodor published "The Art of Port Scanning," a treatise on techniques for determining which ports on a host are listening — accompanied by the source code for a small tool that implemented them.1 That tool was Nmap. Nearly three decades later it is written in C, C++, Python, and Lua, ships under a modified GPL, and remains under active development by its creator, Gordon Lyon, with stable releases continuing into 2025.2

What began as a port scanner has accreted a remarkable surface area: host discovery, TCP/UDP/SCTP port scanning across a dozen techniques, application version detection, TCP/IP stack OS fingerprinting, and a full embedded scripting engine. Around the core scanner sits a suite — Ncat for arbitrary network I/O, Nping for packet crafting, Ndiff for comparing scan results, and the Zenmap GUI.2 The throughline is a single philosophy: send carefully chosen stimuli, observe the responses with surgical attention, and infer.

Nmap sends a series of TCP and UDP packets to the remote host and examines practically every bit in the responses.
— Nmap Reference Guide, on OS detection

The tool's cultural footprint is real enough that it has been audited for realism by its own author. In The Matrix Reloaded (2003), Trinity is shown running Nmap 2.54BETA25, locating a vulnerable SSH service, and exploiting it with the SSH1 CRC32 vulnerability from 2001 — a sequence Lyon praised precisely because, unlike most cinematic "hacking," it depicts a plausible real-world chain.3

Where Nmap sits in the modern toolbox

An advanced operator has to understand Nmap relationally. It is not the fastest scanner in existence and was never meant to be. ZMap, introduced by researchers at the University of Michigan at USENIX Security 2013, can survey the entire public IPv4 address space on a single port in under 45 minutes from a single machine, by abandoning per-connection state entirely.4 Masscan, by Robert Graham, transmits up to ten million packets per second with its own asynchronous TCP/IP stack, claiming a full-internet sweep in under five minutes.5 Both deliberately sacrifice the depth that is Nmap's entire point.

Nmap's value is fidelity: stateful retransmission, adaptive timing, service interrogation, scriptable follow-up. The mature workflow, examined in the enterprise section below, often uses a stateless scanner for breadth and Nmap for depth on whatever that breadth turns up. To wield Nmap at that level you have to start where it starts — at the packet.

01 — Packet-Level Understanding

Flags, states, and the logic of inference

Every Nmap scan type is a hypothesis test built on the semantics of TCP/IP. To reason about them you need the TCP state machine and the connection-establishment rules of RFC 793 firmly in mind, because Nmap's cleverest scans exploit the edges of that specification — the behaviors it mandates, and the behaviors implementations get wrong.6

The TCP state machine, abbreviated

A normal connection walks a well-defined path. The listener sits in LISTEN; an active opener sends a SYN and enters SYN-SENT; the listener replies SYN/ACK and moves to SYN-RECEIVED; the opener acknowledges and both reach ESTABLISHED. Teardown traverses FIN-WAIT-1, FIN-WAIT-2, CLOSE-WAIT, CLOSING, LAST-ACK, and TIME-WAIT before returning to CLOSED.6 The control bits driving these transitions — SYN, ACK, FIN, RST, PSH, URG, plus the later ECE/CWR congestion bits — are precisely the levers Nmap pulls.

The cornerstone is the SYN scan (-sS), often called half-open. Nmap sends a SYN; a SYN/ACK signals an open port, a RST signals closed, and rather than completing the handshake it sends a RST to tear the nascent connection down before it reaches ESTABLISHED. This is faster and historically stealthier than the connect scan (-sT), which delegates to the operating system's connect() call and so completes the full three-way handshake — leaving a connection the target's applications may log.7

Scanning the gaps in the spec

Where it gets interesting is the family of scans that weaponize RFC 793's stated behavior for unexpected packets. The standard says a closed port must answer a packet lacking SYN, RST, or ACK with a RST, while an open port must simply discard it. The NULL (-sN, no flags), FIN (-sF), and Xmas (-sX, FIN+PSH+URG lit like a Christmas tree) scans all rely on this: silence implies open-or-filtered, a RST implies closed.7

The technique's signature limitation is also instructive. Many systems — Microsoft Windows being the canonical example — violate the RFC and emit a RST regardless of port state, which makes these scans useless against them but, conversely, turns them into a crude OS discriminator. The Maimon scan (-sM) is a variant of historical interest: named for Uriel Maimon, who described it in Phrack 49 in November 1996 — two issues before Nmap's own debut — it sends a FIN/ACK probe. RFC 793 demands a RST in reply whether the port is open or closed, yet Maimon observed that many BSD-derived stacks silently drop the packet when the port is open, leaking its state.7

Two more scans round out the firewall-reconnaissance repertoire. The ACK scan (-sA) never determines whether a port is open; it sends a bare ACK and uses the presence of a RST (unfiltered) versus silence or an ICMP error (filtered) to map a packet filter's rule set. The Window scan (-sW) is the same probe, reading the TCP window field of returned RSTs to coax port state out of certain stacks.

ICMP and the UDP problem

ICMP is woven through Nmap in two roles. For host discovery it can send ICMP echo requests (-PE), timestamp requests (-PP), and address-mask requests (-PM) to determine which hosts are alive. More subtly, ICMP is the load-bearing signal for UDP scanning. A UDP probe to a closed port should elicit an ICMP port-unreachable message (type 3, code 3); an open port often returns nothing at all. Because absence of a reply is ambiguous — it can mean open, or it can mean a dropped packet — UDP scanning is slow and must retransmit, and an open port and a filtered port frequently collapse into the same open|filtered verdict.7

Fragmentation and MTU mechanics

The most direct packet-level manipulation Nmap offers is fragmentation. The -f flag splits the probe so that only eight bytes of the transport header travel in each IP fragment after the IP header; a 20-byte TCP header therefore arrives as three fragments — two of eight bytes and one of four. Specifying -f a second time doubles the payload to sixteen bytes per fragment, reducing the count. For precise control, --mtu sets an explicit offset, which must be a multiple of eight; you do not combine it with -f.8

The intent is evasion: split the TCP header across packets so that a naive packet filter or intrusion-detection sensor cannot see the whole header at once. The countermeasure is equally clear. Filters that queue and reassemble all fragments before inspection — Linux's CONFIG_IP_ALWAYS_DEFRAG, or stateful firewalls with connection tracking — defeat the technique entirely; some networks leave reassembly off only because they cannot afford the performance cost or because fragments may take divergent paths.8 Fragmentation is supported only for Nmap's raw-packet features — it does not apply to the connect or FTP-bounce scans — and on a misbehaving host OS you may need --send-eth to push frames out below the IP layer.8

Operator's note

Always validate evasion empirically. Run the scan with a packet sniffer such as Wireshark on the source host and confirm the packets actually leave fragmented — several operating systems silently reassemble outbound traffic in the kernel, so the fragmentation you asked for never reaches the wire.8

02 — Advanced Fingerprinting

Teaching a scanner to recognize software

Port state tells you a door is open. Fingerprinting tells you what is behind it. Nmap runs two independent recognition systems — service/version detection and OS detection — and reports them separately, so that you can describe, say, a Check Point firewall forwarding port 80 to a Windows IIS server as exactly that combination rather than collapsing it into one guess.9 An expert tunes both, reads their raw output, and contributes back to their databases.

Service and version detection

Version detection (-sV) interrogates an open port instead of trusting the nmap-services port-number lookup. It draws on nmap-service-probes, a flat file of probes — strings to send — and match expressions to recognize the replies, from which Nmap parses protocol, application name, and version.10 Each probe carries a rarity from one to nine, indicating how seldom it returns useful data, and the scan's intensity (--version-intensity, range 0–9, default 7) determines which probes are eligible: every probe whose rarity is at or below the intensity is tried.10 The convenience aliases --version-light and --version-all map to intensities 2 and 9 respectively.

The probe-selection logic is more elegant than brute force. A probe registered to a port through the ports directive is always tried against that port regardless of intensity — the SSL probe against 443, the DNS probe against 53 — so common services are caught cheaply.10 A softmatch narrows the field: once a service is softly identified, Nmap need only try probes capable of matching that service.11 Crucially, a softmatch does not halt the process the way a hard match does — it is captured but version detection continues, and any subsequent hard match supersedes it, giving fuzzy recognition with precise upgrades.

Two more mechanisms deserve mention. The fallback directive lets one probe borrow another's match lines: an Apache server that ignores the GetRequest probe because no virtual host was specified will still answer HTTPOptions, which falls back to the general GetRequest patterns and is recognized anyway.11 And post-processors extend the engine — the RPC "grinder" enumerates RPC programs, while an SSL post-processor unwraps TLS and re-runs detection against the cleartext service inside.11

The probe file as an editable surface

The whole engine is deliberately "hackable" through a single text file, and an advanced operator treats it as one. The nmap-service-probes grammar includes Exclude, Probe, match, softmatch, ports/sslports, totalwaitms, tcpwrappedms, rarity, and fallback directives.12 A match line pairs a regular expression against a service with template fields for the version data it extracts.

nmap-service-probesmatch grammar
# A probe, its rarity, and which ports get it unconditionally
Probe TCP GetRequest q|GET / HTTP/1.0\r\n\r\n|
rarity 1
ports 80,81,443,8080,8443

# match: regex, then p/product/ v/version/ template fields
match http m|^HTTP/1\.[01] \d\d\d .*\r\nServer: Apache/([\w._-]+)| \
      p/Apache httpd/ v/$1/ cpe:/a:apache:http_server:$1/

# softmatch: recognizes the service without stopping the scan
softmatch http m|^HTTP/1\.[01] \d\d\d|

Filtering this file down to only the services you care about — or adding probes for a proprietary internal protocol — is a standard way to make a recurring enterprise scan both faster and more accurate, since --version-intensity applies globally and cannot, by itself, limit probes per service.

OS detection: the second-generation system

Nmap has performed remote OS detection since 1998; the system in use today is the second generation, released in 2006.9 It sends a precisely choreographed battery of probes to an open and a closed port and examines nearly every bit of the responses, comparing the result against nmap-os-db — a database of more than 2,600 fingerprints. A match yields a free-text description plus a structured classification of vendor, OS family, OS generation, and device type, and most entries carry a Common Platform Enumeration (CPE) string such as cpe:/o:linux:linux_kernel:2.6.13

The probe set is worth knowing by name, because reading a raw fingerprint requires it. Nmap sends: a sequence-generation series of six TCP SYN probes with varying options (the SEQ/OPS/WIN/T1 results), two ICMP echo probes (IE), one TCP ECN probe, six further TCP probes (T2–T7), and one UDP probe to a closed port (U1).14

The SEQ tests deserve a closer look because they encode the most information. GCD is the greatest common divisor of the differences between successive initial sequence numbers; ISR is the sequence rate; SP is the standard deviation that drives the TCP sequence-predictability classification — a side measure that estimates how hard it would be to forge a connection against the host.13 The TI, CI, and II tests characterize IP-ID generation (the very behavior the idle scan exploits, in Section 6), TS the TCP timestamp rate, and SS the shared-IP-ID Boolean.14 The ECN probe sets the congestion-notification bits defined by RFC 316815 and uses an urgent-pointer value of 0xF7F5 even though the urgent flag is clear — a deliberate oddity that good stacks handle distinctively.14 Independent analysis at USENIX WOOT found that a handful of these features — notably initial window size, TCP options ordering, and TTL — carry the bulk of the discriminating power.16

Reading and submitting fingerprints

When Nmap cannot identify a host but conditions are favorable — at least one open and one closed port — it prints a subject fingerprint and a URL for submission. You can also force the fingerprint into normal, interactive, and XML output by enabling debugging (-d).13 A line such as T2(R=N) means probe T2 drew no response, which is itself diagnostic; tests that don't apply or that the scanning host can't support reliably are simply omitted. Tuning levers include --osscan-guess for aggressive matching and --max-os-tries to bound retries — and, when you positively know a host's OS, submitting its fingerprint is how the 2,600-entry database keeps growing for everyone.13

03 — NSE Development

The scanner as a programmable engine

The Nmap Scripting Engine is what turns a port scanner into a reconnaissance and exploitation platform. It lets you write — and share — programs that run during a scan, executed in parallel with the speed Nmap is known for, to perform network discovery, deeper version detection, vulnerability checks, brute forcing, and even exploitation.17 Scripts are written in an embedded build of Lua, version 5.4.18

Categories, phases, and the script database

Every script declares one or more categories, which double as the vocabulary for selecting them: auth, broadcast, brute, default, discovery, dos, exploit, external, fuzzer, intrusive, malware, safe, version, and vuln.17 The -sC flag runs the default set; --script takes explicit names, categories, directories, or shell-style wildcards. For efficiency, scripts are indexed in scripts/script.db, and a name like http-* loads every script beginning with that prefix.17

Structurally, an NSE script has three parts: descriptive fields (description, author, license, categories), a rule, and an action.19 The rule is a Lua function returning true or false that decides whether the action fires, and there are four kinds keyed to the scan's phases: prerule (before any host is scanned), hostrule (per host), portrule (per port), and postrule (after scanning completes). A single script may declare more than one — ssh-hostkey, for instance, carries both a portrule and a postrule.19 The action is the body; its return value may be a table of name–value pairs (auto-formatted into normal and XML output), a plain string, or nil for no output.19

Writing one

The boilerplate is light because the engine supplies rich libraries — shortport for common rule predicates, http, stdnse, nmap, and dozens more. The example below is a complete, well-formed service script: it matches likely HTTP ports via shortport, issues a request, and reports a header.

http-server-banner.nselua 5.4
local http = require "http"
local shortport = require "shortport"
local stdnse = require "stdnse"

-- descriptive fields: metadata the engine reads directly
description = [[Fetches the Server header from an HTTP service.]]
author      = "Field Manual"
license     = "Same as Nmap--See https://nmap.org/book/man-legal.html"
categories  = {"discovery", "safe"}

-- rule: run only on open TCP ports that look like HTTP(S)
portrule = shortport.http

-- action: the work, keyed on (host, port) from the engine
action = function(host, port)
  local resp = http.get(host, port, "/")
  if not resp or not resp.header then
    return nil          -- nil ⇒ no output line
  end
  local server = resp.header["server"] or "unknown"
  return stdnse.format_output(true, "Server: " .. server)
end

From there, the development arc is: extend rather than reinvent (every bundled script is readable source, and most enterprise checks start as a copy of an existing one), wire results into other tooling through the structured-table return, and register custom scripts so --script-help and the category system find them. Parallelism is free — the engine multiplexes script instances itself — but it is also why scripts must be written cooperatively, yielding on I/O rather than blocking.

Enterprise checks

The highest-value NSE work in a mature program is rarely a flashy exploit. It is a small library of safe/discovery scripts that assert organization-specific truths — a mandated TLS configuration, a forbidden default credential, a required management banner — and run them across the estate on a schedule, turning Nmap into a continuously enforced inventory and compliance probe rather than a point-in-time tool.

04 — Scan Architecture

How the engine actually moves

Beneath the flags is a single high-performance scan engine — ultra_scan — and a timing system modeled with surprising directness on TCP itself. Understanding it is what separates an operator who guesses at -T4 from one who can reason about why a scan is slow, when it will lie to you, and how to make it both fast and correct.

Probe design and the block strategy

Nmap may be handed networks containing hundreds of thousands or even millions of hosts, so it breaks them into blocks small enough to handle at once — from dozens up to a few thousand hosts — and works through each block as fast as its congestion controls allow.20 The ordering is the key insight: rather than firing every requested probe at one host and moving on, ultra_scan sends the first probe to all targets in the block, then the second to all, and so on. As soon as a conclusive response arrives for a host it is marked up or down and receives no further probes; a host that never answers, even after retransmission, is marked down.20 This batching is what keeps latency hidden behind parallelism.

Congestion control, borrowed from TCP

Because Nmap is most often used over TCP, it plays by TCP's rules — rules that exist precisely to maximize throughput without degenerating into a tragedy of the commons. Nmap uses three algorithms lifted from TCP to govern aggression: a congestion window, exponential backoff, and slow start.21

  • The congestion window (cwnd) caps how many probes may be outstanding at once; when it is full, Nmap waits for a response or a timeout before sending more.21
  • A congestion threshold (ssthresh/ccthresh) marks the boundary between slow-start and congestion-avoidance modes; below it the window grows rapidly per response, above it it grows gently.21
  • Exponential backoff stretches retransmission timers dramatically when responses dry up, so a congested or hostile network is not hammered.21

Round-trip-time estimation is likewise TCP-flavored: Nmap maintains a running timeout from observed response times, which it starts conservatively high and which can balloon to seconds on a high-latency, jittery path.22 The fine-grained controls --initial-rtt-timeout, --min-rtt-timeout, and --max-rtt-timeout override it — lowering the maximum on a fast LAN can cut scan time substantially.22

You can also step outside the model. --min-rate sends at (or above) a fixed packets-per-second figure regardless of congestion feedback, and --max-retries bounds retransmissions. Setting --min-rate 100 --max-retries 0 makes Nmap behave like a simple stateless scanner — fast, and entirely at your own risk, because an over-fast scan that returns wrong answers is worthless.21

The timing engine

The -T templates, paranoid (-T0) through insane (-T5), are aliases for pre-selected bundles of these timing variables, with -T3 as the default.23 They also make small adjustments with no dedicated fine-grained switch — for example, -T4 forbids the dynamic scan delay from exceeding 10 ms for TCP ports while -T5 caps it at 5 ms.23 Templates and granular options compose: keep -T4 at the front of the command line for its minor optimizations even when you override specific values.

Two more controls matter at scale. --host-timeout abandons hosts that take too long — -T5 sets it to fifteen minutes, and the special value 0 means "no timeout," useful for overriding that cap.22 And on the offensive side, the relationship between aggression and reliability inverts on bad links: pushing -T4 over a satellite or congested VPN path triggers packet loss, which the congestion controller reads as a reason to throttle — so the counter-intuitive fix for a slow, lossy network is to slow down and widen the RTT window.

Parallelization

Parallelism in Nmap has two dials, and conflating them is a common mistake. Host-group size (--min-hostgroup/--max-hostgroup) controls how many hosts are scanned concurrently — the size of the block ultra_scan works through. Parallelism (--min-parallelism/--max-parallelism) controls how many probes are in flight overall.22 Forcing --min-parallelism high keeps the pipeline full when a single host drops packets; setting --max-parallelism 1 serializes probes to a host. By default Nmap chooses both adaptively from the scan type and observed conditions.

scaling a /16 deliberatelybash
$ nmap -sS -T4 \
    # block of at least 256 hosts at a time
    --min-hostgroup 256 --max-hostgroup 1024 \
    # keep at least 100 probes on the wire
    --min-parallelism 100 \
    --max-rtt-timeout 200ms --max-retries 2 \
    -oA campus-sweep 10.0.0.0/16

This is the granular alternative to leaning on -T4 alone: explicit block sizes, an explicit floor on in-flight probes, and bounded retries — the shape of a scan engineered for a known network rather than guessed at.

05 — Enterprise & Internet Recon

From one host to the whole address space

Scanning tens of thousands of hosts is a different discipline from scanning one. The constraints shift from "what does this machine run" to throughput, scheduling, result diffing, and the politics of being a good network citizen. The defining strategic choice is how to spend Nmap's depth where it counts and borrow speed where it does not.

The two-speed methodology

The architectural difference between Nmap and the internet-scale scanners is precisely the trade you exploit. Nmap maintains state for every connection — tracking which hosts have been probed, handling timeouts and retransmissions — which buys accuracy and depth. ZMap forgoes per-connection state entirely, assumes the source network can't be saturated and targets are widely dispersed, skips the kernel TCP/IP stack, and emits Ethernet frames directly at line speed, surveying all of public IPv4 on one port in under 45 minutes from a single gigabit-connected machine.4 Masscan reaches comparable scale — ten million packets per second, a full sweep in under five minutes — using its own asynchronous, ad-hoc TCP/IP stack, and notably mirrors Nmap's command syntax and output formats to slot into existing workflows.5

The mature internet-scale pattern follows from this: use a stateless scanner to find where something is listening across an enormous range, then hand that much smaller target list to Nmap for the what — version detection, OS fingerprinting, and scripted checks. Masscan's documentation reflects the workflow directly; its output is built to feed exactly this kind of pipeline.5

Features that support widespread scanning of many machines are supported, while in-depth scanning of single machines aren't.
— Masscan README, on its deliberate scope

Discovery first, then depth

Even within Nmap alone, the same logic applies at the LAN and campus scale. A pure host-discovery pass (-sn, no port scan) cheaply establishes which addresses are live; a port scan with -Pn skips discovery when you already know hosts are up. Layering these — discover, then deep-scan only the responders — avoids spending OS-detection and version-detection budget on empty address space, and is the single largest determinant of how long a large sweep takes.

Output, scheduling, and asset inventory

Automation lives in Nmap's output formats. Beyond the human-readable default (-oN), there is grepable output (-oG) for quick command-line filtering, XML (-oX) for programmatic consumption, and -oA to emit all formats at once under a common basename.24 XML is the backbone of any inventory pipeline: it is stable, parseable, and the format every downstream tool expects.

Scheduled scanning plus Ndiff is how Nmap becomes a change-detection system. Run the same scan on a cadence, store the XML, and diff successive runs: a newly opened port, a service that changed version, a host that appeared or vanished, all surface as deltas rather than as something a human must spot in a wall of output. An asset inventory built this way is living — it reflects the network as it actually is on each run, and it flags drift the moment it happens.

Citizenship

Internet-scale capability carries obligations. The ZMap authors devote part of their paper to best practices for good internet citizenship — honoring exclusion requests, identifying scan traffic, and never exploiting or accessing protected resources during a survey.4 At enterprise scale, authorization, rate limits, and exclusion lists are not optional courtesies; they are the difference between a sanctioned assessment and an incident.

06 — Detection & Logging

The view from the other side of the wire

An advanced operator has to hold both perspectives at once: what the scan looks like leaving, and what it looks like arriving at a defender's sensor. The same packet-level facts that make a scan work are the features a detection system keys on — so understanding detection is also understanding the ceiling on evasion.

What an IDS keys on

The classic answer in the open-source world is Snort's sfPortscan preprocessor, written at Sourcefire and built explicitly because Nmap "encompasses many, if not all, of the current portscanning techniques."25 It is designed to alert on the spectrum of scans Nmap produces — one-to-one port scans, decoy scans with spoofed addresses intermixed with the real source, and distributed many-to-one scans — and it reasons over statistics rather than single packets.25 Its alert fields are a compact model of scan behavior: a Connection Count of active connections, a Priority Count tracking "bad" responses such as resets and unreachables, an IP Count, and a Port/Proto Count.25 A flurry of probes drawing mostly RSTs and ICMP errors is, statistically, exactly what a closed-port sweep looks like.

Alongside the statistical preprocessor sit signature rules. Snort's scan ruleset includes stateless flag-based detections — the canonical SCAN nmap XMAS rule fires on the FIN/PSH/URG flag combination, classified as attempted reconnaissance.26 Modern engines such as Suricata add threshold logic that counts events over a window; a representative rule alerts when a source sends some number of SYNs within a few seconds, tracked per source address.27 Both approaches feed a SIEM, where scan alerts from many sensors are correlated; a single Snort rule's output is one data point, but aggregated across sensors it reveals distributed and sophisticated patterns a standalone IDS would miss.25

Idle scan: the limit case of stealth

The most elegant evasion Nmap implements turns the IP-ID side channel into a blind scan. The technique was discovered by Salvatore Sanfilippo — "Antirez," later the author of hping and Redis — who posted it to the Bugtraq mailing list on 18 December 1998 after noticing that many stacks increment a single global IP-ID counter on every outbound packet, wrapping at 216−1.28 Originally called the "dumb scan," it was renamed "idle scan" in 1999 after a proof-of-concept by Filipe Almeida (LiquidK), and Nmap implemented it as -sI in version 2.54BETA26 in July 2001.29

The mechanism is a study in indirection. The attacker never sends a packet to the target from their own address; instead they bounce the scan off a third "zombie" host whose IP-ID is observable. Nmap first probes the zombie to confirm its IP-ID increments predictably and that it is genuinely idle — it even spoofs four packets to the zombie as if from the target to verify the counter moves and that the path isn't filtered.30 Then it forges a SYN to the target with the zombie's source address; an open port answers the zombie with SYN/ACK, the zombie replies RST and bumps its IP-ID; a closed port answers RST and the zombie stays quiet. By re-reading the zombie's IP-ID, the attacker infers the target's port state — and any IDS report fingers the innocent zombie.30

The defensive lesson runs in both directions. The idle scan only works against zombies with predictable, global IP-ID sequences; recent OpenBSD, Solaris, and Linux use randomized or per-host sequences that immunize them.30 The broader defenses are structural — egress filtering to stop spoofed packets leaving a network, and stateful firewalls that track connection establishment and drop replies to sessions no one initiated.30

The rest of the evasion toolkit — and its ceiling

Short of the idle scan, Nmap offers decoys (-D, including RND:10 for random decoy addresses), source spoofing (-S), source-port manipulation (firewalls are often lax about traffic from expected ports), host randomization (--randomize-hosts), and the slow timing templates that exist specifically to stay under rate-based thresholds.8 Each has a documented countermeasure, which is the point: at expert level, evasion is not a magic cloak but a negotiation with a specific, knowable detection model. The defender who understands sfPortscan's statistics and the attacker who understands the timing engine are reading the same book from opposite ends.

Coda

Depth is a discipline, not a flag

Nmap rewards study in a way few tools do, because almost nothing about it is arbitrary. The scan types are theorems about TCP; the timing engine is congestion control wearing a different hat; the fingerprint databases are accreted empirical science; the scripting engine is a genuine programming environment. Mastery is not memorizing flags — it is being able to derive, from the protocol, why a given flag does what it does, and to predict how the host and the sensor on the far side will react.

That same depth is what makes the ethical line bright. Nmap maintains scanme.nmap.org as a sanctioned target for practice precisely because scanning systems you are not authorized to scan is both useless as learning and potentially unlawful. The skills in this manual are dual-use by construction; the red-team operator and the blue-team analyst are studying the identical mechanics. Use them where you are permitted, document what you do, and let the rigor that makes Nmap worth learning extend to how you wield it.

Continue: read the canonical Nmap Network Scanning chapters cited below in full; build and submit an OS fingerprint; write an NSE script that encodes one rule your organization actually cares about. The tool gives back exactly as much as you put into understanding it.
