§ I

The iceberg problem.

In 2015, ten Google engineers led by D. Sculley published a short paper at NeurIPS that did more to define the operational reality of machine learning than any framework or platform that came before it. Hidden Technical Debt in Machine Learning Systems argued that machine-learning code is, in any real production system, a tiny fraction of what is actually deployed: a sliver of Python wedged inside an enormous mass of configuration, data verification, feature extraction, monitoring, process management, serving infrastructure, and analysis tooling.1

The paper's now-famous accompanying diagram — a small black box labelled ML Code surrounded by sprawling supporting systems — became the founding image of MLOps. It crystallised a simple, awkward truth: the machine-learning research community had spent a decade optimising the box in the middle, while almost every operational headache lived in the systems around it.

FIG. 01 — After Sculley et al. (2015):  the production ML system, where ML code is a small box inside a much larger machine.1

The diagnostic is sharp because it reframes the problem. The hard part of putting a model into production is not training it. The hard part is everything else: the upstream data pipelines that may silently mutate; the feature transformations duplicated between batch training and online inference; the model registry that nobody updates; the monitoring that catches latency regressions but not silent accuracy collapse; the retraining loops that propagate corruption faster than humans can react.

The paper coined an aphorism that has since been carved over every serious MLOps team's door: CACE — Changing Anything Changes Everything.1 Models entangle their inputs. A “harmless” refactor to a feature pipeline is, mechanically, a change to the model. So is an upstream schema drift. So is a new logging policy that quietly nulls a field. None of these are visible to the type system. None will fire a stack trace. The model will keep returning floats; they will simply be the wrong floats.

Hidden debt is dangerous because it compounds silently. Machine-learning systems have a special capacity for incurring it — they erode the abstraction boundaries software engineering relies on. — Sculley et al., NeurIPS 2015

It is the response to this set of facts that gave us MLOps. The term itself is a portmanteau of Machine Learning and Operations, mirroring the formation of DevOps a decade earlier and signalling the same intent: collapse the artificial wall between the people who build the artefact (data scientists, ML researchers) and the people who keep it alive in production (operations engineers, SREs, platform teams). It is, in the framing Google's authoritative whitepaper popularised, a culture and set of practices that aim to unify ML system development and ML system operation, with continuous training (CT) added as a first-class concern alongside CI and CD.2

The rest of this essay is a practitioner's tour of how the discipline has answered Sculley's diagnosis: through architectural patterns (feature stores, model registries, continuous-training pipelines), through cultural ones (the rise of the ML platform team), through governance frameworks (the NIST AI RMF, the EU AI Act), and most recently through the violent disruption of foundation models, which have spawned a derivative discipline — LLMOps — that is reorganising the field around inference-time rather than training-time concerns. Throughout, we will treat MLOps as a sibling of DevOps, not a subset: deeply analogous, fundamentally distinct.

§ II

A compressed history.

The pre-history of MLOps is a story told inside half a dozen tech companies who hit the same wall, independently, between 2013 and 2017: their data scientists could train good models, but nobody could reliably get those models in front of users, monitor them, or update them without on-call pages. The platforms they built in response — mostly proprietary, mostly later revealed in conference talks and engineering blogs — are the genetic ancestors of every MLOps tool now in production.

Facebook went public first. In 2016, the company described FBLearner Flow, an internal pipelining and experiment system that by then was running tens of thousands of workflows per month across most of Facebook's engineering organisation.3 Twitter followed in the same year. Then came the moment most engineers point to as the inflection point: in September 2017, Jeremy Hermann and Mike Del Balso published the Uber Engineering blog post introducing Michelangelo, the platform Uber had been building since mid-2015.4 The post was unusually generous with architectural detail; it described an end-to-end system for managing data, training, evaluation, deployment, online and offline prediction, and a then-novel component called a “feature store” — arguably the first documented use of the term in industry.5

Within a year Uber published a follow-up describing how Michelangelo had scaled to thousands of models, hundreds of use cases, and millions of predictions per second across the company.6 By 2024, Michelangelo would have evolved through three major phases — predictive ML for tabular data (2016–2019), deep learning (2019–2023), and generative AI (2023–) — and would manage roughly four hundred active ML projects with over twenty thousand model training jobs per month.7

Airbnb's Bighead followed a parallel arc, built atop open-source primitives that practitioners would recognise — Jupyter, Docker, Spark, Airflow, Kubernetes — rather than from scratch.8 Netflix's Metaflow, prototyped internally and open-sourced in December 2019, was a deliberately human-centric Python framework that prioritised data scientists' ergonomics; today Metaflow powers thousands of AI/ML projects at Netflix and runs widely outside it as well.9 LinkedIn shipped Pro-ML, Apple described Overton, and the pattern became unmistakable: every company past a certain scale was rebuilding the same platform.

The community response was open-source. Kubeflow was first announced at KubeCon North America in late 2017 by Google engineers as a way to open-source the patterns by which Google ran TensorFlow internally; Kubeflow 0.1 followed at KubeCon Europe in 2018, and Kubeflow 1.0 in March 2020.10 The project later joined the CNCF as an incubating-stage project in July 2023.10 MLflow, launched by Databricks in June 2018 and announced at Spark + AI Summit, took a different bet: rather than tie itself to Kubernetes, it offered a lightweight, language-agnostic experiment-tracking and model-packaging library that could be dropped into any existing workflow. MLflow 1.0 shipped a year later with over 100 contributors and an active model-registry component.11

By the early 2020s the feature store had emerged as a discipline of its own. The original Michelangelo team — Mike Del Balso, Kevin Stumpf, Jeremy Hermann — left Uber in 2019 to found Tecton, an enterprise-grade feature platform.12 Independently, Willem Pienaar at the Indonesian ride-sharing company Gojek had open-sourced Feast; Tecton later became Feast's principal core contributor.13 Hopsworks shipped what it claimed was the world's first AI cloud platform with an integrated feature store. AWS, Google, and Databricks all added native feature-store offerings within eighteen months.

The cycle was repeated for every component — orchestrators, model registries, monitoring, validation, serving — and by 2024 the MLOps tooling map was unrecognisable from 2018. Then, almost as soon as the field had stabilised, foundation models arrived and the entire centre of gravity shifted toward inference. We will return to that in § X.

What is striking, in hindsight, is how few of the original ideas changed shape. Every Michelangelo concept — feature stores, a model registry, online and offline serving paths, automated retraining — survived intact into the modern open-source stack. What changed was the locus: from a handful of internal platforms at hyperscalers to a fragmented, vibrant, and occasionally bewildering tooling ecosystem that any engineering team can assemble.

§ III

Adjacent disciplines.

It is tempting to treat MLOps as “DevOps for ML.” The framing is half right and dangerously misleading. MLOps inherits enormous amounts of DevOps practice — CI/CD pipelines, infrastructure as code, container orchestration, the SRE error-budget mindset — but it sits at the confluence of three disciplines, not one, and it adds a fourth concern that has no obvious analog in software engineering: the artefact under management is non-deterministic, its correctness is statistical, and its quality decays even when nobody touches it.

MLOps versus DevOps

Both disciplines share the same underlying goal — deliver software changes faster and more reliably — and the same tooling primitives: version control, automated tests, containers, immutable deployments, observability. The differences emerge from the structure of the artefact.

In conventional DevOps there is one mutable input that determines system behaviour: source code. In MLOps there are three, all independently mutable, each capable of changing system behaviour without changing the others: code (the model architecture, the training script, the feature transformations); data (training, validation, and live production inputs); and the model itself (the trained weights produced by a particular run of the code on a particular slice of data). A bit-identical training script run on the same input data, on a different GPU, can produce a different model. A bit-identical model served against subtly different feature pipelines — even semantically equivalent ones — will produce different predictions.20

This is not a minor wrinkle; it shapes everything downstream. CI must validate not only code but data schemas and statistical properties. Releases must be reproducible from code × data × environment, which in practice means data-versioning systems (DVC, lakeFS, Delta Lake), pinned base images, and seed-controlled training runs — with the residual caveat that GPU non-determinism makes bit-perfect reproducibility almost never achievable. Rollback is more delicate than rolling back a container image: the previous model may have been trained on data you no longer have, against features whose definition has since changed.

MLOps versus DataOps

DataOps emerged from the analytics community as the application of agile and DevOps principles to data pipelines: declarative orchestration, contract-tested transformations, lineage, observability, the “analytics engineering” movement most visibly associated with dbt. MLOps consumes DataOps output. The line is rarely clean.

Where they overlap: data validation, data versioning, schema management, lineage tracking. Where they diverge: DataOps treats the data as the deliverable; MLOps treats the data as a means to a different deliverable (the model) whose behaviour depends on the data in non-obvious and stochastic ways. DataOps tests typically check that values are in expected ranges or that a join doesn't fan out. MLOps tests must additionally check that the statistical properties of the data have not drifted in ways that will degrade a downstream model — a property no schema can capture.16 A column that has always been 90% non-null and is suddenly 70% non-null may be a P0 incident for an MLOps team and a non-event for a data engineering team.

The feedback loop is the other axis. In DataOps the data largely flows in one direction: source → warehouse → dashboard. In MLOps the model's predictions become inputs to user behaviour, which produces new training data, which trains the next model — the structure Sculley called hidden feedback loops and warned was a particularly insidious source of debt.1 Manage this badly and the model is literally training itself on its own past mistakes.

MLOps versus DevSecOps

The security concerns of an ML system are a superset of those of a conventional service, and several are genuinely new. The classical software supply chain — dependency confusion, malicious packages, container provenance — is fully present, plus an ML supply chain layered on top: pretrained weights pulled from Hugging Face, pickle files of unknown provenance (Python's pickle is, infamously, a code-execution format), datasets of contested licensing, feature data passing through systems with different access controls than the model itself.

Then there are the genuinely novel attack surfaces. Adversarial examples — inputs perturbed to flip a classifier's output — remain difficult to defend against in any general way. Data poisoning, where an attacker contributes to a training set in a way that influences future model behaviour, is a live concern for any system with a feedback loop. Model extraction and membership-inference attacks let attackers reconstruct training data or steal model capabilities through inference queries. And in the LLM era, prompt injection has become the canonical attack — an instruction embedded in user data that hijacks the system prompt of an agent. DevSecOps tooling does not, by default, cover any of this. The discipline now usually called MLSecOps is still being assembled.

MLOps versus ML Engineering

The role taxonomy is unsettled, but a working set of distinctions has emerged. A data scientist typically owns the experimental loop: formulating the problem, choosing or designing a model, evaluating it offline. A machine learning engineer takes that work toward production: building the training pipeline, integrating with feature systems, owning the model in production. An MLOps engineer (often titled ML platform engineer) builds and operates the platform on which ML engineers work — the feature store, the orchestrator, the registry, the serving infrastructure, the monitoring stack. ML SREs increasingly sit on top of all of it, owning incident response for production models.

DevOps unified two professions divided by an artefact. MLOps must unify four professions divided by three artefacts, one of which doesn't fit in a Git repository and one of which decays even when no one is touching it.

The cultural lesson from DevOps applies: the role names matter less than the elimination of throw-it-over-the-wall handoffs. A team that has separate “data scientists” and “ML engineers” whose only interface is a Jupyter notebook deposited in a shared folder is, structurally, a team that has rediscovered the dev/ops divide. The most operationally mature ML teams either embed platform engineers with model owners or, equivalently, give model owners enough platform leverage to operate their own systems end-to-end.

§ IV

The lifecycle.

There is no single canonical MLOps lifecycle, but Google's three-level maturity model in MLOps: Continuous Delivery and Automation Pipelines in Machine Learning is the most-cited skeleton on which most descriptions hang.2 Microsoft and AWS publish their own variants;22 all three converge on the same essential phases. The stages below describe what a mature pipeline looks like in steady state — closer to Google's Level 2, the fully automated CI/CD/CT pipeline.

FIG. 02 — The MLOps loop:  not a pipeline but a cycle, with continuous training as its motor.

The stages, in practice

Ingestion and validation. Raw data is pulled from operational stores, event streams, or warehouses, and validated against an expected schema and statistical envelope. Tools such as TensorFlow Data Validation (TFDV), Great Expectations, and Soda Core check both structural properties (column types, nullability, foreign-key integrity) and distributional ones (means, quantiles, category cardinalities). The aim is to fail loudly — before training — on data that has silently shifted shape.16

Feature engineering. Raw data is transformed into features. This is where the deepest production-bugs live, because feature computation typically happens twice — once at training time, over historical data, and once at inference time, over a live request — and those two implementations rarely match. The introduction of dedicated feature stores (next section) was a direct response to this hazard.

Experimentation and tracking. Data scientists iterate on architectures and hyperparameters. Every run is logged: code commit, data version, hyperparameters, environment, hardware, metrics, and the resulting model artefact. MLflow, Weights & Biases, Neptune, ClearML, and Comet are the dominant tools. Critically, these systems make experiments comparable across time and across people — without them, ML research at any scale collapses into a folder of model_final_v2_REAL.pkl files.

Training pipelines. Once a model design has stabilised, training is encoded as a reproducible pipeline: a DAG of containerised steps run on an orchestrator (Kubeflow Pipelines, Argo Workflows, Airflow, Flyte, Metaflow, Prefect, Dagster, Netflix's Maestro). The same pipeline that runs in production also runs locally and in CI, with the data sources and compute targets parameterised. This is the architectural shift that moves an organisation from Google's Level 0 (manual notebooks) to Level 1 (pipeline automation).2

Model validation and gating. A trained model passes through a validation harness before it can be promoted: offline metrics on a held-out set, fairness slices, latency and resource benchmarks, behavioural tests on canonical examples, comparison against the production champion. Failure to meet any gate blocks promotion. Eric Breck and colleagues at Google formalised this discipline in The ML Test Score, a rubric of twenty-eight production-readiness checks across data, model, infrastructure, and monitoring axes.23

Registration and packaging. Validated models are written to a versioned model registry along with metadata: the training-data snapshot, the feature definitions, the code commit, the metrics, the responsible owners. The registry is the source of truth from which deployments are drawn. MLflow's Model Registry and SageMaker Model Registry are the common implementations; Kubeflow integrates with its own metadata store.

Deployment. Models are deployed against one of several patterns — online, batch, streaming, or embedded — via a serving framework (TensorFlow Serving, TorchServe, NVIDIA Triton, KServe, Seldon Core, BentoML, Ray Serve). Progressive delivery patterns are standard: shadow deployments (the new model receives traffic but its responses are not used), canaries (a slice of real traffic), A/B tests, and champion/challenger setups.

Serving. The runtime path. Online inference handles synchronous requests, often with strict latency SLOs (a recommendation served in 50 ms p99 from a model behind a feature lookup); batch inference scores millions of records overnight; streaming inference attaches to event buses; embedded inference ships the model into the device (CoreML, TFLite, ONNX Runtime). Serving frameworks add the runtime concerns Kubernetes alone does not give for free: scale-to-zero, request batching, GPU sharing, multi-model loading, A/B routing.24

Monitoring. Latency, throughput, error rate — the classical SRE quartet — plus input drift, prediction drift, performance against delayed ground truth, fairness, and explainability slices. We will dwell on this in § VIII because it is the dimension where MLOps observability genuinely departs from conventional observability.

Retraining triggers. The output of monitoring loops back into the pipeline. Retraining may be scheduled (every night, every week), data-driven (when N new labels accumulate), or drift-triggered (when a PSI metric exceeds threshold). The closing of this loop is what makes MLOps operations rather than merely delivery.

§ V

Architectural patterns.

A handful of architectural patterns repeat across every mature ML platform, regardless of which tools are chosen to implement them. They emerged independently inside different companies, were generalised by the broader community, and are now common enough that an engineer joining a new ML team will recognise the shape of the system within an hour even if every component is unfamiliar.

The feature store

The single most important architectural innovation of the past decade of MLOps is the feature store, and the problem it solves — training-serving skew — is one of the most expensive bugs in production ML.

The pattern: a feature, once defined, is computed by exactly one piece of code. That code emits values into two stores: an offline store (a columnar warehouse such as BigQuery, Snowflake, or a Delta/Iceberg table) where features are time-partitioned for use in training; and an online store (a low-latency key-value system such as Redis, DynamoDB, Cassandra, or Bigtable) where the latest feature values are available for real-time inference. Training and serving consume the same definition, eliminating the most common source of skew.1213

Concept · Training-Serving Skew

The Google Play missing-feature incident

In a 2019 MLSys paper, Polyzotis and colleagues at Google described an incident on the Google Play app-store recommender. An engineer refactored the serving stack and inadvertently pinned a feature to a constant value. The model did not crash; it kept returning predictions, just lower-quality ones. Worse, the broken serving data was logged and reused to train the next model, baking the bug in further. The TFX Data Validation system was built explicitly to catch this class of skew.16 An A/B test in which the broken feature was repaired showed a measurable lift in the app-install rate.

The Google “Rules of ML” states the principle as a golden rule: ensure that training and production mimic each other as closely as possible.25

The deeper innovation of a feature store is point-in-time correctness. When training a fraud-detection model on historical transactions, the features used for each historical transaction must reflect only what was known at the moment of that transaction — not what the data warehouse looks like today. Feature stores expose APIs for these “as-of” joins, sparing data scientists from rewriting them (and getting them subtly wrong) for every project. Tecton and Hopsworks have built much of their commercial positioning around this guarantee; Feast offers an open-source implementation; and AWS, Google, and Databricks have all added native feature-store capabilities to their platforms.

Deployment patterns

The hierarchy of safety for shipping a new model is well-established and worth memorising. From least to most risky:

  • Shadow deployment. The new model receives the same requests as the production model but its responses are logged and never returned to users. This produces an apples-to-apples comparison on live traffic with zero user impact. The cheapest way to discover that your new model is 30 ms slower than the old one or behaves badly on a slice of inputs you didn't have in your test set.
  • Canary. A small percentage of real traffic (1%, 5%, 10%) is routed to the new model and its real-user-impact metrics are compared to the rest. If metrics hold, the percentage is increased. KServe and Seldon Core both express this declaratively as a traffic-split field on a CRD; Argo Rollouts and Flagger can drive the progression automatically against Prometheus metrics.
  • A/B test. Users are deterministically assigned to one of N model variants for statistical comparison over a meaningful time window. Distinct from canary in intent: canary aims to gradually de-risk a launch; A/B aims to measure causal lift.
  • Champion/challenger. A long-running variant of A/B testing in which a designated “champion” model serves the bulk of traffic and a rotating set of challengers compete for promotion.
  • Multi-armed bandit. Online learning over allocations — traffic is reallocated dynamically in proportion to observed performance, formally trading exploration against exploitation.

Online, batch, streaming, embedded

Modern serving frameworks support the four major topology classes within a single declarative model. KServe's InferenceService, for example, can specify a synchronous HTTP/gRPC endpoint backed by autoscaling pods (online), a batch transformation against an object-store input (batch), a Knative event source feeding the predictor (streaming), or, for embedded use cases, a model export pipeline that ships an ONNX or CoreML artefact to a device fleet.24 Different latency, throughput, and freshness regimes require radically different infrastructure decisions: scale-to-zero with Knative may be free under steady traffic but produce 2–5 s cold starts unsuited to interactive use cases; GPU sharing via Triton's instance groups dramatically improves utilisation but requires careful concurrency tuning.

Model cascades and ensembles

Production inference paths are rarely a single model. A search ranker invokes an embedding model, then a retrieval index, then a re-ranker, then a fairness post-processor; a recommender chains a candidate generator and a scoring model; an LLM application is in effect a graph of LLM calls, retrievals, and tool invocations. Serving frameworks have responded with first-class support for inference graphs: Seldon's inference graph, KServe's transformer/predictor/explainer composition, Triton's ensemble scheduler, BentoML's Runners, Ray Serve's deployment graphs. The composition pattern matters because latency and error budgets multiply: a graph of four 50 ms components in series gives 200 ms p50 and worse p99; a four-out-of-four AND of 99.9% availabilities gives 99.6%.

Workflow orchestration: the unsolved war

Apache Airflow, developed at Airbnb and first released in 2015,26 remains the most-deployed pipeline tool on the planet, but it was not designed for ML — its DAGs are static, its Python tasks don't pass typed data between steps in any first-class way, and its scheduler does not understand large artefacts. The ML-native orchestrators — Kubeflow Pipelines, Flyte (born at Lyft), Metaflow (born at Netflix), Argo Workflows, Prefect, Dagster — each address some subset of these gaps. Flyte's typed DAGs and strong caching, Metaflow's Pythonic ergonomics and artefact-versioning-by-default, and Argo's container-first Kubernetes-native semantics each represent a coherent design philosophy. There is no winner. Mature teams routinely run two: Airflow for data engineering, an ML-native orchestrator for model pipelines.

Experiment tracking: the layer everyone agrees on

If MLOps has a stable lower bound, it is experiment tracking. MLflow, since its 2018 release and 2019 1.0,11 has become near-universal because it gets the abstraction right: an experiment contains runs; each run captures parameters, metrics, tags, and artefacts; the model registry promotes artefacts through staging → production → archived. Weights & Biases offers a more polished UX and dominates in research labs; Neptune, Comet, and ClearML compete on integration breadth and on-prem options.

Serving: the layer with the most active design churn

For Kubernetes-native serving, three names dominate: KServe (formerly KFServing, spun out of Kubeflow), Seldon Core, and BentoML. They make different trade-offs. KServe is the most lightweight and the most opinionated about Kubernetes-native primitives — CRDs, Knative for scale-to-zero, native traffic-splitting; it is the right default for teams already running on Kubernetes who want serverless ML inference.24 Seldon Core offers the richest inference-graph semantics: chain a transformer, a predictor, an outlier-detector, and an explainer in one declarative spec. BentoML inverts the priority — its “Bento” bundles a Python service and its model into a deployable container with minimal Kubernetes intrusion, giving the simplest developer experience and the easiest path off-Kubernetes if needed.27

Underneath these orchestration-layer projects sit the inference runtimes. NVIDIA Triton is the dominant choice for GPU inference: framework-agnostic, supports dynamic batching, instance groups, and ensembles, and is increasingly the engine inside higher-level frameworks. TensorFlow Serving and TorchServe remain in heavy use within their respective ecosystems. For LLMs specifically, vLLM (with PagedAttention) and TensorRT-LLM have rapidly become the default high-throughput inference engines.

End-to-end platforms

The managed-cloud platforms — SageMaker, Vertex AI, Azure ML, Databricks — each offer a vertically integrated MLOps stack tightly bound to their parent cloud. They are pragmatic choices for teams not building a platform business of their own, and most large enterprises run at least one of them. The trade-off is the usual one: faster time to first production model in exchange for lock-in and limited extensibility.

The fully open-source end-to-end platforms — Kubeflow, ZenML, MLRun, Metaflow + Outerbounds — serve a different audience: organisations that have decided that ML platforms are core enough to own, that want portability across clouds, or that have regulatory reasons to run on-premises. Kubeflow is the most ambitious of these but also the heaviest; ZenML's pitch is explicitly to glue together whatever pieces a team has already chosen.

§ VII

CI · CD · CT.

Google's MLOps whitepaper is best remembered for adding a third C to the canonical pair: continuous training.2 The point is more than nomenclature. In conventional software, CI/CD describes how new code reaches users. In ML, the new artefact reaching users may have nothing to do with new code — it may be a newly trained model produced by the same code run on fresher data. CT names that distinct release path and forces the platform to treat it with the same rigour as code releases.

What that looks like, concretely, in a Level-2 (fully automated) pipeline:

Continuous Integration

Tests run on every commit, and they cover three artefact classes, not one. Code is unit-tested as usual. Data has its own tests: schema validation, statistical-property assertions, slice coverage. Model code has tests for feature transformations (same input → same output across training and serving paths), for training-pipeline integration (a tiny end-to-end run on a sample), and for model behaviour on canonical examples (the “does our credit-scoring model definitely refuse to use ZIP code” class of test). Eric Breck and colleagues' ML Test Score remains the most thorough enumeration of what these tests should cover.23

Continuous Delivery

What gets delivered is not a binary — it is a pipeline. The artefact produced by CI is a versioned, container-based training pipeline that can be deployed to a target environment. Releasing the pipeline does not, by itself, release a new model; it releases the capability to produce new models. This is the source of one of the most useful mental models in MLOps: deployment is two-stage. The pipeline is deployed; the pipeline then produces models; the models go through their own deployment.

Continuous Training

The pipeline is re-executed automatically. Triggers may be:

  • Schedule-based. Nightly, weekly, monthly. The simplest case; works well when the underlying phenomenon evolves slowly and predictably.
  • Data-driven. Retrain when N new labelled examples accumulate, when a new data slice becomes available, when an upstream table is refreshed.
  • Drift-triggered. Retrain when monitoring detects that the input distribution, prediction distribution, or measured performance has crossed a threshold — PSI, KS, Page-Hinkley, or downstream business metric.
  • Performance-triggered. Retrain when measured live performance (where ground truth becomes available) falls below an SLO.

Each newly trained model is automatically pushed through the same validation gates as any other release, and each is automatically deployed via the canary/shadow patterns of § V. The CT loop is the operational heart of MLOps: it is what makes a model a service rather than a one-time delivery.

pipeline.yaml — argo / kubeflow style excerpt 
# A minimal training pipeline as a typed DAG.
# Cache hits skip steps when inputs are unchanged.
name: fraud-detector-training
on:
  schedule: "0 3 * * *"     # nightly CT trigger
  drift:    "psi > 0.2"     # or fire when PSI breaches

steps:
  - validate_data:
      image: tfdv:1.14
      inputs:  [warehouse://transactions/{{ ds }}]
      asserts: schema_v3.pbtxt, stats_envelope.json
  - materialize_features:
      image: feast:0.43
      depends_on: [validate_data]
  - train:
      image: xgboost-gpu:2.0
      resources: { nvidia.com/gpu: 1 }
      params:   { seed: 42, depth: 8 }
      outputs:  [model.joblib, metrics.json]
  - evaluate:
      image: ml-eval:1.2
      gates:
        - auc >= champion.auc * 0.99
        - latency_p99 <= 80ms
        - fairness.demographic_parity < 0.05
  - register:
      registry: mlflow://prod
      stage: staging
  - deploy:
      strategy: shadow → canary(5%) → canary(50%) → full
      rollback_on: { error_rate: >1%, latency_p99: >100ms }

The example above looks like a CI pipeline because, structurally, it is a CI pipeline — one with steps that happen to validate data, train a model, and gate against fairness metrics. The convergence is the point. Mature MLOps platforms minimise the difference between a code release and a model release; both flow through pipelines, both face validation gates, both deploy via progressive-delivery strategies, both can be rolled back.

§ VIII

Observability for probabilistic systems.

Conventional observability — the discipline of inferring system health from logs, metrics, and traces — transfers cleanly to ML serving for everything except the most important question: is the model still right? A model can be returning 200 OKs at 12 ms p99 while being catastrophically wrong, and no quantity of golden-signals dashboards will tell you. ML observability is the discipline of catching wrongness without errors.

The four layers

Production ML monitoring layers, from cheapest to slowest signal:

  1. Operational metrics. Latency, throughput, error rate, saturation — the conventional SRE quartet. Necessary, not sufficient.
  2. Input drift. Are the features the model is receiving today distributed like the features it was trained on? This is detectable immediately and requires no labels.
  3. Prediction drift. Is the output distribution of the model shifting? Detectable immediately, also label-free. Most useful as a corroborating signal alongside input drift.
  4. Performance against ground truth. The gold standard, but available only when labels arrive — which may be hours later (clicks), days (fraud disputes), or never (a counterfactual outcome).

Drift, taxonomically

The literature distinguishes carefully between two ideas that are routinely conflated:21

Definitions

Data drift vs. concept drift

Data drift (or covariate shift) is a change in the distribution of the input features P(X). The relationship between inputs and outputs has not changed; you are simply seeing a different mix of inputs. Example: your fraud model was trained on 2019 transactions; the post-COVID payment mix is structurally different, but a fraudulent transaction still looks like a fraudulent transaction.

Concept drift is a change in the relationship between inputs and outputs, P(Y|X). Same inputs, different correct answers. Example: customer-churn behaviour after a competitor enters the market — the features look identical, but customers behave differently.

A third class, label drift or prior-probability shift, captures changes in the base-rate distribution of the target itself.

Drift also has a temporal taxonomy: sudden drift (a pandemic, a regulation change), gradual drift (slow demographic change), incremental drift (steady creep), and recurring drift (seasonal patterns). The choice of detector should match the expected shape.

The detection toolkit

For numeric features, two-sample tests of distribution equality are the workhorse: the Kolmogorov–Smirnov test for continuous distributions, the Chi-squared test for categoricals. Distance metrics — Population Stability Index (PSI), Jensen–Shannon divergence, Wasserstein distance — let you reduce a distributional comparison to a single threshold-able number. PSI in particular has become standard in credit-risk monitoring because of its long history in scoring; a PSI above 0.2 is conventionally treated as a significant drift signal.28

For sequential / streaming detection, Page-Hinkley and CUSUM tests detect change points in real-valued streams; ADWIN (Adaptive Windowing) maintains an adaptive window over recent observations and triggers when a statistically significant difference is detected between sub-windows.29 For multivariate drift, lower-dimensional projections (PCA, autoencoders, embedding-space distances) are typically followed by univariate detectors on the projection.

The tooling layer

Open-source: Evidently is the most-installed, providing batch and live monitoring with a rich set of drift detectors and visual reports. NannyML emphasises performance estimation in the absence of labels, using techniques such as Confidence-based Performance Estimation (CBPE). Commercial: Arize, Fiddler, WhyLabs, and Aporia compete on UX, integrations, root-cause analysis, and (increasingly) LLM observability.

A model can be returning 200 OKs at 12 ms p99 while being catastrophically wrong. Observability for ML is the discipline of catching wrongness without errors.

The hard part: explanation

A drift alarm is the start of an investigation, not the end. Most production drift is benign — a Monday with more traffic from a new geographic region, a marketing campaign that pulled in a different demographic. The platform must support root-cause analysis: which features drifted, on which slice, for which model version, since when. The cross-tabulation of monitoring with lineage (which upstream table produced which feature, and what changed when) is where ML observability platforms differentiate.

§ IX

Governance, reproducibility, compliance.

For most of MLOps' short history, governance was an afterthought — if a team had it, it was because a regulated sector (banking, healthcare, insurance) required it. That ended around 2023. The publication of the NIST AI Risk Management Framework in January 2023 and the agreement of the EU AI Act through 2024 collectively turned governance from an enterprise concern into a default one. Any MLOps platform built after 2024 is, implicitly or explicitly, building toward these requirements.

Reproducibility, in practice

Reproducibility is the bedrock requirement of any governance regime: if you cannot reproduce how a decision was made, you cannot audit it, explain it, or correct it. In ML, full reproducibility is a multi-axis problem:

  • Code — Git commit SHAs everywhere; container image digests, not tags.
  • Data — immutable snapshots referenced by content hash; tools such as DVC, lakeFS, Pachyderm, Delta Lake's time-travel.
  • Environment — pinned package versions; locked transitive dependencies; reproducible base images.
  • Configuration — hyperparameters, seeds, hardware specifications, distributed-training topology all recorded by the experiment tracker.
  • Lineage — the upstream provenance of every feature, traced back to source.

Even with all of this, bit-exact reproducibility on GPUs is often impossible because of non-deterministic kernels; the practical goal is statistical reproducibility — a retraining run that lands within a tight metric envelope of the original. The discipline is to know which decisions you made, why, and against what evidence.

Documentation as artefact

Two pieces of academic work have become the de facto documentation standards. Mitchell and colleagues' Model Cards (2019) propose a one-page model fact sheet covering intended use, evaluation data, performance across demographic slices, ethical considerations, and caveats.17 Gebru and colleagues' Datasheets for Datasets proposes the analogous artefact for training data: motivation, composition, collection process, recommended uses, limitations.30 A mature MLOps platform generates both automatically from pipeline metadata, attaching them to the model in the registry.

The regulatory landscape

The NIST AI RMF organises around four functions: Govern establishes policy and accountability; Map identifies AI risks in context; Measure analyses and quantifies them; Manage prioritises and treats them.18 It is voluntary, but it has become the vocabulary regulators and enterprise procurement teams use, which makes it effectively required in many sectors.

The EU AI Act, by contrast, is law. It classifies AI systems into prohibited, high-risk, limited-risk, and minimal-risk tiers. High-risk systems — which include AI used in hiring, credit decisions, education, critical infrastructure, and law enforcement — face hard obligations: formal conformity assessments before deployment, ongoing post-market monitoring, detailed technical documentation, training-data records, human-oversight mechanisms, and demonstration of robustness and accuracy.19 Penalties for non-compliance reach €35 million or 7% of global annual turnover. The Act has extraterritorial reach: any organisation deploying AI to EU users is in scope.

Operationally, what this means for MLOps platforms is that the platform itself is now part of the evidence base. The model registry must hold immutable records sufficient to reconstruct any production decision. Lineage must be auditable end-to-end. Drift monitoring must demonstrably trigger appropriate response. Human-in-the-loop overrides must be logged. The investment in the architectural patterns of § V is increasingly being justified not by engineering excellence but by audit posture.

The new attack surface

The other half of governance is security. The classic AI/ML threat taxonomy — adversarial examples, data poisoning, model extraction, membership inference — has been joined since 2023 by an explosion of LLM-specific threats: prompt injection (direct and indirect), jailbreaks, training-data extraction, agent-tool abuse. MITRE's ATLAS framework (the ML equivalent of ATT&CK) catalogues these systematically and is becoming the reference vocabulary for ML threat modelling. Tools such as Garak, Giskard, and ProtectAI's Adversarial Robustness Toolbox provide automated testing harnesses against many of these threats. Few of them are yet first-class in mainstream MLOps platforms; that gap is where most platform-engineering investment in 2025–26 is going.

§ X

LLMOps — the new frontier.

Late 2022 broke the assumption underneath nearly every component of the MLOps stack: that the model under management is one you trained yourself. The arrival of capable foundation models — GPT-3.5, then GPT-4, Claude, Gemini, Llama and its descendants — recast the typical ML application as a system built around a model the team does not own, cannot fine-tune from scratch, and accesses through an API or an inference server it operates but did not train. The discipline that grew up around this shift goes by LLMOps, and while it inherits much of MLOps' vocabulary, the centre of gravity has moved sharply from training to inference.31

The stack, in flux

The LLM application stack as of 2026 has roughly stabilised into layers, though every layer still has multiple credible competitors:

  • Inference serving. vLLM (with its PagedAttention KV-cache management), TensorRT-LLM, SGLang, llm-d, and the LLM modes of Triton dominate self-hosted serving. For commercial APIs, the equivalent layer is the provider's own infrastructure.
  • Orchestration / agent frameworks. LangChain, LlamaIndex, LangGraph, Haystack, and an increasing number of TypeScript-native equivalents handle prompt templating, chain composition, tool use, and (in newer versions) agentic state.
  • Retrieval. Vector databases (Pinecone, Weaviate, Qdrant, Milvus, Chroma, pgvector) and embedding models form the substrate for retrieval-augmented generation.
  • Evaluation. A wholly new layer with no MLOps equivalent: tools such as Promptfoo, OpenAI Evals, LangSmith, Langfuse, Arize Phoenix, DeepEval, and Ragas provide harnesses for behavioural evaluation, regression testing of prompts, and LLM-as-judge scoring.
  • Observability. LangSmith, Langfuse, Helicone, Arize Phoenix, and others provide tracing of the LLM call graph: every prompt, every tool invocation, every retrieval, every token used and its cost.
  • Guardrails. Input/output filters for prompt injection, PII, toxicity, format compliance. NeMo Guardrails, Guardrails AI, and providers' own safety APIs occupy this slot.

Prompts are now configuration — treat them that way

The single most common LLMOps failure mode is treating prompts as something other than configuration. Prompts must be versioned. Changes to a prompt must go through review. Prompt rollouts must be canaried. Prompt regressions must be detected automatically by behavioural eval suites — the LLMOps equivalent of unit tests. Mature LLM teams maintain test sets in the hundreds or thousands of canonical inputs against which any prompt change is run; the LLM-as-judge pattern, where another model scores outputs against a rubric, has become the dominant scalable scoring technique.33

Eval-driven development

The replacement for “train, look at the loss curve, ship” is eval-driven development: maintain an evolving set of evaluations that capture what the system should and should not do; iterate on prompts, retrieval configuration, and tool definitions against those evals; ship when evals improve and never regress. The eval set, not the model, is the team's asset. It is roughly the LLM-era equivalent of the training set in classical ML, and it requires the same discipline: versioning, slice analysis, deliberate addition of failure cases as they are discovered.

RAG, fine-tuning, and the adaptation hierarchy

The standard cost-ordered hierarchy of adaptations runs: prompt engineering (cheapest, fastest iteration); few-shot prompting; retrieval-augmented generation, in which relevant documents are pulled from a vector store and injected into the prompt;34 parameter-efficient fine-tuning (LoRA, QLoRA, adapters); and finally full fine-tuning. The platform engineering decision of when to climb that ladder — and when to step off, because the next rung's cost and complexity exceed its quality gain — is a recurring LLMOps judgement call. Most production systems use a combination: a base model accessed via API, light fine-tuning or LoRA for domain-specific behaviour, and RAG for knowledge that must be fresh or proprietary.

The agent turn

The frontier of 2025–26 is agentic systems: LLMs that issue tool calls, observe results, plan, and iterate. The operational complications are immediate. Agent traces fan out in unpredictable ways; latencies multiply across tool calls; failure modes include not just hallucination but tool misuse, infinite loops, and prompt injection through tool outputs. The discipline of operating these systems — sometimes called AgentOps, though the term has not yet stuck — is what most of the LLMOps community is building toward in 2026.

Classical MLOps optimised models; LLMOps optimises behaviour. The artefacts that determine system quality have moved out of the weights and into the prompts, the retrieval corpus, the tool definitions, and the eval set.
§ XI

The road ahead.

The hard problems of MLOps in 2026 are not the ones the discipline started with. Feature stores, model registries, drift detectors, and canary deployments are now widely available, well-understood, and increasingly commoditised. The interesting frontier is somewhere else.

Four directions are visibly shaping where the discipline is heading.

The compute layer is being rewritten

The Kubernetes-centric model of ML serving — the one that drove Kubeflow, KServe, Seldon, and BentoML — is creaking under the weight of GPU economics and LLM-shaped workloads. Projects like Ray (and its commercial twin Anyscale), Modal, Replicate, and a new generation of LLM-native systems (llm-d, AIBrix, NVIDIA Dynamo) are building runtimes that treat GPUs, KV caches, and model loading as first-class. This is the layer with the most active research-grade engineering happening today.

The convergence of MLOps and DataOps

The boundary between “the data team” and “the ML team” has been eroding for years, and the lakehouse architecture (Databricks Delta, Snowflake, Iceberg-based stacks) accelerates the trend. Feature stores increasingly run inside the data warehouse rather than alongside it; model training runs directly against warehouse tables; observability stacks for data and ML merge. Five years from now, MLOps and DataOps will probably look like two specialisations of a single discipline whose name has not yet stuck.

On-device and privacy-preserving ML

The infrastructure assumption of MLOps' first decade — that inference happens in a data centre — is now an option, not a default. CoreML, TFLite, ONNX Runtime, llama.cpp, and Apple's MLX have made on-device inference routine. Federated learning, differential privacy, and trusted-execution-environment inference are moving from research curiosities to production patterns in healthcare, finance, and consumer apps. The operational discipline of pushing model artefacts to fleets of millions of devices is structurally different from operating a few replicas behind a load balancer.

Trustworthy AI as a platform concern

The regulatory and security regimes of § IX will increasingly be enforced by the platform itself rather than by audit processes bolted on top. Model registries with built-in bias evaluation, lineage systems that automatically generate EU AI Act technical documentation, monitoring stacks that produce drift evidence by default — these are the next generation of platform capabilities. The shift is from MLOps as engineering convenience to MLOps as governance substrate.

None of this changes the fundamental thing Sculley and colleagues identified a decade ago. ML code remains the small box in the middle of a much larger machine. What the discipline has added since then is the apparatus to build the rest of the machine deliberately rather than accidentally — to make explicit what was implicit, to version what was unversioned, to monitor what was unmonitored, to gate what was ungated. The fact that this once needed to be argued for is what makes the field's history short. The fact that it still needs to be argued for, in any team without a deliberate platform investment, is what makes the field's history not yet over.
