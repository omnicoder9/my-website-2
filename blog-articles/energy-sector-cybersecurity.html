In most industries a breach spills data. In the energy sector it can spin a centrifuge apart, open a circuit breaker, or darken a city — and because everything else runs on electrons and molecules, the failure rarely stays in one sector. A technical briefing on the machines, the adversaries, the cascade, and the long work of defense.

01 / 10  The keystone sector

Why energy sits at the center of everything else

The United States protects sixteen critical infrastructure sectors — from chemical plants and dams to financial services and hospitals — defined as systems so essential that their incapacitation would debilitate national security, the economy, or public health.1 They are not, however, equal. A handful are lifeline functions: communications, energy, transportation, and water. These four underpin the operation of practically every other sector, and the dense web of dependencies between them means that disrupting one can produce cascading effects far beyond its own boundary.2

Energy is the most load-bearing of the four. Hospitals, data centers, water-treatment pumps, traffic signals, payment networks, fuel terminals, and the cell towers that carry emergency calls all assume that electricity will be there. The U.S. Department of Energy is the designated Sector Risk Management Agency for energy precisely because the reliable flow of power and fuel is treated as a precondition for national defense, economic activity, and daily life.3

What makes cybersecurity in this sector categorically different from defending an email server or a retail database is the nature of the consequence. Most information-security failures spill data. A failure in energy can spill energy. The systems at risk do not merely store records; they open and close high-voltage breakers, modulate the speed of turbines, regulate the pressure of gas in a pipeline, and trip the safety interlocks that stand between a running process and an explosion. When an attacker reaches those systems, bits become physics. That is the throughline of this briefing — and the reason the grid, unlike a website, has no undo button.

02 / 10  The machines

Operational technology: the equipment that moves electrons and molecules

Enterprise security has spent thirty years defending information technology (IT): laptops, servers, email, databases — systems whose job is to process data, where the cardinal sin is loss of confidentiality. The energy sector runs on a different class of machine. Operational technology (OT) is the hardware and software that monitors and controls physical processes and the devices that act on the physical world. Here the priorities invert: availability and safety come first, integrity second, confidentiality last. A control system that stops, or that lies to the operator, is far more dangerous than one that leaks.

The category is broad enough that, in 2023, NIST renamed its foundational guidance from the Guide to Industrial Control Systems Security to the Guide to Operational Technology (OT) Security — Special Publication 800-82, Revision 3 — to capture not just classic industrial control systems but building automation, transportation systems, physical access control, and the wider universe of cyber-physical devices.4 That revision also added an OT-specific control overlay — tailored low-, moderate-, and high-impact baselines for the SP 800-53 catalog, aligned to the NIST Cybersecurity Framework.5 Within that universe, the energy sector leans on a recognizable stack of acronyms.

The control-system stack

Industrial control systems (ICS) is the umbrella term. Beneath it sit two architectures: SCADA (Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition), built to monitor and command assets spread across vast distances — substations strung along a transmission corridor, valves along a thousand-mile pipeline — and DCS (Distributed Control Systems), built to run a single, tightly-coupled plant like a refinery or power station. Both rest on the same field-level building blocks: programmable logic controllers (PLCs) and remote terminal units (RTUs) that execute control logic; intelligent electronic devices (IEDs) such as the protective relays that decide, in milliseconds, whether to trip a breaker; human-machine interfaces (HMIs) where operators watch and steer; and historians and engineering workstations that log process data and reprogram the controllers. At the very bottom are the sensors and actuators — the instruments that read pressure, flow, voltage, and temperature, and the motors and valves that change them.4

How the grid is actually built

The electric power system has three functions: generation (turning fuel, fission, wind, water, or sunlight into electricity), transmission (moving it long distances at high voltage), and distribution (stepping it down and delivering it to homes and businesses).6 The high-voltage transmission backbone — the bulk electric system — is operated through energy management systems (EMS) that perform real-time monitoring, state estimation, and control. The distribution layer increasingly runs on distribution management and outage management systems, plus advanced metering infrastructure (AMI) — the two-way smart meters that now sit in tens of millions of homes — and a fast-growing population of distributed energy resources (DERs): rooftop solar, battery storage, and electric-vehicle chargers, each a small networked computer with a path to the grid.6

Oil and natural gas add their own control systems: pipeline SCADA that meters and routes product over continental distances, compressor and pump stations, terminal automation, and the safety-instrumented systems of refineries and processing plants. The Colonial Pipeline — the largest refined-products pipeline in the country — is a single SCADA-governed artery moving fuel from the Gulf Coast to the Eastern Seaboard. We will return to what happened when it stopped.

The protocols, and their original sin

Tying this stack together is a set of industrial communication protocols — Modbus, DNP3, IEC 61850, IEC 60870-5-104, OPC UA — many of them designed decades ago for isolated, physically-trusted serial networks. Most were built with no authentication and no encryption, because the engineers who designed them never imagined the wire would be reachable from the internet. A command that arrives in the right format is simply obeyed. That assumption, baked into the silicon and the standards, is the original sin of OT security, and modern attacks exploit it directly.4

The reference architecture defenders reach for

Because OT cannot rely on the protocols to defend themselves, the dominant defensive idea is architecture: arrange the systems into layered zones, and tightly control what may cross between them. NIST SP 800-82 codifies this with reference OT topologies and the control overlay noted above, so that an operator can map the five Cybersecurity Framework functions — identify, protect, detect, respond, recover — onto a plant floor.5 The conceptual scaffold beneath all of it is the Purdue model: a layered view of a control environment that makes the IT/OT boundary explicit and shows exactly where an intruder must travel to reach the process.

03 / 10  Who is at the door

The threat landscape: actors, access, and the seam where they get in

Three broad classes of adversary contend for energy infrastructure, and their motives shape their methods. Nation-states — principally Russia, China, and Iran — pursue strategic disruption, espionage, and the quiet pre-positioning of access for use in a future crisis. Financially-motivated criminals, above all ransomware crews, monetize disruption: they rarely target OT directly, but an IT-side ransomware detonation can force an operator to shut the physical process as a precaution. Hacktivists pursue attention and symbolism, and historically caused little operational harm — until, recently, they began reaching the control layer too.

For most of the grid’s history, the field equipment was protected by isolation: serial links, air gaps, and obscurity. That protection has eroded. The U.S. Government Accountability Office found that the transmission and generation systems are increasingly vulnerable because their industrial control systems now permit remote access and connect to business networks; it flagged not just classic ICS exposure but high-wattage consumer Internet-of-Things devices and even reliance on GPS for grid time-synchronization as attack surfaces — compounded by the fact that much of the installed base predates the idea of network security entirely.7 A companion review of the distribution systems — the state-regulated last mile — reached a starker conclusion: their sheer size and dispersed geography present an enormous attack surface, the new generation of distributed energy resources adds remotely-updatable devices that are unevenly secured, supply chains introduce their own risk, and the scale of the potential impact is, candidly, not yet well understood.6

The OT environment has become a mainstream target — and even advanced operations are now using unsophisticated tactics to disrupt critical infrastructure.

— Paraphrasing the central finding of the Dragos 2024 year-in-review

The convergence problem

The single most important shift is the collapse of the wall between IT and OT. Plants that ran on isolated networks now stream telemetry to cloud analytics, accept vendor remote-maintenance sessions, and share Active Directory with the business. Each of those conveniences is also a pathway. The industrial-security firm Dragos, which tracks the adversaries that specialize in this terrain, reported a steadily widening field: as of its 2024 retrospective it was following more than twenty distinct OT-focused threat groups, and ransomware activity against industrial organizations had surged — up roughly 87% over the prior year.8 Several of those groups — VOLTZITE, ELECTRUM, CHERNOVITE, KAMACITE, and newcomers like BAUXITE and GRAPHITE — demonstrate dedicated, OT-specific tradecraft against the electric, oil-and-gas, and chemical verticals.8 The same body of intelligence singles out two structural weaknesses in the electric sector: the proliferating, unevenly-secured population of distributed energy resources, and the remote-access points that modern grid operations depend on — the exact doors adversaries reach for first.9

Living off the land

The most consequential development in nation-state targeting of U.S. infrastructure is a tradecraft, not a tool. In a February 2024 advisory, CISA, the NSA, and the FBI warned that a People’s Republic of China state-sponsored group tracked as Volt Typhoon had compromised the networks of U.S. critical-infrastructure organizations across the communications, energy, transportation, and water-and-wastewater sectors — and was not stealing secrets but pre-positioning, maintaining persistent access for potential disruptive or destructive effect in a future conflict.10 Its signature is “living off the land”: rather than deploying malware that scanners can catch, the actors abuse the legitimate, built-in administrative tools already present on the systems, blending into normal traffic and leaving little for conventional logging to see. The government’s framing marked a strategic shift in Chinese cyber activity from espionage toward pre-positioning; CISA’s then-director described the intrusions the agency had already found and eradicated as likely “the tip of the iceberg.”11

This is why an attack can leave almost no malware behind and still be an emergency. Adversaries increasingly exploit the trusted-but-insecure industrial protocols themselves — speaking Modbus or its kin directly to the equipment — an approach that sails past antivirus tools because, at the protocol level, nothing about the command looks wrong.8 The defender’s problem, in short, is no longer only keeping malware out. It is telling a legitimate command from a malicious one when both are perfectly well-formed.

04 / 10  A short, brutal history

Fifteen years of attacks that crossed from the network into the world

The fear that code could break machines was abstract until 2010, when Stuxnet made it concrete. Widely regarded as the first known cyberweapon to cause physical destruction, it was built to sabotage the uranium-enrichment centrifuges at Iran’s Natanz facility: it located specific Siemens PLCs, then commanded the centrifuges through punishing speed changes while replaying recorded “normal” readings so the operators watching their screens saw nothing wrong.12 It reached the air-gapped plant on a USB drive, chained four previously-unknown vulnerabilities, and ultimately wrecked on the order of a thousand of the site’s roughly nine thousand machines — about a fifth of them — before it was found. It has been widely attributed by researchers to a joint U.S.–Israeli operation, though never officially acknowledged.13

Stuxnet was rare in another sense: for years, malware tailored to industrial control systems could be counted on one hand — Stuxnet, the espionage tool Havex, and BlackEnergy among the earliest, with Industroyer soon to follow.14 What changed is that the lineage stopped being rare. The timeline below traces the attacks that taught the energy sector, one disaster at a time, what it was up against.

  1. 2010ICS malware · 1st

    Stuxnet — sabotage becomes possible

    A worm spins Iran’s enrichment centrifuges to destruction by reprogramming their controllers, while feeding falsified telemetry to operators. Proof that software can break hardware.1213

  2. 2013–14Espionage

    Havex / Dragonfly — mapping the targets

    Reconnaissance malware quietly catalogs energy-sector control systems across the U.S. and Europe — the surveillance phase that precedes sabotage.14

  3. 20151st grid blackout

    Ukraine — the lights go out by command

    On 23 December, after months of access gained through spear-phishing and BlackEnergy malware, attackers remotely opened breakers at three regional distribution companies, cutting power to about 225,000 customers, then wiped control systems and flooded the call center to blind the response.15 The U.S. government attributed both this and the next year’s attack to Russia’s GRU.16 It was the first publicly known cyberattack to cause a power outage.17

  4. 2016Purpose-built

    Industroyer / CrashOverride — a weapon for grids

    On 17 December, a modular framework de-energized a Kyiv transmission substation for about an hour. Unlike Stuxnet, it targeted no single vendor: it spoke the grid’s own control protocols natively, making it portable to other grids worldwide. Analysts judged it a proof of concept.18

  5. 2017Built to kill

    Triton / Trisis — attacking the safety net

    At a petrochemical plant, malware targeted the Schneider Electric Triconex safety-instrumented systems — the automated last line of defense against explosion and toxic release. It was the first attack designed to disable safety systems and risk physical harm and loss of life; a flaw in the malware tripped the plant to a safe state and exposed it.19 The U.S. attributed it to a Russian government research institute; the worst-case outcome was a lethal gas release or blast.20

  6. 2021U.S. wake-up

    Colonial Pipeline — ransomware stops the fuel

    DarkSide ransomware hit Colonial’s business IT; the company proactively shut the pipeline to keep the attack from reaching operations.21 Panic-buying and fuel shortages spread across the Southeast.22 The entry point was mundane — one reused VPN password with no multi-factor authentication — and the President declared a state of emergency.23 Operational technology was never directly compromised, yet a $4.4 million ransom was paid and nearly half the East Coast’s fuel had stopped flowing.24

  7. 2022ICS malware · 7th

    PIPEDREAM / INCONTROLLER — caught first

    A state actor’s modular, cross-industry toolkit — able to find and manipulate Schneider and Omron controllers and OPC UA servers across many sectors — was discovered by U.S. agencies and partners before it could be deployed in the wild.25

  8. 2022Relapse

    Industroyer2 — the grid weapon returns

    A variant of the 2016 framework, rebuilt to target high-voltage substation equipment, was aimed at a Ukrainian electricity provider amid the full-scale invasion — and foiled before it caused a blackout.26

  9. 2023–24Pre-positioning

    Volt Typhoon — the adversary is already inside

    PRC state actors are found embedded across U.S. communications, energy, transportation, and water networks — not exfiltrating data, but holding access for disruption in a future crisis, using stealthy living-off-the-land tradecraft.1011

  10. 2024ICS malware · 8–9th

    FrostyGoop & Fuxnet — the toolbox grows

    FrostyGoop manipulated a standard industrial protocol to knock out heating for more than 600 apartment buildings in Ukraine during sub-zero weather — evading antivirus entirely. The eighth and ninth known ICS-specific malware families surfaced this year, several abusing trusted, unmonitored OT protocols.8

Read together, the lineage is a curriculum. Stuxnet proved that software can cause physical destruction. Ukraine proved that a national grid can be switched off from a keyboard. Triton proved that even the safety systems — the controls of last resort — are now targets. Colonial proved that an attacker need not touch operational technology at all: ransomware on the business side can stop the molecules just as effectively, by forcing operators to shut down themselves. And Volt Typhoon proved the most unsettling lesson of all — that for the most capable adversaries, the foothold may already exist, lying dormant, waiting.

Case File — Colonial PipelineMay 2021 · CONUS

The Colonial incident is the clearest domestic illustration of how a routine IT compromise becomes a national fuel crisis — and how mundane the entry point usually is.

Initial access
A single set of valid credentials for a legacy VPN account — a password later found exposed in an unrelated breach and likely reused. The account had no multi-factor authentication.23
What was actually hit
Business IT systems, encrypted by DarkSide ransomware-as-a-service. The pipeline’s control systems were not compromised; the operator shut the line proactively to contain uncertainty about the attack’s reach.21
Physical & economic effect
A multi-day shutdown of the artery that carries roughly 45% of the East Coast’s fuel; panic-buying, station outages, and price spikes across seventeen states; a $4.4 million ransom paid for a decryption tool.24
The lesson
You do not have to breach OT to cause an OT-scale consequence. Identity hygiene — MFA, decommissioning dormant accounts, segmenting remote access — is grid security. Colonial triggered the first mandatory federal cybersecurity rules for U.S. pipelines within weeks.

05 / 10  The blast radius

Why a hit on energy doesn’t stay in energy

The sixteen critical-infrastructure sectors are not a list of silos; they are an interdependent ecosystem in which the failure of one propagates into the others.1 To reason about that coupling, CISA defined a set of fifty-five National Critical Functions — cross-cutting capabilities like “distribute electricity,” “provide medical care,” and “maintain supply chains” — precisely so that risk can be analyzed across sector boundaries rather than within them.27 In 2024 the national policy governing this work was renewed as a National Security Memorandum, which further directs the government to identify Systemically Important Entities: the specific operators whose disruption would cause nationally significant, cascading harm.28 Energy is where that cascade most often begins, because energy is the input to everything else — including, recursively, to the other lifeline sectors that energy itself depends on.

What a severe grid attack does to a country

The most cited attempt to quantify this is the Business Blackout study by Lloyd’s of London and the University of Cambridge’s Centre for Risk Studies. Its scenario imagines attackers inflicting physical damage on a few dozen generators in the northeastern United States, triggering a regional blackout that leaves roughly 93 million people across fifteen states and Washington, D.C. without power. The modeled consequences read like a description of society unthreading: rising mortality as health and safety systems fail, ports and trade seizing up, water supplies disrupted as electric pumps stop, and transport networks thrown into chaos. The estimated hit to the U.S. economy ranges from about $243 billion to more than $1 trillion in the most extreme variant.29 The authors are careful that this is a hypothetical stress test, not a forecast — a tool for thinking about extreme, correlated shocks rather than a prediction of any specific event.30 Its value is in making the coupling legible: the dollar figure is large precisely because the damage refuses to stay in the energy sector.

The dependencies, made concrete

Strip away the abstraction and the couplings are physical and immediate. Communications networks — cell towers, data centers, the internet backbone — run on power, and have only hours of battery and generator backup; yet the grid’s own SCADA depends on those same networks, which is why an attack that degrades both is so dangerous.2 Water and wastewater systems are electric-pump operations end to end: no power means no pressure, no treatment, and eventually no safe drinking water — and these systems share both the threat actors and the weaknesses of energy. Iran-linked hacktivists in late 2023 compromised internet-exposed programmable logic controllers at water utilities across North America, and Volt Typhoon’s footprint sat in water networks alongside energy ones.810 Financial services — payment rails, exchanges, ATMs — are data-center operations that assume continuous power. Healthcare depends on it for ventilators, imaging, refrigerated medicine, and the pumps that keep patients alive. Transportation needs electricity for signals and increasingly for vehicle charging, and it needs fuel — which is exactly what stopped flowing when Colonial went dark, disrupting ground transport and aviation across the Eastern Seaboard.2224 Chemical and food-and-agriculture facilities depend on power for the cooling, pressure, and process control that keep volatile reactions safe and perishable supply chains intact.

This is the reason energy security and national security have become difficult to separate — and the reason the most sophisticated adversary the U.S. has publicly identified did not pick a single target. Volt Typhoon deliberately embedded itself across four lifeline sectors at once — communications, energy, transportation, and water — which is the textbook recipe for a coordinated, mutually-reinforcing crisis rather than an isolated outage.11

06 / 10  Rules of the road

Standards, frameworks, and the patchwork of U.S. regulation

The United States does not regulate energy cybersecurity through a single law. It governs it through a layered patchwork — some of it mandatory and enforceable, much of it voluntary and consensus-built — partitioned by who regulates which slice of the system. Understanding that partition is the key to understanding both the strengths and the conspicuous gaps in the regime.

NERC CIP — the mandatory spine of the bulk power system

The most muscular regime covers the high-voltage backbone. The North American Electric Reliability Corporation’s Critical Infrastructure Protection (CIP) standards are mandatory, enforceable cybersecurity requirements for the bulk electric system, approved by the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission in 2008 and enforced by NERC under FERC oversight, with financial penalties for non-compliance.31 They are a living regime: FERC has continued to modernize them, with recent rulemakings extending secure use of virtualization and raising the baseline controls required for lower-impact systems.32 Prompted in part by the kind of stealthy, internal-pivot tradecraft seen in the Volt Typhoon campaign, FERC ordered the addition of internal network security monitoring — the new CIP-015 standard — so that defenders gain visibility into east-west traffic inside the trusted zone, not just at its perimeter.33 Today the family comprises roughly a dozen enforceable standards spanning domains from asset identification (CIP-002) and electronic security perimeters (CIP-005) to incident response (CIP-008) and physical security (CIP-014).34

TSA pipeline directives — regulation written in the aftermath

Pipelines tell a different, more reactive story. Colonial Pipeline triggered the first mandatory federal cybersecurity rules for U.S. pipelines: within weeks, the Transportation Security Administration issued Security Directive Pipeline-2021-01, requiring covered operators to report cybersecurity incidents to CISA, designate a 24/7 cybersecurity coordinator, and conduct a vulnerability assessment.35 After industry pushback at the prescriptive early versions, a 2022 revision shifted to performance-based requirements — specifying the security outcomes operators must achieve and letting them tailor the means.36 The directives were part of a broader federal push that included an Executive Order on improving national cybersecurity, and they carry enforcement teeth, with civil penalties reaching into the thousands of dollars per violation per day.37 The crucial caveat: the mandate is narrow. It binds only the roughly one hundred pipeline and LNG facilities TSA deems most critical; before Colonial even those were governed only by voluntary guidance, and the rest of the nation’s pipelines largely still are.38

The frameworks everyone builds on

Beneath the regulations sit the technical frameworks that organizations actually use to operationalize them. The federal reference is NIST’s Cybersecurity Framework paired with SP 800-82, whose Revision 3 supplies an OT-specific control overlay and tailored impact baselines — the bridge that lets a power plant map identify-protect-detect-respond-recover onto its own equipment.45 The dominant international standard is ISA/IEC 62443, the framework for industrial automation and control systems security. Its core ideas — partitioning a plant into zones connected by tightly-controlled conduits, and assigning each zone a target security level proportional to the consequence of its compromise — have become the common engineering vocabulary of OT defense; U.S. guidance treats it as complementary to SP 800-82, not in competition with it.39

The gap the patchwork leaves

Lay the regimes side by side and the seam is obvious. The bulk power system is tightly, mandatorily regulated; the distribution systems that deliver the last mile are regulated primarily by individual states, and their cybersecurity remains largely a matter of voluntary effort — even as that layer accumulates the largest, most dispersed attack surface and the fast-growing population of distributed energy resources.6 Pipelines outside the critical hundred sit in a similar voluntary space.38 The result is a defensive map with strong walls around the parts that fail spectacularly and visibly, and thinner coverage around the parts whose failure is more localized — a rational triage that nonetheless leaves real exposure where the grid touches the most people.

07 / 10  The defender’s toolkit

Process, technology, and tools that actually move the needle

Frameworks describe what good looks like; this is the how. OT defense cannot borrow the IT playbook wholesale, because the constraints are different: you usually cannot reboot to patch, you cannot tolerate a scanner that knocks a controller offline, and the system you are protecting may run unchanged for twenty years. What follows is the defense-in-depth playbook the standards converge on, ordered roughly by leverage.

  1. See the estate first. You cannot protect or even risk-rank what you cannot enumerate, yet OT asset inventories are notoriously incomplete — and the active scanning IT relies on can crash fragile devices. A current, passive inventory of every controller, its firmware, and its communication paths is the foundation every other control builds on, and the first thing NIST’s OT guidance establishes.4
  2. Segment ruthlessly. Translate the Purdue model into enforced reality: zones connected by tightly-controlled conduits, an industrial DMZ as the only crossing between IT and OT, and no flat networks where a single foothold reaches everything. Strong segmentation is what turns a breach into a contained incident instead of a control-system catastrophe.394
  3. Watch the inside, not just the wall. Perimeter defense fails against living-off-the-land and protocol-level attacks that look legitimate — recall that FrostyGoop sailed past antivirus by speaking ordinary Modbus. The answer is OT-native monitoring that understands industrial protocols and flags anomalous behavior, plus the internal network monitoring now mandated for the bulk power system, so that lateral movement inside the trusted zone is finally visible.833
  4. Lock the doors people actually use. The dominant entry points are remote access and identity. Colonial fell through one dormant VPN account with no multi-factor authentication; Volt Typhoon lives by abusing valid credentials and poorly-secured edge devices.2410 Enforce MFA, broker all remote and vendor access through monitored jump hosts, retire unused accounts, and instrument authentication so that legitimate-looking misuse can be caught.
  5. Patch what you can; compensate for what you can’t. Many OT devices cannot be patched promptly because of availability requirements, and some never receive fixes at all — a reality the grid’s overseers explicitly acknowledge.76 When patching is impossible, the discipline shifts to compensating controls: network-level mitigations, tighter segmentation around the vulnerable device, and structured exploitability analysis to focus effort where a flaw is actually reachable.40
  6. Plan to lose, and plan to recover. Resilience assumes prevention will sometimes fail. Incident-response plans must be built with the engineers who run the process, not imposed by IT; keep offline, tested backups of engineering files and controller logic; and use application allowlisting so only authorized code runs on field devices.8 Critically, preserve the ability to fall back to manual operation — the Ukrainian operators recovered in 2015 precisely because they could still drive the substations by hand.18
  7. Treat safety systems as crown jewels. The safety-instrumented systems that trip a process to a safe state are the last automated barrier against physical disaster, which is exactly why Triton went after them. They warrant the strictest isolation, change control, and monitoring of anything on the plant floor.19

Above all, OT defense is moving toward consequence-based thinking — an approach NIST now recommends explicitly. Rather than trying to harden everything to the same level, identify the handful of functions whose compromise would be catastrophic, and concentrate defenses there.4 This reframing — from “reduce all vulnerabilities” to “make the unacceptable outcomes impossible” — is the bridge to the most important shift in the field, which we reach at the end of this briefing.

08 / 10  No one defends alone

Coordination, intelligence sharing, and the federal apparatus

The American grid is overwhelmingly owned and operated by private companies — thousands of utilities, cooperatives, and merchant operators — which makes national-scale defense impossible for any single actor. It is, by necessity, a public-private enterprise, and over the past decade the United States has built real machinery to make it work.

At the federal center sits the Department of Energy’s Office of Cybersecurity, Energy Security, and Emergency Response (CESER), which carries DOE’s statutory role as the Sector Risk Management Agency for energy and serves as the day-to-day federal interface to the sector for preparedness, response, and recovery.3 Its signature is not regulation but intelligence flow. Through the Electricity Information Sharing and Analysis Center (E-ISAC) and the Cybersecurity Risk Information Sharing Program (CRISP), utilities deploy sensors on their networks that feed traffic into a shared analysis pipeline — combining open-source intelligence with classified government reporting at a national laboratory — so that an indicator seen at one utility can warn them all.41 CRISP has been managed by the E-ISAC since 2014 and now reaches roughly three-quarters of U.S. electricity customers, with active work to extend the same visibility from IT into the operational-technology environment where the real danger lives.42

Around that core is a wider lattice of coordination. Industry-led Sector Coordinating Councils — the Electricity Subsector Coordinating Council and the Oil & Natural Gas Subsector Coordinating Council — align executives with government, while CISA’s industrial-control-systems response capability provides the federal hands-on team for ICS incidents and advisories.43 The sector rehearses for crisis through large-scale exercises — NERC’s biennial GridEx and DOE’s response drills — and leans on the national laboratories to test the security of the actual components and configurations that operators run.44 Overarching it all, the 2024 National Security Memorandum names the director of CISA as the National Coordinator for the security and resilience of critical infrastructure,28 and a 2021 Executive Order raised the federal cybersecurity floor that much of this activity now builds on.37

An indicator of compromise seen at one utility, shared fast enough, becomes a warning that protects the entire sector. Isolation is the adversary’s advantage; coordination is the defender’s.

— The operating premise of E-ISAC / CRISP

09 / 10  The hard problems

What remains unsolved — and where the research has to go

For all the progress in standards, intelligence sharing, and tooling, the energy sector is defending against an accelerating threat with infrastructure that was, in large part, never designed to be defended. The open problems below are not failures of effort; they are structural, and several will define the field for a generation.

An installed base that is insecure by design — and immortal

The deepest problem is the equipment itself. Much of the grid’s control hardware was built before network security was a concept, runs protocols with no authentication, and was installed to last decades; replacing it wholesale is neither affordable nor operationally feasible.7 Worse, the usual remedy — patching — collides with availability requirements: many devices cannot be taken offline on a vendor’s schedule, and some never receive fixes at all, leaving defenders to manage risk through compensating controls rather than remediation.640 Security cannot simply be bolted onto this base; it has to be engineered around it.

Convergence and the expanding edge

Meanwhile the attack surface grows faster than the defenses mature. IT/OT convergence and the migration of analytics to the cloud keep eroding the boundary that segmentation depends on,8 and the distribution edge is multiplying into millions of small, networked, remotely-updatable devices — rooftop solar inverters, battery systems, EV chargers, smart meters — that are unevenly secured and governed mostly at the state level, where mandatory cybersecurity is thin.6 The uncomfortable pattern is that the attack surface is expanding fastest precisely where governance and visibility are weakest.9

Supply chains, firmware, and measurement

The threat has also moved upstream into the supply chain: components, firmware, and vendor software are themselves vectors, and the most capable ICS toolkits are built to manipulate specific makes and models of controller directly.25 National-laboratory programs now test the security of the actual devices operators deploy, but coverage of a vast, heterogeneous installed base is necessarily partial.3 Compounding everything is a measurement gap that has dogged the sector for years: a longstanding difficulty in defining metrics that show whether security investments actually improve security — and it is hard to manage, or fund, what you cannot measure.45

Artificial intelligence, cutting both ways

AI has arrived as a genuine double-edged sword. On the offense, it can lower the barrier to entry — explaining an unfamiliar control environment to an attacker, helping identify which assets are most valuable or most likely to cause critical damage, and automating parts of the intrusion process.46 On the defense, the same capabilities power anomaly detection and faster triage, and the government has begun standing up AI testbeds aimed specifically at the energy sector’s security.3 Which side AI favors in OT is an open empirical question and an active research front.

The paradigm shift: engineering the risk out

The most important idea now reshaping the field reframes the entire problem. Instead of treating cybersecurity as a layer added after a system is built, Cyber-Informed Engineering (CIE) — advanced through a 2022 national strategy led by DOE and the national laboratories — builds security considerations into the engineering design lifecycle from the earliest possible stage, the cheapest and most effective moment to do so. Its premise is that engineers can “engineer out” cyber risk at design time rather than chasing it forever in operations.47 Its sharpest expression is Consequence-driven Cyber-informed Engineering (CCE), developed at Idaho National Laboratory: identify the small number of functions whose sabotage would be truly catastrophic, then deliberately reduce or eliminate the digital pathways an adversary could use to reach them — sometimes by re-engineering a critical step to depend less on automation at all.48 The goal is not to make attacks harder but to make the worst outcomes unreachable by cyber means.

This reframing carries a hidden dividend for the most stubborn problem of all — people. By extending ordinary engineering practice to include cyber risk, CIE widens the pool of defenders to every engineer who designs and operates infrastructure, letting them make sound security decisions even without deep cybersecurity expertise, and helping address a chronic workforce shortage that no amount of hiring has closed.49 Combined with the internal-monitoring mandates now reaching the bulk power system,33 it points toward the field’s emerging consensus: assume breach, engineer for consequence, and build a grid that bends without breaking.

10 / 10  A system that can’t stop

Defending infrastructure that can never be turned off

The energy sector occupies a singular position in cybersecurity because its failures do not stay digital and do not stay local. A breach here can become a blackout, a fuel shortage, a chemical release — and because every other lifeline runs on power and fuel, the damage propagates outward and, often, loops back. That is the thread running from Stuxnet’s shattered centrifuges to Volt Typhoon’s silent footholds in American utilities: in this domain, code becomes kinetic, and kinetic becomes contagious.

The defensive record is genuinely better than it was a decade ago. The bulk power system now lives under mandatory, evolving controls; pipelines that were governed by suggestion are governed by rule; a real machinery of intelligence sharing turns one utility’s misfortune into the whole sector’s early warning; and OT-native monitoring is beginning to make the inside of the network visible. But the adversary has improved too — growing more patient, more capable, and, in the case of pre-positioned state actors, possibly already inside — while the terrain keeps shifting beneath everyone’s feet as the grid digitizes, decentralizes, and sprawls toward an edge of millions of small, networked devices.

A control system that can never be switched off can never be made perfectly secure, and the field is maturing past the fantasy that it could be. The center of gravity is moving from preventing every intrusion to engineering away the unacceptable outcomes — designing systems, through Cyber-Informed Engineering and its consequence-driven sharpening, so that the catastrophic failures simply cannot be reached by an attacker’s keyboard.47 The honest goal is not an impregnable grid but a resilient one: a system that assumes it will be attacked, detects it fast, contains it, recovers, and is built so that even a successful breach cannot tip into disaster. Treating that resilience as a matter of national security — and prioritizing the operators whose failure would cascade the furthest — is now explicit policy.28

The lights staying on is not a default state of the world. It is the continuous, contested output of people defending machines that were never built to be defended — against adversaries who understand exactly how much depends on the dark.
