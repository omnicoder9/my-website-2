Halt, or
Run Forever

In 1936 a twenty-four-year-old Alan Turing proved that no single procedure can decide, for every program and input, whether the computation will ever stop. It was a mechanical limit to reason — and, folded into the very same proof, the blueprint of the computer.

There is a kind of question that no machine, however fast, however vast, however cleverly built, can ever answer. Not because the question is vague, or ill-formed, or metaphysical, but because answering it in general is provably beyond the reach of any mechanical procedure at all. The plainest example is also the most unsettling: given an arbitrary computer program and an arbitrary input, will the program eventually finish, or will it run forever? This is the halting problem, and Alan Turing proved in 1936 that it admits no general solution.1

The result arrived inside a paper with a forbidding title — “On Computable Numbers, with an Application to the Entscheidungsproblem” — read to the London Mathematical Society in November 1936 and printed, with a short correction the following year, in its Proceedings.12 On its surface the paper is about real numbers and an obscure problem in mathematical logic. Beneath the surface it does three revolutionary things at once. It gives the first precise, durable definition of what it means to compute. It exhibits a single universal machine able to imitate any other. And it proves that there are perfectly definite questions about such machines that no machine can settle. The first two ideas gave us the computer. The third drew a permanent boundary around what the computer could ever do.

To feel why the third idea landed as a wound and not merely a curiosity, you have to know what it interrupted. For three decades the most ambitious mathematicians in Europe had been chasing a dream in which mathematics could be made complete, consistent, and — crucially — decidable: a discipline in which every well-posed question had, at least in principle, a mechanical answer. Turing’s proof, arriving on the heels of Kurt Gödel’s, did not merely fail to deliver that dream. It showed the dream had been impossible all along.

Part I

Hilbert’s Dream

The aspiration was old. Gottfried Leibniz had imagined, in the seventeenth century, a characteristica universalis and a calculus ratiocinator — a universal symbolic language together with a calculus of reasoning, so that two philosophers in dispute might set down their pens and say, “Let us calculate.” Disagreement would dissolve into arithmetic.3 The dream slept for two hundred years and woke in the hands of David Hilbert, the towering Göttingen mathematician who, more than anyone, set the agenda for twentieth-century mathematics.

At the International Congress of Mathematicians in Paris in 1900, Hilbert laid out twenty-three problems to steer the coming century, and behind them a conviction stated with almost religious force: in mathematics there is no ignorabimus — no “we shall not know.” Every definite mathematical problem, he insisted, must be susceptible of an exact settlement.4 The second of those problems asked for a proof that the axioms of arithmetic are consistent — that they can never lead to a contradiction. It was the seed of what became Hilbert’s programme: to put all of mathematics on an unshakeable formal foundation and prove, by safe finitary means, that the edifice was sound.5

By the late 1920s the programme had crystallised into a small set of precise demands on any sufficiently powerful formal system. It should be consistent (no statement provable alongside its negation), complete (every truth expressible in the system also provable in it), and decidable. The last demand — decidability — was given a name in the 1928 textbook Hilbert wrote with Wilhelm Ackermann, Grundzüge der theoretischen Logik. They called it the Entscheidungsproblem, the decision problem, and described it as the central problem of mathematical logic: is there a definite procedure that, applied to any logical formula, decides in a finite number of steps whether the formula is universally valid?6 A positive answer would be the realisation of Leibniz’s fantasy. Feed in any mathematical conjecture; turn the crank; receive a verdict.

Hilbert never doubted the answer would be yes. In September 1930, at a meeting in Königsberg, he closed a famous address to the Society of German Scientists and Physicians with words later carved on his tombstone in Göttingen: Wir müssen wissen — wir werden wissen. We must know; we will know.7 He could not have chosen a worse moment for the boast.

We must know — we will know.David Hilbert, Königsberg, 8 September 1930

Part II

Gödel’s Wound

One day earlier, at a roundtable in the same city, a reticent twenty-four-year-old logician named Kurt Gödel had quietly mentioned a result that would unmake half of Hilbert’s programme. Almost no one in the room grasped its significance; the young polymath John von Neumann was among the few who did, and he immediately understood that the ground had shifted.9 Gödel’s full paper appeared in 1931 under the title “On Formally Undecidable Propositions of Principia Mathematica and Related Systems.”8

Its content was devastating in its precision. Gödel showed how to encode statements about a formal system as numbers within that system — an arithmetisation of syntax — and used it to construct a sentence that, in effect, asserts its own unprovability. If the system is consistent, that sentence can be neither proved nor disproved within it, yet it is true. This is the first incompleteness theorem: any consistent formal system rich enough to express elementary arithmetic contains true statements it cannot prove. The second drove the blade deeper: no such system can prove its own consistency.8 Completeness was gone. The hope of a finitary consistency proof, in the form Hilbert wanted, was gone.

But one pillar of the programme still stood. Incompleteness said that not every truth is provable; it did not directly say that no mechanical procedure could decide validity. The Entscheidungsproblem remained formally open. Perhaps there was still a machine that, given any formula, would correctly announce whether it was a logical theorem — even if no single formal system could capture all arithmetical truth. To kill that last hope, someone first had to answer a question Hilbert had never properly asked: what, exactly, is a mechanical procedure?

Why the gap mattered

Gödel’s proof spoke about a particular formal system at a time. The Entscheidungsproblem asked about procedures in general. Answering it — in the negative — required a mathematically exact, all-encompassing definition of “procedure,” “algorithm,” or “effective method”: something that had always been used informally and never pinned down. Supplying that definition was Turing’s deepest contribution, and Gödel himself would later say so.

Part III

The Confluence of 1936

In Cambridge in 1935, Turing attended lectures by the topologist Max Newman on the foundations of mathematics. It was there he learned of Gödel’s theorem and of Hilbert’s still-unanswered decision problem — and of the deep obstacle blocking any attack on it: the absence of an “unassailably correct” definition of a definite method.10 Turing, a lone worker by temperament, set off after it alone. His answer was not a formula but an image: a person mechanically following rules, idealised down to the barest essentials.

He was not the only one closing in. In the United States, Alonzo Church of Princeton had built an alternative formalism, the λ-calculus, and in a paper presented to the American Mathematical Society in 1935 and published in 1936 — “An Unsolvable Problem of Elementary Number Theory” — he exhibited a problem about λ-expressions that no effective process could solve.11 A companion note, “A Note on the Entscheidungsproblem,” drew the conclusion that Hilbert’s decision problem itself had no solution.12 Church reached the destination a few months before Turing reached print.

The timing produced one of the more poignant episodes in the history of logic. Turing handed Newman a typed draft in April 1936. Shortly afterwards Newman received an offprint of Church’s work; the overlap was unmistakable. Turing inserted a reference to Church, added an appendix proving that his own notion of computability coincided exactly with Church’s λ-definability, and sent the paper to London, where it was received on 28 May 1936.1013 Two men, an ocean apart, working in utterly different idioms, had independently broken Hilbert’s last hope in the same season.

A third had done something similar. In New York, Emil Post published a short paper, “Finite Combinatory Processes — Formulation 1,” describing a worker moving through a sequence of boxes, marking and erasing according to a fixed list of directions — a model strikingly close to Turing’s, arrived at independently.14 That three such different routes — recursion and the λ-calculus, an abstract worker at a tape, a combinatory process — all converged on the same class of computable functions was itself powerful evidence. The claim that this shared class captures exactly what “effectively calculable” means came to be called the Church–Turing thesis. It is not a theorem — it links an informal idea to a formal one and so cannot be proved — but the confluence of 1936 made it nearly irresistible.15

It was Church, reviewing Turing’s paper for the Journal of Symbolic Logic in 1937, who gave the abstract device its lasting name. He called it a Turing machine.16 Turing himself sailed to Princeton later that year, took his doctorate under Church in 1938, and in his thesis introduced the idea of an oracle — a hypothetical black box that could answer questions no ordinary machine could — a notion that would seed the modern theory of relative computability.17

Part IV

The Machine

Turing’s definition of computation is disarmingly concrete. Strip a human calculator of everything inessential — the desk, the coffee, the intuition — and what remains is someone reading and writing symbols on paper according to fixed rules, in a state of mind that depends only on the symbol being looked at and the rule currently in force. Idealise the paper into an infinite one-dimensional tape divided into squares. Allow only finitely many distinct symbols and finitely many distinct “states of mind.” At each step the device reads the symbol under its head, and — according to a finite table indexed by symbol and state — writes a symbol, moves one square left or right, and switches state.1 That is the whole apparatus. Turing called it an automatic machine, or a-machine.

Its austerity is the point. A finite table of instructions, a finite alphabet, a finite set of states, and an unbounded tape are enough to carry out any computation a human clerk could perform by rote. The claim sounds extravagant until you notice that it has never been refuted: every later notion of computation — recursive functions, register machines, every programming language ever designed — has turned out to compute neither more nor less than a Turing machine can.18

The paper’s nominal subject was computable numbers: real numbers whose decimal (or binary) expansion can be churned out, digit after digit, by such a machine. Most familiar numbers qualify — the rationals, the square root of two, π, e — each the output of some finite table.1 But here Turing planted the seed of everything that followed with a simple counting argument. Each machine is specified by a finite table, which can be written as a finite string of symbols, which can be read as a number — its description number. So the machines can be listed: machine 1, machine 2, machine 3, and so on. The computable numbers, being outputs of listed machines, form a countable set. Yet Georg Cantor had shown, decades earlier, that the real numbers are uncountable. The conclusion is immediate and strange: almost every real number is uncomputable. There are vastly more numbers than there are machines to produce them.

The universal machine

Before he drew any limits, Turing built the most consequential positive object in the paper. Since every machine has a description number, and since a machine is just a symbol-manipulator, he constructed one particular machine — the universal machine — that takes as input the description number of any other machine, together with that machine’s input, and faithfully simulates it, step for step.1 One device to run them all. A single piece of hardware whose behaviour is fixed not by rewiring but by the program written on its tape.

This is the abstract ancestor of every computer you have ever used: the insight that program and data are the same kind of thing, both just symbols on a tape, and that one fixed machine can execute any of them. The stored-program computer is the universal Turing machine made of silicon. We will return, at the end, to the irony that this gift and the deepest limit on computing were delivered in the same breath.

Part V

The Diagonal

The weapon Turing turned on the decision problem was forged by Cantor in 1891. To prove the reals uncountable, Cantor had supposed they could be listed and derived an absurdity: take the supposed list, walk down its diagonal, and change every digit you meet. The number you build differs from the first listed number in its first digit, from the second in its second digit, from the n-th in its n-th digit — so it differs from every number on the list, yet by construction it is a perfectly good real number that should have been on it. The list cannot exist.19 This diagonal argument is the engine beneath Gödel’s self-referential sentence, beneath Russell’s paradox of the set of all sets that do not contain themselves, and beneath the halting problem.

Now make the argument bite. Suppose, for contradiction, that the halting problem could be solved — that there exists an algorithm, call it H, which takes the description of any program P and any input x, always finishes, and correctly reports whether P halts on x or runs forever.20 From H we can build a small, perverse program — the contrarian — that uses H to predict its own behaviour and then deliberately does the opposite:

Run D on its own description and the trap springs shut. If D(⟨D⟩) halts, then by its own code the oracle must have answered loop — so H was wrong. If D(⟨D⟩) runs forever, then the oracle must have answered halt — so H was wrong again. Either outcome contradicts the assumption that H is always correct. The only escapable premise is the existence of H itself. No such algorithm can exist.21 This is the proof in its modern, textbook form — the version distilled by later writers from Turing’s original; clean, brief, and airtight.22

A machine that could foresee every ending could be built to defy its own forecast. The contradiction is not in the machine — it is in the foreseeing.

Part VI

What Turing Proved

There is a quiet irony at the centre of this story: the words “halting problem” appear nowhere in Turing’s paper, and the machines he actually built were designed never to halt. Turing was studying computable numbers — real numbers, such as π or √2, whose decimal expansions a machine can churn out digit after digit without end. A machine computing such a number runs forever by design, printing an endless stream of figures. The pathological case for Turing was the opposite of halting: a machine that he called circular, one that gets stuck and prints only finitely many digits, falling silent before it has delivered a complete real number.1 His central undecidable question was whether a given machine is circle-free — whether it will go on producing digits forever — and this is, in a precise sense, the mirror image of asking whether a machine halts.

The now-canonical framing in terms of halting came later, and from other hands. Stephen Kleene’s Introduction to Metamathematics of 1952 presented the self-referential, diagonal argument in essentially the form every textbook now repeats.20 The name itself was coined by Martin Davis: it appears in print in his 1958 monograph Computability and Unsolvability, and Davis recalled having used the phrase in his lectures at the University of Illinois from about 1952.2123 What we call “the halting problem” is therefore a reformulation — faithful in spirit, cleaner in form — of the result Turing proved about circular machines.

Did Turing prove it?

Some historians of logic press the point further. The circle-free problem Turing actually settled is not merely a relabelling of the halting problem; it sits higher in the hierarchy of unsolvability. Deciding whether a machine ever halts is equivalent to answering a single unbounded question (“does it stop at some step?”); deciding whether a machine is circle-free requires answering “does it print infinitely many digits?”, which quantifies over all future stages at once. Joel David Hamkins and Theodor Nenu have argued that the circle-free problem is, in this technical sense, strictly harder than the halting problem — so that crediting Turing with “the halting problem” both overstates and understates what he did.24 The undecidability of halting follows from his methods with ease; but the precise theorem on his page is a different, and in some ways deeper, one.

None of this diminishes Turing. The machinery he invented — the formalisation of an algorithm, the diagonal construction, the self-reference that turns a decision procedure against itself — is exactly what makes the halting problem fall, and what makes a whole family of problems fall with it. The result bears his name not because he wrote the words, but because he supplied the weapon. The reattributions are a historian’s honesty about provenance, not a revision of who reshaped the landscape.

Part VII

The Decision Problem Falls

Recall what Hilbert and Ackermann had asked in 1928. The Entscheidungsproblem — the “decision problem” — sought a single mechanical procedure that, given any formula of first-order logic, would decide whether that formula is universally valid: true under every possible interpretation, provable from the laws of logic alone.6 A positive answer would have been the keystone of Hilbert’s programme. Feed in any precise mathematical conjecture, suitably formalised, and the procedure would grind out yes or no. Mathematics would become, in the last analysis, the turning of a crank.

Turing’s machines let him close this question by a chain of translation. He showed how to encode the complete operation of any Turing machine into a single formula of first-order logic, built so that the formula is provable precisely when the machine does some definite thing — reaches a certain configuration. The encoding is faithful in both directions: the logic says yes exactly when the machine behaves as specified. Now suppose the Entscheidungsproblem had a solution — a decision procedure for validity. Then, to learn whether any machine ever reaches its target configuration, one would simply build the corresponding formula and ask the procedure whether it is valid. But the behaviour of machines is not mechanically decidable; that is exactly what the diagonal argument established. A decision procedure for logic would hand us a decision procedure for the machine, and no such thing can exist. Therefore the Entscheidungsproblem has no solution. First-order validity is undecidable.1

Hilbert had asked for a machine to decide all of mathematics. Turing answered by building the machine — and then proving that this very machine could not decide even the behaviour of machines.

It is worth pausing on the strangeness of the method. To prove that no mechanism could decide logical truth, Turing first had to say with complete precision what a mechanism is. The negative result and the definition of computation are not two achievements but one: the boundary could only be drawn once the territory had been mapped. This is why the paper that killed the decision problem is the same paper that gave us the machine.

And Turing was not alone at the finish. Alonzo Church, working at Princeton with the entirely different apparatus of the λ-calculus, had reached the same destination a few months earlier, publishing his own demonstration that the Entscheidungsproblem admits no solution.12 Church’s route ran through the existence of a specific unsolvable problem in pure number theory;11 Turing’s ran through an idealised machine. That two such different paths arrived at one conclusion in one year was not coincidence but confirmation: the limit they had found was a feature of the mathematical world, not an artefact of either formalism. When Turing proved his notion of computability equivalent to Church’s, the case was sealed.13

Part VIII

The Upset

To understand why this landed as a wound, set it beside the dream it ended. Hilbert’s programme had rested on three hopes for mathematics: that it could be made consistent (free of contradiction), complete (every truth provable), and decidable (every question mechanically settleable). By 1936 all three lay in ruins. Gödel’s first incompleteness theorem had shown that any sufficiently rich, consistent system contains true statements it cannot prove; his second had shown that such a system cannot even prove its own consistency. Now Turing — and Church beside him — had taken the third pillar. There was no decision procedure, and there never could be. The programme was not merely unfinished. It was impossible, and provably so, by its own exacting standards.

What made Turing’s contribution peculiarly disquieting was its mechanical character. Gödel’s incompleteness could be read, by the consolation-seeking, as a statement about the limits of particular axiom systems — perhaps a cleverer system, a richer set of axioms, might do better. Turing slammed that door. His proof was not about any one system of axioms but about any conceivable mechanical procedure whatsoever. The limit was not a failure of ingenuity that a better method might repair; it was a wall built into the logical structure of computation itself. No future machine, no matter how fast or how vast, could climb over it. For the first time, the boundaries of the knowable had been drawn not by human frailty but by mathematics, against the idealised, tireless, infinitely patient machine — and the machine had been found wanting.

It is one thing to say that we cannot answer a question. It is another to prove that no machine, however perfect, ever could. Turing proved the second kind.

Hilbert had declared, in a celebrated 1930 address broadcast over Königsberg radio, that there is no ignorabimus in mathematics — no question we are doomed never to answer — closing with the ringing words later cut into his gravestone: Wir müssen wissen, wir werden wissen. “We must know, we will know.”7 The cruel timing is hard to miss: Gödel had presented incompleteness at a conference in that same city, on the previous day.5 Within six years, Turing had supplied the formal proof that there are indeed definite mathematical questions — about the behaviour of simple machines — that no procedure will ever decide. The ignorabimus Hilbert had banished returned in a sharper, stranger form: not questions beyond human reach, but questions beyond mechanical reach, demonstrated with the very rigour Hilbert had demanded.

And yet the world did not gasp. Turing’s paper, on first appearance, was met with something closer to silence. The work was abstruse, its author young and unknown, and its setting — the analysis of imaginary paper-tape machines — looked eccentric. Andrew Hodges records that the paper drew only a meagre handful of reprint requests; among the very few who wrote for an offprint were the Cambridge philosopher R. B. Braithwaite and, from Münster in Germany, the logician Heinrich Scholz, who is said to have prized it deeply.10 For a result that had just dismantled one of the great mathematical programmes of the age, the immediate reception was muted to the point of indifference.

Recognition came, and from the most authoritative possible quarter. Kurt Gödel, who had been unpersuaded that Church’s λ-definability truly captured the informal idea of computation, found Turing’s machine analysis decisive in a way the rival formalisms were not.26 In a postscript he added in 1964 to his 1934 Princeton lectures, Gödel wrote that Turing’s work gives “a precise and unquestionably adequate definition of the general concept of formal system.”25 It is a remarkable endorsement: the man who had proved incompleteness crediting Turing with finally pinning down what a formal system is — and thereby giving Gödel’s own theorems their fullest generality. The machine that could not decide everything had, at least, made it exact what “everything” meant.

Part IX

Minds and Machines

If no machine can decide every question, and the human mind plainly answers questions, does it follow that the mind is not a machine? The temptation to draw that inference is old and powerful, and it has produced one of the most durable controversies in the philosophy of computation. The argument runs roughly thus: for any consistent formal system — any machine — there is a true statement (its Gödel sentence) that the system cannot prove, yet which we, looking on from outside, can recognise as true. If we can always out-see the machine, then we are not that machine, nor any machine at all.

The philosopher J. R. Lucas gave this argument its sharpest early formulation in a 1961 essay, contending that Gödel’s theorem proves mechanism false as an account of mind.27 A generation later the mathematician Roger Penrose revived and enlarged it, first in The Emperor’s New Mind and then in Shadows of the Mind, arguing that human mathematical understanding cannot be the running of any algorithm, and reaching toward new physics — an as-yet-unknown quantum process in the brain — to explain an insight he held to be non-computational.2829 The halting problem furnishes a parallel version of the same intuition: a person can sometimes simply see that a particular program loops forever, where the general procedure to decide such things cannot exist.

Turing had foreseen the objection and did not accept it. In his 1950 essay “Computing Machinery and Intelligence” — the paper that introduced what we now call the Turing test — he listed the appeal to Gödel’s theorem among the arguments against machine intelligence and gave it short shrift, under the heading of the “Mathematical Objection.”30 His reply, in essence: the argument assumes that the human intellect has no such limitations of its own, and there is no warrant for that assumption. People, too, give wrong answers, fall into contradiction, and fail to settle questions; to hold the machine to a standard of infallibility we do not meet ourselves is to stack the deck. The fact that this machine cannot prove its Gödel sentence shows only that we are not that particular machine — not that we are no machine at all.

Gödel’s own view was subtler

It is often assumed that Gödel, of all people, must have held that his theorems refute mechanism. He did not — at least not in the blunt form Lucas and Penrose later gave the argument. Gödel’s considered position was a careful disjunction: either the human mind surpasses every finite machine, or there exist absolutely unsolvable mathematical problems — he did not claim to have proved which horn holds. Separately, in a note now published as “A philosophical error in Turing’s work,” Gödel objected to one specific step in Turing’s 1936 analysis: Turing’s assumption that a calculating mind passes through only finitely many distinguishable states. Gödel thought the mind might develop without bound, and called this a philosophical, not mathematical, error.31 The disagreement was real, but narrow — and it was not an endorsement of the sweeping anti-mechanist conclusion.32

The Lucas–Penrose argument has been heavily contested, and the standard rebuttal turns on a hidden premise. To “see” that a machine’s Gödel sentence is true, one must first know that the machine is consistent — for an inconsistent system proves everything, Gödel sentence included. But knowing the consistency of a complex formal system is itself extraordinarily hard, and is exactly the kind of thing Gödel’s second theorem says the system cannot establish about itself. Critics such as Solomon Feferman have argued that the anti-mechanist gets his result only by quietly granting the human mind a power — reliable insight into consistency — that he denies the machine, and that once this asymmetry is removed the argument no longer goes through.33 The debate is not settled, and perhaps cannot be settled by logic alone; but the simple version — the theorem proves we are not machines — is widely regarded as proving no such thing.

What is not in dispute is the shape of the question. Turing’s proof did not tell us whether minds are machines. It told us, with finality, what machines cannot do — and so gave the old question of mind and mechanism a precise edge it had never had before. Every serious modern argument about the reach of artificial intelligence still runs along the boundary Turing drew.

Part X

The Reach of It

The halting problem would be a curiosity if it stood alone. Its true significance is that it does not: it is the seed from which a vast forest of impossibility grows. The instrument that propagates it is the reduction. Suppose you want to know whether some other question — call it Q — is decidable. If you can show that a decision procedure for Q could be used to build a decision procedure for halting, then Q must be undecidable too, for otherwise halting would be decidable, which it is not. The unsolvability of halting becomes a lever: prove that solving your problem would solve halting, and your problem is dead.22 Halting is the canonical undecidable problem, the fixed point against which countless others are measured.

How far does this reach? Astonishingly far. In 1953 Henry Gordon Rice proved a theorem of breathtaking generality: every non-trivial property of the behaviour of a program is undecidable.34 By “property of behaviour” Rice meant any property of the function a program computes — what outputs it gives on what inputs — rather than of the program’s text. “Non-trivial” means only that the property holds for some programs and fails for others. Under those mild conditions there is no general algorithm to decide it. Whether a program ever outputs zero; whether two programs compute the same function; whether a program computes the identity; whether it ever enters a given subroutine — all of these, and endlessly many more, inherit the undecidability of halting.

Rice’s theorem is the halting problem written across the whole sky: not one question machines cannot answer, but every interesting question about what a program does.

This is not an abstraction confined to logic seminars. It sets a permanent ceiling over the engineering of software. There can be no perfect, fully general bug-finder — a program that reads any program and infallibly reports whether it crashes, leaks memory, or loops. There can be no perfect malware detector that decides, for every file, whether its behaviour is hostile. There can be no perfect optimiser that decides whether two arbitrary routines are equivalent and freely swaps one for the other. Each of these tasks, posed in full generality, encodes the halting problem, and so each is impossible in principle. The practical tools we do build — type checkers, static analysers, model checkers, verifiers — succeed precisely by retreating from full generality: they restrict the programs they consider, accept answers of “don’t know,” or err deliberately on the safe side. They live, productively, inside the boundary Turing drew, never crossing it.

The theory that grew from Turing’s seed — the study of which problems are solvable, and of the fine gradations of unsolvability among those that are not — became a mature mathematical discipline in its own right, mapping a rich hierarchy of degrees of difficulty far above the merely undecidable.35 The halting problem sits near the foot of that hierarchy, the first rung on a ladder that climbs without end. What began as a question about paper-tape machines turned out to chart the deep structure of the computable universe.

Part XI

The Other Side

There is a redemption folded into this story, and it is the strangest part of all. The 1936 paper that drew the permanent boundary around computation also contained the blueprint for the machine that would remake the century. The two achievements are not neighbours; they are the same idea seen from opposite sides.

To prove his impossibility result, Turing needed a machine that could imitate any other. He constructed exactly that: a universal machine, a single fixed device which, given on its tape a description of any other Turing machine together with that machine’s input, proceeds to simulate it faithfully, step for step.1 One machine to be all machines. The description of a computation and the data of a computation sit together on the same tape, in the same kind of symbols, read by the same head. This is the seed of the most consequential idea in the history of technology: that a program is just another kind of data, and that a single general-purpose machine, by reading different descriptions, can become a word processor, a telescope’s controller, a chess opponent, or a calculator of π — without any change to its hardware at all.

Every general-purpose computer is a physical approximation of Turing’s universal machine. The principle that program and data share one memory — the insight at the heart of the stored-program architecture that took shape in the 1940s — is the engineering incarnation of the universal machine of 1936. Martin Davis traces precisely this road, from Leibniz’s dream of a calculus of reasoning, through Boole and Frege and Hilbert and Gödel, to the moment Turing’s abstract construction became the logical foundation of the device now on every desk and in every pocket.3 The machine you are reading this on is, in its deepest logical structure, Turing’s machine made of silicon.18

In one stroke of imagination, Turing invented the computer and proved what the computer could never do. The boundary and the engine were born together — two readings of a single line of thought.

And the mechanism that links them is the same in both directions: self-reference, a machine that reads descriptions of machines. Feed the universal machine a description and it becomes that machine — the foundation of all programming. Feed a hypothetical deciding machine a description of itself, suitably twisted, and it contradicts itself — the foundation of the limit. The power and the impossibility flow from one source. The same capacity that lets a machine be universal is what forbids it from being omniscient. There is something almost just about that arrangement: the price of a machine that can do anything is that there are things no machine can do.

Hilbert wanted to dissolve mathematics into a procedure and stood at Königsberg insisting that we shall know. Turing, answering, proved that there are questions we shall never mechanically settle — and, in the very same motion, handed us the universal machine with which to ask an endless supply of new ones. The dream of total decidability died in 1936. What was born in its place — a precise science of the computable, and the engine of the computer age — turned out to be larger, stranger, and more fertile than the dream had ever been.
