01 · Introduction

The two-minute warning

On the afternoon of February 5, 2021, an operator at the Bruce T. Haddock Water Treatment Plant in Oldsmar, Florida, watched his mouse cursor drift across the screen on its own. It opened the software that governs chemical dosing and pushed the setpoint for sodium hydroxide — lye — from 100 parts per million to 11,100, a hundredfold jump into dangerous territory, before the operator dragged it back and alerted his supervisor.1 The episode became the defining parable of water-sector cyber risk, even though federal investigators were ultimately unable to confirm that an outside intruder was responsible — the former city manager later argued the "non-event" may have stemmed from operator error.2

Whether hacker or human error, Oldsmar exposed an uncomfortable truth: the machinery that makes water safe to drink can be steered by a hand on a keyboard, and the margin between a near-miss and a public-health crisis can be a single attentive person. The United States runs roughly 150,000 public drinking-water and wastewater systems — on the order of 52,000 community water systems and 16,000 wastewater utilities — and they are increasingly automated, increasingly networked, and unevenly defended.34

The scale of exposure is not hypothetical. A November 2024 audit by the Environmental Protection Agency's Office of Inspector General passively scanned 1,062 drinking-water systems serving more than 193 million people. It flagged 97 systems — serving about 26.6 million users — with critical or high-risk cybersecurity vulnerabilities, and a further 211 systems, serving 82.7 million people, with lower-risk weaknesses such as externally visible open portals. In all, 308 systems serving roughly 109 million people carried exploitable flaws, and the auditors noted the EPA had no centralized way to even receive incident reports.5

This report maps that terrain in four movements: the industry-specific technology and the consequences when it fails; the ways a water-sector compromise ripples into the other fifteen critical-infrastructure sectors; the frameworks, standards, and tools assembled to push back; and the structural problems — legal, financial, and technical — that remain unsolved.

02 · The technology

Anatomy of a utility

A water utility is two worlds bolted together. There is the information technology (IT) world that most organizations share — billing platforms, email, customer portals, business networks — and there is the operational technology (OT) world that opens valves, runs pumps, and meters chemicals into the flow. It is the OT layer that makes water security a physical-safety problem rather than merely a data-privacy one.

At the heart of the OT layer sits the industrial control system (ICS). A supervisory control and data acquisition (SCADA) system gathers readings from across a sprawling, geographically dispersed plant and presents them to operators. Programmable logic controllers (PLCs) and remote terminal units execute the actual control logic at pumps, valves, and dosing stations. Human-machine interfaces (HMIs) — the screens an operator watches — translate all of this into dials, trends, and setpoints. The cursor that misbehaved at Oldsmar was an HMI; the device hijacked in Pennsylvania two years later was a PLC.

Engineers reason about this stack through the Purdue model, a hierarchical reference architecture that segments a plant into levels: physical sensors and actuators at Levels 0–1, supervisory SCADA and HMI systems at Level 2, operations management at Level 3, a demilitarized zone at Level 3.5, and the enterprise IT network above.6 The companion discipline is the ISA/IEC 62443 family of standards, which frames defense in terms of zones and conduits — trusted segments and the controlled channels between them.7

In theory, the DMZ at Level 3.5 keeps the business network from touching the control network. In practice, the boundary erodes. Remote-access software, internet-connected HMIs, cloud telemetry, and inexpensive internet-of-things sensors have blurred the lines the Purdue model was drawn to keep crisp — which is precisely why modern guidance leans on 62443's zone-and-conduit thinking rather than a literal air gap.7

03 · The adversary

The threat landscape

The water sector faces a spectrum of adversaries whose motives range from notoriety to extortion to wartime preparation. Three categories matter most.

Hacktivists and the insecure default

On November 25, 2023, the Municipal Water Authority of Aliquippa, Pennsylvania — a small utility serving roughly 6,600 customers — lost control of a booster station that regulates pressure for two neighboring townships. An alarm tripped immediately, operators switched to manual control, and a defacement message appeared on the screen: the equipment had been claimed as a "legal target."10 The culprit was CyberAv3ngers, a group linked to Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps, and the target was a Unitronics Vision-series PLC — an Israeli-made device exposed to the internet, reachable on its default port, and protected by a default password.11 No deep exploit was required; the attackers simply walked through an unlocked door, and victims spanned multiple U.S. states.

The same pattern recurs. In 2024, pro-Russia hacktivists manipulated internet-exposed HMIs at water and wastewater facilities, driving pumps and blower equipment beyond their normal operating parameters and forcing operators to fall back to manual control.8 CISA's standing advisories catalog the recurring weaknesses: outdated control-system firmware, improperly retained credentials from former employees, and devices that ship insecure by default.9

"Threat actors have demonstrated the capability to find and exploit internet-exposed HMIs with cybersecurity weaknesses easily."
— Joint CISA & EPA fact sheet on internet-exposed interfaces8

Ransomware and the resourcing trap

Criminal ransomware does not need a physical-safety endgame to do harm. CISA documented a July 2021 incident in which actors used remote access to plant ZuCaNo ransomware at a Maine wastewater facility, and an August 2021 case in which a California utility was hit with a Ghost variant.9 The largest such event came in October 2024, when American Water — the country's biggest regulated water utility, serving more than 14 million people across 14 states — detected unauthorized activity, took its MyWater customer portal offline, and paused billing.14 The company found no evidence that its water or wastewater facilities were affected; the damage was contained to the enterprise IT side, illustrating both the value of segmentation and the disruption a purely IT breach can still cause.15

Nation-states pre-positioning for conflict

The most strategically alarming threat is the quietest. Volt Typhoon, a People's Republic of China state-sponsored group, has compromised IT networks across communications, energy, transportation, and water and wastewater organizations — not to steal data, but to pre-position for disruptive or destructive attacks in the event of a major crisis or conflict.12 The group relies on "living off the land," using legitimate built-in tools to blend into normal activity, and U.S. agencies have observed it maintaining undiscovered access in some victim environments for at least five years.12 CISA and its partners describe this as a strategic shift from espionage toward the staging of attacks intended to jeopardize American safety and military readiness.13

The consequence ladder. These incidents trace a hierarchy of harm. At the top is physical manipulation — altering chemical dosing or pressure to threaten health, as the Oldsmar scenario imagined. Below that is denial of service — locking or bricking the controls that keep water flowing or wastewater contained. And at the base is data compromise — theft of customer or operational information, the kind of breach American Water disclosed. Each rung carries its own blast radius, and the lower rungs are far more common than the catastrophic top.

04 · Interdependency

Cascading failure

Water sits at a structural nexus of American infrastructure. Federal policy designates 16 critical-infrastructure sectors, and Water and Wastewater Systems is the one for which the EPA serves as the Sector Risk Management Agency.18 But the sector's importance is less about its own boundaries than about everything downstream of it. CISA notes that a disruption to water or wastewater service flows directly into firefighting and healthcare, and into interdependent sectors including Energy, Healthcare and Public Health, and Critical Manufacturing.17

The dependencies run both ways, which is what makes the coupling dangerous. Water treatment and distribution depend on the Energy sector for the electricity that runs pumps and SCADA systems; on the Chemical sector for treatment reagents; and on Communications for the telemetry that links remote sites. Energy, in turn, depends on water for cooling. SCADA systems control power generation and distribution using monitoring data from communications networks, while the energy sector provides the power those same SCADA systems need to operate — a tight loop in which a failure in one node can propagate outward as a cascading failure before responders can intervene.21

The economic stakes sharpen the picture. The U.S. Water Alliance has estimated that a single day of nationwide water-service disruption could jeopardize roughly $43.5 billion in economic activity.20 Federal strategy now explicitly directs sector risk-management agencies to address cross-sector and regional interdependencies, singling out threats from the PRC, and reaffirms the 16-sector structure under National Security Memorandum 22.19 For an adversary like Volt Typhoon, water is attractive precisely because it is a lifeline that other lifelines depend on — and because many of its operators are small organizations with limited cybersecurity capacity that nonetheless serve critical geographies.12

05 · Mitigation

The defensive toolkit

No single framework governs water-sector security; defenders assemble a layered stack from voluntary standards, federal guidance, and a handful of statutory obligations. The result is more toolbox than blueprint.

The statutory floor: AWIA

The foundational mandate is America's Water Infrastructure Act of 2018. Its Section 2013 amended Section 1433 of the Safe Drinking Water Act to require every community water system serving more than 3,300 people to conduct a Risk and Resilience Assessment (RRA) and to develop or update an Emergency Response Plan (ERP), certifying completion to the EPA.22 The RRA must explicitly address the cybersecurity of process-control and monitoring systems alongside physical and natural-hazard risks; the ERP must follow within six months and be revisited on a five-year cycle.23

The sector standards: AWWA

To operationalize AWIA, most utilities turn to the American Water Works Association. Its J100 standard provides a seven-step risk-and-resilience methodology built around threat–asset pairs; G430 sets baseline security-program requirements; and G440 covers emergency-preparedness practices.24 Crucially, AWWA also publishes free Cybersecurity Guidance and a Use-Case Tool that maps a utility's specific control-system configuration to a prioritized set of recommended controls, lowering the barrier for systems without in-house security staff.25

The operational playbook: WaterISAC and the frameworks

The Water Information Sharing and Analysis Center (WaterISAC) distills the field into 12 Cybersecurity Fundamentals for water and wastewater utilities — a streamlined successor to its earlier 15-point guidance — covering asset inventory, control-system exposure reduction, and incident-response planning, and designed to be actionable for systems of every size.26 Those fundamentals point utilities toward the broader risk-management frameworks: NIST CSF, NIST SP 800-82 for ICS, ISA/IEC 62443, the CIS Critical Controls, and the SANS Five ICS Cybersecurity Critical Controls, supplemented by CISA's Cross-Sector Cybersecurity Performance Goals.27

The federal nudges

On top of standards sit recurring federal interventions. CISA, the EPA, and the FBI jointly publish Top Cyber Actions for Securing Water Systems — a short list of high-impact moves such as reducing internet exposure, changing default passwords, and conducting cyber assessments — updated as the threat evolves.28 The EPA offers a free Water Cybersecurity Assessment Tool and technical assistance, and the agencies increasingly press the Secure by Design principle: shifting responsibility upstream so device manufacturers ship products that are not insecure by default.13

Practitioner's shortlist

Across every advisory, the same handful of measures recur because they defeat the most common attacks: inventory and remove internet-exposed OT; eliminate default and shared credentials; enforce multi-factor authentication on remote access; segment OT from IT with a hardened DMZ; patch known-exploited vulnerabilities; and rehearse a manual-operations fallback — the very move that contained Aliquippa.28

06 · Policy

The governance gap

The toolkit is rich; the obligation to use it is thin. Most of the frameworks above are voluntary, and the sector's defining policy story of the last several years is a failed attempt to make them mandatory.

In March 2023, the EPA issued an interpretive memorandum directing states to evaluate the cybersecurity of operational technology during the periodic "sanitary surveys" they already conduct on public water systems. Within months it was challenged in court by the states of Missouri, Arkansas, and Iowa, joined by the AWWA and the National Rural Water Association. The Eighth Circuit stayed the memorandum in July 2023, and the EPA formally withdrew it that October — while stressing that water-sector cybersecurity remained a top priority.29 The challengers argued the agency had imposed new requirements through interpretation rather than rulemaking, and that state drinking-water programs lacked the expertise and resources to perform cyber reviews.30

The withdrawal left the largest critical-infrastructure sector without binding federal cybersecurity requirements — and exposed how hard regulatory harmonization across the 16 sectors would be.
— On the 2023 sanitary-survey reversal30

The watchdogs have been blunt about the vacuum. The Government Accountability Office concluded in August 2024 that the EPA urgently needed a national cybersecurity strategy for the water sector, had not identified and prioritized the greatest sector-wide risks, and relied too heavily on voluntary participation.3 The EPA responded in January 2025 with a Water and Wastewater Systems Sector Risk Management Plan, but the central tension — whether the agency even possesses the statutory authority to compel security improvements — remains unresolved and under active review.18

Congress has floated structural fixes. The bipartisan Water Risk and Resilience Organization (WRRO) Establishment Act (H.R. 7922), introduced in April 2024, would have the EPA certify a single non-federal body — with combined cyber and water-system expertise — to develop and enforce sector requirements, with the EPA administrator retaining final approval.31 The model deliberately echoes the self-regulatory structure used for the electric grid, leaving the specific requirements to the expert body rather than to statute.32 A separate Cybersecurity for Rural Water Systems Act would expand funding and technical assistance for the smallest utilities.33

Money is trickling in alongside the legislation. The EPA has stood up a multimillion-dollar grant program for drinking-water cybersecurity, and roughly $50 billion in Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act funds is flowing to the sector through 2026 via state revolving funds — though that capital is aimed largely at aging infrastructure and emerging contaminants rather than cyber defense specifically.1634

07 · Open problems

The open frontier

Even a fully funded, fully mandated water sector would face hard, unsolved problems. Five stand out.

The small-utility resourcing crisis

The sector's defining structural fact is fragmentation. Tens of thousands of utilities are small, municipally run, and chronically short of both budget and personnel; as one analysis put it, they operate with limited budgets and even fewer cybersecurity specialists.16 A framework a 14-million-customer utility can implement is meaningless to a town system with one operator and no IT staff. Bridging that gap — through shared services, managed detection, or regional cooperatives — is as much an organizational problem as a technical one.

Insecure-by-default devices and monoculture risk

Many of the control devices in the field were never designed with security in mind, and because the sector relies on a small set of common vendor ecosystems, a single flaw can repeat across hundreds or thousands of facilities at once — the dynamic that let CyberAv3ngers compromise the same model of PLC across multiple states.35 Pushing security upstream into product design, rather than bolting it on afterward, is the structural answer, but the installed base will take years to turn over.

Visibility and incident reporting

Defenders cannot protect what they cannot see. Asset visibility into OT environments remains poor, and at the federal level the EPA's own inspector general found there was no centralized system for water utilities to report cyber incidents — leaving the sector's true exposure unknown and its responses uncoordinated.5

The quantum horizon and AI's double edge

Looking further out, expert testimony to Congress has warned that advances in quantum computing will eventually break the asymmetric cryptography underpinning today's systems, obliging the water sector to plan a transition to post-quantum, quantum-safe cryptography in line with emerging NIST standards.35 Artificial intelligence cuts both ways: it lowers the cost of crafting attacks while also enabling the anomaly detection and proactive threat-hunting that living-off-the-land adversaries like Volt Typhoon demand — an arms race the sector has barely begun.

The harmonization problem

Finally, the water sector's regulatory false start is a microcosm of a national challenge: aligning cyber requirements across sixteen sectors with different regulators, cultures, and capacities, without crushing the smallest operators. The GAO's call for a coherent national strategy, the WRRO's self-regulatory experiment, and the EPA's contested authority are all attempts to answer the same question — who, exactly, is in charge of keeping the water safe from the network.3

08 · Conclusion

Conclusion

The water sector occupies an unforgiving position: it is simultaneously a lifeline that every other sector leans on, a sprawling collection of under-resourced operators, and an increasingly automated target that adversaries from criminal gangs to nation-states have learned to reach. The Oldsmar cursor, the Aliquippa PLC, the American Water portal, and the silent five-year presence of Volt Typhoon are not isolated stories but waypoints on a single trajectory — from opportunistic mischief toward deliberate, strategic pre-positioning.

The defensive knowledge largely exists. AWIA sets a floor, AWWA and WaterISAC supply the methods, NIST and IEC supply the architecture, and CISA supplies the threat intelligence. What remains contested is the connective tissue: the authority to require, the money to implement, and the institutions to coordinate. Until that tissue forms, the sector's safety will keep resting, more than anyone should be comfortable with, on an attentive operator noticing a cursor that moved on its own.
