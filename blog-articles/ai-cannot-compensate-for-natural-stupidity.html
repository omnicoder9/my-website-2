Artificial Intelligence Cannot Compensate for Natural Stupidity

There are problems that compute does not touch — the ones rooted in our minds, our bodies, our morals, and the matter the world is made of. A map of the walls, and an argument against despair.

A recurring premise: the usefulness of any tool is bounded by the wisdom of its user. A lever multiplies whatever force you put into it — including foolishness.

The aphorism is older than the technology it now indicts: artificial intelligence is no match for natural stupidity. It began as a wry remark about computers obeying bad instructions, and it has aged into something closer to a thesis. The most fashionable claims made for large models — that they will dissolve the need to learn, abolish fraud, lift the floor under human ability, repair institutions, and even rescue us from ourselves — collide with a class of problems that more parameters and more electricity simply do not move. Some of those walls are made of physics, some of biology, some of economics, and some of human nature. The point of this essay is to walk the perimeter honestly, and then to argue that the perimeter is a reason for agency rather than gloom.

It is worth being clear at the outset about what is not being argued. Modern AI is genuinely transformative within its domain; the productivity it confers on a skilled user is real and large. The claim here is narrower and more durable: there is no salvation in technology alone. The systems are improving at a pace that has begun to outrun the physical, social, and economic structures meant to absorb them1 — and that mismatch, not any shortfall in raw capability, is where the hardest problems live. The trouble is not that the machine is too weak. It is that the things we most want fixed were never the machine's to fix.

Section I

The Irreducible Cost of Cognition

The first and most personal fantasy is that intelligence in a box removes the need for intelligence in a head — that one can be spared the labour of learning by delegating it. This misunderstands what learning is. Durable competence is not a file that can be transferred; it is a physical change in a nervous system, and that change is produced only by the effortful work people increasingly use AI to skip.

Cognitive science has been unusually emphatic on this point. The conditions that make study feel easy and fluent — rereading, massed repetition, having an answer handed over — reliably produce weak retention, while conditions that feel harder produce learning that lasts. Robert and Elizabeth Bjork named these productive obstacles desirable difficulties: spacing, interleaving, and above all the strain of retrieving information rather than reviewing it.2 The single most robust finding in the field, the testing effect, is that the act of pulling knowledge out of memory strengthens it far more than putting it back in by re-exposure — even though learners perform worse, and feel worse, during the effortful version.3 The deeper lesson, drawn from decades of this work, is that performance and learning are not the same thing, and that smooth, frictionless performance is an actively misleading guide to whether anything durable has been acquired.4 A model that supplies the answer optimises precisely the variable — momentary fluency — that the science warns is a mirage.

Early empirical work on AI use is beginning to put numbers to the worry. In a survey of knowledge workers, higher confidence in a generative tool predicted less critical engagement with its output, with users reporting that they shifted effort away from analysing a problem and toward verifying — or simply accepting — what the machine produced.6 A larger study found a significant negative association between frequent reliance on AI tools and measured critical-thinking performance, an effect that was statistically mediated by cognitive offloading and was strongest among the youngest, heaviest users.5 A neurophysiological pilot using EEG during essay writing found reduced cross-brain connectivity in participants who composed with an assistant compared with those who wrote unaided — a pattern its authors provocatively framed as the accumulation of cognitive debt.7 None of these results is the last word, and each invites the obvious objection that the right pedagogy can turn offloading into scaffolding. But the burden they collectively impose is clear: the brain strengthens only the circuits it actually exercises, and convenience is not exercise.

The work that produces competence is exactly the work people reach for AI to avoid.

This is the biological reality beneath the productivity decks. Expertise — in surgery, mathematics, law, music, or a trade — is built by sustained, effortful, often uncomfortable practice, and the discomfort is not incidental. It is the signal that the system is being remodelled. A model can draft the brief, propose the proof, or play the passage, but it cannot install the underlying understanding into a person, any more than watching an athlete can build muscle. The consequence is unfashionable but unavoidable: mastering anything worth mastering will remain hard, and a life that involves growth, judgement, and real skill will involve strain that no interface removes. The tool can carry what you already understand much further. It cannot do the understanding for you.

Section II

The Genetic Floor

If effort is necessary, it is also — and this is the harder thing to say with the requisite care — not always sufficient. The motivational creed that anyone can become anything given the right tools runs into a stubborn empirical fact: human cognitive abilities, and a great many disabilities, have substantial biological and heritable components that no amount of willpower or prompting erases on demand.

The behavioural-genetic evidence on general cognitive ability is among the most replicated in the social sciences. Across twin and adoption designs, the heritability of measured intelligence is not fixed but rises with age, from roughly 40% in childhood to on the order of 70–80% in adulthood as people increasingly select and shape environments congenial to their dispositions.89 That those figures are real, however, is exactly why they are so easy to misuse, and three caveats are not optional. First, heritability is a statement about the sources of variation within a population under particular conditions — not a measure of how genetic any individual's mind is, and certainly not a verdict on anyone's worth. Second, it is highly conditional on environment: in samples drawn from severe deprivation, the heritability of IQ collapses toward zero and the shared environment dominates, because genetic potential cannot express itself through unmet need.10 Third, high heritability does not mean immutability; the secular rise in test scores across the twentieth century — the Flynn effect — shows that population-level cognitive performance can move substantially through environmental change alone.11

Read with those caveats, the conclusion is neither fatalistic nor flippant; it is humane. AI is, at its best, a powerful accommodation — text-to-speech for the dyslexic reader, structure for the executive-function-impaired, a patient explainer for the learner who needs the fifth pass. Assistive technology genuinely widens the range of what people can do, and that is among the most valuable things the field offers. But an accommodation is a ramp, not a cure. It does not rewrite a person's neurobiology, and it cannot raise a hard cognitive or developmental ceiling simply because someone wants it raised or prompts it skilfully. This is where the essay's title needs its least contemptuous reading. Human cognitive limits are real, widely distributed, and unevenly dealt — and that is an argument for compassion, sound design, and honest expectations, not for the cruelty of telling people that the only thing standing between them and any outcome is their failure to try hard enough.

Section III

The Operator Problem

Even where ability is abundant, a tool's ceiling is set by the person holding it. Extracting good work from a model requires the same scarce ingredients that distinguish expertise everywhere: a well-posed question, the domain knowledge to recognise a wrong answer, and the discipline to check. These are precisely what novices lack — which means the gap between a competent user and an incompetent one does not close with better models. If anything, it widens, because the output is now fluent enough to disguise the difference.

The clearest natural experiment is unfolding in the courts. Language models confabulate: they generate confident, well-formed, professionally formatted falsehoods. A systematic study of legal queries found that general-purpose models hallucinate case law at rates its authors judged far too high for responsible practice.12 The predictable result arrived in Mata v. Avianca, the first widely reported sanction of attorneys who filed a brief citing cases that the chatbot had invented wholesale.13 Far from a cautionary anomaly, it became a template: trackers now count more than six hundred filings worldwide in which lawyers cited authority that did not exist, including a Californian fined for an appeal in which twenty-one of twenty-three quotations were fabricated. The mechanism is the same every time — the model obliges, and the human does not verify — and it is made worse by sycophancy: the harder a position is to support, the more inventive the model becomes in supporting it, which is also the moment a motivated user is least inclined to push back.

600+ Documented court filings, and counting, in which lawyers cited cases or quotations fabricated by AI — a tool producing fluent nonsense, and operators failing to check it.1213

There is a vicious circle embedded here. The vigilance required to catch the machine's errors is the same critical engagement that reliance on the machine erodes6 — so the more one leans on it, the less equipped one becomes to use it safely. The fashionable image of the human–machine "centaur" outperforming either alone is true, but it carries a silent precondition: it works only when the human half is good. A lever multiplies the force applied to it, and it does so in both directions. Hand a powerful amplifier to careful judgement and you get leverage; hand it to carelessness and you get the same carelessness, faster, more confidently, and at scale. The technology does not supply the judgement. It only enlarges whatever judgement is already there.

Section IV

The Amoral Machine

A model has no purposes of its own. It is an instrument, and an instrument serves whoever holds it — wise or foolish, benign or malicious. We sometimes speak of "guardrails" as though they were structural, load-bearing properties of these systems. They are not. They are a trained disposition, and trained dispositions can be untrained.

The evidence is uncomfortably concrete. Researchers showed that the safety alignment of a leading commercial model could be stripped by fine-tuning it on roughly ten adversarial examples, at a cost under twenty cents through the vendor's own interface, after which it would comply with almost any harmful instruction.14 For open-weight models the situation is starker still: a separate team removed the safety fine-tuning from an open Llama-class model in minutes on a single consumer GPU for a few dollars — and on free cloud hardware for nothing — then packaged the result as a small "jailbreak adapter" that anyone could append to their own copy to disable its guardrails instantly.15 Once weights are public, alignment becomes a suggestion. The capability is what is being distributed; the conscience is optional and detachable.

The same instrument-not-agent character shows up in security. Prompt injection — supplying input that overrides a model's instructions — sits at the very top of the industry's catalogue of risks for AI applications, and it has stayed there because it cannot be patched away.16 Unlike a database injection, which a parameterised query can defeat, prompt injection exploits the defining feature of a language model: it processes instructions and data in the same channel and cannot reliably tell a command from the content it is meant to be reading. Indirect injection is the genuinely dangerous form, hiding instructions inside a web page, a document, an email, or an image that an AI agent later ingests.

A human skims the page and sees a sales chart. The agent reads the buried sentence as a command. As these systems are wired into inboxes, browsers, and tools — the agentic turn the whole industry is now taking — the blast radius of that confusion expands from an awkward chatbot reply to the silent exfiltration of real data. The vulnerability is not a bug to be fixed in the next release; it is a property of what the technology is.

And then there are the second-order harms — the ones AI does not merely fail to solve but may actively deepen. On inequality, the International Monetary Fund estimates that around 40% of jobs worldwide are exposed to AI, rising to roughly 60% in advanced economies and falling to about 26% in low-income ones, and concludes that in most scenarios the technology is likely to worsen overall inequality.17 The mechanism is familiar and self-reinforcing: the gains accrue disproportionately to those already positioned to capture them — owners of capital, the highly skilled, the well-connected economies — while the workers and countries least able to adapt absorb the disruption. A general-purpose technology does not distribute its dividends evenly; it distributes them along the contours of existing advantage.

On the environment, the supposedly weightless cloud has a heavy physical footprint. The International Energy Agency projects that electricity demand from data centres will roughly double by 2030 to around 945 terawatt-hours — comparable to the entire electricity consumption of Japan — with AI the single most important driver.1 The water cost is less discussed and equally real: one analysis estimated that training a single large model in a US data centre could evaporate on the order of seven hundred thousand litres of clean freshwater,18 while a national accounting put direct cooling-water consumption by US data centres at roughly sixty-six billion litres in 2023 and projected it to multiply by the end of the decade.19 The machine offered as the answer to climate and scarcity is, in the present, a competitor for the same power and water.

Section V

The Forge of Deception

Some problems do not merely resist AI; they are made worse by it, because they are adversarial. Fraud and deception are games played against an opponent, and a more capable generative tool is, before it is anything else, a better weapon for the deceiver. Here the technology's improvement is the threat.

In early 2024 a finance employee at the engineering firm Arup, working from its Hong Kong office, joined a video call with the company's chief financial officer and several colleagues and, on their instruction, executed fifteen transfers totalling about US$25.6 million. Every other participant on the call was a deepfake, synthesised from ordinary footage scraped off public channels; the employee's initial, correct suspicion of a phishing email was overridden by the apparent corroboration of familiar faces and voices in real time.20 The detail that should unsettle most is not the loss but the accessibility: the firm's own technology chief later recreated a convincing live deepfake of himself using free tools in well under an hour. The capability that powered a multi-million-dollar fraud is, increasingly, lying around for anyone to pick up.

$25.6M Transferred in a single day after a video call in which every participant but the victim was an AI-generated deepfake of a colleague.20

Better detection is not the escape it appears to be, because detection and generation are locked in the same loop: every classifier that learns to spot a forgery becomes training signal for the next, more convincing generator. But the deepest damage is not any individual fake. It is the corrosion of the shared evidentiary baseline on which accountability rests. The legal scholars Robert Chesney and Danielle Citron called this the liar's dividend: once the public knows that audio and video can be fabricated convincingly, the genuinely guilty can wave away authentic recordings as "just a deepfake," and the dividend grows in direct proportion to how successfully society educates itself about the danger.21 Their survey of remedies reaches a sober conclusion — there is no silver bullet. This is an epistemic problem, not a technical one, and it has the perverse structure that the very capability which might authenticate the truth is the capability that makes the truth deniable. A better machine cannot resolve it, because the better machine is on both sides.

Once anything can be fake, the guilty can dismiss what is real — and the dividend rises the more we learn to doubt.

Section VI

The Tyranny of the Physical

The final wall is the oldest. AI is information; the economy is matter. No quantity of inference conjures fresh water into a drought, arable soil into a desert, a navigable coastline onto a landlocked border, or a seam of neodymium into bedrock that lacks it. Software operates on representations of the world; it does not operate on the world's stock of physical things.

Economic geography has long argued that physical endowments shape development directly — that location, climate, disease burden, transport costs, and agricultural productivity leave measurable marks on income and growth, with coastal, temperate, resource-favoured regions advantaged over landlocked or tropical ones.22 The thesis is genuinely contested; an influential rival tradition holds that institutions, not latitude, are the deeper cause, and that geography's apparent effects often run through the human arrangements it shaped rather than acting on their own.23 But the dispute is about the weight of physical constraints, not their existence. Whatever the precise coefficient, a water-stressed, landlocked economy does not acquire a deep-water port or a wet aquifer by training a model. The constraint is in the ground and the map, where software cannot reach.

The sharpest irony is that AI's own existence is bound by the same geology and geopolitics. The advanced chips on which it runs depend on gallium, germanium, and the rare-earth elements, and the supply of these is extraordinarily concentrated: by the IEA's accounting one country controls on the order of 98% of global gallium supply, processes the large majority of the world's germanium, and produces roughly 95% of rare-earth oxides — leverage it began converting into formal export controls on exactly these semiconductor inputs in late 2024.24 The data-centre build-out adds materially to demand for copper and these minerals, even as projected copper supply is set to fall short later this decade.24 You cannot prompt a mine into existence. Exploration, permitting, extraction, and refining take years, are fixed to the places the deposits happen to be, and answer to governments rather than to compute budgets. The most physical, scarcity-bound resources — energy, water, minerals, land — are precisely the ones where AI consumes rather than creates, and where it cannot wish a constraint away.

Coda

Against Despair

Laid end to end, the catalogue can read as pessimism. It is not meant to. The argument has never been that AI is useless — within its proper domain it is one of the most powerful instruments we have ever built — but that there is no salvation in it, no point at which sufficient capability discharges us from the human work of living well. The thread running through every wall in this essay is the same: each is, at bottom, a fact about people or about matter. That learning is effortful. That minds and bodies have limits. That tools magnify their users. That value and judgement must be supplied by us. That truth is a commons we maintain or lose together. That the earth is finite. None of these is abolished by more computation, because none of them was ever a computational problem.

But — and this is the whole point of refusing despair — none of them is fixed, either. The future is not encoded in the technology; it is decided by what we choose to do with it. The same lever that multiplies folly multiplies wisdom in exactly equal measure. Whether AI widens inequality or helps narrow it, whether it accelerates fraud or hardens institutions against it, whether it is built on a burning grid or a clean one, whether it deskills a generation or extends what skilled people can reach — these are choices, overwhelmingly moral and political ones, not technical inevitabilities. Our actions and our inactions both have consequences, and the machine is indifferent to which we pick.

Which leaves the one thing no model can supply. There is no substitute for human virtue — for diligence, honesty, courage, and care for other people — because virtue is not a capability to be automated but a stance to be taken, again and again, by someone willing to take it. The instrument can carry that stance further than ever before, or it can carry its opposite. Artificial intelligence cannot compensate for natural stupidity, and it was never supposed to. Reducing that stupidity — in ourselves first, and then in the world we build with these extraordinary new tools — has always been, and remains, our job.
