Preface

A problem with a very bad reputation

Picture a stretch of pasture open to a whole village. Each herder keeps cattle on it, and each, weighing whether to add one more animal, runs a small private calculation. The extra beast is worth roughly one whole animal’s sale price — and that profit lands entirely in the herder’s own pocket. The cost is overgrazing: a little less grass for every animal already there. But that cost is spread across the whole village, so the herder personally bears only a sliver of it. The arithmetic is lopsided and it always points the same way: add the animal. Every herder reasons identically. The grass thins, the soil compacts, the commons collapses — and the herders, each of whom behaved perfectly sensibly, are ruined together.

This is the parable the biologist Garrett Hardin planted at the centre of twentieth-century environmental thought in a 1968 essay in Science with one of the most quoted titles in the history of the journal.1 Hardin chose the word tragedy deliberately, in its old theatrical sense: not a sad accident but the “remorseless working of things,” a doom that follows inexorably from the premises. Freedom in a commons, he wrote, brings ruin to all. The phrase escaped the page almost immediately. It has since been used to explain overfishing, deforestation, traffic jams, antibiotic failure, groundwater depletion, spam, urban sprawl, the population of Earth, and the warming of its atmosphere. Few four-word phrases have done so much intellectual work.

And yet the parable is, in a precise sense, only half true — and the false half has done real damage. Hardin’s model assumes a resource that nobody governs: an open gate, no rules, no village council, no memory between neighbours. Where those conditions genuinely hold, his logic is iron. But the historical commons he invoked were almost never like that. Real medieval pastures were hedged about with rules — who could graze, how many beasts, in which season — enforced by the commoners themselves. The villagers were not too foolish to see the trap; they had built institutions to climb out of it centuries before an ecologist told them they couldn’t. The political scientist Elinor Ostrom spent a career documenting exactly this, and in 2009 it earned her a Nobel Prize. Late in his life Hardin himself conceded the point, remarking that he should have titled the essay “The Tragedy of the Unmanaged Commons.”

The tragedy is not a law of human nature. It is a property of a particular kind of situation — and situations can be redesigned.

That correction is the spine of this article. We will first pin down, with some rigour, exactly what features a problem must have to count as a genuine tragedy of the commons — because the phrase is now thrown at problems that aren’t one, and the diagnosis matters for the cure. We will trace where the idea came from, long before Hardin. We will examine the two cures the twentieth century reached for by reflex — privatise it, or hand it to the state — and why each is sometimes right and often not enough. We will then turn to the richer, empirically grounded answer Ostrom and others assembled: that humans, under the right conditions, govern shared things rather well, and that those conditions can be specified and built. From there we survey the working toolbox — old instruments and new ones still being invented — and finally walk the great commons still open and unmanaged today: the climate, the fisheries, the antibiotics, the orbit, and the strange new commons of knowledge and attention. One of them, the ozone layer, we have already saved. The rest are the unfinished business of the century.

Origins

Where the idea comes from

Hardin did not invent the commons problem; he gave it a name that travelled. The pieces had been lying around for two thousand years.

The oldest statement of the core intuition belongs to Aristotle, who observed in the Politics that “what is common to the greatest number gets the least amount of care,” since people are inclined to consider chiefly their own and to neglect what they hold jointly with others.2 Two millennia later, in 1833, the English political economist William Forster Lloyd supplied the actual cattle. In a now-obscure Oxford lecture he asked why the animals on a common were so puny and the common itself so worn, when cattle on enclosed private fields were sleek and the fields lush — and answered with the same lopsided arithmetic of the extra beast.3 Hardin credited Lloyd directly; the pamphlet is the true ancestor of the 1968 essay.

In between sat a longer history of the actual English commons and their enclosure — the centuries-long, often violent conversion of common fields into private holdings, accelerated by the Parliamentary enclosure acts of the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries. That history matters because it cuts against the simple story: the commons were enclosed not only, or even mainly, because they were failing, but because enclosure was profitable to those with the power to enact it. The dispossession of the commoners was real, and the moral residue of it still colours every argument about “privatising” a shared resource.

The idea was reborn in modern, mathematical dress in the 1950s, in the unglamorous field of fisheries economics. In 1954 the Canadian economist H. Scott Gordon published a model of an open-access fishery showing that, with free entry, fishing effort piles in until the economic rent of the fishery is dissipated entirely — boats keep coming until the last one barely breaks even, and the stock is fished down far below the level that would maximise sustainable yield.4 Gordon’s fishery is Hardin’s pasture with equations, and it is no coincidence that fisheries remain the cleanest real-world laboratory for the whole problem.

The context Hardin wrote inWhat the 1968 essay was actually about

Read today, Hardin’s essay is often remembered as a tract about pollution and resource depletion. It was really about population. The piece opens by declaring that the population problem belongs to a class of “no technical solution problems” — problems that no clever engineering can dissolve, that demand instead a change in human values. It appeared at the height of a wave of overpopulation anxiety, the same wave that carried Paul Ehrlich’s bestselling jeremiad The Population Bomb into print the very same year, with its opening prophecy that the battle to feed humanity was already lost.5 Hardin’s commons was an argument by analogy: the family deciding whether to have another child, he claimed, faces the herder’s lopsided incentive, reaping the private joys of the extra child while the costs of a more crowded planet fall on everyone.

His proposed remedy was as uncomfortable as the analogy. If appeals to conscience would only select against the conscientious — the responsible would breed less and be demographically swamped by the heedless — then the answer had to be coercion of a particular, carefully bounded kind: what he called mutual coercion, mutually agreed upon. We accept taxes, traffic lights, and bank-robbery laws not because we enjoy being constrained but because we recognise that voluntary restraint would simply reward the unrestrained; the constraint, collectively chosen, makes everyone freer. That single phrase is the most durable thing in the essay, and it is the seed of every serious commons remedy that follows: a regime of rules that participants would themselves consent to, because each prefers being bound together to being ruined together.

A necessary caveat about the author

Hardin’s later writing makes for grim reading. In essays such as “Living on a Lifeboat” he argued against food aid, against immigration, and against helping the global poor, on the theory that a rich “lifeboat” nation that took on more passengers would only sink.6 He held openly eugenicist and racialist views, and the Southern Poverty Law Center lists him as a white nationalist. None of this is incidental: the coercion he had in mind was often coercion of the poor and the foreign. One can take the analytical core of the commons model seriously — many thinkers far removed from Hardin’s politics have — while flatly rejecting the uses he put it to. Keeping the two apart is itself part of reading him rigorously.

Anatomy

What makes a problem a tragedy of the commons

The phrase is now applied so freely that it has lost much of its diagnostic edge — invoked for any situation where things go badly and many people are involved. To make it useful again we have to ask what structural features a situation must actually have. There are four, and a genuine tragedy of the commons needs all of them at once. Where one is missing, the problem is real but it is a different problem, with a different cure.

Property oneSubtractability: the resource is depletable

One person’s use must genuinely subtract from what is left for everyone else. A fish caught is a fish no one else can catch; a tonne of carbon dioxide emitted occupies part of a finite atmospheric budget; a litre pumped from an aquifer is a litre gone. Economists call this rivalry or subtractability, and it is what separates a commons problem from a mere coordination problem. Its absence is just as telling: an idea, a melody, or a line of open-source code is non-rivalrous — your using it takes nothing from me — which is exactly why the “commons” of knowledge behaves so unlike a pasture, a theme we return to near the end.

Property twoNon-excludability: keeping users out is hard or costly

It must be difficult, or prohibitively expensive, to prevent people from drawing on the resource. If you can cheaply fence the field, meter the water, or patrol the fishing grounds, you can simply ration access and the tragedy never gets going. The hardness of exclusion is what makes the problem genuinely hard — and, as we shall see, an enormous share of the modern toolbox consists of technologies and legal devices whose real function is to lower the cost of exclusion until rationing becomes possible.

Rivalrous and hard to exclude from: that combination is what Ostrom named a common-pool resource, and the cleanest way to grasp it is to place it against the three other basic kinds of goods, sorted along exactly these two axes.7

Property threePrivate rationality diverges from collective rationality

This is the engine, the lopsided arithmetic of the opening parable. Each actor’s private benefit from one more unit of use exceeds their private share of the cost, because the cost is externalised — pushed onto the whole group. So the dominant move, for every individual reasoning in isolation, is to keep using; and when all do, the resource collapses and everyone ends up worse off than if all had restrained themselves. The defining, almost paradoxical feature is that the ruin is reached by people behaving rationally, not foolishly. This is structurally identical to the most famous object in game theory, the prisoner’s dilemma, in which mutual defection is the equilibrium even though mutual cooperation would leave both players better off; the commons is simply that dilemma played by many people at once, an N-person prisoner’s dilemma.8

Property fourMany actors, no single hand on the tiller

Finally there must be a plurality of users whose actions are interdependent and who cannot easily coordinate or be coordinated. A resource with a single owner is not a commons: the owner internalises both the benefit and the cost of overuse and has every reason to husband it. The problem appears precisely when benefit and cost are split across many decision-makers — when the gains are concentrated on the user and the losses are diffused across the group. That asymmetry, concentrated benefit against diffuse cost, is the signature of the whole family of problems and the reason they are so stubborn: the beneficiary of restraint is “everyone, a little,” which is to say no one in particular with the motive to insist on it.

Three neighbouring ideas describe the same animal from different angles. The economist Mancur Olson’s Logic of Collective Action showed that the larger and more diffuse a group, the harder it is to get its members to contribute voluntarily to a shared end — large groups under-provide collective goods unless they find some way to coerce or to selectively reward participation.9 The free-rider problem is the mirror image of the commons: where a commons is a shared resource over-used, a public good is a shared benefit under-provided, because each person hopes to enjoy it on the strength of others’ contributions. And the welfare economist A. C. Pigou had, decades earlier, given the general name externality to any cost (or benefit) an actor imposes on others without paying for it (or being paid), proposing that the cure was to make the actor pay — a tax equal to the damage done.10 Each of these — collective action, free riding, externality, prisoner’s dilemma — is a facet of the single underlying fact that private and social incentives have come apart.

The distinction that changes everything

The four properties above describe an unmanaged, open-access regime — a resource with no rules and no gatekeeper. But “commons,” historically and legally, did not mean that. A commons was a resource held and used by an identifiable community under rules: who may graze, how many beasts, in which months, enforced by the commoners themselves. The legal scholar Carol Rose calls this a limited common-property regime, and insists it is a third thing, distinct from both open access and private property.11

This is not pedantry; the policy implications are opposite. If the trouble is that no one owns the resource, the natural fix is to give it an owner — privatise. If the trouble is that a community’s rules have broken down or never existed, the fix may be to build or restore the rules. Hardin’s essay quietly conflated the managed commons with the open-access free-for-all, and then declared the managed commons doomed — which is rather like proving that cars are lethal by describing only cars with no brakes. Everything that follows in this article is, in one way or another, about the brakes.

The reflex

Leviathan, or property

For most of the twentieth century, anyone who accepted Hardin’s diagnosis reached for one of two cures, and the two camps spent decades talking past each other. Either you privatise the commons — carve it into parcels and give each an owner who will husband it — or you hand it to the state, which sets and enforces limits from above. Markets or government; property or Leviathan. The debate was real and the instruments are genuinely powerful. But the framing was a trap, and seeing why is the necessary prelude to everything better.

Cure oneGive it an owner

The case for privatisation rests on a simple insight: a sole owner internalises what a crowd externalises. The owner of an enclosed field bears the full cost of overgrazing it and reaps the full reward of resting it, so the lopsided arithmetic of the commons vanishes. The economist Harold Demsetz turned this into a theory of how property rights emerge in the first place — they tend to crystallise, he argued, precisely when a resource becomes valuable or scarce enough that the gains from internalising its externalities finally exceed the costs of defining and defending ownership.12 On this view, enclosure is not a crime against the commons but its natural maturation.

The deeper theoretical engine is the Coase theorem. In a 1960 paper that would become one of the most cited in all of economics, Ronald Coase argued that if property rights are clearly assigned and the costs of bargaining are low, the parties to an externality will simply negotiate their way to an efficient outcome — and will do so regardless of who initially holds the right.13 If a factory’s smoke harms a laundry next door, it does not ultimately matter (for efficiency) whether the factory has a right to pollute or the laundry a right to clean air; whoever values the contested use more will pay the other to yield, and the resource flows to its highest use. Pollution, fisheries, water — assign the rights cleanly, let people trade, and the tragedy dissolves into a market.

It is a beautiful result, and it is routinely misremembered. Coase’s actual point was the opposite of a blanket endorsement of laissez-faire: he was emphasising how much hinges on the clause “if the costs of bargaining are low.” In the real world those costs are frequently enormous. When the affected parties number in the millions, when they are scattered across generations or borders, when the harm is invisible or its source disputed, the bargain Coase imagined cannot be struck. You cannot convene the eight billion present and future users of the atmosphere to negotiate over a tonne of carbon. And privatisation faces a second, blunter obstacle: some commons resist the fence. You can assign catch shares in a fishery, but you cannot easily parcel out the deep ocean, the night sky, the migratory bird, or the climate. A property right is only as good as the exclusion that backs it, and exclusion is exactly what these resources lack.

There is also a question privatisation tends to bury: who gets the property? The historical enclosure of the English commons was not a neutral act of institutional hygiene. It transferred a resource that poor commoners had relied on into the hands of those with the power to enact the transfer, and it did so over fierce and sometimes violent resistance. Every modern proposal to “create property rights” in a shared resource — to auction fishing quotas, to allocate emission permits, to privatise water — inherits that distributional question. Efficiency tells you the pie may grow; it does not tell you who gets the slices, and in practice the initial allocation is where the real politics lives.

A property right is only as good as the exclusion that backs it — and exclusion is exactly what these resources lack.

Cure twoHand it to Leviathan

The opposite reflex trusts not the market but the magistrate. If individuals left free will destroy the commons, then a central authority must compel restraint — set the quotas, cap the catch, ban the gear, tax the emission, and punish the violators. This was Hardin’s own instinct, dressed as “mutual coercion mutually agreed upon,” and in the 1970s it hardened, in some hands, into something darker. The political theorist William Ophuls argued in Ecology and the Politics of Scarcity that ecological limits would force a retreat from liberal democracy toward a more coercive, centralised politics — that scarcity, in effect, demanded a green Leviathan.14 The state, on this view, is the only agent large enough to hold the whole and impose the discipline that atomised individuals never will.

State authority has, in fact, saved many commons — every enforced fishing quota and protected wilderness is a case in point — and for some problems nothing smaller will do. But the centralised cure has its own failure modes, and they mirror privatisation’s. The regulator suffers a knowledge problem: a distant ministry rarely knows the local stream, the particular reef, the season’s conditions, as well as the people who live by them, and rules written in the capital are often clumsy where they land. Regulators are vulnerable to capture by the very industries they police, so that the quota is set by those who profit from its laxity. Enforcement is expensive, and a rule no one monitors is no rule at all. And for the largest commons of all there simply is no Leviathan: there is no world government to cap the planet’s emissions or police the high seas, only sovereign states that cannot be compelled. The biggest problems are exactly the ones the state-centred cure cannot reach.

So the twentieth century left us with two instruments, each genuinely useful, each failing in a wide band of cases, and a debate that treated them as exhaustive. Privatise or nationalise; the market or the state. Missing from the picture was the very thing the historical commons had actually been: a community governing itself. Filling that gap was the work of one person more than any other.

Governance

Ostrom’s third way

Where Hardin reasoned from a model, Elinor Ostrom went and looked. Trained as a political scientist, she spent four decades assembling a vast comparative archive of real communities managing real shared resources — mountain pastures, irrigation systems, inshore fisheries, groundwater basins, forests — across centuries and continents. What she found contradicted the theorem head-on. Again and again, groups of ordinary people had governed their commons sustainably for hundreds of years, with neither a private owner nor a coercing state, by crafting and enforcing their own rules. In 2009 the work made her the first woman to win the Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences, shared with Oliver Williamson, “for her analysis of economic governance, especially the commons.”15

The cases are concrete and worth holding in mind, because they are the evidence. In the Swiss alpine village of Törbel, documents from as early as 1483 record communal rules governing the high summer pastures, including a stinting rule of remarkable economy: no villager may send more cows to the common meadow than they can feed through the winter on their own land — an elegant cap that automatically scales grazing pressure to each household’s capacity.7 In Japan, thousands of villages managed their iriai common forests and meadows for centuries under detailed local codes. In Valencia and other Spanish cities, irrigation communities have allocated scarce water for roughly a thousand years; the Tribunal de las Aguas, the Water Court of the plain of Valencia, still convenes every Thursday outside the cathedral to settle disputes among farmers, a living institution older than most nations. In the Philippines, farmer-built zanjera irrigation associations organise the labour and water of whole valleys. None of these are utopias, and some have failed; but their mere existence falsifies the claim that the commons must end in tragedy.

The pattern beneath the casesEight design principles

Ostrom’s lasting contribution was to ask what the durable, successful cases had in common that the failed ones lacked, and to distil the answer into a set of design principles — not a recipe to be copied, but a checklist of conditions that long-enduring commons institutions tend to satisfy.7 They have become the single most influential framework in the field.

  1. Clearly defined boundaries. Both the edges of the resource and the set of people entitled to use it are unambiguous. You cannot manage what you cannot bound, or ration access among a membership no one can name.
  2. Congruence between rules, local conditions, and the split of costs and benefits. The rules about how much may be taken fit the particular ecology and culture, and those who reap more from the resource contribute proportionally more to maintaining it. Imported, one-size-fits-all rules tend to misfire.
  3. Collective-choice arrangements. Most of the people bound by the rules can take part in changing them. Rules the users themselves can revise command a legitimacy that rules handed down never do, and they can be tuned as conditions shift.
  4. Monitoring. The condition of the resource and the behaviour of users are actively watched — crucially, by monitors who are accountable to the users or are the users themselves, not distant officials with no stake.
  5. Graduated sanctions. Those who break the rules face penalties that escalate with the gravity and frequency of the offence — a quiet word, then a fine, then exclusion — rather than instant, brittle severity. Mild first sanctions preserve relationships while still deterring.
  6. Conflict-resolution mechanisms. Cheap, fast, local forums exist to settle the disputes that inevitably arise — like Valencia’s Thursday water court — so that disagreements do not fester into the breakdown of the whole arrangement.
  7. Minimal recognition of the right to organise. External authorities — the regional or national government — do not override the community’s right to devise its own institutions. Self-governance survives only where it is not casually overruled from above.
  8. Nested enterprises. For resources that are part of larger systems, governance is layered — small local units nested inside larger ones, each handling what it is best placed to handle. This is polycentricity, and it is how local commons scale up.

The principles are not folklore. In 2010 a careful meta-analysis by Michael Cox and colleagues tested them against ninety-one separate empirical studies of commons institutions around the world and found them broadly and robustly supported, with a few refinements — splitting some of the compound principles apart for sharper diagnosis.16 The framework earns its standing the hard way, by surviving contact with the data.

The lesson was never “communities always succeed.” It was that whether they succeed is decided by the design of their institutions — and design is something we can study, teach, and improve.

Against magic bulletsNo panaceas, and the limits of scale

Ostrom was emphatic that her principles were not a panacea, and she made the warning itself a research programme. In an article pointedly titled “Going Beyond Panaceas,” she and her colleagues argued that the search for a single best institution — always privatise, always regulate, always leave it to the community — is a category error, because the right arrangement depends on the specific attributes of the resource, the users, and their setting.17 To impose order on that complexity she later built a general diagnostic framework for analysing social-ecological systems, a structured way to break any commons problem into its component variables before reaching for a remedy.18

She was equally clear-eyed about the frontier. The self-governing commons she documented were overwhelmingly local: bounded resources, modest numbers of users who knew one another, repeated interaction, the capacity to monitor and to talk. The hardest commons of our age are the opposite on every axis — global, anonymous, billions of users, costs deferred across decades and displaced across borders. In “Revisiting the Commons” and “The Struggle to Govern the Commons,” Ostrom and her co-authors laid out both the achievement and the unfinished work: the conditions for success at small scale are now reasonably well understood, but scaling self-governance up to planetary commons — and stitching the local, national, and global layers together — remains the open problem.1920 Her response was not despair but architecture: a polycentric world of many overlapping institutions at many scales, rather than a single global authority or a single global market. That vision frames the rest of this article.

Step back and the binary of the previous section has quietly become a quartet. There are at least four ways to govern a shared resource, each with its own logic, its own characteristic failure, and its own best domain.

The toolbox

Instruments, old and newly invented

Beneath the proliferation of policies there is a satisfying economy of underlying moves. A tragedy of the commons requires the four properties of the anatomy section to hold simultaneously; break any one of them and the tragedy unravels. That gives us four families of remedy, four levers — and almost every real instrument, from a medieval grazing stint to a blockchain-based funding round, is one of these four in disguise.

Lever oneRe-price: make the user pay the true cost

If the disease is externalised cost, the most direct cure is to internalise it — to bolt the social cost back onto the private decision. This is Pigou’s ancient prescription: levy a tax equal to the harm, and the lopsided arithmetic rights itself. Carbon taxes do this for emissions; congestion charges do it for the commons of road space, and the working examples — Singapore’s electronic road pricing, London’s congestion charge, Stockholm’s cordon toll — measurably cut traffic where they have been tried. Economists have even proposed taxing antibiotic use, so that a patient demanding antibiotics for a minor ailment confronts a price reflecting the global erosion of drug effectiveness they help cause. Pricing keeps the resource open to all but forces each user to reckon with the whole cost, not just their slice.

Lever twoRe-fence: turn an open resource into rationed rights

The mirror-image move is to make exclusion cheap enough that access can be rationed and the resource given the equivalent of an owner. Cap-and-trade does this at the level of a whole resource: a regulator fixes a hard ceiling — a cap on total emissions, a total allowable catch — issues that quantity as tradable permits, and lets a market move them to their highest-value use. The United States Acid Rain Program of the 1990s, which capped sulphur-dioxide emissions and let utilities trade allowances, became the textbook demonstration that a quantity cap plus a market can cut pollution faster and cheaper than command-and-control rules. In fisheries the same logic appears as individual transferable quotas or catch shares, which convert the “race to fish” into a secure share of a sustainable harvest. The evidence here is unusually strong: a global study by Christopher Costello and colleagues, examining the records of more than eleven thousand fisheries, found that those managed with catch shares were substantially less likely to be heading for collapse than those without — the institution measurably bent the trajectory away from the tragedy.21

A subtlety lurks in the choice between pricing (lever one) and quantity caps (this lever). Martin Weitzman’s classic result, “Prices vs. Quantities,” showed that under uncertainty the two are not equivalent: when the danger lies in getting the quantity wrong — a threshold beyond which a fish stock crashes or a climate tips — a hard quantity cap is safer; when the danger lies in costs spiking unpredictably, a price (a tax) is safer.22 The two instruments are tools for different shapes of risk, which is why sophisticated systems increasingly hybridise them — a cap with a price floor and ceiling.

Lever threeRe-group: change who the players are, and how they relate

The tragedy needs many actors who neither coordinate nor expect to meet again. Soften any part of that and cooperation becomes reachable. Ostrom’s self-governing communities are one route — small, bounded, repeating groups that build their own rules. The theoretical underpinning came from Robert Axelrod’s celebrated computer tournaments, in which strategies competed at the repeated prisoner’s dilemma and the winner was the almost absurdly simple tit-for-tat: cooperate first, then mirror whatever the other player did last. Axelrod’s lesson was that cooperation can emerge among self-interested agents without any central authority, provided the “shadow of the future” is long enough — provided players expect to interact again and can reward kindness and punish defection over time.23 Reputation, reciprocity, and the slow accumulation of trust are not soft add-ons to commons governance; they are load-bearing.

At the largest scale, the re-grouping move becomes minilateralism: abandon the doomed quest for universal agreement and form a smaller club of the willing that makes membership pay. William Nordhaus’s “climate club” is the leading example — a coalition of countries that adopt a common carbon price internally and levy a tariff on imports from non-members, converting the global free-rider problem into a private incentive to join.24 The club changes the payoff of staying outside, which is precisely the leverage a universal treaty lacks. We return to it when we reach the climate.

The modern accelerantWhen monitoring becomes nearly free

Lurking inside levers two and three is the single most consequential change of the past two decades. Ostrom’s fourth principle — monitoring — was historically the binding constraint: you could not ration a fishery you could not watch, or enforce a forest boundary you could not see. Technology is dissolving that constraint. Satellites now track the world’s fishing fleets in near-real time: a landmark study mapped the global footprint of industrial fishing by processing billions of position signals broadcast by tens of thousands of vessels, rendering visible an activity that was effectively invisible a generation ago.25 Remote sensing watches deforestation acre by acre; cheap networked sensors meter water and air; emerging systems use distributed ledgers to make emissions reporting tamper-evident. As the cost of observation collapses, exclusion becomes feasible where it never was, and self-governance becomes enforceable at scales Ostrom’s villagers could not have dreamed of. If there is a single reason for measured optimism about the global commons, it is this.

Lever four, and the inverse problemFunding what everyone enjoys but no one will pay for

The fourth lever — re-thinking the resource — sometimes means finding substitutes (the ozone story turned on exactly this), and sometimes means recognising that a resource is not rivalrous at all, which flips the whole problem on its head, as we will see with knowledge. But re-thinking also points at the commons’ twin: the under-provision of public goods. Where a commons is over-used, a public good is under-funded, because each potential contributor hopes to free-ride on the rest. A growing kit of mechanism-design tools attacks this directly. Assurance contracts — the logic behind crowdfunding platforms — collect pledges that only bind if a threshold is reached, so no one risks contributing to a good that fails to materialise. More radically, quadratic funding, formalised by Vitalik Buterin, Zoë Hitzig, and Glen Weyl, designs a matching scheme that weights a public good by the breadth of its support rather than the depth of a few large donors’ pockets, yielding, under its assumptions, the optimal level of provision; it is already used to fund open-source software and other digital public goods.26 These are young instruments, but they represent something genuinely new in the toolbox: engineered incentive structures purpose-built to defeat the free-rider problem rather than merely to ration scarcity.

Case · climate

The master commons

If you wanted to design, from scratch, the most intractable commons problem imaginable, you would arrive at something very like climate change. It possesses all four tragic properties of the anatomy section, each turned up to its maximum. The atmosphere is a finite sink — it can absorb only so much carbon dioxide before the climate shifts — so it is rivalrous: every tonne emitted consumes part of a shared, dwindling budget. It is the least excludable resource conceivable; no fence, meter, or patrol can stop anyone, anywhere, from emitting. The users are not a village but the whole of humanity, eight billion strong. And it adds a fifth cruelty the pasture never had: the costs are deferred across decades and displaced across borders. The carbon a power plant emits today warms a planet that will harm different people, in different countries, generations hence. It is an N-person prisoner’s dilemma in which N spans not just everyone alive but everyone yet to be born — the participants who suffer most cannot sit at the table, because they do not yet exist.

The physical stakes are now stated by the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change with high confidence: to halt global warming at any level, net carbon-dioxide emissions must reach zero, and the longer that takes, the higher the peak temperature and the larger the harm.27 There is, in effect, a finite remaining carbon budget for any temperature target, and humanity is spending it down. This is the commons being grazed, in the most literal sense the metaphor allows.

Why the free-rider problem bites hardest hereThe arithmetic of nations

Climate negotiation founders on the same lopsided incentive as the pasture, now operating at the level of sovereign states. When a country spends to cut its emissions, it bears the entire cost itself, but the benefit — a marginally cooler planet — is shared by all nations. Nordhaus put the asymmetry starkly: no single country captures more than a small fraction of the global benefit of its own mitigation, so each, reasoning nationally, has a powerful incentive to do little and let others bear the burden — to free-ride.24 The result is visible in the prices: as recently as the end of the 2010s the average effective carbon price across the world was close to zero, and most jurisdictions priced carbon at nothing at all, even as fossil fuels continued to receive subsidies that pushed the net price negative.

This is why the Paris Agreement, for all its diplomatic achievement in winning near-universal participation, is structurally weak as a commons institution. Its commitments are nationally determined — each country sets its own pledge — and there is no enforcement: no penalty for falling short, no tariff for staying out, only peer pressure and the ratchet of periodic review. Measured against Ostrom’s principles, it is a treaty with weak monitoring of outcomes, no graduated sanctions, and no answer to the free-rider. It changed the conversation without changing the payoffs, and the payoffs are the problem.

Pulling all four levers at onceWhat is actually being tried

No single instrument can govern this commons, so the real response is a sprawling, overlapping attempt to pull every lever from the toolbox simultaneously.

Re-pricing. Carbon pricing — taxes and emissions-trading systems — has spread steadily. By the World Bank’s count there are now more than seventy carbon-pricing instruments operating around the world, together covering roughly a quarter of global greenhouse-gas emissions.28 That is a real and growing share — but it leaves three-quarters of emissions unpriced, and across most schemes the price still sits well below the level economists estimate as the true social cost of carbon. The instrument works; it is simply not yet applied widely or stringently enough.

Re-fencing. The largest pricing systems are caps, not taxes: the European Union’s Emissions Trading System, the world’s biggest carbon market, fixes a declining ceiling on emissions from power and heavy industry and lets firms trade allowances beneath it — Acid Rain Program logic, scaled to a continent. China now operates the largest ETS by volume of emissions covered. These caps turn a slice of the atmospheric commons into a rationed, tradable right.

Re-grouping. The most structurally interesting move is the turn toward clubs. The European Union’s Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism — a tariff on the embedded carbon of certain imports, phasing in toward full operation in the mid-2020s — is the first real-world implementation of the border carbon adjustment that Nordhaus’s climate club requires.24 By charging imports from countries without comparable carbon pricing, it both protects domestic industry from being undercut and, crucially, alters the incentive to remain outside the club — giving trading partners a reason to adopt carbon pricing of their own. Whether such clubs can grow large enough to matter is one of the open questions of climate policy, but they attack the free-rider problem at its root in a way Paris cannot.

Re-thinking. Finally, the response leans on technology to change the payoffs from below. Nordhaus identified a double externality in climate innovation: clean-energy research is under-provided both because its knowledge spills over to competitors (the ordinary problem with research) and because the carbon it would displace is underpriced. Public investment, deployment subsidies, and innovation policy attack this, and they are working at the level of cost: the price of solar power and batteries has fallen so far and so fast that in much of the world the cheapest new electricity is now also the cleanest. When the low-carbon option becomes the cheap option, the commons dilemma partly dissolves on its own — countries decarbonise not from altruism but from economic self-interest, exactly the realignment lever four promises.

The Ostrom corollaryDon’t wait for the world

Ostrom’s own contribution to the climate debate was to resist the assumption that nothing counts until a single global agreement binds everyone. A planetary commons, she argued, can be addressed through polycentric action at many scales at once — cities cutting their own emissions, states and provinces running their own carbon markets, firms and sectors setting their own standards, regions forming their own clubs — without anyone waiting for the slowest party at the global table. Such efforts capture immediate, local co-benefits, cleaner air, energy security, lower costs, that accrue to the actor directly and now, blunting the free-rider logic. Polycentric action is messier than a grand treaty and no substitute for pricing the whole atmosphere, but it is how every other commons in history was actually governed: not from the top down, but from many overlapping centres of authority, each doing the part it can reach.

The honest verdict is sobering and unfinished. Global emissions have not yet decisively peaked; the gap between current pledges and any safe carbon budget remains wide; and the central structural defect — that there is no Leviathan to cap the planet and every nation has a private reason to free-ride — has not been solved, only chipped at. But the chipping is real. Carbon is priced across a quarter of the world and rising; the largest economies run emissions caps; the first carbon clubs are forming; and clean technology is winning on cost. The climate is the commons we are still failing to govern. It is also the one where the full toolbox is, finally, being deployed at once.

Field survey

The commons still open and unmanaged

The metaphor earns its keep in the field. What follows is a tour of live tragedies of the commons across very different domains — water, medicine, orbit, knowledge — chosen because each illustrates a different facet of the problem and a different state of the struggle to solve it. The fishery comes first, because it is the archetype and the most painful demonstration that the tragedy is not merely theoretical.

Domain one · the archetypeFisheries, and the death of the northern cod

For nearly five centuries the cod of the Grand Banks off Newfoundland were among the most abundant exploited fish on Earth — the foundation of the largest single-species fishery in the world and of the communities built around it. Then, over a few decades of industrial trawling that no authority effectively limited, the stock was fished toward commercial extinction. The spawning biomass of the northern cod fell by roughly ninety-three per cent — from about 1.6 million tonnes in the early 1960s to a few tens of thousands by the early 1990s.29 On 2 July 1992 the Canadian government declared a moratorium and closed the fishery, the single largest industrial layoff in the country’s history, throwing some thirty thousand people in Newfoundland and Labrador out of work overnight. Planners expected the closure to last two years; in the event it ran thirty-two, and was lifted only in 2024, when a cautious quota of 18,000 tonnes reopened a shadow of the fishery that had once fed a continent.30

The cod is the open-access tragedy in its purest and cruellest form, and its lesson rippled outward into a fierce scientific argument about how general the danger was. In 2006 Boris Worm and a large group of co-authors published an analysis whose most quoted line projected that, if existing trends continued, essentially all of the world’s fished seafood stocks would have collapsed by around the year 2048.31 The figure became a global headline — and a lightning rod. Critics, the fisheries scientist Trevor Branch prominent among them, argued that the projection extrapolated recklessly beyond the available data and that not all fisheries were on that path.32 The dispute was productive: it forced a reconciliation, and in 2009 Worm, his former critics, and others published a joint assessment showing that while a majority of assessed stocks were still depleted and in need of rebuilding, the decline was not destiny — where effective management had been applied, stocks were stabilising and recovering.33 The headline pessimism gave way to a more useful finding: the tragedy is real but reversible, and management is the variable that decides.

Which returns us to catch shares. The strong result of Costello’s eleven-thousand-fishery study 21 — that rights-based management measurably reduces the risk of collapse — is the optimistic half of the fisheries story. The cautious half is that the cure is conditional. Quotas only work where catch can actually be monitored; where illegal, unreported, and discarded fish escape the system, the race to fish simply continues underwater. And catch shares carry a distributional sting: tradable quota tends to consolidate into fewer, larger hands, squeezing out small-boat and Indigenous fishers and concentrating a once-common resource — a foreshadowing of a failure mode we will meet shortly, where too much private ownership creates its own kind of tragedy.

Domain two · the slow clockGroundwater and the race to the pumphouse

Beneath the visible world lies a commons with a peculiarly slow and silent clock. An aquifer tapped by many wells is a textbook common-pool resource: each owner pumps for private gain, while the cost — a falling water table that raises everyone’s pumping costs and can dry up shallower wells — is shared across all who draw from it. The incentive is the herder’s, with a twist of urgency: if your neighbour will pump the water anyway, you may as well pump it first. The race is to the pumphouse.

Two features make groundwater especially treacherous. It is invisible, so depletion is easy to ignore until wells fail; and some great aquifers — the fossil waters beneath parts of the High Plains, North Africa, and the Arabian Peninsula — recharge so slowly that pumping them is not harvesting but mining, a one-time withdrawal of water banked over geological time. Satellite gravimetry has made the scale of the problem newly visible: measurements of tiny changes in Earth’s gravity field reveal that many of the world’s major aquifers are being drawn down faster than nature refills them, a slow-motion global groundwater crisis hiding beneath the surface.34

The remedies are the familiar levers adapted to water: metering wells, defining and capping pumping rights, taxing extraction, and actively recharging aquifers in wet years to bank water for dry ones. California’s Sustainable Groundwater Management Act of 2014 is the landmark experiment — a state law that requires the most overdrafted basins to form local groundwater agencies and bring themselves into long-term balance. It is, in effect, mandated Ostrom: the state compels the creation of nested, local, self-governing institutions rather than dictating pumping limits from the capital. Whether it succeeds is still being decided, basin by contested basin, on a timescale of decades.

Domain three · depletion by evolutionAntibiotics, a resource we are using up

The effectiveness of an antibiotic is a shared resource, and an unusual one: it is depleted not by consumption but by evolution. Every dose given anywhere applies selective pressure that favours resistant bacteria, eroding the drug’s power for everyone, permanently. The benefit of use is private and immediate — a patient cured, an animal fattened — while the cost, a marginally more resistant pathogen population shared by all of humanity for all time, is the very definition of an externalised, diffuse harm. Small wonder that antimicrobial resistance is now routinely analysed as a textbook tragedy of the commons. The overuse flows from two taps: over-prescription in human medicine, including antibiotics handed out for viral infections they cannot touch, and, in even greater volume, the routine dosing of livestock for growth promotion and disease prevention.

The toll is moving from projection to measurement. The influential 2016 review chaired by the economist Jim O’Neill projected that, left unchecked, drug-resistant infections could cause as many as ten million deaths a year by 2050 — a figure that became a rallying cry and was also sharply contested as an extrapolation.35 The first comprehensive global assessment, the Global Research on Antimicrobial Resistance study led by Christopher Murray, put hard numbers on the present: in 2019, bacterial antimicrobial resistance was associated with an estimated 4.95 million deaths worldwide and was the direct cause of 1.27 million — already among the leading causes of death on the planet.36 Its 2024 successor forecast that more than 39 million people could die directly from resistant infections, cumulatively, between 2025 and 2050.37

The cures track the levers. Stewardship programmes ration use (community and regulatory limits); surveillance networks supply the monitoring; and economists have proposed pricing the externality directly — a tax on antibiotic use, so that the person demanding a needless prescription faces a cost reflecting the global effectiveness they help destroy, with the revenue funding conservation and new drugs.38 But antibiotics also reveal a cruel double structure, because they are simultaneously a commons being over-used and a public good being under-provided. The new antibiotics we most need are precisely the ones we would want to use as little as possible, to keep them in reserve — which makes them commercially worthless under a pay-per-dose model, and pharmaceutical firms have fled the field. The fix is a “pull” incentive that decouples reward from volume: subscription or “Netflix” payment models, pioneered in the United Kingdom, that pay a developer a fixed sum for access to a drug rather than per unit sold. Governing this commons means working both levers at once — restraining demand while subsidising supply.

Domain four · a commons we can still saveThe orbit, and the Kessler cascade

Five hundred kilometres above our heads lies a commons in the early, still-preventable stage of its tragedy. Low Earth orbit is a finite shared space, and it has its own named catastrophe. In 1978 the NASA scientists Donald Kessler and Burton Cour-Palais warned that as the density of objects in orbit rises, collisions between them throw off clouds of fragments, each of which raises the odds of the next collision — a self-sustaining chain reaction that could, past a critical density, wrap whole orbital bands in a shell of debris and render them unusable for generations.39 This is now called the Kessler syndrome, and its commons logic is exact: each operator captures the full benefit of launching another satellite while externalising the collision risk onto everyone who shares the orbit, so the privately rational strategy is to launch as much as possible before the commons degrades — the very behaviour that degrades it. Worse, debris does not clear quickly; the arithmetic of accumulation runs one way.

The pressure is climbing sharply, with mega-constellations adding thousands of satellites and launch rates at record highs. The mechanism is no longer hypothetical — the 2009 collision between a defunct Russian satellite and an active commercial one was the first major accidental smash-up in orbit — and the European Space Agency’s environment reporting shows debris continuing to accumulate despite mitigation guidelines.40 The remedies are the familiar levers, still nascent: mitigation rules requiring satellites to remove themselves after their working life (the long-standing twenty-five-year disposal guideline, recently tightened toward five years by the United States communications regulator); active debris removal to clean up what is already there; and, hardest of all, a binding international regime with real monitoring and enforcement — for orbit, like the climate and the high seas, has no Leviathan, only sovereign launchers who cannot be compelled. The orbit’s value is that it offers something the cod did not: the chance to govern a commons before it collapses rather than after.

Domain five · the model invertedKnowledge, and the comedy of the commons

The last domain overturns the whole framework, which is exactly why it is worth ending on. Information is non-rivalrous: my using a fact, a melody, or a line of code subtracts nothing from your use of it. For such resources the tragic arithmetic simply does not apply — and the legal scholar Carol Rose argued, in deliberate counterpoint to Hardin, that some shared resources display the opposite property, becoming more valuable the more people use them. She called it the comedy of the commons.41 A road, a public square, a spoken language, an open technical standard — and supremely a knowledge commons such as Wikipedia or open-source software — can be an “antirival” good, where each participant who contributes makes the resource better for all the rest. Wikipedia is grazed by hundreds of millions and only flourishes; the pasture that improves with use is no tragedy at all.

But the digital commons has bred new tragedies of its own, of three distinct kinds. The first is the opposite of Hardin’s: where too few owners cause overuse, too many owners cause underuse. Michael Heller named this the tragedy of the anticommons — a resource squandered because so many parties hold rights to exclude that assembling all the permissions becomes prohibitive.42 With Rebecca Eisenberg he showed it at work in biomedical research, where thickets of overlapping patents on fragments of genes and research tools can block the development of the very therapies they cover.43 The commons has a mirror image: too few gates and the resource is trampled; too many gates and it is frozen.

The second is the slow fencing-off of what was open. James Boyle warned of a second enclosure movement — the steady expansion of intellectual-property rights enclosing the public domain of ideas, much as the first enclosure fenced the pasture, with the same risk of locking away a shared inheritance for private gain.44 The knowledge commons is permanently contested ground between the impulse to open and the impulse to enclose.

The third is a true tragedy of the commons hiding inside the comedy: the problem of maintenance. Even an antirival resource needs upkeep, and upkeep is a classic under-provision problem. A startling share of modern digital infrastructure rests on open-source software maintained, often, by a handful of unpaid volunteers — a commons that almost everyone draws on and almost no one funds. Nadia Eghbal documented the resulting strain: burnout, abandonment, and structural fragility at the foundations of the digital world.45 The danger turned concrete in 2024, when a stealth backdoor was discovered, narrowly before it spread, buried in a compression library used across the internet — inserted by an attacker who had spent years patiently earning the trust of a single exhausted maintainer. When the commons of volunteer labour and trust is starved, it becomes a security risk for everyone who relies on it. The new public-goods funding mechanisms of the toolbox — assurance contracts, quadratic funding, retroactive funding — are aimed squarely at this gap. And a final, recursive twist now looms: the shared discourse of the public sphere is itself a kind of commons, and it is being polluted by spam, bots, and a rising tide of machine-generated content scraped from and flooding back into the very commons it draws upon — a well at growing risk of being fouled by those drinking from it.

Case · ozone

The great success, and what it teaches

It would be a counsel of despair to end the field survey on the climate’s unsolved struggle, because there is one global atmospheric commons that humanity has already, demonstrably, saved. In the 1980s scientists established that chlorofluorocarbons — the inert, useful chemicals then ubiquitous in refrigerators, air conditioners, and aerosol cans — were drifting into the stratosphere and destroying the ozone layer that shields all surface life from the Sun’s ultraviolet radiation. A literal hole had opened over Antarctica. Here was a textbook global commons, the shared ozone shield, being dismantled by the diffuse industrial externality of a few classes of chemical, with consequences — skin cancers, cataracts, crop and ecosystem damage — that would fall on everyone, everywhere.

The response was the Montreal Protocol of 1987, and it is the most successful environmental treaty ever written. Under it, the world has phased out roughly ninety-nine per cent of the controlled ozone-depleting substances, and the ozone layer is now firmly on track to heal: scientific assessment projects a return to 1980 levels around 2040 over most of the planet, around 2045 over the Arctic, and around 2066 over the Antarctic, where the damage was deepest.46 It is, moreover, the only United Nations treaty in history to achieve universal ratification — every country on Earth is a party.47 A 2016 extension, the Kigali Amendment, turned the same machinery against the hydrofluorocarbons that had replaced CFCs — chemicals harmless to ozone but potent as greenhouse gases — a move projected to avoid as much as 0.3 to 0.5 degrees Celsius of warming by 2100, making the ozone treaty quietly one of the most powerful climate instruments as well.46

Why it worked hereThe ingredients of a solvable commons

The natural question — and the painful one — is why Montreal succeeded where climate has so far failed. The political scientist Scott Barrett, who has studied what makes environmental treaties actually work, traces the success to a constellation of unusually favourable conditions, almost every one of which the climate problem lacks.48

There were few producers: ozone-depleting chemicals came from a handful of companies in a handful of countries, not from billions of tailpipes and furnaces, so the behaviour was easy to monitor and the key players easy to bring to the table. There were substitutes: workable replacements for CFCs existed or were rapidly developed, and — decisively — the very chemical companies that made the offending compounds could profit by selling the alternatives, which flipped industry from the treaty’s fiercest opponent into its ally. The science was vivid and frightening: an actual hole in the sky, with a tight, demonstrable causal chain, concentrated public and political will in a way that diffuse, gradual warming never quite has. The treaty had teeth in trade: it banned commerce in controlled substances between members and non-members, a built-in club mechanism — Nordhaus’s border-adjustment logic, decades avant la lettre — that made joining attractive and staying out costly. And it shared the burden: a Multilateral Fund through which wealthy nations helped pay for the transition in developing ones, securing the near-universal participation that gave the regime its force.

Montreal is the existence proof that a global commons can be governed — and a precise inventory of every ingredient the climate problem is missing.

This is why Montreal is both an inspiration and a caution. It refutes, definitively, the fatalism in Hardin’s essay: a planetary commons was identified, negotiated over, and saved, with the layer now visibly healing. But its conditions were the mirror image of the climate’s — few actors against billions, cheap substitutes against fossil fuels woven into the entire economy, a localised vivid threat against a global gradual one, an enforcement club that actually formed against a voluntary treaty that could not. The lesson is not that climate is hopeless but that the ozone template is the right one, and the work is to assemble its missing ingredients: the clubs, the substitutes (now arriving as cheap clean technology), the trade leverage (now emerging as border carbon adjustments), and the burden-sharing. Humanity has solved a global commons exactly once. The task of the century is to do it again, on a far harder problem, having learned how.

Prospect

Beyond Hardin

Half a century after the essay, the idea Hardin named has been transformed almost beyond recognition — not discarded, but corrected, deepened, and turned from a prophecy of doom into a programme of design. The historians have played their part. Working through centuries of archival records, scholars such as Tine De Moor have shown that the real common-pool institutions of the European past were not the heedless free-for-alls of Hardin’s imagination but elaborate, durable, rule-bound arrangements — many of which functioned for five or six hundred years, adapting through plague, war, and market upheaval.49 The commons has completed a remarkable journey, from a byword for inevitable ruin to a living model that an emerging movement now seeks to revive — in digital public goods, in community land trusts, in the municipal and data commons of the present.

If the survey above has a single moral, it is that four lessons now stand on firm ground.

The tragedy is a property of institutions, not of human nature. People are not condemned to defect. Whether they cooperate or destroy what they share is decided by the rules they live under, the monitoring they are subject to, and the relationships among them — by design, in other words, and design is something we can study, teach, and improve. This is the deepest reversal of Hardin: he located the tragedy in human nature and concluded it was inescapable; Ostrom located it in institutional arrangements and concluded it could be engineered away.

The market-versus-state binary was false. The twentieth century’s great debate — privatise or nationalise — left out the very thing the historical commons had been, a community governing itself, and in practice nearly every durable solution turns out to be a polycentric hybrid: local co-management nested inside a national market nested inside a regulatory frame nested inside an international agreement. The right question is never “market or state?” but “which combination, at which scale, for this particular resource and these particular users?”

Context defeats panaceas. There is no master instrument. Catch shares that rescue one fishery wreck the social fabric of another; a carbon tax that suits one economy is politically impossible in a second; a self-governing arrangement that thrives among a hundred neighbours collapses among a hundred thousand strangers. The work is diagnosis before prescription — Ostrom’s enduring methodological gift.

Scale is the unsolved frontier. The conditions for governing a local commons are now reasonably well understood; the conditions for governing a global one are not. Every success in this article at planetary scale — really only the ozone layer — depended on conditions that cannot be summoned at will. The defining drama of the coming century is whether the lessons learned on the village green can be scaled up to the atmosphere, the ocean, and the orbit, in the time available.

The question is no longer whether humans can govern what they share. The village greens, the lobster fisheries, and the healing ozone layer have answered that. The question is whether we can build the institutions fast enough, and large enough, to reach the commons we have left.

What is changing in our favourThe levers of the next fifty years

Several developments now bend the odds, and they are worth naming because they are where the next solutions will come from. The most important by far is the collapse in the cost of monitoring. Ostrom’s binding constraint was always observation — you cannot ration what you cannot see — and satellites, cheap sensors, and machine analysis are dissolving it. For the first time in history, exclusion and accountability are becoming technically feasible at planetary scale: a fishing fleet on the open ocean, a forest’s edge, a methane plume, an aquifer’s level, all can now be watched in something close to real time. If any single trend justifies optimism about the global commons, it is this one.

Alongside it sits a maturing craft of mechanism design — quadratic funding, retroactive public-goods funding, dominant-assurance contracts — that engineers incentive structures to defeat free-riding rather than merely exhorting people to be good; and the spread of minilateral clubs as the realistic route to governing global commons where universal treaties stall, with carbon border adjustments giving the climate effort, at last, the kind of trade leverage that made Montreal work. The knowledge commons, meanwhile, will be defined by an ongoing contest between the impulse to enclose and the impulse to keep open, a contest whose outcome shapes how freely the digital century’s most valuable resource — information itself — can circulate.

And there is a final resonance worth ending on. Artificial intelligence is at once the sharpest new tool for governing commons — the engine that reads the satellite feeds, models the fish stocks, prices the carbon, and watches the forest — and the source of fresh commons dilemmas of its own. The shared pool of high-quality human-created data on which these systems train is being depleted and polluted, partly by their own output flooding back into it. Computing power is a contested resource. The public information sphere strains under machine-generated noise. And the development of the technology itself has the structure of a commons of caution, in which each builder may face an incentive to move faster and externalise the risk onto everyone — the oldest dynamic in this article, wearing the newest face. The tragedy of the commons, it turns out, is not a relic of the medieval pasture. It is the recurring shape of every problem in which what is good for each is ruinous for all — and learning to govern it, resource by resource and scale by scale, may be the most important institutional skill our species ever acquires.

On the commons

This essay follows a single idea — that a resource belonging to everyone is too easily cared for by no one — from the sheep pasture of Hardin’s thought experiment to the atmosphere, the ocean, low Earth orbit, and the open-source libraries beneath the internet. Its argument, owed above all to Elinor Ostrom, is that the tragedy is not written into human nature but into the rules a community adopts, and that designing better rules is an engineering problem as much as a moral one.
