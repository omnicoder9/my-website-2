What Is a Red Teamer?

In military doctrine, "red team" denotes an independent group tasked with thinking and acting like an adversary — probing plans, assumptions, and defenses for weaknesses that friendly forces cannot or will not see.1 Information security adopted the term and the logic: a red teamer is a security professional who simulates realistic, goal-oriented attacks against an organization's people, processes, and technology to determine whether existing detection and response capabilities would actually work when it counts.2

The defining characteristic of the red team role — what separates it from related disciplines — is its full-scope adversary emulation mandate. Where a penetration tester is typically engaged to find and document vulnerabilities within a bounded scope, the red team is engaged to behave like a specific threat actor: pursuing objectives, evading detection, maintaining persistence, and pivoting through the environment until an objective is achieved or the engagement is terminated.3

The NIST Cybersecurity Framework describes red team exercises as a mechanism for organizations to "identify weaknesses in [their] cybersecurity posture" through "authorized, simulated attacks."4 The US Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA) formally defines red team assessments as exercises that test "an organization's detection and response policies, procedures, and technologies."5

History & Innovation

Structured adversarial red teaming originated in Cold War-era defense analysis. The RAND Corporation and the US Air Force developed red team methodologies in the 1960s to stress-test war plans and intelligence estimates against a simulated Soviet adversary.8 The National Security Agency (NSA) began applying analogous logic to computer security in the late 1970s and early 1980s, commissioning "tiger teams" to attempt unauthorized entry to government systems.9

The 1988 Morris Worm demonstrated for a broad audience that networked systems were unexpectedly brittle, accelerating institutional interest in proactive adversarial testing.10 During the 1990s the US Department of Defense formalized red team evaluations and issued the first directives mandating penetration testing for certain categories of federal systems.11

The early 2000s saw the emergence of structured red team programs in the financial sector, driven by payment card breaches and early compliance regimes (PCI DSS, published in 2004).12 The field matured considerably after 2013, when the publication of Mandiant's APT1 report demonstrated that sophisticated nation-state adversary behavior could be systematically characterized — and therefore systematically emulated.13

The most significant structural innovation of the last decade is the shift from generic penetration testing to threat-intelligence-led red teaming: engagements designed around the specific tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs) of threat actors known or suspected to target the client organization. Frameworks including MITRE ATT&CK (released publicly in 2015), TIBER-EU (2018), and CBEST (2013) operationalized this model at scale.14,15,16

Defining Innovation: MITRE ATT&CK

MITRE ATT&CK (Adversarial Tactics, Techniques & Common Knowledge) is a globally accessible knowledge base of adversary behavior, organized by tactic categories (Initial Access, Execution, Persistence, Privilege Escalation, Defense Evasion, Credential Access, Discovery, Lateral Movement, Collection, Command and Control, Exfiltration, Impact) and populated with documented real-world techniques.14 It has become the de facto lingua franca of red teaming, enabling red team operators to anchor their TTPs to empirically observed threat actor behaviors and allowing blue teams to measure detection coverage against a common taxonomy.

Qualifications

Formal Education

No single academic pathway is normative. Employers commonly seek degrees in computer science, information systems, cybersecurity, electrical engineering, or related STEM fields, though many practitioners enter through self-study, bootcamps, or military/government service.18 Graduate education (MS in Cybersecurity or Information Assurance) is increasingly common for senior and leadership roles, particularly in regulated industries and government positions requiring security clearances.19

Prior Experience

Entry-level red team roles are uncommon. Employers typically expect three to five years of prior experience in penetration testing, vulnerability assessment, network engineering, or security operations before a candidate is considered for a dedicated red team function.18 Military or intelligence community backgrounds — particularly in signals intelligence or cyber operations — are highly valued and frequently accepted in lieu of commercial experience for roles requiring clearances.17

Bug bounty participation, capture-the-flag (CTF) competition performance, and disclosed public research (CVEs, conference presentations at DEF CON or Black Hat) function as supplementary credentialing signals, particularly for candidates from non-traditional backgrounds.27

Responsibilities & Challenges

Core Responsibilities

Pre-Engagement Planning

Red teamers develop rules of engagement (ROE), define threat scenarios grounded in threat intelligence, and coordinate deconfliction procedures with the security operations center (SOC) or with a separate "white team" that manages engagement oversight. Scoping decisions — what systems are in-scope, what techniques are authorized, what physical or legal boundaries apply — are documented and agreed upon prior to any technical work.3

Threat Intelligence Integration

Modern red team engagements begin with intelligence gathering on the threat actors most likely to target the client. This involves reviewing finished intelligence products, OSINT, industry threat sharing, and commercial feeds to construct a threat profile. The selected threat actor's documented TTPs are then mapped to the MITRE ATT&CK framework to guide technique selection throughout the campaign.14

Technical Execution

Red team operators execute multi-phase campaigns spanning initial access, post-exploitation, lateral movement, and objective attainment. Unlike penetration testers, red team operators are expected to evade detection throughout — the goal is not to trigger alerts, but to remain undetected long enough to demonstrate what a real adversary could accomplish. Techniques span phishing, physical social engineering, external network exploitation, web application attacks, credential harvesting, internal network pivoting, Active Directory attacks, and data exfiltration simulation.28

Reporting & Debrief

Red team operators produce detailed reports characterizing the attack narrative chronologically, mapping each technique to ATT&CK, documenting evidence of detection (or absence thereof), and providing risk-graded findings with remediation guidance. Crucially, red team reports address not only what was found but what the blue team failed to detect — a fundamentally different emphasis than a standard vulnerability assessment.3

Key Challenges

Detection Evasion at Scale

Endpoint detection and response (EDR) platforms have substantially raised the technical bar for red team operators. Tools and techniques that were effective five years ago are frequently signature-detected today, requiring operators to develop custom tooling, in-memory execution techniques, and living-off-the-land (LotL) approaches that abuse legitimate system binaries and APIs to avoid behavioral detection.29

Attribution Risk

Red team operators work under explicit authorization, but the potential for a blue team or SOC analyst to mistake an operator's activity for a real intrusion — triggering IR procedures, legal escalation, or law enforcement contact — is a real operational and legal risk. Robust deconfliction protocols and documented authorization chains are essential safeguards.30

Keeping Pace with the Adversary

Threat actors evolve continuously. A red team that emulates TTPs from two years ago provides diminishing assurance. Operators must sustain ongoing technical development — studying newly disclosed vulnerabilities, reversing malware samples, reading adversary reports — to remain credible threat emulators.28

Measuring Impact

Demonstrating the value of red teaming to non-technical leadership is an enduring challenge. Translating technical findings into business risk language — and distinguishing the value of red teaming from that of penetration testing or vulnerability scanning — requires both communication skill and a defensible measurement framework.31

Processes & Methodologies

Red team engagements follow structured methodologies that sequence activities and ensure consistency, repeatability, and legal defensibility. Several frameworks govern this practice:

The Red Team Engagement Lifecycle

  1. 01
    Threat Intelligence & Scenario Development

    Identify likely threat actors using finished intelligence and OSINT. Construct a threat scenario grounded in the actor's documented objectives and TTPs. Map to MITRE ATT&CK. Define engagement objectives (e.g., access crown-jewel data, achieve domain compromise, exfiltrate PII without detection).

  2. 02
    Rules of Engagement & Authorization

    Document scope, out-of-scope systems, authorized techniques, authorized hours of operation, emergency stop procedures, deconfliction contacts, and legal authorization. All parties — client, red team, and white team — sign off before engagement begins.3

  3. 03
    Reconnaissance

    Passive and active intelligence gathering on the target: DNS enumeration, ASN and IP space mapping, employee OSINT (LinkedIn, breach databases), technology fingerprinting, and physical site reconnaissance where in-scope. The goal is to construct an accurate target model before any exploitation attempt.

  4. 04
    Initial Access

    Establish a foothold via the chosen attack vector(s): spear-phishing, credential stuffing, exploitation of externally facing services, vishing, physical access, or supply chain vectors. Initial access techniques are selected to match the emulated threat actor's known methods.14

  5. 05
    Post-Exploitation, Persistence & Lateral Movement

    Once a foothold is established, operators execute post-exploitation: privilege escalation, credential harvesting, internal discovery, and lateral movement toward the engagement objective. Persistence mechanisms are installed to simulate long-dwell adversary presence. All actions are logged for reporting.

  6. 06
    Objective Attainment & Exfiltration Simulation

    The operator achieves the predefined objective — accessing a target system, retrieving simulated sensitive data, demonstrating the ability to disrupt an operational process — and documents evidence. Exfiltration of real data is typically avoided; operators use test artifacts or hash values as proof of access.

  7. 07
    Cleanup & Debrief

    Persistence mechanisms, implants, and test accounts created during the engagement are removed. A technical debrief with the blue team ("purple team" session) walks through the attack timeline, each detection opportunity, and whether each step was detected. Findings are then documented in a formal report.

Formal Frameworks

PTES — Penetration Testing Execution Standard

PTES is a community-developed standard that defines seven phases of penetration testing engagement, from pre-engagement to reporting. While designed for penetration testing broadly, it is widely used as a structural baseline by red teams.32

TIBER-EU

Threat Intelligence-Based Ethical Red-teaming for the European Union. Developed by the European Central Bank, TIBER-EU is a framework for regulators and central banks to oversee intelligence-led red team exercises against critical financial infrastructure. It mandates a separation between a Threat Intelligence Provider and a Red Team Provider to ensure independence.15

CBEST

A UK framework developed by the Bank of England and CREST, analogous to TIBER-EU, for intelligence-led penetration testing of UK financial sector firms. CBEST-accredited providers must meet CREST quality standards and work within a structured oversight model.16

DETT&CT

Detection Tactics, Techniques & Combat (DeTT&CT) is a methodology and toolset for mapping an organization's detection coverage against MITRE ATT&CK, enabling data-driven red team planning and blue team gap analysis.33

Domain & Industry-Specific Variations

Financial Services

The financial sector has the most mature regulatory red team ecosystem. CBEST (UK), TIBER-EU (European Union), TIBER-NL, iCAST (US OCC), and the NY DFS cybersecurity regulation all mandate or strongly incentivize intelligence-led red team assessments for systemically important firms.15,16,36 Financial red team engagements frequently target SWIFT messaging infrastructure, core banking platforms, and payment processing rails, with particular emphasis on business continuity and fraud scenario simulation.

Healthcare

Healthcare red teams must navigate HIPAA authorization requirements and extreme sensitivity around operational disruption — a red team action that inadvertently affects clinical systems could endanger patients. Engagements frequently focus on medical device security, EHR platform access, and ransomware resilience (simulating the Ryuk and BlackCat/ALPHV attack patterns documented in numerous healthcare incidents).37

Critical Infrastructure & OT/ICS

Red team operations against operational technology (OT), industrial control systems (ICS), and SCADA environments require specialized safety training and a completely different attack surface knowledge base. Disruption of physical processes can have immediate safety consequences. Frameworks including ICS-CERT guidance and ISA/IEC 62443 govern authorized testing of these environments.38 Key tooling includes PLCpwn, GRASSMARLIN, and Claroty's platform.

Federal Government & Defense

US federal red team engagements are governed by NIST SP 800-115, NIST SP 800-53 CA-8, and associated FedRAMP requirements. DoD red team activities fall under USCYBERCOM and NSA/CSS frameworks, with classified variants. Personnel require appropriate security clearances, and foreign nationals face significant restrictions.4,39

Cloud Environments

Cloud red teaming requires understanding of shared-responsibility models, cloud-native attack paths, and provider-specific authorization requirements. AWS, Azure, and GCP each publish penetration testing policies specifying what activities are permitted without prior notification and which require written approval.40 Cloud-specific tooling includes Pacu (AWS), AzureAD PowerShell attack modules, and ScoutSuite.

Sub-Specialties

Physical Red Teaming

Physical security assessment specialists test access controls, badge systems, tailgating vulnerabilities, and the interface between physical and logical security. This includes lock picking, RFID cloning, surveillance system analysis, and social engineering of on-site staff. Physical red teamers frequently operate alongside technical operators to simulate realistic blended intrusions.41

Social Engineering Specialist

Specialists in human-targeted attack vectors: phishing campaign design, vishing (voice-based phishing), pretexting, and in-person impersonation. These operators may hold psychology or communications backgrounds alongside technical credentials. The discipline is increasingly important as adversaries de-emphasize technical exploitation in favor of credential theft and business email compromise.6

Exploit Developer / Vulnerability Researcher

A subset of red team operators focus on developing novel exploits for use in engagements — zero-day research, weaponizing recently disclosed CVEs, and developing custom shellcode or payloads that bypass current EDR signatures. This sub-specialty demands deep knowledge of assembly language, memory management, operating system internals, and software reverse engineering.23

AI/ML Red Teaming

An emerging sub-specialty focused on adversarial evaluation of artificial intelligence and machine learning systems: prompt injection attacks, adversarial input crafting, model inversion, data poisoning, and jailbreak research. The White House Executive Order on Safe, Secure, and Trustworthy AI (2023) formally recognized AI red teaming as a national security practice, and Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, and Microsoft have all developed internal AI red team functions.42

Cloud & Container Security

Specialists focusing on cloud-native attack paths: IAM privilege escalation, S3 bucket misconfiguration exploitation, container escape, Kubernetes cluster compromise, and serverless function abuse. This sub-specialty is among the fastest-growing given the broad organizational migration to cloud infrastructure.40

Closely Related Roles

Penetration Tester

The most adjacent role. Penetration testers perform time-bounded, scoped assessments to identify and document exploitable vulnerabilities. Unlike red teamers, they are typically not tasked with stealth or adversary emulation — finding vulnerabilities is the goal, not demonstrating what goes undetected. Many practitioners hold both roles depending on engagement type.3

Blue Teamer / SOC Analyst

The organizational counterpart to red teamers: defensive security professionals responsible for monitoring, detecting, analyzing, and responding to threats. Red team outputs are fundamentally evaluated against blue team performance. Understanding blue team operations — SIEM logic, alert thresholds, IR playbooks — is essential for effective red team planning.43

Purple Teamer

Purple teaming is a collaborative model in which red and blue team practitioners work simultaneously — red team operators execute techniques while blue team analysts attempt detection in real time. The purple teamer role facilitates this collaboration, often using automated simulation tooling (Atomic Red Team, CALDERA) to run ATT&CK technique libraries systematically. This role has grown significantly as organizations recognize the learning value of transparent red team operations.44

Threat Intelligence Analyst

Threat intelligence analysts produce the actor profiles, TTP documentation, and campaign reports that red teams use to select and justify their engagement scenarios. In TIBER-EU and CBEST-regulated engagements, the Threat Intelligence Provider role is formally separated from the red team provider to ensure independence.15

Vulnerability Researcher

Vulnerability researchers discover previously unknown security flaws in software and hardware through code review, fuzzing, and reverse engineering. Their work produces the CVEs and exploits that red teams may weaponize. Some red team operators conduct vulnerability research; others consume it as an input.27

Incident Responder

Incident responders are activated when a real (or suspected) intrusion occurs. Red team reports are a primary input for building IR playbooks — the scenarios red teams demonstrate are the scenarios IR teams must be prepared to handle. In some organizations, red teamers and IR practitioners deliberately cross-train to improve both functions.43
