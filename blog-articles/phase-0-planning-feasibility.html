PHASE 0 Planning & Feasibility Analysis · The Software Development Life Cycle

The most consequential decisions are made when you know the least

Every software project opens with a paradox. The choices that most tightly bind cost, schedule and outcome — whether to build at all, what problem to solve, which architecture to commit to — are made at the moment of maximum ignorance, before a single requirement is validated or a line of code is written. Phase 0 is the discipline of making those choices deliberately rather than by default.

Section 01

The asymmetry of the front end

Software engineering is unusual among engineering disciplines in how cheaply it lets you begin and how expensively it lets you be wrong. A bridge cannot be a quarter poured before anyone notices the span was mis-specified; the physical world imposes its own gates. Software imposes none. A team can spend eighteen months and tens of millions of dollars building, with great craft, precisely the wrong system — and discover it only when the finished artifact meets the world it was meant to serve. Phase 0, the planning and feasibility phase that precedes the project proper, exists to make that discovery early, on paper, when it is still nearly free.

The economic case for taking the front end seriously rests on a single, well-documented curve: the cost of correcting a defect rises steeply the later it is caught. In the data Barry Boehm assembled for Software Engineering Economics, an error introduced in requirements but not corrected until operation could cost on the order of a hundred times what it would have cost to fix at the point of introduction.1 Later work refined the picture — the escalation is real, it is large, and it compounds across phases — while attributing much of the leverage specifically to decisions made before construction.1,2 It is worth being precise about the evidence here, because the tidy "1:10:100" ratio is often repeated with more confidence than its provenance warrants; careful re-examination shows the multiplier is highly context-dependent and the original datasets smaller and more heterogeneous than the folklore implies.3 The defensible claim is not a magic number but a direction and a shape: requirements and feasibility defects are the most expensive class of defect precisely because they are structural, and Phase 0 is where they are cheapest to prevent.

The second structural fact of the front end is uncertainty, and its characteristic geometry is the cone shown above. At the outset of a project the ratio of any honest estimate to the eventual actual — of effort, cost, duration, scope — spans a wide band, and that band narrows only as the project does the work that resolves the underlying unknowns.1 Steve McConnell gave the shape its enduring name, the Cone of Uncertainty, and its enduring warning: the cone represents the best case attainable accuracy at each stage, not a guarantee. A team that mistakes an early point estimate for a commitment has not narrowed the cone; it has merely stopped looking at it.4 Phase 0 does not eliminate this uncertainty — nothing can, that early — but it does two things that matter enormously: it makes the uncertainty explicit and quantified, and it identifies which unknowns are cheap to retire now, on paper, versus which will only resolve through building.

The stakes are not hypothetical. The industry's long history of expensive failure is, on close reading, largely a history of front-end failure — of problems mis-defined, feasibility unexamined, and estimates that were commitments in disguise. Robert Charette's survey of software project failures catalogues a recurring cast of causes, and the leading ones are overwhelmingly Phase 0 concerns: unrealistic or unarticulated goals, poor requirements, badly estimated resources, and sponsor commitment secured before feasibility was established.5 The most widely cited attempt to quantify the pattern, the Standish Group's CHAOS research, reported in its foundational 1994 study that only about one project in six was delivered on time, on budget and with the intended scope; a majority were "challenged," overrunning by an average approaching 190 percent, and roughly a third were cancelled outright.6

Those figures deserve an expert's skepticism as well as an expert's attention. The CHAOS numbers have been influential precisely because they are alarming, but Magne Jørgensen and Kjetil Moløkken-Østvold showed that the study's methodology — its definition of "success," its opaque sampling, its conflation of estimation error with project failure — systematically overstates the problem, and that a project labelled a cost "overrun" is frequently a project whose estimate was wrong rather than whose execution was.7 Larger and more rigorously sampled studies find failure common but not epidemic, and confirm the single most robust predictor of trouble: size. Big projects fail far more often than small ones, and they fail in ways that trace directly to decisions taken at the front end.8 Read together, the empirical literature does not say that software is doomed; it says that the front end is where the largest, most avoidable losses are decided — which is exactly the argument for Phase 0.

This article treats Phase 0 as three tightly coupled activities. First, problem definition: establishing what business need actually exists, independent of any proposed solution. Second, the feasibility study: a structured, multi-dimensional interrogation of whether a solution is worth pursuing — technically, economically and operationally. Third, resource estimation: producing honest, range-based projections of time, cost and personnel, and understanding why the honest version is so hard to produce and so easy to corrupt. The three culminate in a single deliverable that is also a decision: the business case, and the go / no-go gate it feeds. We will treat each in turn, and ground each in the historical record of what happens when it is skipped.

Section 02

A short history of the front end

The idea that software should be preceded by an explicit planning and feasibility phase is not native to programming; it was imported, somewhat awkwardly, from established engineering and systems disciplines as software projects grew past the scale a single mind could hold. The canonical origin point in the software literature is Winston Royce's 1970 paper on managing the development of large systems — the paper universally, and unfairly, remembered as the birth of the "waterfall" model.9 Royce did draw the now-infamous cascade of sequential phases, beginning with system and software requirements. But his actual argument was that the naive sequential model is risky, and that it must be augmented with prototyping, iteration and early analysis to be workable at all. The discipline the paper actually advocated — do the hard thinking up front, prototype to retire risk, expect to revisit — is closer to modern practice than the strawman that bears its name.

What Royce assumed, and what the surrounding systems-analysis tradition of the 1960s and 1970s made explicit, was the presence of a feasibility study before commitment. The mainframe-era systems development life cycle placed feasibility as its first formal stage: a structured assessment, delivered to a steering committee, of whether a proposed system was justified before analysts were assigned to specify it. That governance instinct — a gate before the spend — is the direct ancestor of Phase 0. It was codified over subsequent decades into international standards. The framework now published as ISO/IEC/IEEE 12207 defines the software life-cycle processes an organization draws on, explicitly separating the agreement and project processes — the deciding and the planning — from the technical processes of building.10 The standard's lineage runs from its first edition in the mid-1990s through repeated revisions; the essential point for our purposes is that "should we, and can we" was elevated to a named, auditable set of activities distinct from "how."

Boehm's contribution, beginning with Software Engineering Economics in 1981, was to make the front end quantitative. By treating software development as an economic activity subject to estimation, trade-off and diminishing returns, he gave planning a mathematics: cost models, the value of information, the economics of prototyping, and the risk-driven reasoning that would later crystallize as the spiral model.1 The intellectual arc from Royce to Boehm is a movement from "plan because it is prudent" to "plan because the numbers say the leverage is here." Everything in this article is downstream of that shift.

Why "Phase 0"

The numbering is deliberate. Requirements, design, implementation and testing are conventionally numbered as the phases of a project — but a project only exists once someone has decided it should. The planning and feasibility work happens before the clock starts, before there is a budget line, a team or a charter to overrun. Calling it Phase 0 marks it as the phase that decides whether the other phases occur at all. It is also, not coincidentally, the phase most often compressed to nothing under schedule pressure, precisely because it produces no code and its value is the invisible value of disasters averted.

A final piece of vocabulary belongs to the modern era. Product and design practice speaks of the "fuzzy front end" — the ambiguous, pre-project space where opportunities are still being framed and no one yet agrees on the problem. The term is apt. The front end is fuzzy not because practitioners are careless but because the work of the front end is the work of removing fuzz: converting a vague sense that something is wrong or possible into a defined problem, a bounded solution space, and a defensible estimate. The rest of this article is a tour of the instruments for doing that conversion well — and a gallery of what the fuzz costs when it is left in place.

Section 03

Problem definition: what business need actually exists

The most expensive error in software is not a bug. It is building the wrong thing well. Problem definition is the activity that guards against it, and it is the single most under-practised part of the front end — because it feels like delay, it produces no artifact anyone can demo, and it requires the discipline to resist the most seductive move in engineering: jumping to a solution.

3.1The solutioning reflex

Requests for software almost never arrive as problems. They arrive as solutions. "We need a mobile app," "we need to migrate to microservices," "we need a dashboard" — each is a proposed answer to a question no one has yet stated. The discipline of problem definition is to walk the request backwards to the need it presumes. Why an app? To reach field staff. Why reach them? Because data entered hours late is causing stockouts. What is the actual problem? Latency between an event in the field and its visibility to planners. Stated that way, the solution space suddenly includes options — an SMS gateway, a change to the existing web form's offline behaviour, a process change — that the original "mobile app" framing had silently excluded, several of which may be an order of magnitude cheaper.

The formal name for getting this wrong is the error of the third kind: not rejecting a true hypothesis (Type I) or accepting a false one (Type II), but solving the wrong problem precisely. Ian Mitroff and Thomas Featheringham named it in 1974, observing that the most consequential errors in systems work are errors of formulation — that "one of the most important determinants of a problem's solution is how that problem has been represented or formulated in the first place."11 A perfectly engineered answer to a mis-framed question is not a partial success; it is a total loss dressed as an achievement, and it consumes the budget that the right question needed.

3.2From symptom to root cause

Problem definition therefore begins as a diagnostic exercise, and it borrows its tools from other diagnostic disciplines. The five whys — iteratively asking why a stated problem occurs until a root cause rather than a symptom is reached — is crude but remarkably effective at exposing the gap between presenting complaint and underlying condition. Ishikawa (fishbone) diagrams organize candidate causes into categories so that a team argues about a structured space rather than a random one. The goal in every case is the same: to ensure that the problem the project will spend money solving is a cause and not a consequence, because building software to manage a symptom leaves the generator of the symptom untouched and productive.

A complementary lens, drawn from product strategy, is to ask what job a user is trying to get done — the outcome they are hiring any solution to achieve, independent of the solution's form. Framing needs as jobs and desired outcomes rather than as features keeps the problem statement solution-agnostic for as long as possible, which is exactly as long as possible before the cone of uncertainty forces commitment. The longer the solution space stays open, the more likely the cheapest adequate solution is still in it.

Needs versus requirements

A need is a condition in the world that the stakeholder wants changed. A requirement is a property a system must have to change it. Problem definition operates at the level of needs; requirements engineering, the phase that follows Phase 0, translates needs into specifications. Collapsing the two — writing requirements before the need is agreed — is how projects end up with meticulously specified systems that satisfy every requirement and no actual need. The requirements were never wrong; they were answers to the wrong question.

3.3Stakeholders and the problem statement

Whose problem is it? The question is not rhetorical. Every non-trivial system serves multiple stakeholders whose needs conflict, and a problem defined from only one vantage point will be feasible for that party and unworkable for the others. Identifying the full stakeholder set — sponsors, end users, operators, regulators, the people whose workflow the system will disrupt — and surfacing where their interests diverge is a core Phase 0 deliverable, because an unrepresented stakeholder is a feasibility risk that has not yet been costed. We will see this failure mode in its purest form later in this article, when a dispatch system was specified without consulting the dispatchers, and an airport baggage system without consulting the airlines.

The output of the activity is a problem statement: a short, solution-free articulation of the need, its affected stakeholders, its magnitude, and the consequence of not addressing it. From it, two governing artifacts are assembled. The business case sets out the justification — the need, the options considered, the expected costs and benefits, and the recommendation — and is the document the feasibility study exists to populate. The project charter, in the vocabulary of the project-management body of knowledge, formally authorizes the project and names its sponsor and high-level constraints once the case is accepted.12 These are not bureaucratic overhead. They are the written record of what was decided and why, which is the only thing that lets an organization later distinguish a project that failed from a project that succeeded at the wrong goal.

3.4When there is no blueprint

The consequences of skipping this work are visible in one of the most thoroughly documented failures in the history of government computing: the FBI's Virtual Case File.

Case · Problem definition
The FBI Virtual Case File
2000–2005 · Washington, D.C.

In the wake of a mandate to modernize its paper-bound case management, the FBI launched Virtual Case File as the software centrepiece of its larger Trilogy program, approved in 2000 at roughly $380 million over three years. The Bureau knew it needed to replace an obsolete system. What it had not done was the patient work of defining, in enterprise terms, what the new system had to be — there was no agreed blueprint, no enterprise architecture, and requirements that shifted continuously as the project ran.13

The Department of Justice's Inspector General, auditing the program, identified the front-end failures directly: poorly defined and evolving requirements, an absence of the architectural and design discipline needed to manage a system of that scale, and a plan to cut over to the new system in a single step with no phased fallback.14 By the time an independent technical review examined the delivered software, it concluded the product was so incomplete and poorly structured as to be effectively unusable. The Bureau abandoned Virtual Case File in 2005 after spending roughly $170 million, of which on the order of $105 million bought code that was thrown away.15

The lesson is not that the FBI chose bad programmers. It is that no amount of programming skill can rescue a project whose problem was never defined at the level the problem actually lived — the enterprise. The replacement, Sentinel, eventually delivered, but years late and hundreds of millions over the original Trilogy envelope: the price of doing the front-end work second.

Every downstream difficulty — the churn, the unusable delivery, the impossible cutover — grew from the front-end omission of a defined problem and an architecture to match it. It is the most expensive way there is to learn that Phase 0 is not optional; it is merely deferrable, at compounding interest.

Section 04

The feasibility study

Once a problem is defined, the feasibility study asks the deceptively simple question that governs everything downstream: is a solution to this problem worth building, and can this organization build it? "Worth" and "can" are not one question but several, and the discipline of feasibility is to refuse to let a strength on one axis paper over a fatal weakness on another.

The classic teaching framework decomposes feasibility into a small set of dimensions, often taught under the mnemonic TELOS — technical, economic, legal, operational and schedule feasibility.16 Different traditions draw the boundaries slightly differently; the software engineering body of knowledge folds these considerations into project initiation and the management of risk, scope and resources rather than a fixed acronym.17 The specific taxonomy matters less than the discipline it enforces: examine the proposal from genuinely independent angles, because the angles fail independently. A venture can be technically elegant and economically ruinous; economically compelling and operationally rejected by the people meant to use it; both, and illegal. This article treats the three dimensions that dominate software decisions — technical, economic and operational — in depth, and folds legal and schedule considerations into them where they bite.

The crucial structural property of the feasibility study is that it behaves like a logical AND, not a weighted average. A high enough technical score does not buy back a failing operational score; a project must clear every dimension to proceed. This is the essential difference between a feasibility gate and a business pitch. A pitch accentuates strengths; a gate hunts for the disqualifying weakness, because the disqualifying weakness is what turns a spend into a loss.

Two failure modes bracket the feasibility study and are worth naming before we examine the dimensions. The first is the rubber-stamp: a feasibility study commissioned after the decision to proceed has already been made politically, whose function is to manufacture justification rather than to test the proposition. Such a study is not merely useless; it is actively dangerous, because it launders an unexamined commitment as a validated one and disables the gate precisely when it is needed. The second is analysis paralysis: treating feasibility as a demand for certainty it cannot provide, and refusing to proceed until every unknown is retired — which, at the wide mouth of the cone, means never. A good feasibility study threads between them. It resolves the unknowns that are cheap to resolve on paper, quantifies the residual risk on the ones that are not, and makes an explicit, reviewable recommendation under acknowledged uncertainty. The dimensions that follow are the instruments for doing exactly that.

Section 05

Technical feasibility: do we have the stack?

Technical feasibility asks whether the proposed solution can be built with available technology, skills and time — and, just as importantly, whether the parts of it that are genuinely novel have been separated from the parts that are routine. Most technical-feasibility failures are not failures to recognize that something is hard. They are failures to recognize that something is unprecedented, and to price the difference.

5.1Capability, not just possibility

The question is rarely "is this possible in principle?" — almost anything is. The useful questions are sharper. Does the team possess, or can it acquire in time, the specific skills the architecture demands? Do the chosen technologies exist at the maturity level the project needs, or are they research-grade dressed as production-grade? Where the system must integrate with existing systems, how large and how well-understood is that integration surface? Each is a question about this organization's capability to build this thing on this timeline, not about the abstract state of the art. A technology can be entirely mature in the industry and entirely infeasible for a team that has never used it and cannot hire for it before the deadline.

A disciplined technical feasibility assessment therefore inventories the solution's components and rates each on a readiness scale — borrowed, in spirit, from the technology-readiness levels used in aerospace — distinguishing components that are proven and understood from those that are novel, immature, or unproven at the required scale. The novel components are where estimates are least reliable and where the cone is widest, so they are exactly the components that warrant retiring risk early through a spike or proof of concept: a small, time-boxed build whose only purpose is to convert a critical unknown into a known before the full commitment is made. The economics are those of the value of information — spend a little now to buy a fact that de-risks a large later spend.

5.2Build, buy, or assemble

Technical feasibility is inseparable from the build-versus-buy decision. For most needs, some combination of commercial software, open-source components and cloud services can supply capability that would be ruinous to build from scratch, and a feasibility study that assumes a bespoke build without examining the alternatives has already failed one of its jobs. The countervailing risks — integration complexity, vendor lock-in, the loss of control over a core differentiator, licensing cost at scale — belong in the same assessment. The point is not that buying is always right, but that the make/buy/assemble question is a technical-feasibility question and must be answered explicitly rather than defaulted.

5.3Unproven technology at unprecedented scale

The definitive cautionary tale of technical feasibility is Denver International Airport's automated baggage system — a project in which each individual technology was plausible and the combination, at the scale and complexity demanded, was not.

Case · Technical feasibility
The Denver Airport baggage system
1991–1995 · Denver, Colorado

Denver set out to build the most advanced automated baggage-handling system ever attempted: a network of thousands of independent "telecars" on miles of track, routing bags across the entire airport under centralized software control. An automated system of modest scope was proven technology. What Denver commissioned was categorically larger and more complex than anything previously operated — a difference in kind that the plan treated as a difference in degree.18

The technical difficulties compounded: telecars mistracked and jammed, bags were misloaded and mangled, and the control software could not reliably choreograph the whole at load. The consequences fell on the schedule. The airport, physically complete, sat unable to open while the baggage system was debugged, and each month of delay cost the city on the order of tens of millions of dollars in interest and operating expense. The Government Accountability Office, reviewing the program as the delays mounted, documented the schedule slippage and its financial toll on the project.19

Critically, the risk had been foreseeable and, by some accounts, foreseen: feasibility analysis had raised doubts about whether the system could be delivered as specified in the time available, and those doubts were not allowed to halt a project with enormous political momentum behind it.20 The airport eventually opened sixteen months late using a conventional manual system as fallback; the automated system, never fully reliable, was abandoned years later.

Denver is technical infeasibility in its most instructive form, because nothing about it was impossible — the failure was one of scale and integration risk that a rigorous readiness assessment would have flagged as unproven. The tell was present at the front end: a critical, novel component on the project's critical path, whose feasibility was contested and whose failure had no cheap fallback. That is the exact profile a technical feasibility study exists to surface — and, when the risk cannot be retired in the time available, to recommend against. The Denver system also illustrates a second dimension we take up shortly: the scope expanded mid-project from a single airline's concourse to the entire airport and all its carriers, which is a governance and operational failure as much as a technical one. Feasibility dimensions fail together.

Section 06

Economic feasibility: does the return justify the spend?

Economic feasibility asks whether the value a system will create exceeds the cost of building, running and eventually retiring it — accounting honestly for the fact that money and benefits arriving in the future are worth less than money and benefits arriving today. It is the dimension most amenable to numbers, which makes it both the most rigorous and the most easily manipulated.

6.1Costs are larger than they look

The first discipline is to count all the costs, not just the visible development cost. A useful decomposition spans the whole life of the system: development cost (people, tooling, infrastructure to build it); operational cost (hosting, licensing, support, the staff to run it); maintenance and evolution (the majority of most systems' lifetime cost); and the opportunity cost of the capital and the talent, which could have been deployed elsewhere. The aggregate is the total cost of ownership, and it routinely dwarfs the development figure that dominates the conversation. A system that is cheap to build and expensive to operate can be a worse investment than the reverse; a feasibility study that stops at the build cost is measuring the tip of the iceberg.

6.2Benefits, tangible and otherwise

Benefits divide into the tangible — quantifiable in currency, such as reduced labour, higher throughput, avoided penalties, incremental revenue — and the intangible — real but hard to price, such as improved decision quality, customer satisfaction, strategic optionality or regulatory goodwill. The temptation is to overweight the tangible simply because it is measurable and to wave at the intangible in prose. The better practice is to make intangibles explicit and, where possible, to bound them: not "improves customer experience" but "if it reduces churn by one percentage point, that is worth X per year." Bounding an intangible converts an article of faith into a testable assumption, which is what the feasibility study is for.

6.3The time value of money

Because costs and benefits arrive on different schedules — costs concentrated up front, benefits spread over years — they cannot simply be summed. A dollar next year is worth less than a dollar today, both because today's dollar could be invested and because the future is uncertain. The standard apparatus of capital budgeting handles this by discounting future cash flows to their present value at a chosen rate, then summing them into a single figure of merit.21 The core measures are worth stating precisely, because software feasibility studies invoke them constantly and misuse them frequently:

  • Net present value (NPV) — the sum of all cash flows, each discounted to today. A positive NPV means the investment creates value above the discount rate; it is the single most defensible go/no-go number economics offers.
  • Internal rate of return (IRR) — the discount rate at which NPV equals zero; loosely, the investment's intrinsic yield, compared against a hurdle rate. Useful but treacherous when cash flows change sign more than once.
  • Payback period — the time until cumulative (ideally discounted) cash flow turns positive. Intuitive and popular, but blind to everything that happens after payback and to the time value unless the discounted form is used.
  • Break-even — the point at which benefits offset costs; a close cousin of payback, framed in units of output rather than time.

A worked example makes the machinery concrete. Consider a system costing $1.2 million to build in year zero, then returning growing net benefits over five years, discounted at ten percent.

Two features of the figure carry the lesson. First, discounting visibly shrinks the later benefits: the year-five benefit is nominally the largest single inflow, but after five years of discounting it contributes markedly less to the decision than its undiscounted height suggests. Second, the nominal payback and the discounted payback are not the same point — the discounted one arrives later — and a feasibility study that quietly uses undiscounted payback flatters the project. The honest figure of merit is the NPV, and it should be reported with the discount rate it assumes stated in the open, because the choice of rate is itself an assumption that can be tuned to produce a desired answer.

The discount rate is an argument, not a constant

Nudge the discount rate down and distant benefits swell; nudge it up and they evaporate. Because the rate encodes assumptions about the cost of capital and the riskiness of the returns, it is one of the easiest places to bias a business case while remaining technically defensible. The remedy is not a single "correct" rate but transparency and sensitivity analysis: report the NPV across a range of rates, and show where the recommendation flips. A business case whose conclusion survives only at one carefully chosen rate is not a case; it is a preference.

6.4Sunk cost and the failure to stop

Economic feasibility does not end at the gate. Its most expensive failures occur after a project is under way, when the rational basis for continuing has evaporated but the money already spent — the sunk cost — exerts a psychological gravity that keeps a doomed project alive. Economics is unambiguous that sunk costs are irrelevant to a forward-looking decision: what matters is the future cost to complete versus the future benefit, not the past cost already gone. Human beings, and the organizations they compose, systematically violate this. Mark Keil's studies of "runaway" software projects documented the pattern of escalation of commitment: the tendency to pour additional resources into a failing course of action to justify the resources already committed, so that the very size of the prior investment becomes the argument against cutting it.22

The Denver baggage system is a canonical case here too — a project whose continuation past the point of evident trouble owed much to the momentum of what had already been spent and promised — and the de-escalation literature uses it precisely to study how organizations extract themselves from such commitments.23 The implication for Phase 0 is that a good business case does not only justify starting; it specifies, in advance and in cold blood, the conditions under which the project should be stopped. Predefining kill criteria — a technical spike that fails, a cost estimate that breaches a threshold, a benefit assumption that testing invalidates — is the only reliable antidote to sunk-cost reasoning, because it makes the decision to stop before the sunk cost exists to distort it. We return to this idea, and to the discipline of the stage gate, in the final section.

Section 07

Operational feasibility: will the organization actually use it?

Operational feasibility asks whether a solution, once built, will actually function within the human and organizational system it enters — whether people will adopt it, whether it fits the workflows and skills and politics of the place, whether it survives contact with the way work is really done. It is the most neglected dimension of feasibility and, by a wide margin, the one whose failures are most lethal, because a system that is technically sound and economically justified but operationally rejected is a total loss that arrives after the money is spent.

7.1Fit, not just function

The core question is fit. A well-worn checklist framing — sometimes taught as PIECES, covering performance, information, economics, control, efficiency and service — is one way to interrogate whether a proposed system improves the organization's actual operations rather than merely automating them. But the deeper frame is socio-technical: a work system is a joint product of its technology and its people, and changing the technology necessarily changes the work, the roles, the skills required and the informal knowledge that made the old system function. A design that optimizes the technical subsystem while ignoring the social one will be locally efficient and globally rejected. The people whose work is being redesigned are stakeholders whose feasibility constraints are as binding as any server's — and, unlike the server, they can refuse.

7.2Adoption is the deliverable

It follows that user adoption is not a post-launch concern to be handled by training and change management after the system ships; it is a feasibility question to be answered before the system is committed. Will the intended users accept the new system, or will they route around it? Does it demand a change in behaviour that the organization has the will and the means to sustain? Is there a migration path from the current way of working, or does the plan assume a discontinuous jump that real organizations rarely survive? A system's operational feasibility is, in the end, a prediction about human behaviour under change — and predictions about human behaviour under change are exactly the kind of unknown that the front end must surface and the plan must accommodate.

Political and legal feasibility

Two sub-dimensions live inside the operational question. Political feasibility asks whether the coalition of stakeholders whose support the project needs actually exists and holds — a technically and economically sound project can die because a key constituency was neither consulted nor won over, and mobilized against it. Legal and regulatory feasibility asks whether the solution is lawful, compliant and defensible: data-protection regimes, sector regulation, accessibility law, contractual and licensing constraints. Either can be an absolute bar. A system that cannot be operated lawfully, or that no one with power will let operate, is infeasible no matter how well it runs.

7.3When the organization is not consulted

The purest illustration of operational infeasibility — a system that worked, in a narrow technical sense, and failed catastrophically the moment it met the organization it was built for — is the London Ambulance Service's computer-aided dispatch system.

Case · Operational feasibility
The London Ambulance Service dispatch system
1992 · London, United Kingdom

In October 1992 the London Ambulance Service — then the largest in the world, covering some seven million people — switched on a new computer-aided dispatch system intended to automate the allocation of ambulances to emergency calls. Within hours it was in crisis. The system depended on a near-perfect flow of accurate status information from crews and an idealized model of how dispatch worked; the reality of London's streets, crew behaviour and imperfect data diverged from that model immediately. Calls were lost or duplicated on screen, the system generated a rising tide of exceptions, response times collapsed, and the service was forced back to manual methods.24

The official inquiry's findings read as a catalogue of operational-feasibility failures upstream of a single line of code. The timetable was unrealistic and the system was put live with inadequate testing. The staff who would use it — control-room dispatchers and ambulance crews with deep tacit knowledge of how allocation actually worked — had been neither adequately consulted in its design nor adequately trained in its use, and the design embodied assumptions about their behaviour that were simply false. The scope was over-ambitious for a first release, and it was procured from a consortium without the relevant experience. An earlier attempt had already been abandoned; the organizational lessons had not been learned.25

Media reports at the time linked the failure to deaths among patients awaiting ambulances; the inquiry found the claims impossible to substantiate and no direct causal link was ever established — a fact worth stating carefully, because the case is often dramatized beyond the evidence. What is not in doubt is that a system which functioned in the developers' model failed completely in the organization's reality, and that the reasons were visible, in principle, at the front end.

The London Ambulance Service failure is the strongest possible argument that operational feasibility is not a soft, secondary concern to be addressed once the "real" technical work is done. The technical system was, in isolation, buildable and largely built. It failed because the front-end work of understanding the organization — its people, their tacit knowledge, the real shape of the work, the change the system demanded of them — was not done, and no quantity of downstream engineering could compensate. The dispatchers were the feasibility constraint the project could not see, precisely because it never asked them.

Section 08

Resource estimation: time, cost and people

The final activity of Phase 0 is to estimate what the endeavour will require — how long, how much, how many people — and to do so honestly, which means as ranges rather than points, with the reasoning exposed. Estimation is where the front end's uncertainty becomes a number someone will be held to, and it is therefore where the pressure to lie, to oneself and to others, is greatest. Understanding the methods is necessary; understanding the psychology is what separates estimates that inform decisions from estimates that corrupt them.

8.1Estimates are ranges

Everything in this section descends from the cone of uncertainty introduced at the outset. At the feasibility stage a single-number estimate is not merely imprecise; it is dishonest, because it conceals a range that the estimator knows to be wide. The correct output of Phase 0 estimation is an interval with a stated confidence — "between eight and eighteen months, most likely twelve" — not a point, and every method below should be read as a way of producing and narrowing that interval rather than collapsing it prematurely.4 Tom DeMarco's aphorism from the era that made estimation a discipline — that you cannot control what you cannot measure — is often quoted to justify precision; its deeper reading is that measurement includes measuring your own uncertainty, and reporting it.26

8.2The methods, and when to trust them

Estimation methods form a rough progression from the subjective to the parametric, each appropriate to different information conditions:

  1. Expert judgment. An experienced practitioner's estimate, ideally decomposed and cross-checked. Fast and, in skilled hands, surprisingly good — but subject to every cognitive bias in the catalogue, and only as reliable as the expert's experience is relevant.
  2. Estimation by analogy. Anchor on a completed project genuinely similar in size and character, and adjust. Powerful precisely because it is an outside view in miniature; only as good as the analogy's honesty and the comparability of the reference.
  3. Parametric models. Algorithmic estimation from a size input and calibrated coefficients — COCOMO and its descendants being the archetype. Repeatable and explicit, but critically dependent on the accuracy of the size estimate they take as input, which merely relocates the hardest problem.
  4. Bottom-up estimation. Decompose the work into small pieces, estimate each, and aggregate. More accurate when a credible decomposition exists — which, at the wide mouth of the cone, it often does not — and prone to omitting whole categories of work that only become visible later.
  5. Group and three-point methods. Wideband Delphi convenes experts to estimate independently and converge through structured discussion, damping individual bias. Three-point (PERT-style) estimation asks for optimistic, most-likely and pessimistic figures and combines them, making the uncertainty itself an explicit input rather than a hidden one.

No method is authoritative alone. The mature practice is to estimate a critical figure by two different methods — say, a parametric model and an analogy — and to treat a large divergence between them not as an inconvenience but as information: a signal that some assumption is wrong and worth hunting down before the number is committed.

8.3Sizing, and the diseconomy of scale

Every parametric estimate needs a size input, and how to size software that does not yet exist is one of the oldest problems in the field. Lines of code are the historical unit — concrete, countable after the fact, but notoriously hard to estimate in advance and perverse as an incentive, since they reward verbosity. Function points, introduced by Allan Albrecht at IBM in 1979, size a system instead by the functionality it delivers to users — its inputs, outputs, inquiries and data stores — independent of implementation language or style, and remain a widely used, standardized measure of functional size.27 Agile practice adds story points and other relative-sizing schemes that deliberately abandon absolute units in favour of comparative estimation within a team's own velocity. Each unit trades precision for availability differently; none dissolves the underlying difficulty, which is that you are measuring something not yet built.

Barry Boehm's Constructive Cost Model, calibrated on sixty-three projects at TRW and published in 1981, remains the clearest window onto why software estimation resists intuition.1 In its basic form, effort in person-months is a coefficient times size in thousands of lines of code raised to an exponent — and the exponent is greater than one. That single fact, that the exponent exceeds unity, encodes a diseconomy of scale: doubling the size of a system more than doubles the effort to build it, because the communication and integration burden grows faster than the code. COCOMO's three modes — organic, semi-detached and embedded — capture increasing levels of constraint and coupling, each with a steeper exponent than the last. The model was recalibrated for modern practice as COCOMO II two decades later, but the shape is the enduring lesson.28

8.4People: the man-month fallacy

The diseconomy of scale has a direct corollary for the personnel dimension of estimation, and it is the most quoted law in software management. Fred Brooks, reflecting on the troubled development of IBM's OS/360, formulated it as: adding manpower to a late software project makes it later.29 The reason is the same communication burden that bends the COCOMO curve. Effort and progress are not interchangeable — the "man-month" is a fallacious unit — because work on a complex system is not perfectly partitionable, and every new person added must be trained by existing staff (subtracting their effort) and must communicate with all the others, and the number of communication paths grows quadratically with team size. Beyond a point, adding people subtracts progress. For Phase 0 estimation this means that schedule is not freely purchasable with headcount: there is a minimum time below which a given system cannot be built at any staffing level, and a plan that assumes a late project can be rescued by reinforcement has misunderstood the nature of the work.

The bearing of a child takes nine months, no matter how many women are assigned.

— Fred Brooks, on the limits of partitioning effort

8.5The planning fallacy and the outside view

Even with sound methods and honest sizing, estimates are pulled systematically in one direction: too optimistic. This is not carelessness but a robust cognitive bias, and it has a name. Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky described the planning fallacy: the tendency to underestimate the time, cost and risk of a plan even when one has ample experience of similar plans overrunning.30 The bias is stubborn because it survives knowledge of itself — people who know that their past projects ran long still expect the next one to run to plan. Roger Buehler and colleagues demonstrated it experimentally, showing that people's confident predictions about their own task completion were routinely optimistic relative to their own histories, which they discounted as unrepresentative.31

The diagnosis points to its own cure. Kahneman and Dan Lovallo distinguished the inside view, which builds an estimate from the specifics of the case at hand — this team, this design, these tasks — from the outside view, which ignores the specifics and asks how a whole reference class of similar endeavours actually turned out.32 The inside view feels rigorous and is chronically optimistic, because it reasons forward from a plan and a plan contains no allowance for the unknown unknowns that history is full of. The outside view feels crude and is far more accurate, because the base rate already incorporates everything that went wrong in comparable projects, including the surprises.

Bent Flyvbjerg turned the outside view into an operational method — reference class forecasting — and validated it on the largest available dataset of infrastructure megaprojects. His findings are sobering and directly relevant: across hundreds of transport projects, costs were underestimated in the overwhelming majority of cases, with rail projects overrunning by an average around 45 percent and roads around 20, while rail passenger forecasts were overestimated by an average around half.33 Reference class forecasting corrects an estimate by identifying the relevant class of past projects, establishing the distribution of their outcomes, and adjusting the naive estimate toward that distribution — literally replacing part of the optimistic inside view with the empirical outside one. The method has since been adopted by governments as a mandated corrective ("optimism bias uplifts") on major project estimates, and popularized for a general audience in Kahneman's later synthesis of the field.34

8.6Error, or lie?

Not all optimism is honest. Flyvbjerg and colleagues confronted an uncomfortable possibility directly in a paper whose subtitle asks whether cost underestimation is error or lie: some systematic underestimation is not cognitive bias at all but strategic misrepresentation — estimates deliberately lowballed by promoters who understand that a project, once begun and sunk-cost-laden, is very hard to stop, so that an optimistic figure at the gate is a rational tactic for getting a favoured project approved.35 The distinction matters for Phase 0 governance because the two causes call for different remedies. Cognitive optimism is corrected by method — the outside view, reference classes, independent estimation. Strategic misrepresentation is corrected only by incentives and accountability: holding estimators to their estimates, funding in stages against validated milestones, and insulating the feasibility study from the political will to build. A gate that cannot tell an honest optimistic estimate from a deliberately low one will be defeated by the latter every time.

8.7Contingency is not padding

The proper response to irreducible uncertainty is not a falsely precise point estimate but an explicit reserve. Well-run projects distinguish contingency reserve — an allowance, sized to the assessed risk, for the known unknowns within the project's scope — from management reserve, held above the project for the genuinely unforeseen. Neither is padding, and the distinction from padding is precisely that reserves are explicit, quantified against identified risks, and governed: they are drawn down against specific events, not quietly consumed. An estimate presented as a range with a stated confidence and an explicit, risk-sized reserve is the honest deliverable of Phase 0 estimation. A single number with silent slack baked in is the dishonest one — and it is dishonest in a way that corrodes the whole gate, because it hides the very uncertainty the decision-maker most needs to see.

Section 09

The go / no-go decision

Everything in Phase 0 converges on one decision: proceed, revise, or stop. The decision is the point of the phase, and the discipline that makes it trustworthy is the willingness to sometimes decide no — because a gate that only ever says yes is not a gate, it is a formality with a budget.

The governance instrument for this is the stage gate (or phase gate): a review at the boundary between phases at which a project must re-justify its continuation against updated information before more resources are committed. Phase 0 feeds the first and most important gate, the one before construction. But the deeper idea is that the front-end question — is this still worth doing, and can we still do it? — is not answered once and retired. It recurs at every gate, re-asked against what has since been learned, so that a project that has quietly become infeasible or unjustified can be caught and stopped before it consumes its full budget on the way to a failure everyone can already see coming.

This reframes the most counter-intuitive competency of the front end: killing projects well is a feature, not a failure. An organization that cannot stop a failing project cannot run a real gate, and will therefore convert its worst bets into its largest losses through sheer inability to fold. The Virtual Case File should have been stopped and re-architected long before $105 million had been spent on code that was discarded; Denver's automated system should have been de-scoped when its feasibility was first seriously contested. In both, the machinery to say no existed on paper and was overpowered by the momentum of prior commitment. The empirical failure literature — the Standish data and its more careful successors alike — is, read one way, a measurement of how often organizations cannot bring themselves to stop. The kill decision is where feasibility analysis either earns its keep or reveals itself as theatre.

Q1Problem. Is there a clearly defined business need, stated independently of any solution, that at least one accountable stakeholder owns?
Q2Technical. Can we build it with available skills and sufficiently mature technology, with the novel components identified and their risk retired or costed?
Q3Economic. Is the net present value positive across a defensible range of assumptions, with the discount rate and its sensitivities in the open?
Q4Operational. Will the organization actually adopt and sustain it, and have the people whose work it changes been consulted rather than assumed?
Q5Estimate. Is the projection a range with a stated confidence and an explicit reserve, cross-checked by an outside view rather than a single inside-view number?
Q6Exit. Have we defined, now, the conditions under which we will stop — before the sunk cost exists to distort the judgement?

A project that cannot answer all six affirmatively has not failed the gate so much as revealed that the front-end work is not yet done. The right response is rarely a flat "no"; more often it is "not yet — resolve these, and return." That is the gate functioning exactly as intended: not as an execution but as a demand for the specific missing evidence.

9.1Feasibility in an iterative world

A fair objection closes this article. In an age of agile and lean development, does a heavyweight up-front feasibility phase still make sense — or is Phase 0 a relic of the waterfall era it was born in? The answer is that Phase 0 has not disappeared; it has been redistributed. The risk-driven insight was there from the start in Barry Boehm's spiral model, which organized development around iterated cycles of identifying and retiring the greatest remaining risk first — feasibility not as a single gate but as the organizing principle of every iteration.36 Boehm's parallel work codifying software risk management gave the front end its explicit vocabulary of identifying, prioritizing and mitigating risk continuously rather than once.37

Agile practice inherits and extends this. The "iteration zero" or inception that opens many agile efforts is Phase 0 by another name — establishing the problem, the rough shape of a solution, and the viability of proceeding before the team commits to a cadence.38 And the lean-startup reframing makes the continuity explicit: rather than attempt to establish feasibility fully in advance, treat the entire early product as a sequence of tests of the riskiest assumptions, building the minimum needed to get real evidence and pivoting or persevering on what the evidence shows.39 This is not the abandonment of feasibility analysis; it is feasibility analysis made continuous and empirical — the outside view sought from the market rather than from a document. The unit of the gate shrinks from a phase to an iteration, but the question at each gate is the ancient one of Phase 0: given what we now know, is this still worth building, and can we still build it?

Which returns us to where we began. The front end is the region of maximum leverage and minimum information, and there is no method that abolishes that tension. What Phase 0 offers is not certainty but honesty under uncertainty: a defined problem instead of a presumed solution, a multi-dimensional test instead of a hopeful pitch, a range instead of a false point, and a decision — including, when the evidence demands it, the decision to stop. The projects in this article's gallery of failures did not lack talent or money or technology. They lacked, at the front end, the discipline to make the cheap decision to not-yet-build, and paid, at the back end, the most expensive version of the same decision. That asymmetry is the whole case for taking Phase 0 seriously — and it has not changed in fifty years of trying to engineer our way around it.
