Orientation

A sector that everything else depends on

In 2013, Presidential Policy Directive 21 organized the United States’ critical infrastructure into sixteen sectors and assigned each a federal steward. Fifteen of those sectors describe a tangible thing the country needs — power, water, hospitals, pipelines, ports. The Information Technology (IT) sector is different. It is the only one whose product is the machinery the other fifteen run on, and the Department of Homeland Security, acting through the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA), serves as its Sector Risk Management Agency.1

This horizontal position is the single most important fact about IT-sector security. A breach of a water utility endangers one community; a breach of the software, cloud platform, identity provider, or network management tool that thousands of water utilities depend on endangers all of them at once. The sector’s own planning documents acknowledge this directly: its functions are “virtual and distributed,” produced by a shifting constellation of owners and operators who collectively “maintain and reconstitute” the network — including, in partnership with the Communications sector, the Internet itself.2

This article is a technical reference for that problem. It proceeds in five movements: the technologies and risks specific to the sector; the mechanics by which an IT compromise cascades into other critical infrastructure; the frameworks, standards, and tooling that exist to manage the risk; and the unresolved challenges that still demand research and investment. Throughout, claims are grounded in primary sources — CISA and NIST publications, government advisories, and incident post-mortems — cited in Vancouver style and listed at the end.

The sector, defined

What “the IT sector” actually means

The Information Technology sector is not a list of companies; it is a list of functions. The sector’s baseline risk assessment and Sector-Specific Plan define it through six critical functions that, taken together, keep the digital economy running. Understanding these functions is the key to understanding where the sector’s risk concentrates, because each function is a potential single point of failure for everyone who relies on it.3

F1
Provide IT products and servicesHardware, operating systems, applications, firmware, and the global supply chains that produce them.
F2
Provide incident management capabilitiesThe detection, coordination, and response infrastructure that lets the sector recover from disruption.
F3

F4

F5

F6
Provide Internet routing, access & connectionThe Border Gateway Protocol (BGP), peering, and transit that physically move packets between networks.

Notice how foundational these are. F3 and F6 are the addressing and routing layers of the Internet; if they fail, name resolution and reachability fail for everyone downstream regardless of how well-defended those downstream systems are. F4 is the trust layer; a compromise of a major certificate authority or identity provider lets an attacker impersonate legitimate services at scale. F1 is the supply chain; a single tampered software update propagates to every customer that installs it. The sector’s defining risk is not that any one organization is weak, but that its functions are shared.

Who is responsible, and under what authority

Federal responsibility for the sector flows through a chain of policy. PPD-21 (2013) established the sixteen-sector model and the Sector Risk Management Agency (SRMA) construct, designating DHS as the SRMA for Information Technology.1 In April 2024, the Biden administration replaced PPD-21 with National Security Memorandum 22 (NSM-22), which reaffirmed the sector model, codified CISA’s role as National Coordinator for critical infrastructure security and resilience, and strengthened the SRMA responsibilities first written into law by the FY2021 National Defense Authorization Act.4 The sector also coordinates through public-private bodies — the IT Sector Coordinating Council and IT Government Coordinating Council — and an Information Technology Information Sharing and Analysis Center (IT-ISAC) for operational threat sharing.2

A note on scope

The IT and Communications sectors are deliberately distinct in U.S. policy but inseparable in practice. The IT sector produces the products, software, cloud, DNS, identity, and routing logic; the Communications sector owns much of the physical transport. This article centers IT, but where a risk straddles the boundary — as routing and DNS do — it is treated as the shared infrastructure it is.

What makes the sector genuinely hard to govern is that the federal government owns almost none of it. The hyperscale clouds, the dominant operating systems, the certificate authorities, the open-source libraries embedded in everything — these are private, often global, and frequently maintained by a vanishingly small number of people. Security in this sector is therefore an exercise in influence and shared responsibility rather than direct control, a theme that recurs in every section that follows.

Technology, risk & consequence

The attack surface is the stack itself

In most sectors, information technology is a tool used to run the real business. In the IT sector, the technology is the business, which means the attack surface is the entire stack that everyone else builds on. To see where the risk lives, it helps to walk the stack from silicon to service.

The stack, layer by layer

Hardware and semiconductors. At the base sit processors, memory, network silicon, and the firmware that boots them. This layer carries supply-chain risk (counterfeit or tampered components), firmware-level implants that survive operating-system reinstalls, and hardware side-channel weaknesses. It is also strategically fragile: advanced chip fabrication is concentrated in a handful of foundries, making the hardware supply chain a matter of national security as much as engineering.

Operating systems and commercial software. Above the hardware sits the software that defines the computing environment. Because a few operating systems and productivity platforms dominate enterprise and government computing, a vulnerability in one of them is, by definition, a vulnerability almost everywhere. Monoculture is efficient and dangerous in equal measure.

Open-source components. Modern applications are overwhelmingly assembled, not written. A typical enterprise codebase is a thin layer of original code atop a deep stack of open-source libraries, many maintained by volunteers. This is the most under-appreciated layer in the sector: code that runs in millions of systems but is sustained by a few unpaid people, as the Log4j and xz Utils episodes made unforgettable (both detailed below).

Cloud and hyperscale platforms. Compute, storage, and an ever-growing menu of managed services have consolidated onto a small number of hyperscale cloud providers. This delivers world-class engineering and security to customers who could never build it themselves — and concentrates systemic risk so that a single region or service failure ripples worldwide, as the October 2025 Amazon Web Services outage demonstrated (see §03).

DNS and Internet routing. The Domain Name System and the Border Gateway Protocol are the addressing and routing fabric of the Internet. Both were designed in a more trusting era and retain weak default authentication. DNS hijacking and BGP route leaks can silently redirect traffic for entire networks; even non-malicious DNS automation errors can take down large swathes of the Internet.

Identity, certificates, and trust. Authentication systems, certificate authorities, federated identity providers, and the public-key infrastructure behind https are the trust anchors of digital life. They are also high-value targets: compromise an identity provider and you can mint legitimate-looking access to everything that trusts it. Much of modern intrusion tradecraft is, at root, identity abuse.

Managed services and security tooling. Managed service providers (MSPs), remote monitoring and management (RMM) platforms, and endpoint detection and response (EDR) agents are granted deep, privileged, often automatic access across their customers’ estates. That privilege is precisely what makes them attractive: a single compromised management tool is a skeleton key to every network it touches, a lesson written in the Kaseya and CrowdStrike incidents.

The protocols that trust too much

Two foundational protocols deserve special attention because their weaknesses are architectural rather than implementational — they were designed when the network was small and its participants were assumed to be cooperative. The Border Gateway Protocol (BGP), which determines how traffic is routed between the autonomous networks that make up the Internet, accepts route announcements largely on trust. A network that announces a route it does not legitimately own — whether by misconfiguration or malice — can attract and inspect or black-hole traffic destined for someone else, a class of incident known as BGP hijacking or route leaking. Because routing is global, a single bad announcement can misdirect traffic for major services in minutes.

The Domain Name System (DNS) has the analogous problem at the naming layer. Because resolvers historically accepted answers without strong cryptographic verification, attackers who can inject forged responses can redirect users from a legitimate address to one they control — DNS spoofing or cache poisoning. Hardening efforts exist for both protocols — Resource Public Key Infrastructure (RPKI) for route-origin validation, and DNSSEC for signed DNS records — but adoption remains incomplete, and the October 2025 AWS event (§03) is a reminder that even non-adversarial DNS automation failures inside a single provider can cascade globally. These are not edge cases; they are the addressing and routing fabric on which every other sector’s connectivity silently depends.

Risk class one: the software supply chain

The defining IT-sector risk is that trust flows downhill through software updates. Customers install vendor patches automatically because doing otherwise is itself a security risk. An attacker who can poison the update mechanism inherits all of that trust at once. The canonical example reshaped U.S. policy.

Supply Chain · APT SUNBURST / SolarWinds Orion Disclosed Dec 2020 · Attributed to Russia (SVR)

Beginning in 2019, operatives later attributed by the U.S. government to Russia’s SVR foreign-intelligence service compromised the build system of SolarWinds, a Texas-based maker of the widely deployed Orion network-management platform. They injected a backdoor — SUNBURST — into a routine software update, which SolarWinds then digitally signed and distributed to customers starting in March 2020.5 Because Orion is trusted with broad visibility into customer networks, the tampered update opened a stealthy door into thousands of organizations through a single point of compromise.6

Up to roughly 18,000 customers downloaded the malicious version, though the attackers cherry-picked a far smaller set for active exploitation — including at least nine U.S. federal agencies and around 100 private companies. Victims spanned the Departments of Treasury, Commerce, State, Justice, Energy, and Homeland Security — the last being the agency charged with defending federal networks.5 The intrusion was not discovered by any government monitoring program but by the security firm FireEye, investigating its own breach.6

SolarWinds proved that a defender could do everything right — patch promptly, trust signed vendor code — and still be compromised because the vendor was the victim. A second 2024 episode showed how close the open-source supply chain came to an even broader catastrophe.

Supply Chain · Near-Miss xz Utils backdoor · CVE-2024-3094 CVSS 10.0 · Discovered 29 Mar 2024

Over roughly two years, an actor using the persona “Jia Tan” patiently built trust as a contributor to xz Utils — an unglamorous but ubiquitous compression library bundled in virtually every Linux distribution — until granted co-maintainer status by its overworked sole maintainer. In versions 5.6.0 and 5.6.1, this actor planted a sophisticated backdoor that targeted the OpenSSH server through the library’s presence in certain build configurations, enabling remote code execution on potentially hundreds of millions of servers.7

The malicious code lived only in release tarballs, not the public source repository, and was obfuscated across binary test files — invisible to standard scanning. It was caught by accident: a Microsoft engineer, Andres Freund, noticed that SSH logins on a test machine were running about half a second slow, traced the latency, and found the backdoor days before it could reach mainstream stable releases.8 It remains the closest near-miss in open-source security history, and a stark illustration of how thin the human layer beneath the stack really is.

Risk class two: vulnerability at the scale of ubiquity

When a flaw lands in a component that is everywhere, the question is not whether it will be exploited but how fast. The archetype is Log4Shell.

Ubiquitous Vuln Log4Shell · CVE-2021-44228 CVSS 10.0 · Disclosed 9 Dec 2021

Apache Log4j is a Java logging library so common that it is embedded in countless consumer and enterprise applications — and, CISA noted, in operational-technology products as well. In December 2021 a critical remote-code-execution flaw, nicknamed Log4Shell, was disclosed in versions 2.0-beta9 through 2.14.1. An unauthenticated attacker could take control of a system simply by getting a crafted string logged — for example, in a username field or an HTTP header.9

The vulnerability earned the maximum CVSS base score of 10.0; exploitation required no privileges and almost no skill, and attackers began mass-scanning within hours of disclosure. Because Log4j is a dependency buried inside other software, many organizations could not even tell whether they were exposed.10 Log4Shell is the definitive case study in why software transparency — knowing what is actually inside your systems — became a federal priority.

Risk class three: concentration and the single point of failure

The previous two risks are about malice. This one is about structure, and it is arguably the sector’s most distinctive hazard. As computing has consolidated onto a few cloud platforms, a few operating systems, and a few security vendors, the blast radius of any single failure — malicious or not — has grown to span the economy. A bad configuration push or a flawed update at one vendor can now ground airlines and close hospitals across continents. The mechanics and the worked examples (CrowdStrike, AWS) belong to the cross-sector cascade, and are treated in §03.

Risk class four: identity as the new perimeter

As workloads moved to the cloud and the traditional network boundary dissolved, identity became the control plane. Attackers followed. Phishing, token theft, multi-factor-authentication fatigue, and the abuse of federated trust between identity providers are now central techniques precisely because a valid identity grants access without tripping perimeter defenses. This shift is the conceptual foundation of the Zero Trust model examined in §04.

From compromise to consequence

The consequences of an IT-sector compromise sort cleanly along the classic confidentiality–integrity–availability triad, plus a fourth dimension unique to this sector:

  • Confidentiality — espionage and data theft. SolarWinds and the mass file-transfer breaches (§03) were, at their core, intelligence and extortion operations: read access to email, source code, and sensitive records at scale.
  • Integrity — tampering and manipulation. A backdoored update or a poisoned dependency lets an attacker alter what systems do, not merely observe them. Integrity attacks are the hardest to detect and the most corrosive to trust.
  • Availability — disruption and destruction. Ransomware, wiper malware, and even faulty updates can render systems unusable. When the affected systems run hospitals or flights, an availability event becomes a safety event.
  • Systemic — cascading failure. Unique to horizontal infrastructure: a single IT failure propagates into multiple other critical sectors simultaneously. This is the subject of the next section.
The cross-sector cascade

How one IT compromise becomes everyone’s emergency

CISA describes IT and Communications as enabling sectors — horizontal layers on which the other fourteen depend. That word carries the whole argument. A vertical-sector failure is contained by definition; a horizontal-sector failure is not. The same software, cloud, identity, and routing infrastructure that makes modern energy, finance, water, and healthcare possible also makes them co-dependent on a shared set of failure points.

The cascade follows a recognizable shape regardless of whether the trigger is a nation-state, a ransomware crew, or a botched update.

Case study A — the managed-service skeleton key

MSPs and RMM platforms sit one level above their customers and hold privileged, automated access to all of them. Attackers learned to ask not “who do I want to hit?” but “through whom can I hit them all?”

Ransomware · MSP Supply Chain Kaseya VSA · CVE-2021-30116 REvil / July 4 weekend 2021

Over the 2021 U.S. Independence Day weekend — chosen for minimal IT staffing — the REvil ransomware group exploited a zero-day in Kaseya VSA, an RMM platform used by managed service providers to administer client networks. They pushed ransomware downstream through roughly 60 direct Kaseya customers, about 30 of which were MSPs, reaching an estimated 800 to 1,500 downstream businesses across at least 17 countries.11

The real-world effect landed far from any IT department: the Swedish grocery chain Coop had to close roughly 800 stores because its point-of-sale system was managed through an affected MSP. REvil demanded US$70 million for a universal decryptor; Kaseya ultimately obtained one without paying ransom, and a perpetrator was later extradited and pleaded guilty.12 One vulnerability in one management tool became a retail-sector outage on another continent — the cascade in miniature.

Case study B — one flaw, a hundred million records

Managed file-transfer (MFT) appliances move sensitive bulk data — payroll, health, and financial records — between organizations. A single flaw in one such product became one of the largest data-theft campaigns on record.

Mass Exploitation · Data Theft MOVEit Transfer · CVE-2023-34362 Cl0p / TA505 · from 27 May 2023

The Cl0p ransomware syndicate (also tracked as TA505) exploited a previously unknown SQL-injection flaw in Progress Software’s MOVEit Transfer, deploying a custom web shell named LEMURLOOT to siphon data from the underlying databases. CISA and the FBI noted this was a repeat of Cl0p’s playbook against earlier file-transfer products — a deliberate strategy of hunting the data-movement layer that so many sectors quietly rely on.13

Because MOVEit is used by payroll processors, benefits administrators, government contractors, universities, and financial firms, the breach radiated across sectors through their shared vendors. By the following October, analysts counted more than 2,500 victim organizations and roughly 64.5 million affected individuals, with U.S. entities making up the overwhelming majority of known victims.14 No single victim was the target; the file-transfer tool itself was.

Case study C — when nobody attacked anything

The most sobering cascades of recent years involved no adversary at all. They demonstrate that the sector’s concentration risk is structural: a single vendor’s mistake can do what an enemy could only dream of.

Concentration · No Attacker CrowdStrike Falcon faulty update 19 July 2024 · “largest IT outage in history”

On 19 July 2024, the cybersecurity vendor CrowdStrike pushed a defective configuration update to its Falcon EDR sensor. Falcon runs deep in the operating-system kernel, so the flaw sent Windows machines into the “blue screen of death” on reboot. Roughly 8.5 million Windows devices were affected — less than 1% of all Windows machines, but disproportionately the ones running critical operations.15

The cross-sector damage was immediate and physical: more than 10,000 flights cancelled worldwide as airline systems failed; hospitals reverting to paper and postponing procedures; banks and payment platforms disrupted. Insurers later estimated direct losses to Fortune 500 companies alone at about US$5.4 billion, with healthcare (~$1.94B) and banking (~$1.15B) hit hardest.16 No breach, no malware, no adversary — just one trusted vendor’s update, and a demonstration of exactly how a malicious actor could weaponize the same dependency.

Concentration · Cloud AWS US-EAST-1 outage 19–20 Oct 2025 · DNS / DynamoDB

In October 2025, a latent race condition in the automated DNS management of Amazon DynamoDB corrupted the DNS records for the database’s API endpoint in AWS’s busiest region, US-EAST-1 in Northern Virginia. Because countless AWS services — and AWS’s own internal systems — depend on DynamoDB, the failure cascaded across more than a hundred services and took hours to fully resolve.17

The outward ripple was global. Downdetector logged millions of user reports as a remarkable cross-section of digital life went dark at once: social platforms, banks and brokerages (Coinbase, Robinhood), payment apps, smart-home devices, airlines, and enterprise tools all depending, directly or indirectly, on that one region.18 A single DNS automation error reproduced, at planetary scale, the lesson of CrowdStrike: in a concentrated stack, the failure of an invisible internal dependency is everyone’s failure.

Case study D — the cascade as strategy

The previous cases were espionage, extortion, or accident. The most strategically alarming use of IT-sector access treats the cascade as a deliberate wartime weapon: quietly burrowing into the infrastructure now so it can be disrupted later, at a moment of geopolitical choosing.

Nation-State · Pre-Positioning Volt Typhoon (PRC) Joint advisory AA24-038A · Feb 2024

In February 2024, CISA, the NSA, the FBI, and international partners assessed that “Volt Typhoon” — a People’s Republic of China state-sponsored actor active since at least 2021 — had compromised IT networks of U.S. critical infrastructure not for espionage but to pre-position for disruptive or destructive attacks during a future crisis. Confirmed intrusions clustered in the Communications, Energy, Transportation Systems, and Water and Wastewater sectors.19

Volt Typhoon relied on “living off the land” — abusing built-in administration tools and valid credentials rather than malware — to blend into normal activity and evade detection, in one case maintaining access for years. CISA Director Jen Easterly warned that the activity already found was “likely the tip of the iceberg,” and the Justice Department disrupted a botnet of compromised home and small-office routers the group used to hide its operations.20 Here the IT sector is not collateral damage; it is the chosen beachhead into the physical sectors it underpins.

Five cases, five mechanisms — signed-update poisoning, privileged MSP access, a shared file-transfer flaw, an over-privileged kernel agent, a single cloud region, and stealthy nation-state persistence — all producing the same outcome: a failure that originates in the IT sector and lands, simultaneously, across the sectors that depend on it. This is what makes IT-sector security a precondition rather than a parallel concern, and it is what the mitigation ecosystem in §04 is built to address.

Frameworks, standards & tooling

The machinery of defense

No single control addresses a horizontal sector. What has emerged instead is a layered ecosystem of governance frameworks, supply-chain controls, architectural models, vulnerability-management tooling, assurance programs, and operational collaboration — much of it driven by the very incidents catalogued above. The remainder of this section is a guided tour of that machinery, from boardroom governance down to cryptographic primitives.

Governance and risk frameworks

The most widely adopted starting point in the United States is the NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF). Originally built in 2014 for critical infrastructure, its version 2.0 — released in February 2024 — was the first major revision in a decade. It expanded the framework’s scope to organizations of every size and sector and, most significantly, added a sixth core function, Govern, alongside the original Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, and Recover. CSF 2.0 organizes cybersecurity outcomes into 6 functions, 22 categories, and 106 subcategories, with Govern sitting at the center to tie cyber risk into enterprise risk management and leadership accountability.21

Where CSF describes outcomes, the NIST Risk Management Framework and its control catalog, SP 800-53, provide the detailed, auditable controls and the process for selecting, implementing, and assessing them — the backbone of federal system authorization.22 Internationally, ISO/IEC 27001 offers a certifiable information-security management system standard widely used by vendors to demonstrate baseline rigor,23 while the CIS Critical Security Controls distill the landscape into a prioritized, prescriptive set of safeguards that smaller organizations can actually implement.24

Securing the software supply chain

SolarWinds and Log4Shell turned software-supply-chain security from a niche concern into federal policy. The pivot point was Executive Order 14028, “Improving the Nation’s Cybersecurity,” signed in May 2021, which directed NIST to develop supply-chain security standards and required federal software suppliers to meet them.25 NIST’s response was the Secure Software Development Framework (SSDF), SP 800-218 — a technology-neutral set of practices grouped into four families: Prepare the Organization, Protect the Software, Produce Well-Secured Software, and Respond to Vulnerabilities. Federal vendors must now self-attest to conformance with the SSDF, a requirement that has rippled into private-sector procurement.26

CISA pushed the burden further upstream with its Secure by Design initiative, which argues that the responsibility for security should fall on manufacturers building products, not on the under-resourced customers operating them. Its accompanying voluntary pledge has been signed by hundreds of software manufacturers committing to measurable security improvements such as default multi-factor authentication and the elimination of entire vulnerability classes.27

Two complementary instruments address the Log4Shell “what is even inside my software?” problem. A Software Bill of Materials (SBOM) is a machine-readable inventory of the components and dependencies in a piece of software — the transparency that would have let organizations instantly locate every vulnerable Log4j instance.28 NIST’s SP 800-161 provides the broader discipline of Cyber Supply Chain Risk Management (C-SCRM) for assessing and governing supplier risk across the lifecycle.29 At the build layer, the open SLSA framework (Supply-chain Levels for Software Artifacts) defines verifiable provenance — cryptographic proof of how and from what source an artifact was built — directly targeting the SolarWinds-style build-system compromise.30

Architecture: the Zero Trust shift

The dissolution of the network perimeter demanded a new architectural philosophy. Zero Trust, formalized in NIST SP 800-207 (2020), abandons the assumption that anything inside the network is trustworthy. Its seven tenets treat every resource access as untrusted until proven otherwise: all communication is secured regardless of location, access is granted per-session on least-privilege terms, and trust is continually re-evaluated from identity and device posture rather than network position.31

Zero Trust moved from theory to federal mandate quickly. Building on EO 14028, OMB Memorandum M-22-09 (January 2022) ordered federal agencies to meet specific Zero Trust goals — including phishing-resistant multi-factor authentication and enterprise identity management — on an aggressive timeline.32 CISA’s Zero Trust Maturity Model operationalizes the journey across five pillars — Identity, Devices, Networks, Applications and Workloads, and Data — giving organizations a graduated path from traditional to optimal implementations.33

Why Zero Trust matters for the cascade

Every case study in §03 turned on trust that propagated automatically — a signed update, a privileged MSP, a federated identity. Zero Trust is, at its core, an attempt to break that automatic propagation: to ensure that compromising one component does not silently grant access to everything that component could reach. It does not prevent the initial breach; it constrains the blast radius.

Detection, response, and the language of adversaries

Prevention fails; detection and response must catch what gets through. The operational layer here rests on endpoint detection and response (EDR/XDR) agents, security information and event management (SIEM) platforms, and increasingly automated security orchestration and response (SOAR) playbooks. Tying them together is MITRE ATT&CK, a public knowledge base of real-world adversary tactics and techniques that gives defenders a shared vocabulary for describing, detecting, and hunting attacker behavior — the same taxonomy used to dissect the incidents in this article.39

The Volt Typhoon campaign exposed a hard limit of signature-based detection: when an adversary “lives off the land,” using only legitimate built-in tools, there is no malware to flag. The official response — multi-agency guidance on hunting living-off-the-land activity — emphasizes behavioral analytics, anomaly detection, and comprehensive logging to distinguish malicious use of administrative tools from legitimate use.20 This is among the hardest problems in detection, and a major driver of investment in behavioral and machine-learning approaches.

In practice, these techniques compose into a layered detection-and-response stack. Telemetry from endpoints, network sensors, identity providers, and cloud control planes feeds a SIEM, where analytics and increasingly machine-learning models surface anomalies; SOAR playbooks then automate containment steps such as isolating a host or revoking a session. Because identity is now the primary attack surface (§02), much of the most valuable signal is identity-centric — impossible-travel logins, anomalous token use, sudden privilege escalation, and abuse of federated trust. Mapping detections to MITRE ATT&CK techniques lets defenders reason about coverage systematically: which adversary behaviors they can see, and where they are blind.39 The recurring lesson of the incidents in this article is that comprehensive logging and retention are preconditions for detection — an attacker living off the land is invisible precisely where telemetry is absent.

Assurance and certification

Because customers cannot independently audit every provider, a layer of third-party assurance lets organizations trust at scale. For cloud services sold to the U.S. government, FedRAMP provides a standardized, government-wide approach to security assessment and authorization — “authorize once, use many times.”40 In the commercial market, a SOC 2 report (governed by the AICPA) attests to a service organization’s controls around security, availability, and confidentiality,41 while FIPS 140-3 validation certifies that cryptographic modules meet federal security requirements — a baseline for any product making cryptographic claims to government buyers.42 Together with ISO/IEC 27001, these regimes form the assurance fabric that lets a hospital or bank place justified trust in a vendor it will never inspect itself.

Operational collaboration and incident reporting

Because the sector is overwhelmingly private, defense depends on information flowing between government and industry. CISA’s Joint Cyber Defense Collaborative (JCDC) brings together federal agencies and private-sector partners to plan and coordinate cyber defense — the mechanism that organized the national response to Log4Shell.43 Sector-specific Information Sharing and Analysis Centers (for IT, the IT-ISAC) circulate threat intelligence among members. And the Cyber Incident Reporting for Critical Infrastructure Act (CIRCIA), enacted in 2022, will require covered critical-infrastructure entities to report significant cyber incidents and ransomware payments to CISA within defined windows — an effort to fix the visibility gap the Cyber Safety Review Board found so crippling during Log4j, when no authoritative source could even measure exploitation.44

Cryptography and the quantum horizon

Encryption and public-key infrastructure underpin every trust relationship described so far — and a slow-moving threat now hangs over all of it. A sufficiently capable quantum computer would break the public-key cryptography (RSA, elliptic-curve) that secures most Internet traffic today. The threat is not purely future: adversaries can harvest now and decrypt later, capturing encrypted data today to decrypt once quantum capability arrives, which puts any data with a long secrecy lifetime at present risk.45

The defensive milestone arrived in August 2024, when NIST finalized its first three post-quantum cryptography standards after an eight-year global competition: FIPS 203 (ML-KEM) for key establishment, FIPS 204 (ML-DSA) and FIPS 205 (SLH-DSA) for digital signatures.45 For national-security systems, the NSA’s CNSA 2.0 suite sets a migration deadline approaching the end of the decade, with full transition expected by 2030 — making cryptographic agility, the ability to swap algorithms without re-architecting systems, an urgent design requirement across the sector.46

The throughline across all of this machinery is a shift in philosophy — away from perimeter defense and toward assumed compromise, constrained blast radius, and shared responsibility. The frameworks differ in scope and the tools differ in layer, but they converge on the same recognition that drives this entire article: in a horizontal sector, security cannot be a property of any single organization. It must be a property of the system.

Open problems & research

What we still cannot solve

For all the machinery in §04, the IT sector’s hardest problems remain unsolved — and several are getting worse. These are the frontiers where policy, investment, and research are most needed.

1. The open-source sustainability crisis

The modern digital economy runs on open-source software maintained, in critical cases, by a handful of unpaid volunteers. When the Cyber Safety Review Board — the body established under EO 14028 and modeled on the National Transportation Safety Board — investigated Log4Shell, it reached a sobering conclusion: Log4j is an “endemic vulnerability” whose vulnerable instances would persist in systems for a decade or longer, and the open-source ecosystem that produces such foundational code is not adequately resourced to secure it. The Board found that a single federal department spent some 33,000 staff-hours responding to Log4j alone, and it called for sustained public and private investment in open-source security as a matter of shared responsibility.47

The xz Utils backdoor crystallized the human dimension of this problem: a critical library guarded by one exhausted maintainer became the target of a patient, well-resourced social-engineering takeover. The near-miss was caught by luck, not by any systemic safeguard. The open research questions are concrete — how to fund maintenance of critical dependencies, how to verify maintainer identity and intent without destroying open collaboration, and how to detect malicious commits that hide outside the source repository entirely.

2. The vulnerability-data infrastructure is straining

The triage systems described in §04 depend on a data pipeline that is now buckling under volume. Beginning in early 2024, NIST’s National Vulnerability Database developed a large backlog of unenriched CVEs and could not keep pace with submissions — even after enriching a record number of vulnerabilities in 2025.48 NIST responded by narrowing its analysis to prioritize the highest-impact flaws — those in the KEV catalog, in federal software, or in “critical software” under EO 14028 — while deferring enrichment of everything else, leaving defenders who relied on complete NVD data with a coverage gap.49 A subsequent inspector-general review faulted the database’s management and ordered a formal recovery plan.50

The deeper lesson is structural: the global vulnerability-identification system that the entire security industry depends on rests on government-funded programs of surprisingly fragile capacity. Building resilient, well-resourced, and ideally distributed vulnerability-data infrastructure is an unglamorous but urgent need.

3. Concentration risk has no easy fix

CrowdStrike and the AWS outage made the systemic hazard impossible to ignore: a few cloud platforms, operating systems, and security vendors have become single points of failure for the entire economy. Yet the consolidation exists for good reasons — hyperscale providers deliver security and reliability that individual organizations cannot match. The research and policy tension is genuine and unresolved: how to capture the benefits of concentration while limiting its blast radius, through redundancy, multi-cloud and multi-vendor architectures, staged update rollouts, tested failover, and perhaps a frank conversation about whether some digital infrastructure is now “too big to fail” and should be regulated accordingly.

A resilience-engineering question

Both the CrowdStrike and AWS events were triggered by an automated process pushing a flawed change broadly and fast — the same automation that makes modern operations efficient. The open question is how to preserve that efficiency while engineering in the circuit-breakers, canary deployments, and graceful-degradation paths that keep a local fault from becoming a global one.

4. Artificial intelligence cuts both ways

AI is simultaneously reshaping attack and defense. On the offensive side, generative models are lowering the cost and raising the quality of phishing, social engineering, and malware development; by some industry estimates a large majority of attacks now incorporate AI in some form.51 On defense, AI accelerates anomaly detection, alert triage, and the behavioral analytics needed to catch living-off-the-land activity. A third front is emerging alongside these: securing AI systems themselves against adversarial manipulation, data poisoning, model theft, and prompt injection — a problem CISA has placed at the center of its AI roadmap and cross-sector risk assessments.52 As AI is woven into the IT sector’s own products, its failure modes become the sector’s failure modes.

5. The quantum migration is a multi-year race

Standardizing post-quantum algorithms (§04) was the easy part; deploying them across the world’s embedded cryptography is a migration of historic scale. Cryptography is buried in protocols, hardware, and decades-old systems with operational lifespans of ten to twenty years — many in critical infrastructure that cannot simply be swapped out. Against the “harvest now, decrypt later” clock, the research and engineering challenge is to inventory cryptographic usage, achieve true cryptographic agility, and complete the transition before adversaries’ capabilities mature.46

6. The workforce gap persists — and is changing shape

There are not enough people to do this work. The 2025 ISC2 Cybersecurity Workforce Study put the global shortfall near 4.8 million professionals, with hundreds of thousands of unfilled roles in the United States alone. Notably, the binding constraint has shifted: for the first time, economic and budget pressures — layoffs, hiring freezes, and cuts to security budgets — overtook the raw shortage of talent as the primary driver of understaffing, even as the rise of AI reshapes which skills are most in demand.53 A sector defended by overstretched teams under budgetary austerity is a structural vulnerability in its own right.

7. From espionage to disruption: the strategic shift

Finally, the Volt Typhoon disclosures mark a change in adversary intent that the sector is only beginning to reckon with: a shift from stealing secrets toward pre-positioning for disruption of physical infrastructure. Defending against a patient nation-state that lives off the land, hides in compromised consumer routers, and waits for a geopolitical trigger requires capabilities — deep behavioral visibility, threat hunting, and resilience engineering — that most critical-infrastructure operators, especially smaller ones, do not yet possess.19 Closing that gap, at the scale of an entire economy’s worth of small utilities and service providers, may be the defining cyber-defense challenge of the decade.

Synthesis

Security as a property of the system

The Information Technology sector is the load-bearing layer of American critical infrastructure. Its hardware, software, cloud platforms, identity systems, and routing fabric are the foundation on which energy, finance, healthcare, water, transportation, and government now operate — which is exactly why its compromise is never contained to itself.

The pattern recurs across every case in this article. SolarWinds turned a trusted update into a doorway for espionage across the federal government. Log4Shell and the file-transfer breaches showed how a single flaw in a ubiquitous component radiates through every sector that shares it. Kaseya weaponized the privileged access of managed-service tooling. CrowdStrike and the AWS outage proved that concentration alone — with no adversary at all — can ground flights and close hospitals worldwide. Volt Typhoon revealed an adversary treating the IT sector not as a target but as a beachhead into the physical world. Different mechanisms, one lesson: in a horizontal sector, the failure of one provider is the failure of many.

The most important aspects of securing the IT sector should not surprise anyone who understands the reality of our complex, interconnected world: security here is not the achievement of any single organization, but an emergent property of the whole system. — a paraphrase of the Cyber Safety Review Board’s central finding on Log4j

The mitigation ecosystem has matured impressively in response — NIST’s frameworks, Secure by Design, SBOMs, Zero Trust, the KEV catalog, post-quantum standards, and the operational collaboration of JCDC and CIRCIA all push toward the same goal of assumed compromise, constrained blast radius, and shared responsibility. But the open problems are sobering: an under-funded open-source commons, a straining vulnerability-data pipeline, irreducible concentration risk, the double-edged arrival of AI, a years-long cryptographic migration, a persistent workforce gap, and a nation-state shift from espionage to disruption.

None of these yields to a single control or a single agency. They yield, if at all, to a sustained, collective effort that treats the security of the digital substrate as the shared infrastructure problem it has become. The other fifteen sectors are counting on it — whether they know it or not.
