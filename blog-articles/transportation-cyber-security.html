Critical Infrastructure  ·  Cyber-Physical Security / TRANSPORTATION SYSTEMS SECTOR

The Lifeline
That Everything Rides On

Movement is the substrate of modern life — and increasingly, it runs on software. This is a technical account of how the United States Transportation Systems Sector is attacked, what breaks when it fails, how a single compromise can ripple into the energy, water, financial, and chemical sectors, and the frameworks now racing to close the gap.

Every other thing a society does — feeding itself, treating its water, moving money, generating power, manufacturing chemicals — assumes that people, fuel, and freight can get from one place to another. The United States government recognizes this dependence formally: the Transportation Systems Sector is one of the sixteen critical infrastructure sectors whose incapacitation would have a debilitating effect on national security, economic security, or public health and safety, as designated under Presidential Policy Directive 21.1 But transportation is not merely one sector among sixteen. Alongside energy, communications, and water, it is one of the four lifeline functions — the systems nearly every other sector depends on to recover after a major disruption.23

That status is what makes the sector's growing cyber exposure a national problem rather than an industry one. The Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA) and the Department of Transportation serve as co–Sector Risk Management Agencies for transportation, with the Transportation Security Administration (TSA) and the U.S. Coast Guard (USCG) carrying mode-specific regulatory authority.4 Their job has become harder for a simple reason: the machinery of movement has quietly merged with the machinery of computing, and the seam between them is where physical consequences now live.

Seven modes, continental scale

The sector divides into seven subsectors, or modes, each with distinct technology and distinct failure characteristics. Their combined scale is what gives a compromise its reach.4

The Department of Defense has designated roughly 30,000 miles of freight track and structure as critical to the mobilization and resupply of U.S. forces4 — a reminder that civilian transportation infrastructure is also a defense asset, and therefore a strategic target. Pipelines alone move nearly all of the nation's natural gas and about 65 percent of its hazardous liquids.4 A sector this large is not defended as a monolith; it is defended one control system at a time.

Industry-specific technology

The IT/OT divide, and why it matters here

Transportation security cannot be understood through the lens of conventional information technology alone, because the sector runs on two intertwined classes of system with opposite priorities. Information technology (IT) handles ticketing, scheduling, dispatch, email, logistics, and corporate data — its design priority is confidentiality and integrity. Operational technology (OT) is the hardware and software that senses and acts on the physical world: the supervisory control and data acquisition (SCADA) systems, distributed control systems (DCS), and programmable logic controllers (PLCs) that open valves, throw switches, hold trains apart, and steer cranes.5 For OT, the overriding priority is availability and safety: a control loop that stops, or that acts on falsified data, can injure people and wreck equipment.

NIST's SP 800-82 Rev. 3, the federal reference for securing operational technology, makes the consequence inversion explicit. Where IT risk management centers on data, OT risk management must account for physical outcomes — equipment damage, production loss, environmental harm, and personnel safety — and must therefore prioritize defense around the scenarios where a cyber event could cause physical harm.6 The single most important change in its latest revision is the elevation of IT/OT convergence itself as the primary attack surface: the historical "air gap" between business networks and control networks has largely dissolved, and the bridge between them is now the route most intrusions travel.6

A second structural problem compounds the first: much of the installed OT base speaks legacy industrial protocols that were never designed with security in mind. CISA has warned that many owners and operators across transportation, water, chemical, energy, and food sectors still run insecure legacy protocols that lack basic authentication and integrity checks — meaning a threat actor on the network can impersonate a device or modify a message in transit. Secure versions of these protocols have existed for more than two decades, but adoption has been slow because control systems are long-lived, safety-certified, and expensive to touch.7

Why a 30-year-old controller is still running

An industrial controller installed in 1998 may have a 25-year service life, sit inside a safety case that must be re-certified if modified, and have no vendor patches available. The economics that make this rational for an operator are exactly what make the sector a soft target: the defensive cycle moves in decades while the offensive cycle moves in days.

Mode-specific technology & risk

Where each mode breaks

Each mode has a signature technology and, with it, a signature weakness. Understanding the sector means understanding these distinct attack surfaces rather than treating "transportation" as uniform.

Aviation — trust in unauthenticated signals

Modern air traffic management depends on systems built for a pre-adversarial era. ADS-B (Automatic Dependent Surveillance–Broadcast), now central to U.S. airspace surveillance, has aircraft self-report their position over an open, unencrypted, unauthenticated broadcast. Because the protocol implements no transmitter authentication, it is comparatively simple to inject fabricated tracks — ghost aircraft — or to delete a real aircraft's messages so that it disappears from a controller's display.8 The navigation layer is equally exposed: civil GPS signals are public and unencrypted, so a modest spoofing transmitter can feed a receiver counterfeit signals and induce hazardously misleading position, velocity, and time errors. Spoofing is more dangerous than jamming precisely because the receiver cannot tell it is being deceived; the navigation solution simply becomes confidently wrong.9 The U.S. Department of Transportation's Volpe Center flagged civil-GPS spoofing as a transportation-infrastructure vulnerability as far back as 2001.9

Maritime — the crane as a foreign-built computer

Ports are dense knots of OT, and one component has become the focus of national concern: the ship-to-shore (STS) crane. Roughly 80 percent of the cranes moving trade at U.S. ports are manufactured in China and run Chinese software, with a single state-owned firm — Shanghai Zhenhua Heavy Industries (ZPMC) — dominating the market.10 The cranes carry sophisticated sensors and, depending on configuration, can be controlled, serviced, and programmed remotely, which is exactly what makes them attractive both as a sabotage vector and as a surveillance platform for tracking cargo.11 A congressional investigation reported that some installed cranes carried cellular modems not documented in any contract between U.S. ports and the manufacturer, and that the FBI found intelligence-gathering equipment associated with cranes arriving at the Port of Baltimore — leading lawmakers to describe the equipment as a potential "Trojan horse."12 Maritime risk extends past cranes to the LOGINK logistics platform, which aggregates sensitive shipping data, and to Chinese-made port-inspection equipment with embedded AI and facial-recognition capability.13

Surface rail & transit — IT that strands physical service

Passenger rail, freight rail, and transit run train-control, signaling, and dispatch systems that are increasingly networked, but the most common disruption is more mundane: ransomware that paralyzes the IT estate — ticketing, scheduling, dispatch support, payroll — and forces degraded manual operation even when train-control safety systems are untouched.14 The segregation of safety-critical control from business IT is the property that keeps a ticketing outage from becoming a collision; preserving that boundary is a core defensive objective for the mode.

Pipeline — billing systems with physical leverage

Pipelines are governed by SCADA and PLC systems that meter flow and operate pumping and compressor stations.5 The lesson of 2021, examined below, is that an operator can be forced to halt physical delivery even when the attack lands only on IT — because the business systems that authorize, bill, and account for product movement are themselves indispensable to running the line.

Highway & connected vehicles — the expanding edge

The highway mode now includes traffic-management systems, vehicle and driver-licensing systems, and the cyber systems used for operational management, alongside a fast-growing population of connected and increasingly automated vehicles.4 As vehicles, signals, and roadside units acquire wireless interfaces and shared dependence on satellite positioning, the attack surface migrates outward to the edge — and inherits the same unauthenticated-signal problem that haunts aviation's reliance on GPS.9

Adversaries & consequences

Who attacks, and what it costs

Two threat profiles dominate the transportation risk picture, and they require different defensive postures.

Financially motivated ransomware

Criminal ransomware crews target transportation because operators provide essential functions and therefore have an acute need to resume operations quickly — which makes them more likely to pay larger ransoms.15 Modern crews compound the pressure with double extortion: data is exfiltrated before encryption, so refusing to pay risks both prolonged downtime and the public release of stolen information.16 The SamSam campaign that struck U.S. critical-infrastructure victims illustrated the playbook — gain access, escalate to administrative rights, and deploy ransomware network-wide, because organization-wide infections command the largest payments.15

Nation-state pre-positioning

The more strategic threat is quieter. In 2023, Microsoft and U.S. agencies identified Volt Typhoon, a People's Republic of China state-sponsored actor, inside U.S. critical infrastructure — including communications, energy, transportation, and maritime ports.17 Volt Typhoon's tradecraft is distinctive: it favors "living off the land," using legitimate credentials and built-in system tools rather than custom malware, which lets it dwell undetected for long periods. At a Massachusetts municipal electric utility, the group moved laterally across IT and OT networks, harvested grid schematics and control-system procedures, and remained undiscovered for more than 300 days.18 The assessed purpose is not espionage for its own sake but pre-positioning — establishing footholds that could be used to disrupt or destabilize lifeline services during a future crisis or conflict.18

A ransomware crew wants to be noticed and paid. A pre-positioning actor wants to be invisible until the day it matters. Transportation must defend against both at once.

The consequence model follows from the sector's lifeline status. A localized OT failure rarely stays localized: because energy, water, communications, and the economy all draw on movement, a transportation disruption propagates. CISA's dependency analysis frames this directly — an asset failing within one infrastructure system degrades the systems that rely on it, and those second-order failures cascade further still, with dependencies that may be physical, geographic, cyber, or logical in nature.19

Case studies

Four incidents that set policy

U.S. transportation cyber policy was not written in the abstract. It was written in response to specific failures, each of which exposed a different fault line.

01 · Colonial Pipeline (2021) — IT compromise, physical shutdown

Colonial Pipeline is the largest pipeline for refined petroleum products in the United States, running some 5,500 miles from Gulf Coast refineries toward the New York City area.20 In May 2021, the DarkSide ransomware group gained entry through an outdated, exposed VPN account and exfiltrated roughly 100 gigabytes of data.21 Critically, the attack hit the company's IT systems — not its OT — yet Colonial proactively shut the line down for about five days, triggering panic-buying and localized fuel shortages across the Southeast.20 The company paid roughly US$4.4 million in ransom.16 The episode's enduring lesson is that the IT/OT distinction offers little comfort to the public: when the systems that bill and account for product are down, the product stops moving regardless of whether the control systems were ever touched.5

The policy response was swift and precedent-setting. Within days, TSA issued Security Directive Pipeline-2021-01 — converting what had been a regime of voluntary guidance into mandatory requirements for critical pipeline operators, including incident reporting and cybersecurity assessments.5 The Colonial event, together with the SolarWinds/Sunburst supply-chain compromise, became the proximate cause of nearly every transportation cyber regulation that followed.20

02 · SFMTA "Muni" (2016) — the first transit ransomware

Over Thanksgiving weekend 2016, the San Francisco Municipal Transportation Agency was hit by ransomware widely regarded as the first successful such attack on a transit agency.22 The malware encrypted on the order of 900 to 2,000 office computers and reached fare-ticketing machines; the agency turned off ticket vending machines and fare gates as a precaution and let riders travel free while it recovered from backups.14 What kept the incident from becoming a safety event was architectural: ticketing and email were separate from train-control and safety systems, so Muni operations and safety were unaffected.14 The case is still taught as a demonstration of two principles — segregate safety-critical OT from business IT, and keep restorable backups.22

03 · The FAA NOTAM outage (2023) — fragility without an attacker

Not every catastrophic disruption requires an adversary; some merely reveal how little margin a legacy system has. In January 2023, the FAA's Notice to Air Missions (NOTAM) system — which distributes safety-critical flight hazard information — failed, leading to the first nationwide ground stop of U.S. departures in decades, with roughly 1,300 flights canceled and nearly 10,000 delayed.23 The cause was traced to a database file problem rather than an attack, but the systemic point stands: the FAA subsequently implemented a staggered backup-database approach specifically to avoid a cascading system failure, and Congress passed the NOTAM Improvement Act mandating modernization and a backup system.24 A system that can be felled by an internal error is, by definition, also exposed to a deliberate one.

04 · The ZPMC cranes (2024) — supply chain as attack surface

The crane concern produced the most assertive recent federal action. In February 2024, the President signed Executive Order 14116, expanding DHS and Coast Guard authority over maritime cybersecurity, and the Coast Guard issued MARSEC Directive 105-4 imposing cyber risk-management requirements on owners and operators of Chinese-manufactured STS cranes — the directive's specific contents withheld as security-sensitive.11 The administration declined a "rip-and-replace" mandate, opting instead to manage risk on the installed base while investing over US$20 billion to re-establish domestic crane manufacturing.11 Of more than 200 PRC-manufactured cranes at U.S. ports, Coast Guard cyber teams had assessed 92 at the time of the announcement.25

Cross-sector consequences

The cascade: one sector, sixteen exposures

The reason a transportation compromise is a whole-of-nation problem is that transportation is a lifeline on which the other fifteen critical-infrastructure sectors depend. CISA groups energy, communications, water, and transportation together precisely because they are fundamental to the operation of almost all other sectors and to one another.2 When movement degrades, the failure does not announce itself as a transportation problem; it surfaces as a fuel shortage, an empty hospital shelf, an idle treatment plant, or a stalled factory.

The pathways are concrete, not theoretical:

Energy. Pipelines are simultaneously a transportation mode and the delivery mechanism for the fuels that power generation, heating, and the vehicle fleets every other sector relies on. The Colonial shutdown showed how a single pipeline halt produces regional fuel scarcity within days.20 The dependency runs both ways — transportation needs electricity to operate, and energy needs transportation to deliver fuel — making energy and transportation mutually dependent.19

Water and wastewater & chemical. Treatment plants depend on the delivery of treatment chemicals (chlorine, coagulants) that move by truck, rail, and pipeline. A disruption to hazardous-materials transport — or to the chemical plants that produce those inputs — can idle a water utility that is itself perfectly healthy. CISA's secure-OT-communications work groups water, chemical, and transportation together for exactly this reason: they share both the vulnerable protocols and the supply linkages.7

Food & agriculture, healthcare, financial. Modern supply chains run just-in-time, holding minimal inventory on the assumption that freight will arrive on schedule. After Superstorm Sandy, the National Infrastructure Advisory Council documented how unforeseen and severe liquid-fuel shortages in New Jersey — a transportation-and-energy failure — rippled through the regional economy, and how substitutes such as "bus bridges" became the only way to maintain functional continuity while damaged assets were restored.3 Hospitals depend on supply deliveries and on staff being able to reach work; financial services depend on cash logistics and on the same communications and power that transportation underpins.

Defense. Because the DoD has designated tens of thousands of miles of commercial track as critical to force mobilization,4 a civilian rail compromise is also a potential brake on military readiness — the strategic logic that makes pre-positioning actors interested in the sector in the first place.18

Mitigation

Frameworks, standards, and the tools that apply them

Defending the sector is a layered exercise spanning voluntary frameworks, binding regulation, information sharing, and concrete control-system engineering. No single instrument is sufficient; the practical state of the art is to combine them.

The framework layer

The foundational reference is the NIST Cybersecurity Framework, whose 2.0 revision organizes security around six functions — Govern, Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, and Recover. CISA translated the CSF specifically for this sector in its Transportation Systems Sector Cybersecurity Framework Implementation Guidance, which lets owners and operators characterize their current posture, find tools and standards to support implementation, and establish risk-based priorities where no formal program exists.26 For the OT environment specifically, NIST SP 800-82 Rev. 3 supplies the control selection and overlay, endorsing defense-in-depth built on the Purdue reference model and a consequence-based approach to risk.6

The complementary engineering standard is the ISA/IEC 62443 family, developed by control-system engineers rather than IT specialists. Its defining contributions are the zone-and-conduit model — partitioning a plant into security zones connected only through controlled conduits — and a graduated Security Level scale (SL 1–4) defined by the capability of the threat actor a control must withstand, from casual to nation-state.27 In practice, mature programs use IEC 62443 for governance structure, SP 800-82 for control selection, and the CSF's Govern function for executive accountability.27

The defender's stack, in one line

Govern with NIST CSF 2.0 → structure the program with IEC 62443 zones & conduits → select OT controls with NIST SP 800-82r3 → translate to the sector with the TSS Implementation Guidance.

The regulatory layer

Post-Colonial, transportation cybersecurity shifted decisively from voluntary to mandatory. The current architecture has several pillars:

TSA security directives. Beginning in May 2021, TSA issued a series of directives for pipeline and, later, freight and passenger rail. The current pipeline regime requires covered operators to maintain a TSA-approved Cybersecurity Implementation Plan (CIP) describing the specific measures protecting their Critical Cyber Systems, and a Cybersecurity Incident Response Plan (CIRP) that must be exercised annually against at least two objectives.28 These directives are not one-off: the rail directive, for instance, was renewed with revisions in late 2025 to run through October 2026, keeping the performance-based requirements continuously in force.29 In March 2023, TSA extended emergency cybersecurity requirements to airport and aircraft operators, bringing aviation into the same performance-based posture.30

The proposed surface rule. In November 2024, TSA published the Enhancing Surface Cyber Risk Management notice of proposed rulemaking, which would codify the directives into durable regulation. Higher-risk pipeline, freight-rail, passenger-rail, and rail-transit operators would have to establish comprehensive cyber risk-management programs aligned to the NIST CSF and CISA's cross-sector performance goals, conduct annual enterprise-wide evaluations against a target profile, and report cybersecurity incidents to CISA within 24 hours; certain bus operators would face lighter reporting duties.28 TSA estimated the rule would reach roughly 300 entities — about 73 freight railroads, 34 public-transportation systems, 71 intercity bus operators, and 115 pipeline facilities.31

Maritime. Executive Order 14116 and the Coast Guard's MARSEC Directive 105-4 brought ports under cyber risk-management requirements, with the Coast Guard also pursuing a rulemaking to set minimum cybersecurity requirements for U.S.-flagged vessels, Outer Continental Shelf facilities, and MTSA-regulated facilities.25

Cross-cutting reporting. The Cyber Incident Reporting for Critical Infrastructure Act of 2022 (CIRCIA) layers in a general obligation, requiring covered entities to report significant cyber incidents to CISA within 72 hours and ransom payments within 24 hours; TSA designed its surface rule to dovetail with CIRCIA so operators are not subject to conflicting reporting clocks.20

The information-sharing layer

Because most infrastructure is privately owned, defense depends on collective intelligence. The Surface Transportation and Public Transportation Information Sharing and Analysis Centers (ST-ISAC and PT-ISAC) collect, analyze, and disseminate threat alerts and incident reports across the mode, and help government understand sector-wide impact — a free service that TSA actively points operators toward.32 These sit within the broader national coordination architecture that National Security Memorandum 22 reorganized in 2024, assigning CISA the role of national coordinator and tasking each Sector Risk Management Agency to identify, assess, and prioritize risk and feed it into a cross-sector assessment.33

The engineering layer — what operators actually do

Frameworks become protection only through specific control-system practice. The recurring techniques across mature transportation OT programs include:

  • Segmentation along the Purdue model. Isolating field devices and controllers (Levels 0–1) from supervisory and enterprise layers, with traffic crossing only through controlled conduits — the structural defense against the lateral movement that defines both ransomware and pre-positioning.27
  • Passive OT network monitoring. Baselining normal industrial-protocol behavior and alerting on deviations — unexpected function codes, new devices, anomalous traffic to control zones — without injecting active polling that could disrupt deterministic control loops.27
  • Securing the convergence boundary. Hardening remote access and vendor connections (the Colonial entry vector) and migrating off unauthenticated legacy protocols toward their secured variants.7
  • Tested incident-response and recovery. Exercised CIRPs and restorable, segregated backups — the property that let SFMTA recover without paying and that pipeline operators must now exercise annually.2822
  • Planned isolation and recovery. CISA's 2026 "CI Fortify" guidance pushes transportation and other operators to plan for a geopolitical crisis by proactively disconnecting OT from third-party and business networks ("isolation") and rehearsing restoration ("recovery") — a posture that treats nation-state pre-positioning as a planning assumption rather than a contingency.34

Open problems

What remains unsolved

Regulation and frameworks have closed the most egregious gaps, but the sector's hardest problems are structural and persistent. The Government Accountability Office, reviewing TSA's progress, has repeatedly found that taking steps is not the same as reducing risk.

Measuring whether any of it works

GAO's central critique is methodological: TSA has built workforce capacity and issued requirements, but still needs to develop ways to measure the effectiveness of its efforts against threats such as ransomware.35 A compliance regime that cannot demonstrate it reduces incidents risks becoming an audit ritual rather than a defense.

The legacy and convergence trap

The deepest technical problem is the one introduced at the outset: an installed base of long-lived OT speaking unauthenticated protocols, now exposed by IT/OT convergence.6 Secured protocol variants have existed for over twenty years yet remain widely unadopted because of the cost and safety friction of touching certified systems.7 Closing this gap is less a research problem than an economic and incentive one — but research into non-disruptive retrofits, transparent authentication overlays, and safe in-service migration would materially accelerate it.

Supply-chain trust without domestic capacity

The crane episode exposed a problem no firewall solves: critical equipment built and remotely serviceable by a strategic competitor, in a market where domestic alternatives barely exist.11 The chosen response — manage risk on the installed base while spending years and tens of billions to rebuild manufacturing capacity25 — is an admission that for some hardware dependencies, cybersecurity controls are a holding action, not a cure.

Positioning, navigation, and timing as single points of failure

Aviation, maritime, surface, and emerging automated modes share a brittle dependence on unauthenticated GPS/GNSS signals for both position and precise time.9 Resilient PNT — signal-authentication schemes, multi-constellation and terrestrial backups, and machine-learning spoofing detection that works within the size, weight, and power limits of small platforms — is an active and unfinished research frontier with direct safety stakes.8

The next adversary: AI on both sides

The threat is also evolving faster than the rulebook. Proposed federal legislation would compel CISA to update every sector's cybersecurity plan to address risks amplified by emerging technology — including attempts to compromise AI systems and their supply chains, from training data to the computing environments used to operate them.36 As transportation operators adopt AI for anomaly detection, predictive maintenance, and increasingly for autonomy itself, the models become both a defensive multiplier and a new attack surface.

Thin defenders at the edges

Finally, the sector's weakest links are often its smallest operators — municipal transit agencies and small utilities with thin budgets and thin security staff. The Massachusetts municipal utility where Volt Typhoon dwelt for 300 days was undone by an unpatched firewall18; the SFMTA attack exploited an underfunded transit IT estate.22 National resilience is gated by the security of organizations least able to afford it — an argument for shared services, free intelligence channels, and funded mandates rather than unfunded ones.

Conclusion

The road ahead

The United States has, in the span of a few years, transformed transportation cybersecurity from a patchwork of voluntary best practices into a regulated, framework-driven discipline — pushed there by a pipeline shutdown, a transit ransomware first, a national ground stop, and a fleet of foreign-built cranes. The instruments now exist: performance-based directives, a proposed durable rule, OT-specific standards, sector implementation guidance, mandatory reporting, and information-sharing channels.

What remains is the harder work that frameworks cannot do by themselves — proving effectiveness, retiring unauthenticated legacy systems without breaking the physical processes that depend on them, rebuilding trusted supply chains, hardening the navigation signals every mode now relies on, and lifting the security floor of the smallest operators. Transportation is the lifeline the other sectors ride on; the measure of the sector's defense is not whether it can survive an attack in isolation, but whether the cascade stops at its door.

A sector this interconnected is only as secure as the assumption that, tomorrow morning, things will still move.
Editorial note

This article reflects the U.S. policy and threat landscape through early 2026. The TSA Enhancing Surface Cyber Risk Management rule remained at the proposed stage following the close of its comment period, with security directives serving as the operative regime in the interim; readers should verify the current status of pending rulemakings and directives, which change frequently.
