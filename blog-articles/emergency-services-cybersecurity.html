§ 01The Sector

The sector that guards every other sector

Every other entry in the catalog of American critical infrastructure — the power grid, the pipelines, the water plants, the banks, the chemical works — shares an assumption so basic it is rarely stated: that when something goes catastrophically wrong, someone will answer the call. The Emergency Services Sector is that someone. It is the only one of the nation’s sixteen critical infrastructure sectors whose primary product is the protection of all the others, which makes its own security a uniquely load-bearing problem. The Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA), inside the Department of Homeland Security, is its designated Sector Risk Management Agency.1

Officially, the sector is a federation of five disciplines — law enforcement, fire and rescue, emergency medical services, emergency management, and public works — backed by specialized capabilities such as public safety communications, search and rescue, and hazardous-materials response. Its 2015 Sector-Specific Plan describes it in plain terms: because its operations are the first line of defense for nearly all of the other sectors, a failure or disruption could mean significant loss of life, major public-health emergencies, long-term economic damage, and cascading harm across the rest of critical infrastructure.2 The same document underscores a structural fact that defines the cybersecurity challenge: the vast majority of emergency services are organized, funded, and operated at the state, local, tribal, and territorial (SLTT) level, and are therefore highly distributed and locally governed.2

That distribution is not an abstraction. The country routes its emergencies through roughly 5,748 primary and secondary Public Safety Answering Points (PSAPs) — the 911 centers — fielding on the order of 240 million calls a year, the great majority now originating from wireless devices.3 Each of those centers is, in effect, its own small enterprise: its own procurement, its own network, its own staff, its own budget, and increasingly its own attack surface. There is no single “911 system” to defend. There are thousands.

It is the only critical infrastructure sector whose primary product is the protection of all the others.

Why the policy framework matters

The sector designation is not bureaucratic trivia; it determines who is responsible when defenses fail. In April 2024 the White House issued National Security Memorandum 22 (NSM-22), the first comprehensive rewrite of national critical-infrastructure policy in over a decade. It replaced the Obama-era Presidential Policy Directive 21 and named CISA the National Coordinator for the Security and Resilience of Critical Infrastructure, while explicitly reframing the threat environment around nation-state competition, artificial intelligence, and the convergence of information technology with operational technology.4 Crucially for emergency services, NSM-22 reaffirmed the existing sixteen sectors and their Sector Risk Management Agencies rather than redrawing the map, and it signaled a shift away from purely voluntary measures toward minimum security and resilience requirements.5

Within that architecture, Emergency Services is the seventh of sixteen sectors, with DHS and CISA as its risk-management lead.6 The point of the framework is coordination: a ransomware crew that locks a county dispatch center is not just attacking that county; it is degrading a node in a national system whose failures propagate. The rest of this briefing follows that propagation — first into the technology the sector runs on, then into the threats against it, then outward into the other fifteen sectors that depend on it.

§ 02The Technology

A response built on software

A generation ago, the emergency-services technology stack was electromechanical and largely air-gapped by accident: copper trunk lines, analog radios, paper run-cards. That world is gone. The modern response runs on Internet Protocol, commercial cloud, and general-purpose computing, and every gain in capability has widened the surface an attacker can reach. To understand the risk, it helps to walk the actual systems on a watch floor.

Field Note — Anatomy of a 911 center
What a PSAP actually is

A Public Safety Answering Point is the call center where 911 calls terminate. Call-takers work call-handling equipment tied to a Computer-Aided Dispatch (CAD) system, which plots caller location, recommends the nearest available unit, and logs every action. Dispatchers reach field units over land mobile radio; units carry mobile data computers that query records systems. A modern PSAP is therefore not one system but a tightly coupled mesh of telephony, mapping, dispatch, radio, and records — any one of which, if disrupted, degrades the whole.

Next Generation 911 and the ESInet

The defining transition in the sector is the move from legacy analog 911 to Next Generation 911 (NG911), an IP-based architecture that lets the public reach help by voice, text, image, and eventually video, and that allows centers to share calls and data across regions. CISA is blunt about the trade-off: because NG911 systems operate on an IP platform and interconnect a wide range of public and private networks — wireless carriers, the internet, and the legacy phone network — they introduce new vectors of attack that can disrupt or disable PSAP operations and complicate cyber-risk management at every level of government.7

The backbone of NG911 is the Emergency Services IP Network (ESInet) — a managed IP network that carries 911 traffic the way the public internet carries everything else, using packet routing rather than the dedicated circuits of the old system. CISA’s NG911 cybersecurity guidance warns that a breach can do far more than drop calls: it can expose sensitive records including citizens’ personal information, law-enforcement data, critical-infrastructure information, and healthcare data, with attendant legal liability.8 The transition is also unfinished and expensive. An NTIA cost assessment put the remaining nationwide bill for completing NG911 at between roughly $5.8 billion and $9.27 billion, and there is no federal deadline forcing the pace — jurisdictions migrate at wildly different speeds, leaving a long, awkward period in which legacy and next-generation systems coexist and must both be defended.9

FirstNet: a dedicated lane for responders

Voice radio is no longer the only mission-critical link. After the 9/11 Commission found that incompatible communications had cost lives, Congress in 2012 created the First Responder Network Authority — an independent authority within the Department of Commerce — and set aside 20 MHz of prized 700 MHz “Band 14” spectrum for a nationwide public-safety broadband network; in 2017 the Authority signed a 25-year partnership with AT&T to build and run it.10 The network, FirstNet, launched its Band 14 buildout and delivered its dedicated, physically separate “FirstNet Core” in 2018; the core is the security-relevant piece, isolating public-safety traffic from the commercial network.11 By the time of its 2024 reinvestment, FirstNet carried more than 5.5 million connections across roughly 27,500 public-safety agencies, with always-on priority and preemption so that responders keep bandwidth even when commercial networks are saturated.12 The concentration is a double-edged design: a single, purpose-built network is easier to harden — and a more consequential target.

The radio problem nobody fixed

Beneath the broadband layer, day-to-day tactical communication still rides on land mobile radio, overwhelmingly the Project 25 (P25) digital standard used by police, fire, and EMS across the country. P25 offers encryption — and that encryption has been shown, repeatedly, to be fragile in practice. A landmark University of Pennsylvania study presented to the public-safety community found that operators frequently left encryption off, or believed their traffic was encrypted when it was not, that a roughly $30 toy could jam the signal, and that the protocol could be coerced into transmitting on command so that direction-finding gear could locate a radio.13 The same researchers’ security analysis documented protocol, implementation, and user-interface weaknesses that leak information to a passive eavesdropper and enable highly efficient, hard-to-detect active attacks — including traffic analysis that surreptitiously locates a radio’s user and selective jamming that targets specific traffic such as encrypted or key-management messages.14 More than a decade later, P25 remains ubiquitous, and the gap between “has encryption” and “is secure” remains a live operational hazard.

The data underneath: CJIS and the records systems

Finally, emergency services run on sensitive data. Officers and dispatchers query the FBI’s National Crime Information Center and a constellation of records systems holding criminal-justice information, all governed by the FBI’s CJIS Security Policy. That policy is not advisory: as of October 1, 2024, any agency touching criminal-justice information must use multi-factor authentication, satisfying two of three factor categories — something you know, something you have, something you are — with annual self-audits and a formal audit every three years.15 The most recent rewrite points agencies to NIST’s digital-identity guidance for phishing-resistant authentication and is described by its own custodians as roughly half new material, a measure of how fast the security baseline for police data is moving.16 Each system in this stack — telephony, dispatch, radio, broadband, records — is a dependency. The next section is what happens when those dependencies are attacked.

§ 03The Threat Landscape

The threat landscape

The threats against emergency services are not exotic. They are the same ransomware crews, denial-of-service techniques, and supply-chain fragilities that menace every enterprise — but aimed at systems where the cost of downtime is measured in minutes that decide whether someone lives. What follows are the dominant patterns, each illustrated by an incident that actually happened.

Ransomware: the dominant threat

Ransomware is the signature crime against public safety because it does not require sophistication so much as access. In March 2018, the CAD system supporting Baltimore’s 911 and 311 calls was hit; the city took the affected server offline and reverted to manual dispatch — call-takers relaying details to dispatchers by voice and paper rather than by screen — for about 17 hours before service was restored.17 The intrusion was mundane in origin: an IT team troubleshooting an unrelated problem had inadvertently changed a firewall and left a port open to the internet for roughly a day.17 Officials stressed that calls were still answered, but the episode established the template — a single misconfiguration, a mission-critical dispatch system down, and a fallback to a slower manual mode that erodes response time exactly when it matters.18

Five years later, Dallas showed the upper bound. The Royal ransomware group quietly held access inside the city’s network for about a month — entering through compromised credentials and abusing third-party remote-management tools — before detonating on May 3, 2023.19 The blast radius spanned city government, but the public-safety impact was the headline: the computer-aided dispatch systems for police, fire-rescue, and emergency medical services were knocked offline, forcing call-takers and officers back to handwriting.19 During the month of dwell time, the attackers exfiltrated an estimated 1.169 terabytes of data,20 and the breach ultimately exposed the personal — including health — information of more than 30,000 individuals, with the city council approving an $8.5 million remediation budget.21 Ransomware against a 911 center is thus two attacks at once: an availability attack that disables dispatch, and a confidentiality attack that spills criminal-justice and medical records.

Denial of the line: TDoS and the fragility of 911

A second class of attack targets the phone line itself. Telephony Denial of Service (TDoS) floods a PSAP with calls until legitimate emergencies cannot get through — the digital equivalent of jamming the only door to the building. The canonical case is almost accidental in character. In October 2016, an 18-year-old shared a link containing an iOS exploit that forced any iPhone visiting it to dial 911 repeatedly. Within minutes the 911 center in Surprise, Arizona received more than 100 hang-up calls, and the Maricopa County Sheriff’s Office warned that the volume had the potential to shut down 911 service across the county.22 The Department of Homeland Security ultimately tied the malware to disrupted 911 operations across twelve states.23 The link had been clicked roughly 1,849 times, and researchers have since underlined the uncomfortable arithmetic it exposed: it can take only a few thousand compromised smartphones to overwhelm an entire state’s 911 capacity.24

Field Note — TDoS vs. DDoS

TDoS saturates the voice path — the 911 trunks and call-takers — with bogus calls. A DDoS (Distributed Denial of Service) saturates the data path — the ESInet and web-facing systems — with bogus traffic. NG911’s move to IP means a center can now be hit on both fronts at once, and the same internet exposure that lets it receive a text message also lets it receive an attack.

Swatting: weaponizing the response itself

The most insidious attacks do not disable emergency services — they turn the response into the weapon. Swatting is the act of placing a hoax emergency call to provoke an armed police response at a victim’s address. It is a cyber-physical crime in the fullest sense: perpetrators routinely use caller-ID spoofing, VoIP, anonymizing tools, AI-generated voices, and even “swatting by proxy” — feeding a false claim to a crisis hotline that then calls police on the swatter’s behalf.25 The consequences are not hypothetical: a 2017 swatting call over a video-game dispute led police to fatally shoot an uninvolved man in Wichita, Kansas.25 The volume has since exploded — the FBI, which began building a national swatting database in 2023, has documented hundreds of incidents a year against schools, houses of worship, judges, and elected officials, many using spoofed numbers to mask their origin.26 In 2025 one prolific offender was sentenced to four years in federal prison for at least 375 hoax calls placed across the country, a marker of how seriously the crime is now prosecuted.25

The patient adversary: nation-state pre-positioning

The most strategically alarming threat is the one designed never to be noticed until it is too late. In a February 2024 advisory, CISA, the NSA, and the FBI assessed that the People’s Republic of China’s “Volt Typhoon” group had compromised the IT environments of multiple U.S. critical-infrastructure organizations — primarily in the Communications, Energy, Transportation, and Water and Wastewater sectors — not to steal data but to pre-position for disruptive or destructive attacks in the event of a future crisis, using stealthy “living off the land” techniques that blend into normal administration.27 A companion concern, the “Salt Typhoon” campaign, established long-term covert access in backbone telecommunications infrastructure, positioning an adversary to disrupt critical functions at a moment of its choosing.28 For emergency services the implication is grave: an adversary capable of degrading the lifeline sectors during a conflict has every incentive to degrade the response to that degradation at the same moment, silencing the call for help at the very instant it is needed most.

The dependency lesson: when nobody attacked at all

Not every catastrophic failure is malicious, and the most instructive recent event was an accident. On July 19, 2024, a faulty content update from the cybersecurity vendor CrowdStrike crashed Windows machines worldwide, and emergency services were among the hardest hit. The Congressional Research Service documented 911 centers where call-takers could still receive voice calls but their computer systems were inoperable, including Phoenix, whose police department reported its computerized dispatch center was affected.29 The scale was staggering: an estimated 8.5 million Windows machines, roughly one percent of all such devices worldwide, were knocked out.30 Hospitals simultaneously diverted ambulances and delayed procedures, with the American Hospital Association reporting that emergency call centers going dark was itself a source of indirect harm to patient care.31 The lesson cuts deeper than any single ransomware case: emergency services now share a software supply chain with everyone else, and a single defective update — pushed automatically, trusted implicitly — can blind the watch floor without a single adversary lifting a finger — and, as the next section shows, a failure at this hub never stays contained to the hub.

§ 04Cross-Sector Cascade

When the lifeline fails

The reason a county dispatch outage is a national-security problem and not merely a local one is interdependency. Emergency services sit at the center of the critical-infrastructure web in two opposite directions at once. Outward, the sector is the response mechanism for incidents in every other sector — its own Sector-Specific Plan calls it the first line of defense for nearly all of them.2 Inward, the sector cannot function without the services the other sectors provide: it needs the Communications sector to carry its calls, the Energy sector to power its centers, the Water sector for fire suppression, the Information Technology sector for its software, the Financial sector for payroll and procurement, and the Healthcare sector as the destination for its patients. Compromise anywhere in that ring can starve the hub; compromise of the hub degrades the entire ring’s ability to recover.

The sixteen sectors and their federal risk-management leads make the stakes concrete. Emergency Services is one node; every other node is a customer of its response and, in several cases, a supplier of something it cannot do without.

The cascade runs both ways

The cleanest illustration of bidirectional cascade is the relationship between Emergency Services and Healthcare. Emergency medical services deliver patients to hospitals; when a hospital is crippled by ransomware, it goes on ambulance diversion, pushing EMS transports to more distant facilities and lengthening the time between a patient’s pickup and treatment. The health-outcome evidence is now substantial. A study of hospital ransomware attacks found that for patients already admitted when an attack began, in-hospital mortality rose by between 1.28 and 1.87 percentage points — a relative increase of roughly 36 to 55 percent over the pre-attack baseline.32 A separate analysis of two emergency departments adjacent to a hospital under a month-long attack found significant spillover at the unaffected facilities: emergency medical services arrivals rose about 35 percent, alongside increases in waiting times, patients leaving without being seen, and countywide EMS diversion — leading the authors to argue that healthcare cyberattacks should be treated as regional disasters requiring coordinated planning.33 And the threat is intensifying: U.S. healthcare organizations absorbed roughly 460 ransomware attacks in a single recent year, up from 238 the year before.34 An attack on the Healthcare sector is therefore, within hours, an attack on the Emergency Services sector’s ability to function — the bidirectional arrow in Figure 1 made literal.

An adversary who can darken the grid has every reason to silence the call for help at the same moment.

The compound-attack scenario

This is why critical-infrastructure planners increasingly reason about simultaneity rather than isolated incidents. Recall that Volt Typhoon was assessed to be pre-positioning specifically to disrupt the Communications, Energy, Transportation, and Water sectors during a future crisis.27 Now layer the CrowdStrike lesson on top: a single failure in shared software blinded 911 centers across the country in an afternoon, with no malice required.29 The compound scenario writes itself — a deliberate strike on lifeline sectors, timed to coincide with a strike on, or a coincidental failure of, the emergency-communications systems meant to coordinate the response. In that scenario the hub and the spokes fail together, and the mechanism society relies on to recover from infrastructure attacks is itself unavailable. Defending emergency services is, in this light, not parochial; it is a precondition for the resilience of every other sector. The remainder of this briefing turns to how that defense is actually mounted.

§ 05Mitigation

Defending the watch floor

There is no single control that secures an emergency-services agency, and no national authority that can simply mandate one for thousands of independent jurisdictions. What exists instead is a layered system of frameworks, mandates, shared services, funding, and operational practice. The layers are uneven and contested — but together they describe the state of the art.

The framework layer

The common language of the field is the NIST Cybersecurity Framework, whose version 2.0, published in February 2024, was its first major revision in a decade. CSF 2.0 expanded the framework’s scope from critical infrastructure to organizations of every kind and added a sixth core function, Govern, to the original five — Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, and Recover — organizing cybersecurity outcomes into six functions, 22 categories, and 106 subcategories.35 The addition of Govern is precisely the gap that small public-safety agencies most often fall into: not a missing tool, but a missing owner, policy, and risk-management process. Beneath the framework sit the detailed NIST special publications agencies map to — among them SP 800-53 for controls, SP 800-61 for incident handling, SP 800-82 for operational-technology security, and SP 800-207 for Zero Trust Architecture, the “never trust, always verify” model that CISA now actively promotes for the converged IT/OT environments emergency services increasingly run.

The mandate layer

Above the voluntary framework sit rules with teeth. The CJIS Security Policy already obliges any agency handling criminal-justice information to deploy phishing-resistant multi-factor authentication, and its latest evolution — CJIS 6.0 — pushes agencies further, away from checklist compliance and toward continuous risk management, governance, and demonstrable evidence of control, with full compliance expected by October 1, 2027.36 For 911 specifically, the National Emergency Number Association’s NG-SEC standard makes cybersecurity mandatory for any organization operating part of an NG911 system, requiring a documented security program, formal risk assessments, and explicit plans for denial-of-service attacks.9 And at the policy apex, NSM-22 has begun shifting the federal posture from voluntary best practice toward minimum security and resilience requirements across sectors,4 complemented by incident-reporting obligations advancing under the Cyber Incident Reporting for Critical Infrastructure Act.6

Field Note — Living off the land

The tradecraft behind the most dangerous intrusions, including Volt Typhoon, is “living off the land”: instead of dropping detectable malware, the attacker abuses legitimate administrative tools already present on the network, blending into normal activity. The defensive answer is not a single product but disciplined logging, behavioral analytics, and anomaly detection — capabilities that small PSAPs are least equipped to staff, which is exactly why shared, regional defense matters so much.

The shared-defense and funding layer

Because most agencies cannot defend themselves alone, the model relies on pooled capability. CISA’s Cyber Resilient 911 (CR911) program, established through the March 2022 Consolidated Appropriations Act, exists specifically to address the operational cybersecurity challenges emergency communications centers face as they migrate to NG911.37 It is paired with the SAFECOM/NCSWIC 911 Cybersecurity Resource Hub, a no-cost clearinghouse that helps centers report incidents, study real-world cases, and adopt best practices.7 On the funding side, the State and Local Cybersecurity Grant Program (SLCGP) — created by the 2021 infrastructure law and jointly run by CISA and FEMA — appropriated $1 billion over four years, deliberately structured to push the bulk of its funding down to the least-mature local governments that operate so much of the sector.38 And for two decades the connective tissue of shared defense has been the Multi-State Information Sharing and Analysis Center (MS-ISAC), run by the nonprofit Center for Internet Security, which provided SLTT governments with 24/7 threat monitoring, incident response, and the Albert network sensors that watch for intrusions.39 As the next section shows, that connective tissue has just been badly frayed.

The operational layer

None of the above substitutes for execution. The practices that actually blunt the incidents catalogued earlier are unglamorous and well understood: enforce multi-factor authentication everywhere; segment networks so that a foothold in administrative IT cannot reach dispatch or radio control; deploy endpoint detection and response and centralized logging; maintain immutable, tested backups so that ransomware means recovery rather than ransom; rehearse incident response and continuity-of-operations plans through tabletop exercises; and build genuine redundancy into 911 itself — the ability to reroute calls to neighboring PSAPs and to fall back to manual dispatch without losing the thread. The federal posture is converging on this operational realism: CISA’s recent guidance presses critical-infrastructure operators to plan explicitly for the ability to operate in isolation and recover when upstream services or vendors fail — precisely the discipline the CrowdStrike outage showed was missing.40

§ 06Open Problems

Open problems & the frontier

The defenses just described are real, but they are not winning cleanly. The sector’s hardest problems are structural — matters of money, governance, and pace — and several took a sharp turn for the worse in 2025. These are the open fronts where research, investment, and policy are most urgently needed.

The funding cliff

The most immediate problem is that the shared-defense scaffolding is collapsing just as the threat intensifies. The State and Local Cybersecurity Grant Program had, by the federal government’s own accounting, funded 839 state and local cybersecurity projects with roughly $172 million disbursed as of August 2024 — money that went to exactly the activities this sector needs, from multi-factor authentication to equipment upgrades.41 Yet the program’s authority lapsed at the end of September 2025 and was only narrowly revived inside a stopgap spending deal, its long-term future still unsettled.41 The same continuing resolution extended both the grant program and the 2015 cyber-information-sharing law only through the end of January 2026, leaving the legal foundation of threat sharing on a short and uncertain lease.42

Worse, the single most important shared resource for local defenders was defunded outright. After 21 years, federal funding for the MS-ISAC ended on September 30, 2025, stripping away the cooperative agreement that had effectively subsidized cybersecurity services for state and local governments that could not otherwise afford them — a resource that accounted for much of the federal government’s visibility into threats at the local level.43 Forced into a member-funded model, the center within eight months had lost dozens of states and more than ten thousand local jurisdictions that could no longer pay for the service, even as the attacks against them grew more numerous and more dangerous.44 For the smallest PSAPs and rural agencies, this is not an abstraction; it is the removal of the one defensive capability many of them had.

For thousands of small agencies, the defense just lost its connective tissue in the same year the threat learned to wait.

Fragmentation and the small-agency problem

Underlying the funding crisis is the sector’s defining structural fact: defense does not scale across roughly 5,748 PSAPs and the thousands of independent law-enforcement, fire, and EMS agencies behind them.3 A Fortune-500 enterprise can afford a security operations center; a rural county 911 center staffed by a handful of telecommunicators cannot, and yet it runs a function whose failure is just as lethal locally. The entities least able to fund cybersecurity operate some of the most critical local infrastructure in the country — an inversion that no framework resolves and that pooled, regional, “whole-of-state” models are still struggling to address at scale.

The unfinished transition

The NG911 migration that is supposed to modernize the sector is itself an open wound. With a remaining nationwide cost estimated between roughly $5.8 and $9.27 billion and no federal deadline compelling completion, jurisdictions advance at radically different rates, guaranteeing a prolonged, dangerous period in which brittle legacy systems and immature next-generation deployments coexist and interconnect.9 The patchwork extends to security itself: with thousands of ESInets and centers each making independent choices, defenses are incompatible and unevenly applied, and the very interconnection that makes NG911 powerful also means one poorly secured node can endanger its neighbors.

The next adversary: AI, and the patient one

Two threat trajectories should shape the research agenda. The first is artificial intelligence, which is already lowering the barrier to attacks on the sector: synthesized voices and spoofed origins now feature in swatting campaigns, making hoaxes more convincing and harder to attribute, and the same generative tools sharpen phishing against under-resourced agencies.25 The defensive use of AI — for anomaly detection, call triage, and faster incident response — is a genuine frontier, but it remains immature relative to the offensive uses already in the wild. The second trajectory is the patient adversary. Volt Typhoon and Salt Typhoon demonstrated that capable nation-states are not merely raiding emergency-adjacent infrastructure but embedding in it, holding covert access for use at a moment of geopolitical choosing.2728 Detecting living-off-the-land persistence, building secure-by-design public-safety systems, developing meaningful resilience metrics, and creating incident-data-sharing regimes that actually function across thousands of jurisdictions are the research problems on which the sector’s long-term security now depends. There is also the unresolved governance question raised a decade ago by the P25 findings — how to make responder communications genuinely secure without sacrificing the public accountability that open radio once provided — a tension modernization has sharpened rather than settled.14

§ 07Coda

The line must hold

Every model of critical-infrastructure resilience contains a quiet assumption that the response will work — that the grid can fail, the pipeline can rupture, the plant can leak, and someone will still answer the call and coordinate the recovery. The Emergency Services Sector is where that assumption is either true or it is not. Its security is therefore not one problem among sixteen; it is the keystone that lets the other fifteen fail safely.

The threats are no longer theoretical. Ransomware has put dispatch centers on paper for hours and spilled the medical and criminal records of tens of thousands. A teenager’s exploit nearly silenced 911 across twelve states. A defective software update blinded watch floors worldwide without an attacker at all. And capable adversaries are now embedding in the lifeline sectors, waiting. Against this, the defenses — the frameworks, the mandates, the shared services, the grants — are sound in design but uneven in reach, and in 2025 they lost some of their most important connective tissue exactly when the smallest agencies needed it most. Closing that gap — funding the watch floor, hardening the systems it runs on, and rehearsing the day it has to operate alone — is the work. The call for help has to get through. Everything else depends on it.
