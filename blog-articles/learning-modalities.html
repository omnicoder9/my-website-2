The Learning Sciences Review

How We Learn: A Scientific Reckoning with Every Major Learning Medium

From lecture halls to podcast feeds, the evidence on what actually sticks — and what we only think does — is richer and stranger than most educators admit.

by Nicholas Knapp April 24, 2026 Approx. 4,800 words

The classroom has never been the only place where learning happens, and it has seldom been the most efficient one. Yet the question of how information moves from the outside world into durable long-term memory — and which medium carries it most reliably — is rarely asked in explicit, evidence-based terms. Educators have debated learning styles for decades, mostly to limited effect. Neuroscientists have refined cognitive load theory into a precision instrument. And a wave of meta-analyses since 2014 has, at last, started giving us answers that are both general enough to be useful and specific enough to be honest about trade-offs.

What follows is a medium-by-medium audit of the science. Each format — live lecture, webinar, video, print reading, digital reading, hands-on practice, and pure audio — will be examined on its cognitive demands, its empirical strengths, its documented weaknesses, and the contexts in which it most rewards investment. No medium is condemned wholesale; all have niches in which they excel. The goal is calibration, not verdict.

"The evidence now strongly suggests that how information is delivered matters — but less than what learners do with it during delivery."

Synthesis of cognitive load and active-learning research, 2014–2024

Theoretical Foundations

Before comparing formats, two frameworks from cognitive science deserve brief introduction, because they underlie virtually every finding discussed below.

Cognitive Load Theory (Sweller, 1988)

Working memory — the mental scratchpad where active thinking happens — holds roughly four to seven distinct elements at once.[1] Intrinsic load is the irreducible complexity of the material itself. Extraneous load is friction introduced by poor instructional design (redundant text, split-attention layouts, irrelevant detail). Germane load is productive mental effort that builds durable schemas. Good instructional design minimizes extraneous load while optimizing germane load relative to what the learner can handle.[2]

The Multimedia Principle (Mayer, 2001)

Richard Mayer's cognitive theory of multimedia learning holds that humans possess separate visual and auditory processing channels, each with limited capacity. Pairing complementary words and pictures exploits both channels without overwhelming either — the "multimedia principle." But when modalities carry redundant information simultaneously (e.g., narration and identical on-screen text), cognitive load climbs and learning suffers.[3]

These two frameworks act as a diagnostic lens: a format that introduces needless extraneous load, or fails to engage the learner's germane-load processing, is one that wastes precious cognitive bandwidth. With that orientation in place, we can examine each medium in turn.

The Learning Media

Medium One

Live Lecture Audio + Slides / Visuals
Evidence: Mixed-to-Negative for Passive Delivery

The lecture is the oldest formal educational institution in the Western canon, and for centuries it survived on the strength of tradition rather than evidence. The most comprehensive reckoning came in 2014, when Scott Freeman and colleagues at the University of Washington published a meta-analysis of 225 studies comparing undergraduate STEM courses taught by traditional lecturing against those using active learning. The results were decisive: student exam scores improved by 0.47 standard deviations under active learning, and students in lecture-only classes were 1.5 times more likely to fail.[4] Average exam scores were roughly 6% higher in active-learning sections — enough to shift a grade boundary.[5]

A subsequent 2022 meta-analysis extended Freeman's findings to the humanities and social sciences, finding an almost identical advantage for active instruction (0.489 SDs, p < 0.001).[6] The evidence against passive lecturing is now as robust as any in education research.

The lecture is not, however, irredeemable. Research consistently distinguishes between passive reception and cognitively engaged listening. When a lecture is dense with signaling cues — gestures, verbal emphasis, organized outlines — comprehension improves markedly.[7] And lectures retain an irreplaceable function as demonstrations of expert reasoning: a skilled lecturer models how a domain expert navigates uncertainty, a meta-skill that is difficult to replicate in text.

Medium Two

Webinar Synchronous Online Delivery
Evidence: Comparable to Live Lecture, With Added Risks

A webinar is essentially a lecture mediated through a screen — and that mediation introduces several compounding challenges. Physical co-presence, which in live settings provides subtle motivational cues (social accountability, instructor eye contact, ambient peer engagement), is absent. Research on online synchronous learning consistently finds that engagement and completion rates lag behind face-to-face equivalents when learner motivation is not otherwise scaffolded.[8]

That said, a pre-post quasi-experimental study (n=215) assessing cognitive load theory-based online lectures found significant improvement in learning outcomes after intervention (η²=0.19), suggesting that well-designed online synchronous instruction can be effective when it minimizes extraneous load.[9] Meta-analyses on distance learning more broadly have found it broadly comparable to face-to-face instruction in outcome measures, with the caveat that the most common delivery strategy — asynchronous video — differs meaningfully from live webinar.[10]

The key differentiator for webinars is interaction. Polling, breakout rooms, live annotation, and chat-thread questions can transform a webinar into a hybrid active-learning environment. Without these, the webinar carries all the disadvantages of lecture (passive reception, no pacing control) plus the additional friction of digital mediation.

Strengths
  • Geographic reach without travel cost
  • Recordable for asynchronous follow-up
  • Chat/polls enable pseudo-active elements
  • Screen sharing supports rich visual media
Weaknesses
  • Loss of social presence reduces motivation
  • Zoom fatigue compounds over sessions
  • Distraction environment is learner-controlled
  • Technical friction adds extraneous load
  • Lacks the haptic cues of physical attendance

Medium Three

Instructional Video Asynchronous Visual + Audio
Evidence: Strong When Designed Well; Highly Variable in Practice

Instructional video is now among the most widely used educational formats globally, accelerated by the COVID-19 pandemic and the proliferation of platforms from YouTube to institutional MOOCs. The evidence on its effectiveness is both encouraging and sobering.

On the positive side, meta-analyses suggest that well-designed video can rival or exceed face-to-face instruction in outcome measures. Learner control — the ability to pause, rewind, and re-watch — directly addresses the transient information effect: video's inherent weakness that spoken information vanishes before it can be processed.[11] Segmenting (breaking videos into discrete chunks) and signaling (highlighting key concepts with visual cues) are evidence-based design features that reduce extraneous load significantly.[12]

On the negative side, engagement is a chronic problem. One large study found that only about one-third of students in a flipped learning classroom watched all assigned out-of-class videos before the final week, and about 25% watched none.[13] Video promotes passive processing by default. Research using the ICAP framework (Chi & Wylie, 2014) — which classifies engagement from passive to interactive — finds that learners watching without annotation or self-explanation remain largely in passive mode, the least effective learning state.[14]

Importantly, positive learning outcomes are observed when videos incorporate deeper processing tasks such as embedded quiz questions and reflection prompts. However, researchers have found no significant learning gains from "talking heads" — instructor-on-screen overlays that are popular with producers but cognitively inert for learners.[15]

Strengths
  • Learner controls pace and replay
  • Multimedia principle exploits dual-channel processing
  • Animations can show dynamic processes
  • Embedded quizzes dramatically increase learning
  • Scalable with no marginal cost per learner
Weaknesses
  • Default mode is passive — engagement not guaranteed
  • Poor design (redundant narration+text) increases load
  • Completion rates are low without accountability

  • No instructor feedback on learner misconceptions

Medium Four

Reading — Print Physical Books, Journals, Documents
Evidence: Consistently Strong for Deep Comprehension

Print reading is the medium against which all others are implicitly compared, and in the core task of deep comprehension, it continues to hold an empirical advantage. The spatial stability of print — fixed words on a fixed page — supports what researchers call cognitive mapping: the construction of a mental topography of the text, where readers intuitively know that a particular idea appeared on the left-hand page, two-thirds through the chapter.[16] This proprioceptive grounding of text in physical space aids recall and sequential reasoning.

Mangen and colleagues demonstrated that Kindle readers, despite reading identical content, were significantly less accurate than print readers in recalling the chronological order of a novel's events — a finding attributed to the loss of sensorimotor cues: the weight of pages read, the physical feedback of turning, the shrinking "remainder" pile.[17] Three separate meta-analyses have confirmed a measurable comprehension advantage for paper over screen, particularly for longer and more complex texts.[18]

For the specific task of reading informational texts rich in hierarchical structure — textbooks, academic papers, legal documents — print's non-linear navigation (annotations, sticky notes, free-hand marginal notes) supports elaborative processing in ways that most digital interfaces still do not fully replicate. Print also removes the distraction environment entirely: there is no notification layer, no temptation to open another tab.

Strengths
  • Spatial-proprioceptive cues aid memory and sequencing
  • Supports deep, non-linear annotation
  • No distraction environment
  • Strong meta-analytic advantage for complex texts
  • Reader controls pace fully — can reread freely
Weaknesses
  • Not portable for large libraries
  • Cannot be searched electronically
  • No multimedia supplement inline
  • Updates/corrections require new editions
  • Environmental and distribution costs

Medium Five

Reading — Digital E-Readers, Tablets, Screen Text
Evidence: Near-Equivalent for Simple Texts; Lags for Complex Material

The findings on digital reading are genuinely nuanced. A 2021 meta-analysis of 817 health-professional-education participants found only a negligible, non-significant advantage for paper (SMD = −0.08; 95% CI −0.28 to 0.12).[19] But subgroup analyses revealed that when participants were reading discipline-specific technical texts — the most demanding condition — paper showed a statistically significant advantage (SMD = −0.36).[20]

A broader 2023 meta-analysis of 470,000 participants across more than three dozen countries found a small negative relationship between digital leisure reading and reading comprehension (r = −0.06), with stronger negative associations in younger children and small positive associations at university level — suggesting that the deficit may reflect reading habits and cognitive development rather than an intrinsic screen limitation.[21] One theorized mechanism is that digital text imposes higher extraneous load due to pixelation, scrolling demands, and the cognitive burden of spatial disorientation as content moves rather than staying anchored to a fixed location.[22]

Scrolling, specifically, appears to be an underappreciated problem. When text moves through the visual field, the reader cannot build the stable cognitive map that aids recall. Working memory must simultaneously process the current sentence and track spatial position, leaving less capacity for comprehension.[23] Paginated e-readers (like Kindle's page-turn model) perform better than infinite-scroll interfaces on comprehension tasks.

Strengths
  • Entire libraries in a single device
  • Instant full-text search across documents
  • Adjustable font, brightness, and contrast
  • Accessible for those with visual impairments
  • Inline dictionary, annotation sync to cloud
Weaknesses
  • Scrolling impairs cognitive mapping
  • Device-based distraction environment
  • Screen fatigue over extended sessions
  • Measurably weaker for technical comprehension
  • Loss of physical spatial cues

"As you're reading a narrative, knowing where you are in a book helps you build the arc of narrative. Scrolling demands more from our working memory — we have to remember what we just read without the spatial cues to help."

Lauren Singer Trakhman, University of Maryland, College Park

Medium Six

Hands-On / Experiential Learning Practice, Labs, Simulation, Project Work
Evidence: Superior for Procedural, Applied, and Motor Skills

For a substantial class of skills, hands-on practice is not merely one option among many — it is the only option. No amount of lecture or video will teach a surgeon to suture, a programmer to debug, or a baker to develop gluten feel. But beyond skills that are inherently motor, experiential learning shows broad advantages.

A substantial body of research demonstrates that active engagement with hands-on learning using authentic tools is the most effective way for students to learn and retain science knowledge.[24] Kolb's experiential learning cycle (1984) — Concrete Experience → Reflective Observation → Abstract Conceptualization → Active Experimentation — provides the theoretical scaffolding: learners who complete all four phases achieve deeper and more transferable understanding than those who receive only abstract exposition.[25]

Studies on hands-on science learning consistently find higher levels of intrinsic motivation and participation alongside better academic outcomes. One mixed-methods study found that students who learned through hands-on experiments obtained better results, remembered material more durably, and showed substantially higher motivation compared to lecture-only controls.[26]

The COVID-19 pandemic provided a natural experiment: when hands-on labs moved online, student-reported skill development dropped even as synchronous and asynchronous alternatives were introduced. A 2021 study on construction management programs found that hybrid courses incorporating in-person hands-on activities outperformed both fully asynchronous and pre-pandemic traditional delivery on technical skill ratings.[27] The message is clear: for applied knowledge, real-world practice is not safely substituted.

Medium Seven

Pure Audio Podcasts, Audiobooks, Recorded Lectures
Evidence: Competitive for Narrative; Lags for Dense Technical Material

Audio is the oldest human learning technology, and its rehabilitation in the age of podcasts and audiobooks is warranted — but requires careful qualification. The neuroscience is encouraging: a 2019 study from UC Berkeley using fMRI found that the semantic representations activated when listening to stories and when reading them are nearly identical, with similar activity in both cognitive and emotional brain regions.[28] Listening and reading are, at the neural substrate level, remarkably close cousins.

A 2016 randomized study (n=91) found no significant difference in 2-week retention of nonfiction material between participants who read, listened, or did both simultaneously — suggesting that for well-structured narrative text, audio is a viable alternative to reading.[29] Podcast listeners show high completion rates (over 80% of episodes), a figure that dwarfs typical video completion, suggesting that the conversational intimacy of audio sustains engagement unusually well.[30]

However, the picture darkens for technical, hierarchically organized material. A 2010 study found that students who listened to a podcast version of a lesson scored an average of 28% lower on a comprehension quiz than students who read the same content — a gap the study's authors compared to the difference between an A and a D grade.[31] The disadvantages are structural: audio is inherently transient; the learner cannot easily scan back, cross-reference, or re-read a passage the way a reader can. For expository content with precise terminology, formulae, or nested logical arguments, the working memory demands of audio become prohibitive.

Multitasking — audio's signature advantage — is also its cognitive Achilles heel. Divided attention reliably reduces encoding depth. Emotional or narrative content survives divided attention better than technical content does, because narrative structure itself aids recall through cause-effect and temporal scaffolding.[32]

Strengths
  • Enables learning during otherwise idle time
  • High completion rates — conversational format sustains engagement
  • Speaker intonation conveys nuance text cannot
  • Near-identical neural processing to reading for narrative
  • Strong for language learning, pronunciation modeling
Weaknesses
  • No spatial anchoring — cannot scan or reread
  • Divided attention degrades encoding significantly
  • 28% comprehension gap documented for technical content
  • Loses nuance of tables, diagrams, and notation
  • Learning quality varies sharply with listener focus

Cross-Cutting Principles

Several findings transcend individual media and apply broadly to any learning context.

Retrieval Practice Beats Re-Exposure

Across formats, the single highest-leverage thing a learner can do is test themselves — not re-read, re-watch, or re-listen, but actively attempt to reconstruct knowledge from memory. The "testing effect" is among the most replicated findings in cognitive psychology: retrieval practice produces durable long-term retention across text, lecture, and video formats, with effect sizes substantially larger than re-study.[3]

Spaced Practice Compounds over Time

Distributing learning across sessions — the spacing effect — consistently outperforms massed ("cramming") study across all formats. The mechanism involves memory consolidation during rest intervals and the desirable difficulty introduced by forgetting, which forces deeper retrieval effort. The spacing effect is arguably the most transferable finding from cognitive psychology to educational practice.[3]

Active Engagement Is the Decisive Variable

The consistent thread across all high-performing formats is that the learner is doing something with the material: generating questions, explaining concepts, applying knowledge to problems, receiving corrective feedback. The ICAP framework (Interactive > Constructive > Active > Passive) maps engagement level to learning outcomes, with interactive engagement producing the greatest schema formation regardless of medium.[14]

Signaling Reduces Unnecessary Load

Whether in lecture, video, or text, cueing learners to what is important — through visual highlighting, verbal emphasis, or structural outlines — significantly improves retention and transfer. Four recent meta-analyses found significant small-to-large effect sizes for signaling in multimedia instruction over non-signaled formats.[33]

Multitasking Reliably Degrades Learning

No format survives divided attention without loss. This is the core limitation of audio consumed during exercise or commuting, of lecture attended while messaging, and of reading done in a notifications-enabled browser. Depth of processing is proportional to sustained attention — and sustained attention is incompatible with multitasking.

Practical Guidance by Learning Goal

With the evidence assembled, some practical guidance by learning objective becomes possible:

For Conceptual Understanding of Complex Material

Start with print reading of high-quality primary sources. Supplement with lecture or video for expert framing — but only if those lectures incorporate retrieval practice, pauses for reflection, or active problem-solving. Follow up with spaced self-testing. Audio is not recommended as a primary medium for this goal.

For Procedural or Applied Skills

Hands-on practice is non-negotiable. Instructional video can scaffold the initial cognitive model — particularly if it shows a worked example with step-by-step annotation — but it must be followed immediately by independent practice with corrective feedback. The "flipped classroom" model, in which video delivers conceptual content and class time is reserved for practice, is well-supported by evidence.[9]

For Broad Awareness or Informal Learning

Audio (podcasts, audiobooks) performs well when the goal is exposure, inspiration, or narrative-structured content. The medium excels at making use of otherwise idle time and sustaining engagement through conversational intimacy. It is not appropriate as the primary source for any domain requiring technical precision.

For Formal Courses and Examinations

Active learning formats — peer instruction, problem sets, retrieval quizzes, spaced review — consistently outperform lecture-only delivery. The medium delivering the content matters less than whether learners are required to actively engage with it. Combine any medium with retrieval practice for dramatically better outcomes.

Conclusion

The science of learning has matured to the point where format comparisons yield genuine insights rather than noise — but it has also made clear that the format itself is rarely the most important variable. What matters most is the cognitive work the learner performs: retrieving, applying, elaborating, and practicing. A lecture that requires students to answer questions outperforms a textbook read passively. A podcast listened to with total attention outperforms a video played on a second monitor. An e-reader with active annotation outperforms a physical book skimmed without intention.

This does not render the format choice irrelevant. Print reading reliably supports deeper comprehension of complex material than digital scrolling. Hands-on practice is irreplaceable for applied skills. Audio carries significant risks for technical content under typical listening conditions. These distinctions are real and consequential — particularly for educators designing curricula and for self-directed learners allocating finite time.

The practical upshot is a two-level design principle: choose the format best suited to the nature of the material and the learner's context, then layer in active engagement strategies — retrieval, spacing, elaboration, feedback — regardless of which format is chosen. No medium has a monopoly on effective learning, and no format, no matter how well designed, rescues a learner in purely passive mode.

Key Takeaway

Format matters — but engagement matters more. Any medium paired with active retrieval practice outperforms passive consumption of a "better" format.

The Six Principles

1. Retrieval practice — test yourself, don't re-read.

2. Spaced practice — distribute study over time.

3. Interleaving — mix topics rather than blocking.

4. Elaboration — explain the "why," not just the "what."

5. Concrete examples — abstract concepts need anchors.

6. Dual coding — pair words with visuals when possible.

On Multitasking

No format survives divided attention without loss. Audio while exercising, lecture while messaging, video on a second monitor — all reduce encoding depth, regardless of how excellent the content is.

Print vs. Digital

Scrolling is an underappreciated enemy of comprehension. Fixed-page layouts (print, paginated e-readers) allow readers to build spatial cognitive maps of texts. Infinite scroll does not.
