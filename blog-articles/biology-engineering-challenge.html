Life's Impossible Machine

Why the most complex objects ever built by human hands are mere toys compared to the microscopic engines that have been quietly running for four billion years.

By Nicholas Knapp — Published April 10, 2026 — 15 min read
✦ ✦ ✦

Engineers measure their greatest achievements in superlatives. The ASML TWINSCAN EXE, the most advanced extreme ultraviolet (EUV) lithography machine ever constructed, costs roughly $370 million, weighs 180 tonnes, requires several Boeing 747 cargo jets to transport, and contains more than 700,000 individual parts.[1] It takes a global network of over 5,100 suppliers, decades of cumulative research, and billions of euros in development investment to produce even a single unit.[2,3] The machine operates inside a near-perfect vacuum, fires a laser at tin droplets 50,000 times per second to generate light of a wavelength invisible to any instrument but the finest mirrors on Earth, and maintains positional tolerances measured in single nanometres.[4] MIT Technology Review called it "the machine that saved Moore's Law."[5] It is, by almost any measure, the most complex object humanity has ever assembled.

And yet it is catastrophically less sophisticated than a bacterium. Not a clever bacterium. Not a particularly evolved one. The simplest self-replicating organism science has yet identified — a parasitic speck called Mycoplasma genitalium — operates machinery so far beyond anything human engineers have produced that the comparison borders on the absurd. Biology, it turns out, presents an engineering challenge of an entirely different order: not a frontier to be reached, but a horizon that perpetually recedes.

The Machine That Saved Moore's Law

To appreciate what biology surpasses, one must first appreciate what human engineering has achieved. The story of EUV lithography is one of the most audacious technological quests in history. Researchers first began exploring the principle in the 1980s; serious industrial pursuit kicked off in 1994; and ASML ultimately spent more than €6 billion in R&D over 17 years before delivering a commercially viable machine.[3] The challenge was not merely engineering — it was physics pushed to its absolute limits.

EUV light sits at a wavelength of 13.5 nanometres, roughly 5,000 times thinner than a human hair.[9] At this scale, the light is absorbed by virtually everything — air, glass, almost all known materials — so the entire optical path must be maintained in a hard vacuum.[4] There are no lenses; instead, the system uses a series of six to twelve multilayer mirrors, each composed of up to 50 alternating layers of molybdenum and silicon, each layer only a few atoms thick, figured to tolerances that Zeiss — the German optics company that makes them — describes as "the most precise mirrors in the world."[5] Even with this perfection, each mirror reflects only about 70% of the light that strikes it; by the time EUV photons reach the silicon wafer, as little as 1–5% of the original input energy remains.[10]

"Even with the most precise mirrors ever manufactured, only 1–5% of input energy reaches the silicon. Biology achieves its chemistry at near-Carnot efficiency without a single engineer in the room."

The light source itself is a pyrotechnic marvel. A CO₂ laser fires precisely timed pulses at droplets of molten tin — each one mere micrometres across — vaporising them into a plasma that emits EUV radiation.[4] This happens up to 50,000 times per second, continuously, for months of operation.[3] The splattered tin residue gradually coats the machine's internal components, necessitating elaborate contamination management systems and periodic expensive maintenance.[10] Meanwhile, the wafer stage and the reticle stage must be synchronised to nanometre precision while accelerating in opposite directions at 5 g and 15 g respectively — equivalent, ASML notes, to a car going from 0 to 100 km/h in 0.1 seconds — without generating a single perceptible vibration.[11]

The resulting machine, roughly the size of a city bus and requiring seven Boeing 747s to transport,[1] can etch features as small as 8 nanometres onto silicon — 1/10,000th the width of a human hair.[2] It is a genuine triumph of civilisation, a distillation of contributions from physicists, chemists, materials scientists, software engineers, and precision machinists across dozens of countries.

Enter the Bacterium

Mycoplasma genitalium is not an organism that inspires awe at first glance. A flask-shaped parasite measuring about 0.3–0.7 micrometres — invisible without an electron microscope — it causes urethritis and has the ignominious distinction of being a sexually transmitted infection.[12] Scientists chose to study it specifically because it seemed simple: its genome, at 580,070 base pairs, is the smallest of any known organism that can be cultivated independently in a laboratory.[6,13] It was, researchers hoped, biology stripped to its bare minimum — a window into the machinery of life itself.

The window, when they finally pried it open, revealed an abyss.

To model M. genitalium computationally — to simulate, on a supercomputer, what the cell does naturally without thought or effort — a team led by Stanford bioengineering professor Markus Covert spent years synthesising data from more than 900 scientific publications.[7] They modelled the organism's biology as 28 separate interlocking sub-models, each governed by its own mathematical formalism, communicating with every other sub-model after each one-second time step of simulated life.[8] The result — a paper published in Cell in 2012 — represented the first complete computational model of an entire living organism. It required a 128-core Linux cluster simply to run.[14]

What does the simulation model? Every molecule involved in metabolism, RNA transcription, protein synthesis, DNA replication, and cytokinesis — the process by which a cell physically divides itself in two. The model accounts for the specific function of every annotated gene product in the organism's 525-gene genome, integrated through 16 cellular state variables updated continuously across the cell's 9-hour lifecycle.[8,14]

And this, again, is the simplest free-living organism known to science.

Scale

What Complexity Actually Means

The comparison between EUV machines and bacteria requires some care. Raw parts count is a blunt instrument; a pile of 700,000 identical ball bearings is not complex. What matters is the number of distinct functional components, the intricacy of their interactions, and the degree to which the behaviour of the whole cannot be deduced from the behaviour of the parts.

By any of these measures, biology wins decisively. The M. genitalium genome encodes 482 distinct protein-coding genes, nearly all of which are essential — remove almost any one of them and the organism dies.[6] Those 482 genes produce proteins that fold into three-dimensional structures, bind to other molecules with exquisite specificity, catalyse chemical reactions at rates millions of times faster than the same reactions proceed unaided, and regulate one another's activity through cascades of molecular signalling. Roughly a quarter of these essential proteins have functions that remain, to this day, entirely unknown to science — even after decades of intensive study.[6,15]

Move up to Escherichia coli — the workhorse of laboratory biology, a bacterium considered ordinary to the point of mundaneness — and the numbers escalate dramatically. E. coli encodes approximately 4,285 proteins from a genome of 4,401 genes.[16] At any given moment, the cell expresses thousands of these proteins simultaneously, each present in copy numbers ranging from fewer than 10 molecules to more than 100,000, all coordinated in real time without centralised control.[17] The cell's chromosome, a single circular DNA molecule roughly 1.5 mm long when stretched out, is compacted to fit inside a cell about 2 micrometres across — a compression ratio that would be equivalent to folding a motorway into a thimble — while remaining readable, replicable, and transcribable at all times.[18]

"The bacterium's chromosome, if stretched out, would be 1.5 mm long. The cell that contains it is 2 micrometres across. It is readable, replicable, and transcribable at all times — folded into a thimble without losing a single letter."

Self-assembly

The Feature No Engineer Has Replicated

ASML's machines are extraordinary, but they are built. Teams of specialists assemble them in cleanrooms, and they require constant maintenance, software updates, and controlled environments to operate. They do not repair themselves when damaged, adapt their behaviour to novel conditions, or make copies of themselves. A lithography machine that stopped working would stay broken.

A bacterium, by contrast, assembles itself. From a handful of nutrient molecules, M. genitalium synthesises its own proteins, replicates its own DNA, constructs its own membrane, and divides — producing two daughter cells, each a complete and functional organism.[8] This self-replication occurs with an error rate for DNA copying of approximately one mistake per billion base pairs replicated, achieved through an elaborate proofreading mechanism that is itself encoded in the genome and synthesised by the cell.[19] The EUV machine cannot correct its own manufacturing errors; it is biology that has solved the reliability problem at the nanoscale.

Self-assembly extends to every component. The ribosome — the molecular machine that translates genetic information into protein — is itself a protein-RNA complex assembled from dozens of individual components that spontaneously find one another in the cellular interior and snap into their correct positions. A single E. coli cell contains tens of thousands of ribosomes, each one a nanoscale factory operating continuously.[20] Human engineers have not come close to constructing anything that self-assembles at this scale with comparable reliability.

Adaptation

The Problem of Robustness

Engineers design systems for anticipated conditions. EUV machines operate within carefully controlled parameters — temperature, humidity, vibration, power supply — and their tolerances are narrow. Deviation from specification causes failure. This is not a criticism; precision requires constraint.

Biological systems have evolved for robustness under uncertainty. E. coli can survive in temperatures ranging from 7°C to 49°C, shift between aerobic and anaerobic metabolism depending on oxygen availability, alter its gene expression programme within minutes in response to nutrient changes, and tolerate partial damage to many of its molecular systems through built-in redundancy.[17] The bacterium runs its own quality control, identifies and degrades misfolded proteins, and repairs double-strand breaks in its DNA — the most catastrophic form of genetic damage — using molecular machinery of staggering intricacy.

This robustness is not an accident; it is the result of four billion years of selection pressure that killed every organism that could not handle disruption. The engineering lesson is humbling: the systems we most admire for their resilience are precisely the ones we understand least.

Why This Matters for Engineering

The recognition that biology surpasses human engineering is not merely an occasion for humility. It is a research agenda. Synthetic biology — the discipline that attempts to design new biological systems or re-engineer existing ones — has grown rapidly in recent decades, producing engineered organisms that produce pharmaceuticals, detect environmental toxins, and manufacture novel materials. But the field is still largely in the position of a craftsperson who has learned to assemble a few new sentences from an alphabet they don't fully understand.

The goal of whole-cell modelling — pioneered by the M. genitalium work at Stanford — is to change this. Covert's team argued that comprehensive computational models of cells could do for bioengineering what CAD software did for civil engineering and aeronautics: replace laborious trial-and-error with principled design.[7] Subsequent work has begun extending this approach to other organisms, including E. coli and the more complex Mycoplasma pneumoniae.[21] The aspiration — to design a living cell from scratch, specifying its genome and predicting its behaviour — remains distant, but is no longer obviously impossible.

What makes it difficult, ultimately, is not that biology is mysterious. It is that biology is complex in a way that resists reduction. An EUV machine is complicated — it has many parts interacting in many ways — but a sufficiently patient engineer could, in principle, trace every causal chain from input to output. A cell is complex in a different sense: its behaviour emerges from interactions among thousands of components in ways that cannot be decomposed and reassembled without loss. The whole is, genuinely, more than the sum of its parts. Modelling even the smallest living organism requires 28 interlinked mathematical formalisms running in parallel on a computing cluster.[8]

"The EUV machine is complicated. The bacterium is complex. The difference is not of degree but of kind — and it is the kind that four billion years of selection pressure produces, and that no human institution has yet begun to match."

The Horizon

There is a lesson here that extends beyond biology and engineering. Human civilisation tends to anchor its sense of technological achievement to its own artefacts: the tallest building, the fastest computer, the most precise machine. These are real achievements, hard-won and worth celebrating. But they share a common vulnerability: they are all evaluated relative to one another, on a scale defined by what humans have managed so far.

Biology offers a different scale. It is one set not by ambition or resources but by physics, chemistry, and three and a half billion years of differential survival — a process with no engineering budget, no project manager, and no deadline, but with ruthless and continuous feedback. The results are systems of a complexity and efficiency that remain, despite the best efforts of the most capable scientists alive, substantially beyond our understanding.

That we can build a machine that costs $370 million, weighs 180 tonnes, requires the coordinated expertise of tens of thousands of engineers, and prints features at 8-nanometre resolution is extraordinary.[1,2] That a single living cell, self-assembled from raw chemistry, does all of this and more — and then makes another one of itself — is something rather more than extraordinary. It is, perhaps, the most important fact about the universe that most people never think about.
