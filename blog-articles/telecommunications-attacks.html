CH00Overview / Threat model

A network that trusts its own plumbing

Every call you place, every text you send, and every byte your phone moves leans on a set of protocols whose oldest layers were designed when a "computer crime" meant prying open a coin box. Signaling System No. 7 — the machinery that still glues the world's telephone networks together — traces its lineage to common-channel signaling work that began around 1975, and it was formalized as an international standard in the ITU‑T's 1980 Yellow Book recommendations.1 It was, in the words of one analyst, created before there was an Internet and was never built to be secure in the world we now live in.2

That single sentence is the thread running through this entire dossier. The public switched telephone network, and the mobile networks layered on top of it, were engineered around a comfortable assumption: that the only parties who could reach the signaling plane were other licensed operators, and that operators are trustworthy. SS7 carries the requests that set up and tear down calls, translate numbers, port subscribers between carriers, handle prepaid billing, and deliver SMS — all of it as out-of-band signaling running on a network that, for decades, was a closed club.3 When the club's membership swelled to hundreds of operators, resellers, and aggregators reachable over IP, the assumption quietly stopped holding. The locks were never installed because the doors were supposed to be internal.

The mobile industry's response over the past two decades has been to build successive generations — 3G, 4G, and 5G — each promising stronger authentication than the last. But generations do not replace one another so much as accrete. A modern handset speaks 5G when it can, drops to 4G when it must, and falls all the way back to 2G when nothing else is available. The signaling protocols mirror that layering: SS7 for legacy circuits, Diameter for 4G, and an HTTP/2 service‑based architecture for 5G. Crucially, the old layers never fully retire, because backward compatibility is a feature subscribers demand. The attack surface is therefore cumulative, not generational, and a defender has to reason about all of it at once.

A brief vocabulary makes the rest legible, because nearly every attack here turns on an identifier. A subscriber is known to the network by an IMSI, the international mobile subscriber identity stored on the SIM; by an MSISDN, the dialable phone number the public actually uses; and, on the device side, by an IMEI that names the hardware. To limit exposure, networks issue temporary identifiers — a TMSI, and in 5G a concealed SUCI — so the permanent IMSI need not travel in the clear.1 The recurring move throughout this dossier is the conversion between these names: an attacker who has a phone number wants the IMSI, because the IMSI is the key that unlocks location queries, interception, and the targeting of the SIM and the radio. Much of telecom security, in the end, is the question of who is allowed to ask which identity this is, and where it is right now.

One more term recurs: the interconnect. It is the meeting point where one carrier's network exchanges signaling and traffic with another's, directly or through intermediaries, and it is the privileged position nearly every core attack here assumes. The central failure of telephone-network security is that, for most of its history, simply reaching the interconnect was treated as proof of good standing.3

Figure 1. Each mobile generation brought a new signaling protocol and stronger authentication, yet the interconnect trust surface persists because legacy roaming and fallback are never switched off. Dates mark milestones referenced throughout this dossier.

What follows is organized as seven channels, each a distinct class of attack. The first two — SS7 and Diameter — are core signaling attacks that operate inside the network, often from anywhere on Earth, needing only access to the interconnect and a target's phone number. The next three — IMSI catchers, SIM swapping, and baseband exploitation — are edge attacks that touch the subscriber directly, whether over the air, through a carrier's support desk, or against the radio chip in a phone. The last two — 5G slicing and VoIP — show how the same trust-by-default pattern reappears in the newest architectures and in the IP telephony that now carries most of the world's voice traffic. Throughout, the emphasis is on how these attacks work conceptually, the documented cases that made them matter, the tools the research community uses to study them, and the defenses that actually move the needle.

Orientation

This is a defensive and educational reference. It explains attack mechanisms at the level found in academic papers, conference disclosures, and regulator advisories — the same material network engineers, penetration testers, and policy teams use to harden systems. It deliberately does not provide operational recipes for attacking live networks or defrauding people; the goal is comprehension and defense, not a playbook.

CH01Core signaling ⋅ 2G/3G interconnect

SS7: locating, tracking, and intercepting from anywhere

Signalling System No. 7 — known in North America as Common Channel Signaling System 7 (CCSS7) and historically as Common Channel Interoffice Signaling — is the set of protocols, developed beginning in 1975, that lets one telephone network connect to another. It performs the out-of-band signaling behind call establishment, billing, routing, and number translation across the public switched telephone network, exchanging the control messages that calls and texts need without ever touching the voice path itself.4 It was first introduced inside the Bell System for signaling between electronic toll switches; the ITU‑T standardized its predecessor SS6 in 1977 and SS7 in 1980, and the IETF later defined the SIGTRAN suite, which lifts the whole common-channel paradigm onto IP using SCTP transport — the bridge that ultimately exposed a once-isolated network to anyone who could reach it over the internet.1

MechanismWhy a routing protocol becomes a tracking tool

SS7's exploitable power comes from a design choice that made perfect sense in a trusted network: the application-layer services that ride on top of it — the Mobile Application Part (MAP) and CAMEL — operate without authentication. Any node that the network believes is a legitimate roaming partner can ask questions and issue instructions, and the network answers. Researchers showed that this lets an attacker who reaches the SS7 network sit in a man‑in‑the‑middle position to re-route calls and SMS, carry out denial‑of‑service, and — most alarming for physical safety — learn and track a person's location.5

Figure 2. A simplified location query. By asking the home register for routing data and then querying the serving switch, an attacker with interconnect access can resolve a subscriber to a cell. The MAP messages carry no proof that the requester is who it claims to be.

The location attack is deceptively simple in outline. A query such as a MAP SendRoutingInfo asks the victim's home register where to deliver traffic; the answer reveals the international mobile subscriber identity (IMSI) and the address of the switch currently serving the phone. A follow-up such as ProvideSubscriberInfo can return the serving cell, which maps to a physical area. None of these exchanges asks the requester to prove anything, because in the original threat model the only requesters were peer carriers. Interception works by a related trick: instruct a foreign network to forward a target's calls and texts, or manipulate the registration so that traffic routes through attacker-controlled infrastructure.

AnatomyThe layers an attacker speaks to

To see why authentication was never added, it helps to know what the stack contains. SS7 is built in layers: the Message Transfer Part (MTP, in three levels) provides reliable transport and routing; the Signalling Connection Control Part (SCCP) adds global-title addressing, so a message can be sent to a logical address — a phone number or a home register — rather than a physical point code; the Transaction Capabilities Application Part (TCAP) carries the request-and-response dialogues; and on top sit the application parts, including ISUP for call setup and MAP for the mobile operations that location and interception attacks abuse.1 Routing across networks relies on global title translation, in which intermediate nodes resolve an address and forward the message onward — a mechanism designed for cooperative carriers, with no notion of an adversary forging the questions.4

That layering is also why the move to IP mattered so much. SIGTRAN preserves these upper layers but carries them over SCTP on ordinary IP networks, so an entity that obtains interconnect access — whether a small licensed operator, a leased global title, or a compromised node — can emit MAP and SCCP traffic that the rest of the world processes as though it came from a trusted peer. The protocol's elegance for routing is exactly its fragility for security: every node is taught to be helpful, and helpfulness, at global scale, becomes hard to distinguish from gullibility.

This reframes what "an SS7 attacker" actually means. They need not breach a carrier in the cinematic sense; access has been obtained by becoming a small licensed operator, leasing connectivity or a global title from one, or compromising equipment somewhere in the chain — after which the messages they send inherit the trust the whole system extends to its peers.4

HistoryFrom an academic curiosity to a spy's dream

The vulnerabilities surfaced gradually. Ways to abuse SS7 began coming to light in 2008, when the German researcher Tobias Engel demonstrated at the Chaos Communication Congress how the protocol could be used to determine a phone's location. Interest surged after 2013, when documents leaked by Edward Snowden showed intelligence agencies using SS7 to help track targets.6 The watershed came in December 2014, when Engel of Sternraute and Karsten Nohl of Security Research Labs — working in parallel — presented sweeping findings at the 31st Congress under the unambiguous title SS7: Locate. Track. Manipulate.5 Engel's own materials laid out, message by message, how an attacker armed only with a phone number could find and follow anyone on the planet.7

How sharp was the edge? At a newspaper's request, Engel tracked one of its employees, with her consent, to within a city block using nothing but her phone number.2 And the threat was not theoretical: Engel said he was aware of a real-world attack carried out in Ukraine and detected by a local operator, traced to a Russian SS7 network.5

The public reckoning arrived in 2016, when a widely watched television segment let researchers demonstrate the attack against a sitting U.S. legislator. With his permission, Nohl eavesdropped on the mobile calls of Congressman Ted Lieu, knowing only his number.6 Lieu's own account of the demonstration captured the asymmetry plainly: the flaw had been exposed years earlier, yet hundreds of operators retained access to a system where security was hit‑or‑miss, and obtaining access without a carrier's blessing was not especially hard.8

Case ⋅ The first documented SS7 bank heist

In January 2017, criminals turned the research into theft. As reported by the German newspaper Süddeutsche Zeitung, attackers first phished victims' online banking credentials, then exploited SS7 to hijack the delivery of the one-time codes — mobile transaction authentication numbers, or mTANs — that banks sent by SMS, redirecting them to attacker-controlled phones and draining accounts.9

It was the first publicly documented case of cybercriminals exploiting known SS7 weaknesses for profit. The affected operator, O2‑Telefónica, responded by blocking outside parties from setting up call forwarding on its network, and other German operators followed.9 The episode hardened a lesson regulators had already drawn: codes sent over SMS can be intercepted, which is why bodies such as Germany's BSI and the U.S. NIST moved to deprecate SMS as an out-of-band authentication channel.6

Tooling & defenseStudying the interconnect

Carrying out these attacks historically required specialized signaling hardware and the codes needed to interoperate with other carriers, but the research community built an open toolchain to study the problem — software-defined radios paired with open base-station stacks for the radio side, and SS7 software development kits and mapping projects that charted the global exposure of the roaming infrastructure. The mitigation playbook that emerged is consistent: block externally originated call-forwarding and registration changes, deploy a signaling firewall that validates message plausibility at the network border, and continuously monitor cross-border signaling for the query patterns that precede an attack.9 Those controls reappear, in updated form, in every later generation — and we return to them in CH08.

CH02Core signaling ⋅ 4G/LTE interconnect

Diameter: a cleaner protocol, the same old habits

When the industry moved to 4G, it also moved past SS7 for core signaling. The replacement was Diameter, the protocol defined by the IETF in RFC 6733 to provide an authentication, authorization, and accounting (AAA) framework for billing and policy across LTE networks.10 Diameter is the evolutionary successor to both the RADIUS AAA protocol and, functionally, to SS7: it carries the same kinds of subscriber-management and roaming messages, but with a modern transport and a security model that, on paper, is far stronger.11

The catch is that the security model is largely optional, and operators frequently leave it switched off. Diameter can be deployed with the protections that would prevent SS7-style abuse, yet incorrect or incomplete configuration leaves 4G networks exposed to a strikingly familiar set of weaknesses — the very ones Diameter was supposed to prevent.12 Security researchers found that poor configuration in LTE systems was the root cause: features that would authenticate and authorize signaling messages simply were not turned on, leaving subscribers and providers reachable through the interconnect much as they were under SS7.13

FindingsWhat auditors actually see on 4G networks

The most systematic public measurements came from a security firm that had analyzed SS7 in 2016 and Diameter in 2017, culminating in a Diameter vulnerabilities exposure report drawn from audits of fifteen operators, the majority of them large carriers with tens of millions of subscribers.13 The recurring goals of Diameter signaling attacks mirror SS7's: subscriber-information disclosure — tracking a target's location, revealing profile details, and determining the IMSI that downstream attacks need — alongside denial of service against subscribers and the network, and account fraud.14

Even interception, the attack Diameter's authentication was meant to foreclose, remains reachable by a back door. Because much of the 4G ecosystem still rides on older technology, an attacker can downgrade a Diameter-capable connection to a previous generation and use SS7 flaws instead: SMS often traverses a 3G channel where SS7, not Diameter, handles authentication, while voice over LTE has its own documented weaknesses.13 The mixed-generation reality means a clean 4G core does not, by itself, protect a subscriber whose traffic can be nudged back onto a dirtier path.

AnatomyRealms, AVPs, and the roaming interfaces

Diameter's structure explains both its promise and its exposure. Messages are composed of attribute-value pairs and are scoped to realms — the domains that identify operators — with each function selected by an application identifier, so that mobility, policy, and charging each ride their own Diameter application.11 In an LTE core, the interfaces that matter for roaming carry names like S6a and S6d, connecting the mobility-management entity to the home subscriber server to authenticate a subscriber and fetch their profile — precisely the exchange an attacker wants to impersonate in order to learn a target's keys, location, or identity.11

Crucially, Diameter peers establish direct connections and can require transport security between them, which is the protection that, used properly, distinguishes it from SS7. The trouble is operational rather than theoretical: across a sprawling roaming ecosystem of operators and intermediaries, ensuring that every hop authenticates the next is difficult, and the audits keep finding hops where it simply does not happen.10 The data-bearing companion protocol, GTP, carries weaknesses of its own, so that even a hardened Diameter control plane can sit beside a user plane with problems all its own.15

Regulator view ⋅ relative exposure

A 2018 advisory from ENISA, the EU's cybersecurity agency, formally warned operators about SS7 and Diameter weaknesses in modern networks.13 A U.S. FCC advisory committee placed the risk in proportion: Diameter presents a smaller attack surface than SS7 simply because SS7 remains the dominant deployed technology — one analysis found SS7 traffic traversing more than 200 countries against roughly 60 for Diameter, with far more discrete SS7 sources. The same report concluded that the SS7 use cases — location tracking, voice and SMS interception, subscriber denial of service, and account fraud or modification — carry over to Diameter, which additionally exposes user data sessions to interception.10

Denial of service deserves particular attention because of what now depends on these networks. Researchers demonstrated that DoS attacks could be conducted across mobile networks of every generation through Diameter, and warned that as more critical machines come online the consequences widen.15 The same misconfigurations that expose a phone also expose the long tail of cellular-connected infrastructure — the report-cited risk of sudden failures in ATMs, payment terminals, utility meters, and surveillance systems is a reminder that "the subscriber" is increasingly a machine whose loss of connectivity is not merely inconvenient.12

The defensive prescription tracks the SS7 case almost exactly: correct configuration of the Diameter signaling controller, intelligent routing, and a Diameter firewall at the network edge, backed by constant analysis of signaling as it crosses the border — monitoring that, the researchers noted, is exactly what industry guidelines encourage.1215

CH03Edge ⋅ radio access ⋅ surveillance

IMSI catchers: the cell tower that isn't

Where SS7 and Diameter reach a target from inside the network, the IMSI catcher reaches them through the air. At its simplest it is a device that impersonates a legitimate base station so that nearby phones connect to it, revealing their international mobile subscriber identities; modern units go much further, acting as full base-station emulators that sit man‑in‑the‑middle between the handset and the real network.16 The whole family of tools — called false base stations, rogue base stations, IMSI catchers, or cell-site simulators — exploits weaknesses present across cellular generations, from 2G through 5G.17

The technique works because of an asymmetry baked into early cellular design. Phones authenticate to the network — they prove they are legitimate subscribers — but in 2G the network does not authenticate back to the phone. A handset will therefore attach to whichever cell presents the strongest signal, with no way to tell a real tower from a counterfeit one.16

Figure 3. The catcher advertises itself as the strongest local cell, forces the handset down to 2G where mutual authentication is absent, can disable ciphering, and relays traffic onward — capturing identifiers and, in active configurations, content along the way.

HistoryHow "Stingray" became a common noun

The generic term traces to a brand. The StingRay is a specific product made by the Harris Corporation — now L3Harris Technologies — of Melbourne, Florida, sold to law enforcement and military agencies; like "Xerox" for photocopying, the name became shorthand for the whole category.16 Harris's briefcase-sized device can be run from a vehicle, and the company built out a product line around it.18 The original StingRay handled 2G; the StingRay II added 3G and 4G; the Hailstorm targeted 4G LTE; portable variants such as the KingFish supported surveillance on foot, with related models including the AmberJack and ArrowHead.1619 The hardware is expensive — a basic StingRay configuration ran on the order of $400,000, with the price climbing as agencies added capabilities.20 Harris is far from the only maker; other manufacturers include Rohde & Schwarz, Septier, and PKI, alongside numerous others whose products are widely available.16

What makes the catcher effective is the very behavior that keeps a phone reachable. Handsets periodically and automatically announce their presence to the nearest tower — even when idle, with no call in progress — so that the network can deliver service, and each such exchange reveals the IMSI tied to the SIM.18 A passive catcher need only listen for these announcements; an active one does considerably more.

MechanismDowngrade, strip, and relay

An active IMSI catcher typically chooses a frequency and cell parameters unused by nearby legitimate stations, forces the target handset to re-register to the fake cell, pushes it to downgrade from 3G to the weaker 2G network, sets the cipher mode to A5/0 to disable encryption, and then forwards the victim's traffic so that calls, data, and SMS still flow — allowing interception in the clear.21 Disabling or weakening encryption is the point: the GSM standard permits null or easily broken ciphers, turning intercepted traffic into recoverable audio and text.22

Later generations close the original hole. The 3G standard, and 4G and 5G after it, require mutual authentication so that the network must also prove itself to the handset, which defeats the classic GSM false-base-station attack.22 But sophisticated attacks sidestep this by forcing a downgrade to a generation that does not demand mutual authentication, and because most phones prefer the fastest available technology, a catcher may have to jam 3G and 4G frequencies to herd a device onto 2G.17 The gap, in other words, does not so much get closed as get relocated to whatever fallback the handset still permits.

Context ⋅ secrecy and oversight

Cell-site simulators have been deployed by law enforcement under court orders that often fall short of a search warrant, relying instead on the lower standard of a pen-register and trap-and-trace order, and agencies have been notably reluctant to disclose their use — Harris required purchasers to sign non-disclosure agreements.22 Under public pressure, Harris stopped selling the technology to local agencies while continuing to work with the federal government.19

5G and detectionConcealed identities, persistent gaps

5G adds a meaningful defense: the permanent subscriber identifier can be transmitted in encrypted form as a subscription concealed identifier (SUCI) rather than as a cleartext IMSI, denying a passive catcher the easy harvest it relied on.17 Yet researchers have shown the protection is not airtight — the standards still permit the network to request an identity at any moment, and by injecting a crafted identity request an attacker can prompt a handset to surrender its SUCI, which can then be tested against a known subscriber even much later.23

Because prevention is imperfect, detection matters. Open-source projects — the Osmocom mobile stack and Security Research Labs' IMSI-Catcher-Catcher for Android among them — let phones flag the anomalies a fake cell produces, while network-side approaches analyze standardized measurement reports to spot a rogue cell from the operator's vantage point.22 Machine-learning detectors trained on radio measurements have reached high detection rates with low false positives in controlled experiments, pointing toward defenses that watch the radio environment itself rather than trusting any single cell.24

What does a detector actually look for? The tell-tales of a false base station are the side effects of its tricks: a cell appearing with an unusually strong signal and no history, a sudden change of location or tracking area that forces every nearby phone to re-register, the absence of a sensible neighbor-cell list, a refusal to offer the usual ciphering, and a push toward an older radio technology than the surroundings would justify.22 None of these is conclusive alone — legitimate networks reconfigure and phones roam — which is why both app-based and network-side detectors lean on combinations of signals and, increasingly, on models trained to separate ordinary variation from the signature of a catcher.24 The deeper point is architectural: because a 2G handset cannot verify the network, robust detection has to come from watching patterns over time rather than trusting any single attach.

CH04Edge ⋅ carrier authentication ⋅ fraud

SIM swapping: when the phone number is the password

The cleverest attacks in this dossier require no protocol flaw at all. SIM swapping — also called a port-out scam — works by convincing a mobile carrier to transfer a victim's phone number to a SIM card the attacker controls, abusing the entirely legitimate feature of number portability. Once the swap completes, the victim's phone goes dark, and the attacker begins receiving all of the target's calls and texts, including the one-time passcodes used to log in and reset accounts. With those codes in hand, accounts protected by SMS-based two-factor authentication fall open.25

Figure 4. The chain pivots on social engineering, not cryptography. The decisive steps are persuading carrier staff and re-binding the number; everything downstream follows because so many services treat possession of a phone number as proof of identity.

EvidenceCarriers are the weak link

The definitive academic measurement came from Princeton. In a study presented at the 2020 Symposium on Usable Privacy and Security, Kevin Lee, Ben Kaiser, Jonathan Mayer, and Arvind Narayanan examined the authentication procedures five prepaid carriers used when a customer requested a SIM change, and found that all five relied on insecure challenges that an attacker could easily subvert — and that attackers needed only to defeat the single weakest challenge, because the rest could be bypassed.26 The researchers signed up for fifty prepaid accounts, built up call and text histories, then posed as fraudsters and called support; when asked for a PIN they would deliberately give the wrong one, then feign ignorance or claim careless data entry to talk past the follow-up questions. They further found that of 145 websites using phone-based authentication, seventeen could be fully compromised through a SIM swap alone.27

The Princeton work did not stay in the academy. It was cited by the Federal Communications Commission as the agency built consumer protections, and on November 15, 2023 the FCC adopted rule FCC 23‑95, setting baseline authentication and notification requirements specifically targeting SIM-swap and port-out fraud after years in which inconsistent carrier practices left customers exposed.25 Advocates pressed for more, arguing that carriers' duty to protect customer proprietary network information (CPNI) under Section 222 of the Communications Act means a successful SIM swap can itself implicate that obligation, and that SMS-delivered codes should not count as a safe harbor.28

Case ⋅ Terpin v. AT&T

The landmark dispute began with a January 2018 SIM swap that cost cryptocurrency investor Michael Terpin roughly $24 million; he sued AT&T for $224 million.29 According to the record, a teenager paid an employee at an AT&T authorized retailer to bypass the carrier's safeguards and move Terpin's number to a device the attackers controlled; they then triggered password resets to the hijacked number, reached his cloud storage, and recovered wallet credentials from a text file.29 Terpin's complaint detailed how his number was ported despite enhanced-security promises, after attackers had failed repeatedly at AT&T stores.30

The litigation has been long and consequential. A 2019 dismissal found Terpin had not adequately tied the swap to his loss, even as a separate civil judgment ordered the actual perpetrator to pay him more than $75 million; Terpin's open letter to the FCC pushed for measures like hiding customer PINs from employees and offering a "no-port" option.31 In 2024 the Ninth Circuit revived his Section 222 theory, and in July 2025 a district court partly denied AT&T's renewed bid for summary judgment, putting the question of carrier liability for SIM-swap losses on a path toward trial.29

Terpin's case is not unique. SIM swapping has reached prominent targets — the chief executive of Twitter was hit in 201932 — and prosecutors have secured convictions, including a British hacker known as "PlugwalkJoe," Joseph O'Connor, sentenced to five years in U.S. prison after stealing roughly $794,000 in cryptocurrency through a 2019 SIM swap.33 The scale has grown alongside the prominence: the FBI investigated 1,075 SIM-swap attacks in 2023, with losses approaching $50 million.25

The defenses are mostly procedural, which is why they have been so uneven. Customers can set a carrier PIN or passcode, request a port freeze or "no-port" lock, and — most importantly — move high-value accounts off SMS-based codes onto authenticator apps or hardware keys. Carriers, for their part, can restrict support-representative access to account data before a caller authenticates, deploy biometric or app-based verification, and run real-time anomaly detection on swap requests.2531 None of it is exotic; the gap has always been incentive and consistency rather than technology.

EcosystemA criminal supply chain

SIM swapping has matured from an opportunistic trick into an organized service. Reporting and case records describe a division of labor in which some actors specialize in social engineering or in recruiting and bribing insiders at carriers and their authorized retailers, while others buy access to perform swaps to order — the precise pattern visible in the Terpin matter, where an employee at an authorized retailer was paid to bypass safeguards.29 The buyers are often after high-value targets: cryptocurrency holders whose irreversible transactions make theft final, and account-takeover crews who use a hijacked number to defeat the SMS codes guarding email, which is itself the master key to most other accounts.27

This is why the defensive advice converges on removing the phone number from the security-critical path. Authenticator applications and hardware security keys break the chain because they never transmit a code an intercepted number could capture, and regulators have pushed carriers toward stronger customer authentication and prompt notification of any SIM change, so that a victim has a chance to react before the damage spreads.25 The number, in this telling, should be treated as a routing label and never as a credential.

Adjacent threat ⋅ Simjacker

Not every attack on the SIM requires a human at a support desk. In 2019, AdaptiveMobile Security disclosed Simjacker, an exploit that abuses a legacy SIM-card application called the S@T Browser by sending a specially formatted binary SMS whose instructions the SIM silently executes — retrieving the device's location and identifiers and returning them without any sign to the user.34 Unlike earlier SIM attacks, it required no secret key, only that the targeted card carried the vulnerable browser, and researchers assessed it could affect over a billion phones; a surveillance firm had reportedly used it for years.35 The vulnerable technology was found across at least 61 operators in 29 countries, with exploitation observed against subscribers in Mexico, Colombia, and Peru — a vivid illustration that the SIM itself is an attack surface.36

CH05Edge ⋅ radio chip ⋅ remote code execution

Baseband exploitation: attacking the radio's brain

Inside every phone there is a second computer most users never think about. The baseband processor runs the cellular protocol stack — the firmware that connects to the network, makes voice calls, and moves data — and it operates largely independently of the application processor that runs the visible operating system. Flaws in this firmware are uniquely dangerous: they can let an attacker eavesdrop on a phone's communications, place calls, send premium-rate SMS, or trigger large data transfers, all without the owner's knowledge.37

The reason the baseband is such an inviting target is a stark difference in hardening. Attacks on smartphones long concentrated on the application processor, where vendors deploy mitigations like address-space layout randomization, data-execution prevention, and code signing that make memory-corruption exploitation slow and expensive. The baseband stack, running on a separate processor, has historically received far less of that scrutiny — if it is hardened at all.38

Figure 5. The two processors keep two different security postures. The modem parses untrusted radio input with few defenses; a successful exploit there can attempt to cross the shared-memory boundary into the comparatively trusting application side.

History"All your baseband are belong to us"

The field was opened largely by one researcher. In 2010, Ralf-Philipp Weinmann, then at the University of Luxembourg, presented work titled "All Your Baseband Are Belong To Us," demonstrating over-the-air exploitation of memory corruptions in GSM software stacks and walking through the layered structure of the air interface that the baseband must parse.38 The enabling insight was economic: cheap, open-source packages such as OpenBTS made it feasible to stand up a malicious base station, a scenario existing security models had not accounted for. At Black Hat in 2011 he showed bugs in the firmware used by Qualcomm and Infineon chips to process GSM signals; his research had already produced fixes, including an Apple patch crediting him for a baseband bug affecting the iPhone 3G and later models.39

The work was formalized in a 2012 paper that won best-paper honors at the USENIX Workshop on Offensive Technologies, in which Weinmann analyzed two widely deployed baseband stacks and showed memory corruptions that could be leveraged to inject and execute arbitrary code on the baseband processor.40 The details underscored how broad the exposure was: with GSM carrying the great majority of cellular connections at the time, insufficient length checks emerged as the most common bug class, exploitation required nothing more than proximity to a malicious base station and no interaction from the user, and a compromised baseband could reach sensitive hardware such as the microphone and camera.41

Case ⋅ a modern baseband zero-day

Baseband research did not stop with GSM. In 2017, Weinmann — by then at the firm Comsecuris — disclosed a zero-day in the HiSilicon Balong integrated LTE modem used in Huawei devices, exploitable over the air via memory corruption.37 One reason it was unusually tractable: the vendor had published the baseband's source code, an unusual move that eased analysis. The flawed firmware reached a range of high-end Honor and Mate smartphones and extended beyond phones into laptop WWAN modules and IoT components, broadening the population at risk.42

PivotFrom the modem into the phone

The deeper worry is that a compromised baseband need not be the end of the chain. Follow-on research explored how code execution on the modem could be used to pivot across the inter-processor boundary into the application processor — a concern reinforced when Google's Project Zero published work by Gal Beniamini on compromising Broadcom Wi-Fi chips and elevating access toward the application side. The pattern, the researchers argued, was not specific to one vendor but reflected a design issue in modern mobile operating systems: the main OS tends to trust the integrity of its peripheral processors and does little to defend against them.43

The remedies follow directly from the diagnosis. The same disciplined practices used to harden desktop operating systems — systematic code audits, rigorous length and bounds checking on every field parsed from the radio, and exploit mitigations — need to be applied to baseband firmware, and the boundary between the modem and the application processor should enforce privilege separation and hard limits rather than implicit trust.4143 Treating the radio chip as a hostile parser of attacker-controlled input, rather than a trusted component, is the shift that matters.

AnatomyWhy the modem is hard to defend

Several properties of the baseband conspire to make it a soft, high-value target. It runs a real-time operating system tuned for the unforgiving timing of radio protocols, where the engineering priority is to never drop a frame rather than to validate every byte; it parses an enormous, layered grammar of signaling messages defined across decades of specifications; and it communicates with the application processor over an interface — historically a set of AT commands and a radio interface layer — and through regions of shared memory.37 Each of those is attack surface: the parser is reachable over the air from a malicious base station, and the inter-processor channel is reachable from the modem once it is compromised.

That second channel is why baseband bugs are more than a telephony nuisance. If the main operating system trusts whatever the modem places in shared memory, then code execution on the radio can become code execution — or at least data corruption — on the side of the device that holds the user's data, which is exactly the pivot researchers mapped from peripheral processors toward the application processor.43 Hardening therefore has two fronts: make the radio parser resilient, and stop the application side from extending blind trust across the boundary.

The economics have steadily shifted the target. As application processors grew harder to exploit, attention moved to the components that had not kept pace — the modem among them — and the same logic that once made GSM stacks attractive now applies to LTE and 5G basebands, whose growing complexity only widens the grammar an attacker can probe.42

CH06Core ⋅ 5G service-based architecture

5G slicing attacks: logical walls, shared foundations

5G introduces a feature with no real precedent in earlier networks: the same physical infrastructure can be partitioned into many independent virtual networks, each tuned to a different purpose. Network slicing carves a carrier's hardware into logical blocks — one slice optimized for the ultra-low latency a connected car needs, another for the reliability a hospital demands, another for the throughput of streaming entertainment — with each tenant, or vertical, getting what looks like its own dedicated network.44 The promise is efficiency and customization; the risk is that "looks like its own network" and "is its own network" are not the same thing, and the difference lives in software.45

Figure 6. Slices appear isolated to their tenants, but they share common network functions underneath. When a shared function cannot reliably tell which slice a request belongs to, the logical wall between tenants becomes porous.

DisclosureA flaw in the isolation model

In March 2021, AdaptiveMobile Security — later part of Enea — disclosed a fundamental security flaw in the design of 5G core network slicing. The problem lay in network functions that are hybrid, serving more than one slice at once: the architecture lacked a reliable mapping between a request's application-layer identity (which slice it claimed to belong to) and its transport-layer identity, so a shared function could be persuaded to act on behalf of a slice the requester had no right to touch.46 The consequence was a breakdown of the very isolation slicing is meant to guarantee, the kind of cross-tenant leakage that undermines the business case for dedicating a slice to a sensitive vertical in the first place.45

The researchers laid out three concrete classes of impact. An attacker positioned with access to one slice could extract data — including location information — about devices on a different slice; could mount a denial-of-service attack against a network function serving another slice; and could gain a measure of access to a different vertical's slice and its services entirely.46 Each maps onto the familiar triad of confidentiality, availability, and access, now expressed in the language of multi-tenant infrastructure rather than circuits or radio.44

Coordinated disclosure ⋅ proportion and response

The finding was handled through coordinated vulnerability disclosure with the industry's standards and operator bodies, and the researchers were careful to put the threat in proportion: with very few networks running multiple live slices at the time, the immediate real-world exposure was limited, even though the design issue was real and needed fixing before slicing scaled up.47 That framing — a serious architectural gap whose practical risk depended on deployment maturity — shaped the measured response from standards groups rather than any sense of imminent mass exploitation.4546

Beyond isolationSpillover and the research frontier

Isolation failures are not the only slicing concern. Because slices ultimately draw on a shared pool of radio and compute resources, a denial-of-service attack confined to one slice can spill over into others — a compromised fleet of IoT devices on a low-priority slice flooding the network can degrade the latency and availability that a critical slice was provisioned to guarantee, so that an attack on the weakest tenant becomes an attack on the most important one.48 The shared substrate that makes slicing efficient is exactly what makes this spillover possible.

The security research community has responded with tooling and systematic study aimed at this new surface — frameworks for replaying and fuzzing 5G traffic and for detecting cross-slice anomalies, alongside surveys cataloguing the threats to slice isolation, inter-slice communication, and the management plane that orchestrates it all.49 The throughline is consistent with everything before it in this dossier: a powerful new abstraction inherits security only to the extent that its shared underpinnings can authenticate who is really asking for what.

AnatomyInside the service-based core

Slicing rides on a deeper change in how the core is built. Where 4G connected a fixed set of network elements, 5G's service-based architecture decomposes the core into network functions that expose services to one another over web-style HTTP/2 interfaces — among them the access and mobility function, the session-management function, the user-plane function, and a repository function through which the others discover and call services.44 A slice is, in effect, a selected set of these functions identified by slice-selection information, with some functions dedicated to a single slice and others shared across many — and it is those shared, hybrid functions where the isolation flaw lived.49

The shift to APIs is a double-edged inheritance. It brings the maturity of web security — tokens, transport encryption, and well-understood authentication patterns — but it also imports the web's failure modes, where an over-trusting service that does not rigorously check the authority behind each request becomes a lever for lateral movement. The slicing disclosure is best read as that lesson arriving in the telephone core: even with strong protection at the perimeter, a function that cannot bind a request's claimed identity to its actual source can be made to act for the wrong tenant.49

CH07Edge ⋅ IP telephony ⋅ SIP/RTP

VoIP attacks: the protocol that replaced the dial tone

Most of the world's voice traffic no longer rides dedicated circuits; it travels as packets over the internet using the Session Initiation Protocol. SIP is the IETF signaling protocol that sets up, modifies, and tears down the calls in a voice-over-IP system, while the conversation itself flows in a companion media stream. That move from purpose-built telephony hardware to general-purpose IP brought enormous flexibility — and inherited every weakness of an internet-facing service, which is why freely available SIP scanning tools have been turned to malicious ends almost since the protocol became common.50

The foundational problem is that SIP and its media companion, the Real-time Transport Protocol, were designed to transmit in cleartext by default, exposing both call setup and call content to anyone who can observe the traffic.51 The relevant ports are well known — SIP commonly listens on 5060 over UDP or TCP, with 5061 reserved for the TLS-secured variant — so an internet-exposed VoIP endpoint advertises exactly where to knock, and when TLS is not configured, what it says is in the clear.52

Figure 7. A normal SIP exchange registers a user, invites the far end, and then carries media directly between endpoints. Each leg is a pressure point: registration can be forged, the proxy can be flooded or enumerated, and unencrypted media can be captured or injected.

MechanismThe catalogue of VoIP attacks

The attack surface is broad because each phase of a call is separately abusable. Eavesdropping is the most direct: with access to the network path, an attacker captures the unencrypted RTP stream and reconstructs the audio of a conversation.53 Registration hijacking subverts call routing — by sending forged SIP REGISTER messages, an attacker can bind a legitimate user's address to attacker-controlled equipment, diverting that user's incoming calls.54 Beyond those, security assessments of SIP systems routinely probe for user-extension enumeration, denial of service through floods of SIP requests, man-in-the-middle interception, and media-path attacks such as RTP injection and the family of "RTP Bleed" issues that let an attacker inject or siphon audio without fully compromising the signaling.55

Two abuses translate most directly into money and deception. Caller-ID spoofing lets an attacker present any number they choose, the technical enabler behind voice phishing, or "vishing," in which a victim is socially engineered by someone appearing to call from a trusted institution. Toll fraud turns a compromised phone system into a profit center, with attackers placing large volumes of calls to premium-rate or international numbers and leaving the victim with the bill.54

Tooling & defenseSIPVicious and the hardened edge

The same dual-use dynamic seen with SS7 research recurs here. Tools built to test VoIP security are openly available and widely used by defenders — and, predictably, by attackers: one such suite was observed being pushed in malicious campaigns against VoIP systems.50 The best-known example, SIPVicious, is an open-source toolkit from Enable Security whose components map cleanly to the early stages of an assessment — svmap to discover SIP devices on a network, svwar to enumerate valid extensions on a PBX, and svcrack to test credentials — the same reconnaissance an auditor performs with permission to find exposure before a criminal does.5651

The defensive answer is to stop trusting the network and the caller alike. Encrypting signaling with SIP over TLS on 5061 and media with the Secure Real-time Transport Protocol closes off eavesdropping and tampering; placing a session border controller at the edge of the VoIP network filters malformed traffic, rate-limits floods, hides internal topology, and enforces policy; and the STIR/SHAKEN framework attaches a cryptographic attestation to the calling number as it crosses carrier networks — rated full, partial, or gateway — to blunt caller-ID spoofing at the root.54 Together they turn an open, trusting endpoint into one that authenticates its peers and protects its content.

AnatomySIP messages and signed calls

A little more structure clarifies both the attacks and the cure. SIP is text-based and request-driven, with methods such as REGISTER to bind a user to a location, INVITE to start a session, BYE to end one, and CANCEL to abort a pending one, each answered by numeric responses much like the web's. That legibility is convenient for engineers and attackers alike: a flood of INVITE requests can exhaust a server, a forged BYE or CANCEL can tear down someone else's call, and a forged REGISTER can steal their incoming calls.55 Because the signaling is plainly readable when TLS is absent, an observer on the path learns who is calling whom and can tamper with the exchange.51

STIR/SHAKEN attacks the spoofing problem at its root by making the calling number accountable. As a call leaves an originating provider, the network attaches a signed token — a PASSporT carried in the SIP signaling — vouching for the relationship between the caller and the number, and the terminating provider verifies that signature, assigning an attestation level reflecting how confident the origin was in the number's legitimacy.54 It does not by itself stop fraud, but it gives carriers and analytics systems a cryptographic basis for trusting or distrusting a displayed number — the first real dent in caller-ID spoofing after decades in which the displayed number was simply asserted.

Adoption, not availability, is the gating factor. The encryption that closes off eavesdropping and tampering has existed for years, yet plenty of deployments still run signaling and media in the clear for reasons of cost, compatibility with legacy endpoints, or simple inertia — the same pattern of optional-security-left-off seen throughout the older telephone network, now replayed on IP.52

CH08Defense ⋅ across the stack

Defending the stack: filtering the edge, watching the middle

No single control answers the threats in this dossier, because they span the radio, the signaling core, the carrier's support desk, and the phone's own silicon. What works is defense in depth — layered controls at each tier, backed by continuous monitoring — and a clear-eyed acceptance that the oldest, weakest layers will outlive every plan to retire them. The industry's own guidance reflects this: the GSMA publishes a body of recommendations covering SS7 and SIGTRAN security, signaling implementation, LTE and EPC roaming, and the monitoring of interconnect traffic, giving operators a concrete baseline to measure themselves against.3

Figure 8. Controls stack from the handset outward. The decisive additions are a filtering layer at the signaling edge — firewalls and the 5G security gateway — and anomaly detection that watches cross-border traffic for the patterns that precede an attack.

Lessons unlearnedWhy the gaps persisted

It is worth pausing on a recurring theme: many protections were designed and then never adopted. Security mechanisms existed on paper for the legacy signaling protocols — encryption and authentication layers for MAP and the transaction sublayer, and options to run Diameter over IPsec or DTLS — yet they went largely unused across the deployed networks, leaving the door open that researchers kept walking through.57 The lesson 5G's designers drew was that optional security becomes absent security, so the newest architecture builds protection into the perimeter rather than offering it as a setting.

5G's answerThe Security Edge Protection Proxy

5G's signaling defense centers on a new network element. The Security Edge Protection Proxy (SEPP) sits at the boundary of a carrier's network and mediates all control-plane signaling that crosses between operators, over an interface known as N32, as specified in the 3GPP security standard TS 33.501.58 Rather than trusting a peer simply because it reached the interconnect, the SEPP enforces a security association: the two proxies establish a protected channel, applying transport-layer security and protecting the integrity and confidentiality of signaling messages using JSON Web Encryption and JSON Web Signature constructs, so that messages are authenticated and tamper-evident end to end.59

The design also accounts for the messy reality that operators rarely connect directly. Roaming traffic typically transits intermediaries — the IPX providers that sit between carriers — and 5G addresses this with Protection for Inter-Network Signaling (PRINS), splitting the N32 interface into a control connection that negotiates security and a forwarding connection that carries protected messages, with mutual TLS and topology hiding so that intermediaries can perform necessary mediation without being handed the keys to everything.60 To keep these elements honest, 3GPP defines security assurance specifications — the SCAS series, including the assurance requirements for the SEPP itself — that turn architectural intent into testable criteria a product must meet.61

The constantFirewall and monitoring

Across every generation, two controls recur because they work. A signaling firewall at the network edge inspects messages for plausibility — does this query make sense coming from this peer, for this subscriber, at this moment — and rejects the implausible. And continuous monitoring of signaling as it crosses borders catches the reconnaissance patterns and anomalous sequences that precede an attack, which is precisely what industry guidance and security researchers advise operators to deploy.1415 Filtering blocks the obvious; monitoring catches the novel; neither alone is sufficient, and together they form the practical floor of interconnect defense.

ProcessPeople, incentives, and regulation

Technology is only part of the answer, because several of these attacks defeat process rather than cryptography. SIM swapping succeeds at a support desk, so the controls that matter are training, restricting what a representative can see or do before a caller is authenticated, and removing the phone number from the security-critical path; regulators have leaned on exactly these levers, adopting baseline authentication and notification duties for carriers.25 The same regulatory pressure reshaped IP telephony, where caller-ID attestation moved from a good idea to a mandated framework as authorities pushed back against the flood of spoofed and fraudulent calls.

Incentives explain much of the historical gap. Optional security mechanisms went unused for years because turning them on cost money and effort while the losses landed on subscribers rather than operators; advisories from bodies such as ENISA, and the shift in 5G toward protection that is built in rather than bolted on, reflect a slow correction of that imbalance.57 The durable lesson across every channel in this dossier is that a control which can be skipped will be skipped, and that monitoring exists precisely to catch the cases where prevention was, once again, not switched on.3

Monitoring deserves that closing emphasis as the backstop that assumes prevention will sometimes fail. Cross-border signaling analysis can flag the reconnaissance preceding an attack — bursts of location queries, anomalous routing requests, sequences no legitimate roaming pattern would produce — turning the interconnect from a blind spot into an observable surface, which is the precondition for responding at all.14

End of dossier

SynthesisOne pattern, seven channels

Step back from the individual attacks and a single shape emerges. SS7 answers any peer it believes is a carrier; Diameter ships with locks its operators leave open; the IMSI catcher exploits a handset's willingness to trust the loudest tower; SIM swapping turns a carrier's helpfulness against its customer; baseband exploitation finds the one processor nobody hardened; 5G slicing shows a shared function that cannot always tell its tenants apart; and VoIP rebuilds the dial tone on a protocol that speaks in the clear. Every one is a failure of authentication — a system extending trust by default to a party that never earned it.

That diagnosis is also the cure, and it explains why the defenses rhyme from chapter to chapter: authenticate the peer, encrypt the channel, filter at the edge, and watch the middle for the patterns that betray an attacker probing for the trust still left to exploit. 5G's SEPP is, at bottom, the telephone network finally deciding to ask other networks to prove who they are. The work that remains is less about inventing new protections than about the harder, unglamorous task of turning them on everywhere and leaving the lights on — because the attack surface is cumulative, the oldest layers endure, and a network is only ever as trustworthy as the least authenticated request it will still answer.
