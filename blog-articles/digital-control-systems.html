A digital controller is, at bottom, a startlingly small idea: read a number, do some arithmetic, write a number, wait for the clock, repeat. Everything difficult about digital control follows from the two boundaries where that loop meets the physical world — the instant a continuous signal is sampled into a number, and the instant a number is held back out as a continuous voltage. Between those boundaries lives a world of discrete-time mathematics; on either side lives the messy analog plant we actually care about. The discipline of digital control is the art of reasoning across that seam.

For most of the twentieth century, control was analog. A pneumatic or electronic controller applied a continuous correction to a continuous error, and the mathematics of Laplace transforms, Bode plots and root loci described it beautifully. The digital computer changed the economics but not the physics. The first industrial process computer, a Ramo-Wooldridge RW-300, went online at Texaco's Port Arthur refinery in Texas on 12 March 1959, running a catalytic polymerization unit and lifting the conversion of propylene to gasoline from roughly 80% to 91% — enough to pay for its $98,000 price in under three years.[1] That machine, however, still only computed setpoints for conventional analog controllers. The decisive step — letting the computer close the loop directly, computing the actuator command itself — is direct digital control (DDC), and it arrived in November 1962 when Imperial Chemical Industries put a Ferranti Argus computer in command of a soda-ash plant at Fleetwood, Lancashire, handling 120 control loops and 256 measurements. The Argus was a repackaged descendant of the guidance computer for the Bloodhound surface-to-air missile.[1,2]

Once the computer is inside the loop, three facts become unavoidable, and they organize this entire monograph. First, the controller sees the plant only at discrete sampling instants; between samples it is blind, and its output is frozen. Second, the natural language for signals defined only at sample instants is not the Laplace transform but the z-transform, and stability lives inside the unit circle rather than the left half-plane. Third, the controller runs on real, finite hardware: it samples at some rate, quantizes to some number of bits, and takes some non-zero time to compute. Get any of these wrong and a design that is flawless on paper will chatter, drift or diverge on the bench.

◆ The through-line

We treat the subject in four movements: (I) the discrete-time signals and systems that sampling creates; (II) how to analyze such systems for stability and response; (III) how to design digital controllers, by emulating a continuous design or by designing directly in the z-domain; and (IV) the real-time implementation issues — sampling rate, quantization, computational delay — that decide whether the design survives contact with hardware.

Part I

Discrete-time signals & systems

1.1Sampling, the zero-order hold, and aliasing

Sampling replaces a continuous signal x(t) with the sequence of its values at the sample instants, x[k] = x(kT), where T is the sampling period and fs = 1/T (or ωs = 2π/T) the sampling rate. The idealized mathematical model of a sampler is impulse sampling: multiply x(t) by a train of Dirac impulses spaced T apart, producing

x*(t) = ∑k=−∞∞ x(kT) δ(t − kT)(1)

This is a modelling fiction — no real converter emits impulses — but it is the fiction that makes the frequency-domain picture clean. Taking the Fourier transform of (1) and using the fact that multiplying by an impulse train convolves the spectrum with another impulse train yields the single most important equation about sampling:

X*(jω) = 1T ∑n=−∞∞ X ( j(ω − nωs) )(2)

The spectrum of the sampled signal is the spectrum of the original, replicated at every integer multiple of the sampling frequency and scaled by 1/T. Sampling in time is periodization in frequency. Everything about aliasing, reconstruction and anti-alias filtering is a corollary of this replication.[3,4]

The sampling theorem

If the replicas in (2) do not overlap, the original spectrum survives intact inside a single period and the signal can, in principle, be reconstructed perfectly. This is the sampling theorem: a signal band-limited to a highest frequency fmax is completely determined by samples taken at a rate fs > 2 fmax. The threshold 2 fmax is the Nyquist rate; half the sampling rate, fs/2, is the Nyquist frequency or folding frequency.

▮ A theorem with five names

The result is a textbook case of Stigler's law. Harry Nyquist stated the underlying criterion for telegraph transmission in 1928;[3] Claude Shannon gave the clean reconstruction theorem in his 1949 Communication in the Presence of Noise — a manuscript actually received in 1940 but held back by the war.[4] Vladimir Kotelnikov had proved an equivalent result in the Soviet Union in 1933, and the mathematician E. T. Whittaker had published the interpolation formula in 1915. It is variously the Nyquist–Shannon, Whittaker–Kotelnikov–Shannon (WKS), or simply the sampling theorem.[3,4]

Aliasing

If fs < 2 fmax, the replicas in (2) overlap. Energy from frequencies above the Nyquist frequency folds down and adds to the baseband, masquerading as a lower frequency. This is aliasing, and it is irreversible: once two frequencies share the same samples, no amount of later processing can tell them apart. Concretely, a sinusoid at frequency f and one at f + n fs produce identical sample sequences.

✓ Anti-alias filtering is not optional

Because real plant signals and sensor noise are never truly band-limited, a continuous-time anti-aliasing filter must precede the sampler to attenuate everything above the Nyquist frequency before it can fold. This is analog hardware — you cannot filter away aliasing after the fact in software. A common rule is to place the anti-alias cutoff below fs/2 and to choose fs high enough that the filter's own phase lag near the control bandwidth stays modest.[5,6]

The zero-order hold

On the output side, a digital-to-analog converter does the opposite of sampling: it turns a number sequence back into a continuous voltage. The overwhelmingly common device is the zero-order hold (ZOH), which simply latches each sample and holds it constant for one full period — the staircase in Fig. 2. Its impulse response is a unit pulse of width T, so its transfer function is

Gh0(s) = 1 − e−sTs(3)

Two consequences of (3) matter for control. Its magnitude response is a sinc that gently attenuates toward the Nyquist frequency, and — more importantly — its phase is that of an average half-sample delay. To a first approximation the ZOH injects a pure time delay of T/2 into the loop. That delay erodes phase margin, and it is a major reason digital loops must sample considerably faster than the naïve Nyquist minimum.[5,6]

1.2The z-transform and its inverse

The Laplace transform is the right tool for continuous signals; for sequences we need its discrete analogue. The (one-sided) z-transform of a sequence x[k] defined for k ≥ 0 is

X(z) = ∑k=0∞ x[k] z−k(4)

where z is a complex variable and the set of z for which the sum converges is the region of convergence. The variable earns its name: z−1 is the unit-delay operator, shifting a sequence one sample into the past. The bridge back to continuous time is the substitution

z = esT(5)

which is exactly how the impulse-sampled Laplace transform collapses to the z-transform. Equation (5) is the master key to the whole subject: it maps the s-plane onto the z-plane, carries the imaginary axis onto the unit circle, and turns the stable left half-plane into the stable interior of the unit disk.

▮ Where the "z" came from

The idea traces to de Moivre's generating functions (1730) and was reintroduced by Witold Hurewicz in 1947 to handle the pulsed-data radar servos of the MIT Radiation Laboratory.[7] The name z-transform was coined in 1952 by John Ragazzini and Lotfi Zadeh in the sampled-data control group at Columbia University — a group that also included Rudolf Kalman, John Bertram, Bernard Friedland and Gene Franklin.[8] Eliahu Jury then systematized the method and the modified (advanced) z-transform.[9,10]

The essential properties are linearity; the delay theorem ℤ{x[k−n]} = z−nX(z) (the workhorse for turning difference equations into algebra); convolution mapping to multiplication; and the final-value theorem, valid when the system is stable,

limk→∞ x[k] = limz→1 (z − 1) X(z)(6)

which reads steady-state values straight off the transform and underlies every steady-state error calculation in §2.2.

Inverting the z-transform

Three techniques recover the sequence from X(z). (i) Long division / power series: divide numerator by denominator in ascending powers of z−1; the quotient coefficients are x[0], x[1], x[2], … directly. This is exact but gives no closed form. (ii) Partial fractions: expand X(z)/z into simple terms, then read each back through Table 1 — the standard route to a closed-form sequence. (iii) Residues / contour integration: the formal inverse is a contour integral around the unit circle, evaluated by the residue theorem; indispensable for theory, seldom needed by hand.[11,13]

◆ Why divide X(z) by z before partial fractions?

z-transforms of interest almost always carry a factor of z in the numerator (see Table 1). Expanding X(z)/z keeps each partial-fraction term in the tidy form z/(z−p) after you multiply the z back in — which is exactly the entry that inverts to pk. Skip the step and the algebra fights you.

1.3Difference equations and pulse transfer functions

Where a continuous system obeys a differential equation, a discrete system obeys a linear constant-coefficient difference equation relating present and past inputs and outputs:

y[k] + a1y[k−1] + … + any[k−n] = b0u[k] + b1u[k−1] + … + bmu[k−m](7)

Two readings of (7) coexist, and both are useful. As a recurrence, solved for y[k], it is literally the code the controller runs each tick — a weighted sum of stored past values. As an algebraic object, apply the delay theorem to every term and the difference equation becomes a ratio of polynomials in z, the pulse transfer function:

G(z) = Y(z)U(z) = b0 + b1z−1 + … + bmz−m1 + a1z−1 + … + anz−n(8)

Its roots behave exactly as in continuous time: poles (roots of the denominator) govern the natural modes and stability, zeros (roots of the numerator) shape the response. The difference is only where "stable" lives — inside the unit circle, per (5).

Discretizing a continuous plant: the ZOH equivalent

Usually the plant is continuous, described by G(s), and it is driven through a zero-order hold. The correct discrete model — the step-invariant or ZOH-equivalent transform — accounts for the hold exactly and is the single most-used discretization in the field:

G(z) = (1 − z−1) ℤ { ℒ−1[ G(s)/s ] }(9)

In words: put a step into the hold-plus-plant, sample the continuous step response, and z-transform it. For the ubiquitous first-order plant G(s) = a/(s+a) (unity DC gain, time constant 1/a), equation (9) gives the clean result

G(z) = 1 − e−aTz − e−aT(10)

Note how the continuous pole at s = −a lands precisely at z = e−aT, faithful to (5). The numerator, however, is not simply the image of the continuous numerator — the hold introduces its own sampling zeros. Discretizing higher-order plants routinely creates zeros with no continuous counterpart, some of them outside the unit circle (non-minimum-phase), which quietly constrains what controllers can do.[11,12]

discretize.pypython
# ZOH-equivalent discretization with the python-control package
import control as ct

a, T = 2.0, 0.1                      # plant pole and sample period
G_s = ct.tf([a], [1, a])             # G(s) = a/(s+a)
G_z = ct.sample_system(G_s, T, method='zoh')
print(G_z)                          # -> (1 - e^-aT)/(z - e^-aT)

# the discrete pole sits exactly at e^{-aT}, matching z = e^{sT}
print(ct.poles(G_z), 'vs', __import__('math').exp(-a*T))

⚠ Samplers do not commute with blocks

A subtle trap: the pulse transfer function of two continuous blocks in cascade depends on where the samplers are. If a single sampler feeds the whole cascade, you transform the product G1(s)G2(s) as one unit — written ℤ{G1G2} = G1G2(z). If a sampler sits between them, you transform each separately and multiply, giving G1(z)G2(z) — a different result. Treating G1G2 as G1(z)G2(z) is one of the most common errors in sampled-data analysis.[13,11]

Part II

Discrete-time analysis

2.1Stability in the z-plane

A discrete linear system is bounded-input bounded-output (BIBO) stable if and only if every pole of its pulse transfer function lies strictly inside the unit circle, |z| < 1. The reason is immediate from the modes: a simple pole at z = p contributes a term proportional to pk to the response, and pk decays only when |p| < 1, holds constant when |p| = 1, and explodes when |p| > 1. Poles exactly on the unit circle are marginal; repeated poles on it are unstable. This is the discrete counterpart of "poles in the open left half-plane," and it follows directly from the map z = esT of (5), which carries Re s < 0 to |z| < 1.[11,13]

Finding the poles directly means factoring the characteristic polynomial, which for anything above second order is tedious and, on quantized coefficients, numerically fraught. Two classical tools decide stability without computing the roots.

Jury's stability test

Just as the Routh–Hurwitz criterion decides left-half-plane stability from the coefficients of a continuous characteristic polynomial, Jury's test decides unit-circle stability from the coefficients of a discrete one. Eliahu Jury introduced it in 1961–62 as the direct discrete analogue.[9] For a characteristic polynomial

P(z) = anzn + an−1zn−1 + … + a1z + a0, an > 0(11)

one builds the Jury array: the first row is the coefficients, the second the same reversed; each new pair of rows is formed from 2×2 determinants of the two rows above, shrinking by one entry each time. The system is stable if and only if a set of conditions on the array holds — beginning with the three easy boundary checks that catch most instabilities on sight:

  1. P(1) > 0
  2. (−1)n P(−1) > 0
  3. |a0| < an

followed by n−1 inequalities on the first entries of successive array rows. For the important second-order case P(z) = z2 + a1z + a0, the three boundary conditions are already necessary and sufficient, and they carve out the celebrated stability triangle in the (a1, a0) plane:

a0 < 1, a0 > −1 + a1, a0 > −1 − a1(12)

◆ Worked example — the stability triangle in action

Take P(z) = z2 − 1.2z + 0.35 (the plant denominator used later, with poles at 0.5 and 0.7). Check: P(1) = 1 − 1.2 + 0.35 = 0.15 > 0 ✓; P(−1) = 1 + 1.2 + 0.35 = 2.55 > 0 ✓; |a0| = 0.35 < 1 ✓. All three hold, so the system is stable — no factoring required. Nudge a0 to 1.05 and the third condition fails instantly: a pole has left the disk.

The bilinear (w) transformation and Routh–Hurwitz

The second route reuses the continuous machinery outright. The bilinear transformation

z = 1 + w1 − w ⟷ w = z − 1z + 1(13)

maps the interior of the unit z-circle onto the left half of the w-plane and the unit circle onto the imaginary w-axis. Substitute (13) into the characteristic polynomial, clear denominators, and apply the ordinary Routh–Hurwitz criterion in w: unit-circle stability in z is now left-half-plane stability in w. The same transformation, as we will see in §3.2, turns z-domain frequency-response design into a nearly ordinary Bode problem. It is worth stressing that this w is a purely algebraic device for stability and design; it is not the physical frequency variable, though a rescaled version tracks real frequency closely below the Nyquist limit.[13,16]

2.2Transient and steady-state response

Pole location inside the disk determines the character of the response, and the map z = esT translates the familiar continuous intuitions into polar coordinates on the z-plane. A continuous complex pole pair s = −ζωn ± jωd maps to a discrete pole at radius and angle

|z| = e−ζωnT, ∠z = ωdT(14)

so the reading of the plane is direct: radius sets decay (poles near the origin are fast and heavily damped; poles hugging the unit circle are lightly damped and ring for a long time), while angle sets oscillation frequency (a pole on the positive real axis, angle 0, is non-oscillatory; angle π, i.e. z=−1, oscillates at the Nyquist frequency, alternating sign every sample). Settling time is governed by the pole of largest radius — the dominant pole — and overshoot by its damping. Pushing all closed-loop poles to z = 0 is the limiting case of "as fast as possible": the response dies in a finite number of samples. That is precisely the deadbeat idea of §3.3.[11,12]

Steady-state error and system type

Steady-state accuracy follows from the final-value theorem (6) applied to the error. For a unity-feedback loop with open-loop pulse transfer function L(z) = D(z)G(z), the system type is the number of open-loop poles at z = 1 — that is, the number of factors of 1/(z−1), each a discrete integrator (accumulator). Type determines which reference signals can be tracked with zero error, exactly as in continuous time, through three error constants:

Kp = limz→1 L(z), Kv = 1T limz→1(z−1)L(z), Ka = 1T2 limz→1(z−1)2L(z)(15)

The factors (z−1)/T are the discrete stand-in for differentiation by s. The resulting steady-state errors are collected in Table 2.

◆ Worked example — reading steady-state error off L(z)

Suppose L(z) = 0.2/(z − 1), a single accumulator (type 1). For a step reference, the loop has an integrator, so the step error is zero. For a ramp, Kv = (1/T) limz→1(z−1)L(z) = 0.2/T, giving a finite steady-state lag ess = 1/Kv = 5T. ⚠ The output between samples can lie to you

Every quantity above describes the response at the sampling instants. A discrete design can look immaculate on its sampled output while the true continuous signal swings badly between samples — so-called intersample ripple or "hidden oscillations," a notorious failure mode of aggressive deadbeat designs. Sampled-instant perfection is necessary but not sufficient; always verify the continuous behaviour (a finer-grid or multirate simulation), especially when the controller cancels plant poles or pushes for finite settling.[11,12]

Part III

Digital controller design

There are two philosophies for arriving at a digital controller, and a practitioner should know both. Emulation designs a continuous controller with the mature classical toolkit and then approximates it digitally. Direct digital design works in the z-domain from the outset, treating the sampled nature of the problem as fundamental rather than as an afterthought. Emulation is faster to reach and intuitive; direct design is more powerful, especially when the sampling rate is not lavish.

3.1Emulation: design in continuous time, then discretize

The recipe is disarmingly simple. Design D(s) for the continuous plant by root locus, Bode, or any method you like, verify it, then replace s with a discrete approximation to obtain D(z) and run it as a difference equation. The whole art is in the choice of approximation, because each substitution distorts the controller differently and treats stability differently. Table 3 collects the standard maps.

The Tustin (bilinear) transformation is the workhorse because it is the only simple substitution that maps the entire stable left half-plane exactly onto the interior of the unit disk — no stable controller is ever accidentally destabilized. Its price is frequency warping: the whole infinite jω-axis is compressed onto the finite unit circle, so critical frequencies shift. Prewarping rescales the substitution so that one chosen frequency (a crossover, a notch) lands exactly where intended.

▮ Arnold Tustin and the trapezoidal rule

The bilinear map is named for the British engineer Arnold Tustin, who in 1947 published a method of analysing linear systems "in terms of time series."[19] The substitution is nothing more exotic than the trapezoidal rule for numerical integration written as a transfer function — which is why it preserves stability and phase so gracefully compared with the cruder rectangular (Euler) rules.

◆ Worked example — emulating a PI controller by Tustin

A continuous PI controller is D(s) = Kp + Ki/s. Applying the trapezoidal (Tustin) rule to the integral term, 1/s → (T/2) (z+1)/(z−1), gives the discrete controller

D(z) = Kp + Ki T2 z + 1z − 1

which, written as a recurrence, is u[k] = u[k−1] + Kp(e[k]−e[k−1]) + (KiT/2)(e[k]+e[k−1]) — the familiar velocity-form PI, with the integral realized as a running trapezoidal sum.

emulate.pypython
# Emulate a continuous lead compensator digitally (three ways)
import control as ct

D_s = ct.tf([1, 2.0], [1, 12.0])          # lead: (s+2)/(s+12)
T   = 0.02                                 # 50 Hz sampling

D_tustin = ct.sample_system(D_s, T, method='tustin')   # recommended
D_mpz    = ct.sample_system(D_s, T, method='matched')  # matched pole-zero
D_bwd    = ct.sample_system(D_s, T, method='backward_diff')

3.2Direct digital design

Direct design accepts the z-domain as home ground. Three techniques dominate.

Root locus in the z-plane

The root-locus construction is identical in form to the continuous case — the branches, asymptotes, and angle/departure rules all carry over — but the plot is read against a different yardstick. Instead of asking when branches cross the imaginary axis into the right half-plane, we ask when they cross the unit circle. The gain at that crossing is the stability limit; design proceeds by choosing loop gain (and compensator poles/zeros) to seat the dominant closed-loop poles at a target radius and angle that deliver the wanted damping and settling per (14).[11,12]

Frequency-response design in the w-plane

Bode and Nyquist reasoning does not apply directly in z, because the frequency axis is a circle and gain/phase plots against it are awkward. The bilinear transformation (13) rescues the method: mapping z → w straightens the unit circle back into an imaginary axis, so a plant expressed in w has an ordinary Bode plot. One designs a compensator in w for the usual targets — gain and phase margins, crossover frequency, bandwidth — then maps the finished compensator back to z for implementation. Frequency warping again requires care: the fictitious w-frequency and the true frequency agree at low values but diverge approaching the Nyquist limit, so margins are specified against the (rescaled) w-frequency with that relationship in mind.[13,16]

Analytical (direct synthesis) design

The most distinctively digital method dispenses with loci and plots and simply demands a closed-loop transfer function. Choose the desired input-to-output response T(z), then invert the loop algebra to obtain the controller that produces it. For a unity-feedback loop, T = DG/(1+DG) rearranges to

D(z) = 1G(z) ⋅ T(z)1 − T(z)(16)

This is the method of Ragazzini, and its power is matched by two constraints that must not be violated — the difference between an elegant design and one that silently harbours a hidden instability:[13,11]

  1. Causality / realizability. T(z) must contain at least as many samples of pure delay as G(z) does, or (16) demands a non-causal controller that predicts the future. In z-transform terms, the pole excess of T must be at least that of G.
  2. Internal stability (no unstable cancellations). The factor 1/G in (16) tries to cancel the plant's poles and zeros. Cancelling a plant zero outside the unit circle, or a plant pole outside it, produces a hidden unstable mode that the sampled output never reveals. The cure is structural: any non-minimum-phase plant zero must be retained as a zero of T(z), and any unstable plant pole must appear as a zero of 1−T(z).

3.3Three canonical digital controllers

The digital PID controller

PID remains the overwhelming majority of loops in service, and its digital form is a direct discretization of the three continuous terms — proportional acting on the present error, integral on its accumulation, derivative on its rate. Discretizing the integral by a backward (or trapezoidal) sum and the derivative by a backward difference yields the positional algorithm; differencing that once more yields the velocity (incremental) algorithm that computes the change in output each step. The velocity form is usually preferred in practice for three reasons: the integral action is implicit (the accumulator is the actuator itself), which makes anti-windup nearly automatic when the output is clamped; transfer to and from manual is bumpless; and there is no large stored integral to unwind after a fault.[11,18]

✓ Four things a real digital PID must do

  • Filter the derivative. A pure difference amplifies quantization and sensor noise enormously; use a first-order-filtered derivative (coefficient N, typically 5–20).
  • Take the derivative on the measurement, not the error, so a step change in setpoint does not produce a violent "derivative kick."
  • Handle actuator saturation (windup). Clamp the output and stop integrating against the limit — trivial in velocity form, explicit (back-calculation or conditional integration) in positional form.
  • Ensure bumpless transfer when switching modes or retuning, by initializing state to the current output.
pid_velocity.pypython
# Incremental (velocity-form) digital PID: derivative on measurement,
# first-order derivative filtering, and inherent anti-windup via output clamp.
class PID:
    def __init__(self, Kp, Ki, Kd, Ts, N=10.0, u_min=-1.0, u_max=1.0):
        self.Kp, self.Ki, self.Kd, self.Ts, self.N = Kp, Ki, Kd, Ts, N
        self.u_min, self.u_max = u_min, u_max
        self.e1 = 0.0                 # previous error (for P, I increments)
        self.y1 = self.y2 = 0.0       # previous measurements (for D)
        self.d  = 0.0                 # filtered derivative state
        self.u  = 0.0                 # current output (holds the integral)

    def update(self, setpoint, meas):
        e  = setpoint - meas
        # proportional + integral increments (backward difference)
        dP = self.Kp * (e - self.e1)
        dI = self.Ki * self.Ts * e
        # derivative on measurement, first-order filtered (avoids kick + noise)
        a  = self.N * self.Ts
        self.d = (self.d + self.Kd * self.N * (2*self.y1 - self.y2 - meas)) / (1 + a)
        du = dP + dI + self.d
        u  = self.u + du
        # clamp -> in velocity form this is the anti-windup (state lives in u)
        if   u > self.u_max: u = self.u_max
        elif u < self.u_min: u = self.u_min
        self.e1, self.y2, self.y1, self.u = e, self.y1, meas, u
        return u

The deadbeat controller

Deadbeat control is direct synthesis pushed to its extreme: demand that the output reach the reference and stay there in the minimum possible number of samples, with no error and no ripple afterward. For an nth-order plant with no delay, the target is simply T(z) = z−n, placing every closed-loop pole at the origin; (16) then delivers the controller. The reward is a response with zero steady-state error, minimum settling time, and typically under 2% overshoot at the sample instants.[11,17] The cost is real and must be weighed:

  • Violent control effort. Squeezing the response into a few samples demands large actuator swings. In the simulation of Fig. 8 the deadbeat control sequence is u = 5, −3.5, 2.5, … — swings that would saturate most real actuators, defeating the design.
  • Intersample ripple. Finite settling at the samples says nothing about the continuous signal between them; naïve deadbeat is a classic source of hidden oscillation. Ripple-free deadbeat requires retaining the plant's zeros in T(z), which lengthens the settling.
  • Fragility. Deadbeat depends on exact pole-zero cancellation and a fixed sampling rate; small model errors degrade it sharply, and it is optimal only for the specific input it was designed for.

The Dahlin controller

Where deadbeat is aggressive to a fault, the Dahlin controller deliberately asks for a gentle closed loop, and it is the method of choice for the sluggish, dead-time-dominated loops of the process industries. Rather than demanding finite settling, Dahlin specifies that the closed loop behave like a continuous first-order-plus-dead-time system with a designer-chosen time constant λ. Sampling that target through the hold gives

T(z) = (1 − q) z−(m+1)1 − q z−1, q = e−T/λ, m = L/T(17)

after which (16) yields D(z). The single knob λ trades speed against robustness continuously: a large λ gives a slow, forgiving response tolerant of model error; shrinking λ sharpens it, and in the limit λ → 0 the Dahlin controller becomes deadbeat. The method handles the integer dead-time m explicitly, which is exactly why it thrives on transport-delay-heavy processes.

▮ Eric Dahlin, IBM, and the paper mills

The controller was introduced by Eric B. Dahlin in a 1968 article, "Designing and tuning digital controllers," in Instruments and Control Systems.[15] Dahlin worked at IBM in San Jose and then at Measurex in Cupertino, and the method took hold first in the paper industry — long webs with large transport delays, precisely where a tunable first-order-plus-dead-time target shines. It remains a staple of chemical and thermal process control.

⚠ The Dahlin "ringing pole"

When the discretized plant has a zero near z = −1 (common for fast-sampled or higher-order plants), the Dahlin controller inherits a pole near z = −1. That pole makes the control signal oscillate in sign at every sample — ringing — which is invisible in the sampled output but hammers the actuator and excites intersample motion. Dahlin's own remedy is to locate the offending numerator factor and set z = 1 within it, removing the ringing pole while preserving the steady-state (unity DC) gain. It is an ad-hoc fix, but a durable one.[15,11]

direct_synthesis.pypython
# Deadbeat and Dahlin controllers by direct synthesis:  D = (1/G)*T/(1-T)
import numpy as np
from numpy.polynomial import polynomial as P

# plant  G(z) = (0.2 z + 0.1) / (z^2 - 1.2 z + 0.35),  written in z^-1
Bp = np.array([0.0, 0.2, 0.1])       # numerator   (b0 + b1 z^-1 + ...)
Ap = np.array([1.0, -1.2, 0.35])     # denominator

def synthesize(BT, AT):
    # D_num = BT * Ap ,  D_den = Bp * (AT - BT)   (poly mult in z^-1)
    n = P.polymul(BT, Ap)
    d = P.polymul(Bp, P.polysub(AT, BT))
    return n, d

# deadbeat: T = z^-2
Dn_db, Dd_db = synthesize(np.array([0,0,1.0]), np.array([1.0,0,0]))

# Dahlin: T = (1-q) z^-1 / (1 - q z^-1),  q = e^{-Ts/lambda}
q = np.exp(-1.0/2.0)                  # Ts/lambda = 0.5
Dn_dl, Dd_dl = synthesize(np.array([0.0, 1-q]), np.array([1.0, -q]))
# -> note the common (1 - z^-1) integrator that direct synthesis produces
Part IV

Real-time implementation

A controller that is correct in the z-domain can still fail on hardware, because real converters and processors are finite. Three implementation realities decide whether the design survives: how often it samples, how finely it quantizes, and how long it takes to compute. These are not footnotes to the theory — they are where a great many "mysterious" field failures actually originate.

4.1Choosing the sampling rate

The sampling theorem sets an absolute floor — sample faster than twice the highest frequency of interest — but for control that floor is uselessly low. A loop sampled at just above its Nyquist minimum performs terribly: the zero-order hold's half-sample delay devours phase margin, disturbance rejection is sluggish because the controller reacts only every long period, and the response looks nothing like the continuous design it emulates. Control demands generous oversampling. The practical guidelines below all point the same way — an order of magnitude or more above the closed-loop bandwidth.[6,12,14,18]

⚠ Faster is not free

Oversampling has a ceiling of diminishing — then negative — returns. Beyond roughly 20–40× the bandwidth, control fidelity barely improves, while three costs grow: numerical conditioning deteriorates (poles crowd toward z=1, so coefficient differences become tiny and round-off matters more), quantization and finite-word-length effects become relatively larger per step, and processor budget is consumed that could serve other loops. The right rate is a window, not a maximum.[6,11]

4.2Quantization and finite word length

Converters and arithmetic carry a finite number of bits, and rounding to the nearest representable level introduces error at three distinct points, each with its own remedy.

Signal quantization at the ADC and DAC rounds each sample to the nearest level, an error no larger than half a least-significant bit (q). Under mild assumptions this is well modelled as additive white noise uniformly distributed on [−q/2, q/2], with variance q2/12 — the standard result of Widrow's quantization-noise analysis — so each extra bit buys about 6 dB of signal-to-quantization-noise ratio.[20] Limit cycles are a subtler hazard: quantization is a nonlinearity, and a quantized loop that is stable in linear theory can settle into a small, persistent oscillation about the setpoint rather than coming fully to rest. Coefficient quantization is often the most dangerous of the three: rounding the controller's own coefficients moves its poles and zeros, and a design with closely spaced poles (the direct forms, especially when fast-sampled) can be shoved to marginal stability by a few bits of rounding.[11,20]

✓ Realize the filter in a robust structure

The mathematics of a controller is fixed, but its realization is a choice, and the choice governs sensitivity. Prefer cascade (series second-order sections) or parallel forms over a single high-order direct form, because they localize each pole's sensitivity. For fast sampling, the delta operator (δ = (z−1)/T) in place of the shift operator keeps coefficients well-scaled as poles approach z=1. And scale signals to use the available dynamic range without overflow.[11]

4.3Computational delay

An idealized controller reads y(kT) and applies u(kT) at the same instant. Real hardware cannot: the ADC conversion, the control arithmetic, and the DAC write all take time, so the command based on the sample at kT actually reaches the plant a little later, at kT + τ. This computational delay — anywhere from a small fraction of a period to a full sample when the output is deliberately deferred to the next tick for scheduling regularity — is pure transport lag, and lag erodes phase margin exactly as the ZOH's half-sample delay does. Ignore it and a loop with thin margin can be pushed to oscillation.[11]

✓ Managing the delay

  • Minimize the read-to-write interval. Structure the code so the sequence is: sample → compute only the output using the newest sample → write immediately; do slower housekeeping (state updates, diagnostics) after the DAC write. This shrinks the effective τ toward zero.
  • Model the delay you cannot remove. If τ is an appreciable fraction of a period (or a full sample), include it as extra z−1 lag in the plant model and design against it, rather than pretending it is not there.
  • For large transport dead time, use a predictor. When the process itself has substantial delay L ≫ T, a Smith predictor — a model-based structure that predicts the delayed output and effectively removes the dead time from the feedback loop — restores the achievable performance far beyond what a plain loop allows.[21]

4.4Best practices and tools

The threads above converge on a short discipline for turning a z-domain design into dependable firmware, and on a mature toolchain for getting there.

✓ An implementation checklist

  1. Put an analog anti-aliasing filter ahead of every ADC; set its cutoff below the Nyquist frequency.
  2. Choose the sampling rate in the 6–25× closed-loop-bandwidth window — fast enough for performance, not so fast that conditioning suffers.
  3. Account for the ZOH half-sample delay and the computational delay in the model before finalizing margins.
  4. Realize the controller in a cascade/parallel (or delta-operator) structure; check coefficient-quantization pole movement at the target word length.
  5. Include anti-windup, derivative filtering, and bumpless transfer in any PID.
  6. Verify intersample behaviour, not just the sampled output, especially for deadbeat or pole-cancelling designs.
  7. Test at the true word length and rate — in fixed point if that is the deployment target.

Tooling. In MATLAB, the Control System Toolbox covers the whole workflow: c2d and d2c convert between domains with every method in Table 3 ('zoh', 'tustin', 'matched', …), rlocus/bode/step analyze the discrete system, pidtune tunes controllers, and Simulink with a code generator carries a block diagram to embedded C. In the open-source world, the python-control package mirrors this API (as used in the listings above), SciPy's signal module provides cont2discrete and discrete simulation, and Scilab/Xcos and GNU Octave offer comparable facilities. Classical treatments to keep at hand span Åström–Wittenmark, Franklin–Powell–Workman, Ogata, Kuo, and Isermann.[11,12,13,14,17] Whatever the tool, the last mile is often fixed-point: scaling coefficients and signals into an integer Q-format, then confirming on-target that overflow, rounding and limit cycles stay within budget.

The core of this subject has been stable for half a century, even as the hardware underneath it went from a room-sized Ferranti Argus to a few kilobytes of firmware on a microcontroller. A digital controller is still a difference equation on a clock, still bounded by the unit circle, still living at the seam between a sampled number and a held voltage. What changed is only that the loop now costs cents instead of a fortune — which is why it is, today, nearly everywhere.
