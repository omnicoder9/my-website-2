A Theory Laughed Out of the Room

The Ground That Would Not Stay Still

Alfred Wegener proposed that the continents drift — that solid ground moves. For half a century the science establishment called it delirious raving, footloose nonsense, "utter, damned rot." Then the floor of the ocean proved him right.

Subject  Continental drift & plate tectonics Protagonist  Alfred Wegener, 1880–1930 Reading time  34 min
The record, read from the ridge outward
I  ·  The Map That Started It

An outsider looks at the Atlantic and sees a tear

On the sixth of January 1912, a thirty-one-year-old meteorologist stood before the Geologische Vereinigung at the Senckenberg Museum in Frankfurt and told a room of geologists that the continents move. Alfred Wegener was not a geologist. He had taken his doctorate in astronomy, made his name as a balloonist and a polar meteorologist, and would die on the Greenland ice cap chasing weather, not rocks. That an interloper from a neighbouring discipline should propose to overturn the foundational assumption of geology — that the great landmasses are fixed in place — was, to many of his listeners, precisely the problem.1

The idea had seized him two years earlier. As he later recounted, the notion of Kontinentalverschiebung — continental displacement — "came to me in 1910 when, on studying the map of the world, I was impressed by the congruence of both coasts of the Atlantic."2 The fit of eastern South America into the bight of western Africa is the sort of thing a schoolchild notices and an adult learns to dismiss as coincidence. Wegener did not dismiss it. He set about asking what the world would have to be like for the resemblance to be real, and in 1912 published the answer in two papers, both titled Die Entstehung der Kontinente, in the two leading German earth-science journals of the day — Petermanns Geographische Mitteilungen and the Geologische Rundschau.23

Then came the war. Wegener served, was wounded twice, and used his convalescence to expand the papers into a book. Die Entstehung der Kontinente und Ozeane — The Origin of Continents and Oceans — appeared in 1915 from Vieweg in Braunschweig, a slim volume of fewer than a hundred pages.1 Strangled by wartime isolation, it went almost entirely unread outside Germany. It was only with the substantially revised editions of 1920 and 1922, and above all with the English, French, Russian, Spanish and Swedish translations that followed the third edition, that Wegener's hypothesis reached the wider world — and the controversy began in earnest.4

What Wegener was actually claiming

The core of the thesis was deceptively simple. In the late Palaeozoic, Wegener argued, all the present-day continents had been welded into a single supercontinent he named Urkontinent — later, borrowing from the Greek for "all earth," Pangaea. Beginning in the Mesozoic, this mass had fractured, and its fragments had slowly ploughed apart over tens of millions of years to their present positions, opening the Atlantic and Indian oceans between them.5 His own term for the process was die Verschiebung der Kontinente, the displacement of the continents; it was the English rendering, continental drift, that stuck — a translation Wegener never chose and that carried, as we shall see, a faint whiff of the frivolous that his American critics would exploit.1

This was not, on its face, an original idea. The thought that the Atlantic coastlines might once have been joined is very old: the Flemish cartographer Abraham Ortelius had floated it in 1596, suggesting the Americas had been "torn away" from Europe and Africa by earthquakes and floods, and Antonio Snider-Pellegrini had drawn the continents reassembled in 1858.6 The Austrian geologist Eduard Suess had already grouped the southern landmasses into a vanished continent he called Gondwana-Land, and the American Frank Bursley Taylor had, in 1908, proposed continental migration to explain the world's mountain belts. The Austrian Otto Ampferer was independently developing a mobilist picture at almost the same moment.6 What set Wegener apart was not the germ of the idea but the rigour and breadth with which he prosecuted it. He was the first to marshal evidence from geodesy, geophysics, geology, palaeontology, palaeoclimatology and biogeography into a single coordinated argument — and, crucially, the first to fit the continents not along their visible coastlines but along the edges of their continental shelves, the true geological margins, where the match is markedly better.7

Where his predecessors had explained the present arrangement of land and sea by vertical motion — the foundering of land bridges and the subsidence of ocean basins, an orthodoxy descended from a contracting, cooling Earth — Wegener proposed something heretical: that the continents had moved horizontally, thousands of kilometres, as coherent rafts.1 It is this horizontal motion of solid ground, and the absence of any visible engine to drive it, that would make the theory unbelievable to a generation of geophysicists.

II  ·  The Case for a Moving Earth

Four lines of evidence that should have been enough

The enduring injustice of Wegener's reception is that his evidence was, in the main, excellent. He was wrong about the mechanism — spectacularly, as it turned out — but the body of observation he assembled for the fact of drift was so coherent that, read today, the wonder is not that some believed him but that so many did not. He drew on four broad classes of data, and modern plate tectonics has vindicated essentially all of it.8

1 — The geometric fit

The starting point was the jigsaw. Matching coastlines is crude, because shorelines shift with sea level and erosion; Wegener's refinement was to recognise that the geologically meaningful boundary is the outer edge of the continental shelf. Fitted there, South America and Africa interlock with an accuracy that strains coincidence past breaking.7 Half a century later, Sir Edward Bullard and colleagues would have a computer perform the fit at the 500-fathom contour and obtain a near-seamless closure of the Atlantic — "Bullard's Fit" — but the insight was Wegener's.910

2 — The palaeontological correspondence

Far more telling than the shapes were the fossils that crossed the joins. The same extinct species turn up in rocks of the same age on continents now separated by thousands of kilometres of deep ocean — and not species that could plausibly have swum or drifted across.11 The freshwater reptile Mesosaurus, a metre-long swimmer incapable of crossing salt water, is found on both the South American and African coasts of the South Atlantic and nowhere else.11 The seed fern Glossopteris, whose seeds were far too heavy to be wind-borne across an ocean, blankets the Permo-Carboniferous strata of South America, Africa, India, Australia and Antarctica — the whole of Suess's Gondwana. The land reptiles Lystrosaurus (Africa, India, Antarctica) and Cynognathus (South America, Africa), unable to swim at all, complete the pattern.7

Orthodox geology had an answer for this, and it is important to be fair to it: the doctrine of land bridges. Faunal continuity was explained by transient isthmuses — vast continental causeways that had risen, ferried the animals across, and then conveniently foundered beneath the waves, leaving the continents permanently sundered.7 Wegener's riposte cut to the physics. Land bridges of the required dimensions could not simply sink, because continental rock is lighter than the material beneath it. This was the principle of isostasy: the continents float in gravitational equilibrium on a denser substrate, like icebergs in the sea, and a sunken bridge of light crust would bob back up rather than stay submerged.12 The faunal bridges demanded by the fixists were, in Wegener's view, a physical impossibility; far simpler to move the continents themselves.

3 — The matching geology

If two continents were once joined, the rocks should match across the seam — not merely in kind but in age, structure and orientation, the way the grain of a torn newspaper continues from one piece to the other. They do. Mountain belts of identical lithology, deformation and date stand truncated at the Atlantic margins and resume on the far shore: the Appalachians of eastern North America align with the Caledonides of Scotland and Scandinavia; the geological provinces of West Africa continue into Brazil.11 Distinctive rock sequences "fit" across the ocean as cleanly as the coastlines do.13

4 — The palaeoclimatic indicators

The fourth line was, in some ways, Wegener's most ingenious, and he developed it with his father-in-law, the climatologist Wladimir Köppen. Rocks carry signatures of the climate in which they formed — and those signatures are wildly out of place on a fixed Earth.12 Thick deposits of glacial till and striated, ice-scoured bedrock of Permo-Carboniferous age sprawl across what are now the tropical regions of South America, southern Africa, India and Australia — unmistakable evidence of continental ice sheets near the present equator. At the same moment, the coal measures of Europe and North America record steaming tropical swamps, and Wegener pointed to fossils of warmth-loving plants in the Arctic strata of Svalbard, far inside the modern Arctic Circle.13 Reassemble Pangaea, place its southern reaches over the South Pole and its coal belts at the equator, and the absurdities resolve into a single coherent climatic map. The contradiction was not in the rocks; it was in the assumption that the continents had never moved.12

III  ·  The Fatal Flaw

No engine that could move a continent

Here is where sympathy for Wegener's critics becomes not merely possible but obligatory. A scientific hypothesis owes its audience a mechanism, and Wegener's was hopeless. He could show that the continents had moved; he could not convincingly say how — and the engines he proposed were so feeble that any competent physicist could dismiss them in an afternoon.

He offered two driving forces. The first he called Polflucht, the "flight from the poles" — a small equatorward force arising from the Earth's rotation, related to the centrifugal effect and the planet's equatorial bulge (the same effect is sometimes named the Eötvös force).14 The second was tidal: the gravitational drag of the Sun and Moon, which Wegener imagined pulling the crust slowly westward relative to the interior.4 The continents, he proposed, were rafts of light granitic rock — what Suess had called sial (silica-aluminium) — ploughing through the denser basaltic sima (silica-magnesium) of the ocean floor, advancing like icebreakers through pack ice.14

The numbers were catastrophic. Both the centrifugal pole-fleeing force and the tidal force are real, but both are smaller than the resistance they would have to overcome by something like a factor of a million. As a final embarrassment, Wegener's own estimate of the speed of drift was wildly too large: relying on flawed longitude determinations that seemed to show Greenland racing away from Europe, he put the rate at around 250 centimetres per year. The true figure for the separation of the Americas from Europe and Africa is about 2.5 centimetres a year — Wegener had overshot by two orders of magnitude, and the implausible velocity handed his opponents a club.6

The objection, stated fairly

Why a careful geophysicist had to say no

The decisive critic was the Cambridge geophysicist Harold Jeffreys, whose treatise The Earth (1924) became the authoritative statement of the case against drift. Jeffreys' argument was not snobbery; it was mechanics. The tidal forces Wegener invoked were so large in their other effects that, had they been strong enough to shift continents, they would long since have brought the Earth's rotation to a halt. And the picture of a coherent continental raft ploughing through the ocean floor was, Jeffreys showed, mechanically incoherent: the mantle behaves as a solid of enormous strength on short timescales, and a continent dragged through it with sufficient force to move at all would crumple and shatter long before it travelled anywhere.1415

This was the legitimate core of the resistance, and it is why "Wegener had no mechanism" is the honest one-line verdict on his theory's first life. The error in the objection lay hidden where no one could yet see it: Jeffreys was right that continents cannot plough through oceanic crust, but wrong to conclude that they cannot move. The resolution — that continents and ocean floor move together, carried atop a slowly convecting mantle — required a mechanism Wegener never grasped and instruments that did not yet exist.

It is worth dwelling on the shape of this failure, because it is frequently mis-told. Wegener was not a crank ignoring physics; he engaged with the mechanical objections directly and revised his book repeatedly, the fourth edition of 1929 replacing some forty per cent of its references and figures as he tried to meet the criticism.4 But he never closed the gap, and an honest reader of the 1929 edition would still have to concede that the central question — what force is adequate to the task? — stood unanswered. The tragedy is that the answer was already being assembled, in Glasgow and elsewhere, by people Wegener would not live to see succeed.

IV  ·  The Laughter

Delirious ravings and the symposium built to bury him

When the third edition was translated and Wegener's argument finally crossed the Channel and the Atlantic in the early 1920s, it did not provoke debate so much as derision. The reaction was unusually personal, unusually venomous, and — this is the part that should give every scientist pause — frequently expressed in the language of disease and madness rather than of evidence.

His German colleagues led the way. The Austrian palaeoclimatologist Fritz Kerner von Marilaun spoke of Wegener's "delirious ravings."16 Others diagnosed him with "moving-crust disease and wandering-pole plague," as though belief in mobile continents were a contagion to be quarantined.17 In Britain the tone was drier but no kinder. At a meeting of the Royal Geographical Society, the geologist Philip Lake charged that Wegener "is not seeking truth; he is advocating a cause, and is blind to every fact and argument that tells against it" — and an audience member, having thanked the speaker for blowing the theory to bits, went on to thank the absent "Professor Wegener for offering himself for the explosion."1617

The Americans came down hardest of all. The president of the American Philosophical Society, the palaeontologist William Berryman Scott, pronounced the whole thing "utter, damned rot."16 A colleague dismissed it as "Germanic pseudo-science" and accused Wegener of toying with the evidence until he had worked himself into "a state of auto-intoxication"; another objected, with revealing candour about the real offence, that it was simply "wrong for a stranger to the facts he handles to generalize from them."17 The lingering anti-German feeling of the post-war years, when American and German scientists had so recently been on opposite sides of a trench, was not incidental to the ferocity — though, as the German epithets show, nationalism only sharpened a hostility that crossed every border.17

"Wegener's hypothesis in general is of the footloose type, in that it takes considerable liberty with our globe, and is less bound by restrictions or tied down by awkward, ugly facts than most of its rival theories."

Rollin T. Chamberlin · University of Chicago · at the 1926 AAPG symposium on continental drift

The animus crystallised into an institution. In 1926 the American Association of Petroleum Geologists convened a symposium in New York on "the theory of continental drift" — an event organised, by most accounts, less to weigh Wegener's idea than to dispatch it.18 The opposition was, in the published record's own phrasing, sometimes "violently opposed."18 The palaeontologist Charles Schuchert performed a now-notorious demonstration with a clay model of the continents, sliding the pieces about to show — to his own satisfaction — that they could never be made to fit.19 Rollin T. Chamberlin of Chicago, son of the eminent geologist T. C. Chamberlin, delivered the most quoted lines of the whole affair, asking whether "geology can still be considered a science if it is possible for such a theory as this to run wild," and relaying a colleague's verdict that "if we are to believe Wegener's hypothesis we must forget everything which has been learned in the last seventy years and start all over again."19 The geologist Franz Kossmat pressed the mechanical case from the other side, insisting the oceanic crust was simply too strong for continents to plough through.20

Even the name was a weapon. Wegener had written of Verschiebung — displacement, a sober mechanical noun. It was the Americans who fixed on "drift," with its connotation of aimless, unmoored wandering, and the rebranding did quiet rhetorical work: a theory of continental drift sounds, before any evidence is weighed, a little ridiculous.19 The proceedings were published in 1928 as a permanent monument to the rejection — a volume in which a roll-call of the era's authorities recorded, for posterity, their conviction that solid ground does not move.18

It is tempting, and partly correct, to read this as a parable of dogmatism: entrenched specialists closing ranks against an outsider, their judgement clouded by professional territoriality and national resentment. But the more uncomfortable truth is that the establishment had a real point buried inside its bad behaviour. Wegener could not supply a mechanism; the contraction-and-land-bridge orthodoxy, whatever its faults, at least invoked forces — cooling, gravity — that everyone agreed existed. The tragedy of the 1920s is that a sound demand (give us a mechanism) and an unsound prejudice (an astronomer-meteorologist has no business lecturing geologists, least of all a German one) arrived fused together, and the prejudice survived long after the demand had, in principle, been met.

V  ·  The Keepers of the Flame

A handful of believers and a missing engine, found

Rejection is rarely total. Even as the symposiums sneered, a small number of first-rate geologists looked at Wegener's evidence, found it compelling, and quietly kept the idea alive through the lean decades. Two of them matter enormously, because between them they supplied what Wegener most lacked — a plausible engine — and tightened the case until it could bear the weight of the data that was coming.

Arthur Holmes and the heat in the rocks

The Englishman Arthur Holmes was, by the 1920s, the world's authority on the age of the Earth. He had pioneered the use of radioactive decay to date rocks and was the first to establish, against fierce resistance, that the planet was billions of years old.21 That same mastery of radioactivity gave him the key insight: the Earth's interior is heated from within by the decay of radioactive elements, and that heat must escape. Holmes proposed that it escapes by convection — that the mantle, though solid, flows over geological time like an immensely viscous fluid, rising where it is hot, spreading laterally beneath the crust, cooling, and sinking again in great cells.22

The consequence was exactly the force Wegener needed. A convecting mantle would drag the continents apart where currents rose and diverged, and — in a startling anticipation of seafloor spreading — Holmes suggested that new ocean floor would form in the gaps thus opened, while old crust was consumed where the currents turned downward.22 He presented the idea in a lecture to the Geological Society of Glasgow on 12 January 1928 and published it in the society's Transactions in 1931; he gave it its fullest statement in the final chapter of his magisterial textbook Principles of Physical Geology in 1944.212324 Holmes himself was scrupulously cautious, presenting convection as speculation rather than proof, and the wider community largely ignored it for thirty years. But the mechanism that would eventually vindicate Wegener had been written down, in essentially correct form, within a year of the New York symposium that declared drift impossible.

Alexander du Toit and the southern evidence

If Holmes supplied the physics, the South African field geologist Alexander du Toit supplied the proof on the ground. Du Toit had mapped vast tracts of southern Africa — much of it, by legend, from a bicycle — and he knew the Gondwana rocks as no northern-hemisphere geologist could. He travelled to South America to compare its strata with Africa's and returned convinced: the sequences matched bed for bed across the South Atlantic.25 In 1937 he set out the case in Our Wandering Continents, a book he dedicated to Wegener's memory.26

Du Toit refined Wegener's single Pangaea into a more durable picture: two supercontinents rather than one — a northern Laurasia and a southern Gondwana, separated by an ancient seaway, the Tethys.27 He was also the first to argue, from the palaeoclimatic record, that the southern continents had shifted their position relative to the South Pole itself.27 His reconstruction of Gondwana — South America, Africa, India, Australia and Antarctica fitted around the southern ice centre — is, in its essentials, the one in textbooks today.5

There is a geography to belief worth noticing here. Drift always fared better in the southern hemisphere, where the Gondwana evidence was literally underfoot, and among independent-minded British geologists like Holmes, than in North America, whose powerful and self-confident geological establishment had the most invested in the fixist orthodoxy.25 Reginald Daly of Harvard was a rare American sympathiser. But these were a scattered few. Between Wegener's death in 1930 and the mid-1950s, the theory gained very few converts and was, for most working geologists, a settled error — a cautionary tale told to students about the perils of breaking with the consensus.25 The physicist Max Planck's bitter maxim — that science advances one funeral at a time — has rarely been better illustrated.25

VI  ·  Death on the Ice

A fiftieth birthday on the Greenland ice cap

It is easy to forget, amid the theory, that Wegener's deepest love was not geology but the poles. He had served as meteorologist on Danish expeditions to Greenland as a young man, and his late appointment in 1924 to a chair of meteorology and geophysics at Graz had finally freed him to mount the great enterprise of his life: a German expedition to overwinter on the Greenland ice sheet and take the first continuous year of weather data from its frozen interior.28

The 1930–31 expedition was his fourth to the island, and he led it. Among its aims was a seismic sounding of the ice cap's thickness — bouncing artificial shock waves off the bedrock far below — and the maintenance of a central station, Eismitte, "mid-ice," where two researchers were to spend the polar winter.28 But the season turned vicious early. Eismitte was dangerously under-provisioned, and in late autumn Wegener set out across the ice with a dog-sledge party to carry supplies to the men marooned there, through temperatures that fell toward sixty degrees below zero.29

He reached the station. There was not enough food for him to remain. On 1 November 1930 — his fiftieth birthday — Wegener and the Greenlander Rasmus Villumsen took two sledges and started back for the coast.25 He never arrived. He is thought to have died on the journey, most likely of a heart attack brought on by the brutal exertion. Villumsen buried his body carefully in the snow, marked the spot with a pair of skis, and pressed on alone — and was himself never seen again; his body has never been found. Wegener's was recovered the following May, exactly where Villumsen had left it, the skis still standing in the ice.29

He died at fifty, in the place he loved, with his great idea still under open ridicule. He would not see the apparent wandering of the magnetic poles, the maps of the ocean floor, or the zebra stripes that would turn "delirious raving" into the organising principle of an entire science. The vindication, when it came, would come from instruments he never held and an ocean he never plumbed — and it would begin with the discovery that the rocks themselves had been keeping a record all along.

VII  ·  The Rocks Remember

Palaeomagnetism, and the first hard evidence from physics

The revival did not come from geology at all. It came from the obscure, instrument-driven study of fossil magnetism — and it arrived precisely because a new generation of physicists, indifferent to the old geological quarrels, asked a question the fixists had never thought to pose: where was the north pole when this rock was formed?

The physics is exact. When a basaltic lava cools through its Curie temperature, the magnetic domains of its iron-bearing minerals lock into alignment with the ambient geomagnetic field and freeze there permanently; sediments record the same information as magnetic grains settle into alignment before burial. Each rock thus carries a fossil compass — a remanent magnetisation preserving the direction, and through its inclination the latitude, of the field at the moment of formation.30 The signals are faint, and the field only became readable when P. M. S. Blackett, the Nobel laureate, built an astatic magnetometer of extraordinary sensitivity in the early 1950s and put it into the hands of a group at Cambridge and, later, Newcastle.30

What that group found broke the deadlock. Working through the 1950s, Keith Runcorn, Edward Irving and their colleagues measured the fossil field-directions in rocks of many different ages from a single continent and plotted, for each, the apparent position of the magnetic pole at that time. The poles did not stay put. Connected in sequence they traced a smooth path across the globe — an apparent polar wander curve.30 Since the magnetic pole is, on geological average, pinned near the rotational pole, the pole itself could not really have roamed so far. Either the rocks had moved beneath a fixed pole, or the pole had wandered beneath fixed rocks — and a single wandering pole could not be in two places at once.

That was the crux. When the same exercise was repeated on a second continent, it produced a different wander path. Europe and North America yielded curves of the same general shape that nonetheless diverged increasingly into the past, the separation widening with age.30 A single pole cannot trace two tracks. But the discrepancy vanished under one operation: slide North America eastward and rotate it until the Atlantic closes, and the two curves fall into near-perfect coincidence.30 The rocks were not recording two histories of a wandering pole; they were recording one history of two continents that had since drifted apart. Here, for the first time, was quantitative geophysical evidence for continental displacement — produced not by a maverick outsider but by hard-nosed physicists with magnetometers, and not easily laughed away.31

Almost simultaneously the same minerals delivered a second shock. Some rocks were found to be magnetised not toward the north pole but precisely away from it — fully reversed.30 The discovery was at first deeply controversial, but as magnetometer surveys multiplied it became clear the reversals were real and global: at intervals through geological time the Earth's magnetic field had flipped its polarity entirely. By the early 1960s Allan Cox, Richard Doell and Brent Dalrymple, dating reversed and normal lavas radiometrically, had begun to assemble a calibrated geomagnetic polarity timescale — a chronology of flips stamped on the rocks of the whole planet.30 On its own this was a curiosity of geophysics. Joined to a discovery being made at the same moment on the floor of the ocean, it would become the single most decisive piece of evidence in the history of the Earth sciences.

VIII  ·  The Floor of the World

Sonar, the great ridge, and Holmes vindicated

While the magnetometers were tracing wandering poles, a second front had opened on terrain no nineteenth-century geologist had been able to reach at all: the two-thirds of the planet hidden beneath the sea. The instrument of discovery was war. Anti-submarine warfare had driven the development of precision echo-sounding, and the Cold War navies poured resources into mapping the deep — none more consequentially than the United States Navy, which kept the geologist Harry Hess at sea with an echo-sounder running even during the Pacific campaign.32

The data accumulated into a portrait of the ocean floor utterly unlike the featureless plain everyone had assumed. At Columbia's Lamont observatory, Bruce Heezen and Marie Tharp compiled the soundings into the first detailed physiographic maps of the sea bed and revealed its dominating feature: a continuous submarine mountain range, the mid-ocean ridge, winding through every ocean basin for some sixty-five thousand kilometres — the largest single geological structure on Earth — and split along its crest by a deep axial rift valley.33 The ridge was seismically alive, threaded by earthquakes; the rift at its centre looked unmistakably like a place where the crust was being pulled apart.

It was Hess who supplied the interpretation, in a 1959 manuscript circulated privately and published in 1962 under the deliberately tentative title History of Ocean Basins — a paper he called "an essay in geopoetry."3234 His proposal was breathtaking in scope and, in retrospect, simply a vindication of Arthur Holmes. Hot mantle rises beneath the mid-ocean ridge; molten rock wells up into the axial rift and freezes onto the edges of the diverging plates; the new sea floor then moves outward, away from the ridge on both sides, like the two halves of a conveyor belt. Far away, at the deep ocean trenches, the old floor turns downward and is consumed back into the mantle.32 The continents do not plough through the ocean crust at all — Wegener's fatal error — but ride passively atop it, carried like freight on a moving floor. The ocean basins are not permanent: they open, widen, and close, and no sea floor is older than the conveyor that made it. The American geologist Robert Dietz arrived at the same picture independently and, in a 1961 paper, gave the process the name it has carried ever since — seafloor spreading.3536

Hess's "geopoetry" was elegant, comprehensive, and — in 1962 — still unproven. It explained why the ocean floor is young (nowhere older than about 180 million years, a geological eyeblink), why sediment thickens away from the ridges, why the deepest earthquakes cluster at the trenches. But explanation is not proof, and a sceptical discipline that had buried drift once was not about to canonise its sequel on the strength of an essay. What seafloor spreading needed was a prediction — something it forbade, that the fixed-Earth picture permitted, on which the two could be made to compete head to head. That test was already implicit in the magnetic stripes nobody had yet learned to read.

IX  ·  The Tape Recorder

Zebra stripes, a daring prediction, and the field turns over

For several years the two discoveries — spreading floors and reversing fields — sat side by side without anyone joining them. The catalyst was a pattern so strange that, for a while, it seemed merely an anomaly to be explained away. Magnetic surveys towed behind ships off the Pacific coast of North America, processed by Ronald Mason and Arthur Raff, had revealed that the total magnetic field over the ocean floor varied in a regular, astonishing way: long parallel stripes of alternately high and low magnetic intensity, running for hundreds of kilometres like the bars of a barcode, or the markings of a zebra.31 They were real, they were enormous, and nobody could say what they were.

In 1963 two groups made the same leap within months of each other. At Cambridge, the graduate student Fred Vine and his supervisor Drummond Matthews put the pieces together; in Canada, working entirely independently, Lawrence Morley reached the identical conclusion.36 The argument is one of the most beautiful in the physical sciences, and it is a pure deduction. Suppose Hess is right and the sea floor spreads outward from the ridge. Suppose too that Cox, Doell and Dalrymple are right and the geomagnetic field reverses at intervals. Then the consequence is forced: as each ribbon of magma freezes at the ridge axis, it locks in the polarity of the field at that instant; the spreading floor carries it away; the next reversal stamps the opposite polarity on the next ribbon. The ocean floor must therefore be a magnetic tape recorder, preserving the entire history of geomagnetic reversals as a sequence of normally and reversely magnetised bands — and because the floor spreads in both directions at once, the pattern must be symmetric about the ridge axis, each stripe matched by its mirror twin on the far side.3736 The zebra stripes were not an anomaly. They were the predicted, unavoidable fingerprint of seafloor spreading.

This was no longer geopoetry. It was a falsifiable, quantitative prediction with no room to hide: the widths of the stripes had to match the independently dated reversal timescale, the pattern had to be mirror-symmetric across every spreading ridge, and the spreading rate implied by stripe width against age had to come out geologically sensible — a few centimetres a year. A fixed Earth forbade all of it. The hypothesis was so far ahead of received opinion that Morley's paper was rejected by both Nature and the Journal of Geophysical Research, one reviewer reportedly remarking that such speculation made fine talk for a cocktail party but had no place in a serious journal.36

The confirmation came fast and it was overwhelming. In 1965 Vine and the Canadian geophysicist J. Tuzo Wilson analysed the Juan de Fuca Ridge off Vancouver Island and found the measured anomalies matched the model in detail.38 Then came the clincher: a magnetic profile recorded by the research vessel Eltanin across the Pacific–Antarctic Rise — the celebrated Eltanin-19 profile — showed a pattern of such flawless symmetry that the two halves were near-perfect mirror images of each other about the ridge crest, the stripe widths tracking the reversal timescale exactly.39 One could lay a tracing of one flank over the other and watch them coincide. Against a prediction that precise, scepticism had nowhere left to stand. Between roughly 1963 and 1966 the magnetic stripes converted the discipline that the symposiums of the 1920s had mobilised against drift — and they did it by the most stringent test science knows: a bold prediction, made in advance, confirmed in detail.31

X  ·  A New Global Tectonics

Rigid plates on a sphere, and the synthesis of 1965–1968

Seafloor spreading had been proven, but spreading is not yet a theory of the Earth. It described what happens at the ridges; it did not yet say what the moving floor and the riding continents were, nor how their motions fit together over the whole closed surface of a globe. The last act — compressed into an astonishing three or four years — supplied that architecture, and it turned a collection of vindicated hunches into the rigorous, predictive science of plate tectonics.

The pivotal conceptual move was made by J. Tuzo Wilson in 1965. In a short, dense paper in Nature, "A new class of faults and their bearing on continental drift," Wilson argued that the Earth's mobile features — the spreading ridges, the deep trenches, and the great strike-slip faults — are not isolated structures but the connected boundaries of rigid blocks, and that the three kinds of boundary join end to end into a single continuous network girdling the planet.40 The strike-slip boundaries he christened transform faults: a third class of plate edge, along which crust is neither created nor destroyed but where two plates simply slide past one another, the motion transformed at each end into spreading at a ridge or consumption at a trench.41 This was the birth of the plate concept proper — the recognition that the lithosphere is broken into a small number of internally rigid pieces whose entire action is concentrated at their margins. Wilson, more than anyone, became the public champion of the revived theory, and his transform fault supplied the missing geometric ingredient.10

Once the surface was understood as a mosaic of rigid plates, the problem became one of geometry — and the geometry of rigid motion on a sphere had been solved by Euler in the eighteenth century. Any displacement of a rigid cap over a sphere is a rotation about an axis through the centre, which pierces the surface at an Euler pole; every point on the plate traces a small circle about that pole, moving fastest at the equator of rotation and not at all at the pole itself. In 1967 Dan McKenzie and Robert Parker showed, in a paper titled "The North Pacific: an example of tectonics on a sphere," that the earthquakes and slip directions ringing the Pacific behaved exactly as Euler's theorem demanded for a single rigid plate rotating about a pole — the first rigorous demonstration that real plate motions obey the mathematics of rotations on a sphere.4243

The generalisation to the whole planet followed within a year, twice over. Working at Princeton, W. Jason Morgan set out the global scheme in 1968 — the lithosphere divided into about twenty rigid blocks, each in motion as a rotation about its own Euler pole, the relative motion at any boundary fixed entirely by the two plates' poles and rotation rates.44 Almost simultaneously Xavier Le Pichon reduced the picture to a working quantitative model of six major plates and computed their relative motions worldwide, demonstrating that the spreading measured at the ridges and the consumption required at the trenches balanced consistently around the entire globe — the kinematic capstone of the theory.4510 Bullard's computer-fitted reconstruction of the Atlantic continents, published in 1965, had already shown that the matching of coastlines Wegener noticed by eye was statistically near-perfect when done rigorously at the true edge of the continental shelf.943

The final pillar was seismological, and it arrived with peculiar force because the evidence came from instruments installed for an entirely different purpose. The global network of seismographs built in the early 1960s to monitor underground nuclear tests had, as a by-product, produced earthquake locations and fault-motion data of unprecedented quality. In 1968 Bryan Isacks, Jack Oliver and Lynn Sykes drew it all together in "Seismology and the new global tectonics."46 The deep earthquakes of the world, they showed, are not scattered at random but trace narrow planes — the Wadati–Benioff zones — that dip away from the trenches into the mantle to depths approaching seven hundred kilometres, exactly delineating the cold, brittle slabs of lithosphere plunging back into the interior.33 First-motion analyses of earthquakes at ridges, trenches and transform faults confirmed in every case the sense of slip the plate model predicted. They recast the Earth's outer shell as a strong lithosphere riding on a weak, deformable asthenosphere above a deeper mesosphere, and they stressed the sheer economy of the result: a single framework now accounted for the distribution of earthquakes, volcanoes, mountain belts, island arcs and ocean trenches that had been disconnected mysteries a decade before.47

The pieces locked together at a sequence of now-historic meetings — a 1966 conference at NASA's Goddard Institute, symposia at the Royal Society — and the assembled evidence simply overwhelmed the old fixism.10 Within about three years, from Wilson's transform faults in 1965 to the Morgan–Le Pichon–Isacks synthesis of 1968, the Earth sciences underwent the fastest and most complete paradigm shift in their history. The discipline that had organised a symposium to bury continental drift in 1926 now taught, as its central unifying principle, that the continents move. Wegener's conclusion had been vindicated entirely. His mechanism had been replaced wholesale.

XI  ·  Vindication, and the Verdict on the Laughter

What the rocks decided, and what the episode is for

What we now know

The theory Wegener could not get a hearing for is now the unremarkable foundation on which the whole of the Earth sciences rests. We no longer infer continental motion from the shapes of coastlines or the distribution of fossils; we measure it directly. Space-geodetic techniques — satellite laser ranging, very-long-baseline interferometry, and the dense global network of GPS receivers — clock the plates as they crawl past one another at rates of a few centimetres a year, the same order of magnitude that seafloor-spreading rates had implied, and entirely consistent with the spreading recorded in the magnetic stripes.6 The continents Wegener could only argue had once been joined are now watched, in real time, continuing to move apart. The Earth's rigid outer shell is resolved into roughly seven major plates and a larger number of minor ones, the strong lithosphere sliding over the weak asthenosphere beneath, exactly as the seismology of 1968 required.33

And yet the vindication is pointedly partial, in a way that matters for the moral of the story. Wegener was right about the fact and wrong about the cause. His continents do not plough through the ocean floor under the feeble tug of Polflucht and the tides; they ride atop a sea floor that is itself in motion, conveyed by the slow overturn of the mantle that Holmes had divined.22 Even Holmes's picture has been refined: the modern consensus holds that the plates are not merely passive passengers dragged along by convection currents from below but are, to a large degree, self-propelled — the dominant force being the slab pull of cold, dense lithosphere sinking at the trenches and dragging the rest of the plate behind it, assisted by the gravitational ridge push sliding off the elevated ridges, with mantle convection intimately coupled to the sinking slabs rather than simply ferrying inert rafts.47 The deeper dynamics — the structure of flow in the lower mantle, the role of rising plumes, the precise partition of the driving forces — remain live questions under active investigation. The man whose mechanism was ridiculed as impossible was, on the mechanism, genuinely mistaken; the irony is that he was mistaken in the service of a conclusion that was entirely correct.

Were they wrong to laugh?

This brings us to the question the whole episode exists, in the popular imagination, to answer — and which it is almost always made to answer wrongly. Wegener is the patron saint of the ridiculed visionary, his name a standing rebuke to scientific orthodoxy, invoked by anyone who has ever been told their idea is unserious: they laughed at Wegener, too. The raw materials for the legend are real and damning. He was dismissed as the author of "delirious ravings" and a sufferer of "moving-crust disease"; a past president of a learned society pronounced his theory "utter, damned rot"; his work was disparaged as Germanic pseudo-science in the raw years after the First World War; and the American establishment convened, in 1926, what was by most accounts a symposium designed less to weigh his idea than to bury it.161719 If that were the whole story, it would be a simple parable of prejudice, and the maverick's moral would be the right one.

But the historians who have studied the controversy most closely — Naomi Oreskes in her detailed account of the American rejection, Henry Frankel in his four-volume history of the whole debate — tell a more uncomfortable and more instructive story.4849 The hard truth is that Wegener's critics had a real and decisive point, and that on the specific matter in dispute they were right. His proposed mechanism was not merely unproven; it was demonstrably impossible. The forces he invoked were off by something like a factor of a million, far too weak to move a continent, and his estimated drift rates — the Greenland measurements he leaned on implied speeds orders of magnitude faster than anything real — were extravagantly wrong.614 When Harold Jeffreys calculated that the Earth was far too rigid for Wegener's forces to do what he claimed, Jeffreys was not being a reactionary; he was being a competent geophysicist reporting an accurate result about the physics on the table.154 A discipline that withholds assent from a theory whose proposed cause violates the known mechanics of the planet is not being closed-minded. It is doing its job. The demand "give us a mechanism that works" was the right demand, and the moment a workable mechanism arrived — convection, spreading, subduction — together with a stringent confirmed prediction in the magnetic stripes, the community accepted continental mobility with remarkable speed. On this reading the system did not fail; it functioned exactly as it should, holding out until the evidence became compelling and then turning over almost at once.

The honest verdict is that both stories are true, and that the discomfort lies precisely in their entanglement. There was a legitimate scientific objection — no mechanism, impossible physics, overstated rates — and there was, simultaneously, an ugly tangle of illegitimate ones: the snobbery that an astronomer-meteorologist had no standing to instruct geologists, the nationalism that let a German theory be dismissed as German pseudo-science, the rhetorical sleight by which the sober word displacement was recoded as frivolous drift, and the organised effort to discredit rather than merely to test.1719 The tragedy of the affair is that the good reason and the bad reasons all pointed the same way, so the prejudice could clothe itself in the respectability of the legitimate doubt — and outlive it. For the legitimate doubt had, in principle, been answered by 1931, when Holmes published a viable engine; yet drift remained a settled error in most of the profession for a further quarter-century, long after the mechanical objection had a candidate solution sitting in the literature.2125 That residue — the decades of dismissal that persisted after the strongest objection had been met — is the part that prejudice, not rigor, must answer for.

And so the moral that the maverick draws from Wegener is precisely the wrong one. The lesson of the episode is not that the ridiculed genius is usually right and the establishment usually blinkered; the overwhelming majority of ridiculed theories are ridiculed because they are wrong, and being laughed at is no evidence whatever that one is correct. Wegener was not vindicated because he believed more fervently or shouted more loudly than his detractors. He was vindicated because the Earth turned out, on independent and rigorous examination — magnetometers, echo-sounders, a falsifiable prediction confirmed in stone — to be built his way on the one question that mattered.4849 The right lesson is the harder, less romantic one: that conviction is not evidence, that a correct conclusion married to an impossible mechanism is still owed scrutiny, and that science advances not by the boldness of its heretics but by the eventual, grinding accumulation of data that no framework but the true one can accommodate.

Alfred Wegener did not live to learn that he had been right. He lies somewhere still beneath the Greenland ice that he loved more than any argument, lost on his fiftieth birthday in the service of a different science, his great idea consigned at the moment of his death to the catalogue of brilliant errors.29 The wandering of the magnetic poles, the great submarine ridge winding through every ocean, the zebra stripes laid down in mirror image on either side of the spreading rifts — every instrument that would have absolved him was still decades from his reach. He had looked at a map, seen two coastlines that wanted to fit together, and refused to call it coincidence; and for that refusal he was told he was deranged. He was not deranged. He was early. The ground, it turned out, would not stay still.
