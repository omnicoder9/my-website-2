A field manual for wireless networking — from the 13.56 MHz handshake of a contactless card to the 320 MHz channels of Wi-Fi 7, the megaconstellations overhead, and the survey methodology that decides whether any of it actually works in your building.

Subject Wireless Networking Topics Wi-Fi · BLE · 5G · IoT · LPWAN · Security Format Long-form Technical Citation Vancouver

Wireless networking is the practice of moving information between devices without a physical conductor between them — a discipline that, despite its apparent magic, comes down to disciplined use of a shared, finite, and physically lawful resource: the radio spectrum. Every wireless protocol you use is a negotiated treaty between physics and policy: a modulation scheme that has to survive multipath fading, a regulatory band carved out by international agreement, and a media-access protocol that arbitrates among devices that cannot, fundamentally, talk and listen at the same time.

This field manual surveys the landscape — the technologies, the standards, the deployment craft, and the security postures — that together let billions of devices coordinate across a medium none of them can see.

§ 01 — Origins

A short history of cordless data.

The story of modern wireless data is, in part, the story of regulators getting out of the way. In 1985 the U.S. Federal Communications Commission opened a slice of the 2.4 GHz ISM band for unlicensed use — a frequency range already shared with microwave ovens and cordless phones, and therefore considered unattractive enough to give away. That decision, more than any single piece of engineering, made consumer Wi-Fi possible.1

The technical lineage is older. In 1971, Norman Abramson’s ALOHAnet at the University of Hawaii became the first wireless packet network, using UHF radio to connect terminals across the Hawaiian islands. Its random-access medium-access protocol — transmit when you have data, retry on collision — is the conceptual ancestor of Ethernet’s CSMA/CD and Wi-Fi’s CSMA/CA.2 In 1991, engineers at NCR Corporation and AT&T in Nieuwegein, the Netherlands, invented a precursor to 802.11 originally intended for cash-register networks, marketed as WaveLAN.3

The standardization effort that produced Wi-Fi was chaired, from 1990 through 2000, by Vic Hayes — a Dutch engineer at NCR who has since been called the “Father of Wi-Fi” for his decade leading the IEEE 802.11 Working Group.45 The first 802.11 standard was ratified in 1997 at 1–2 Mbit/s, and the brand name “Wi-Fi” was coined two years later by the consultancy Interbrand as a deliberately catchier alternative to “IEEE 802.11b Direct Sequence” — a pun on hi-fi, with no expansion of its own.36

§ 02 — The Technology Landscape

One spectrum, many treaties.

Different wireless protocols solve different problems by making different trade-offs in the four-cornered space of range, throughput, power, and cost. A contactless payment card needs centimetres of range and no battery; a satellite link needs thousands of kilometres of range and a megawatt-class ground station. A single building today commonly runs five or six of these simultaneously, all sharing the same crowded slice of the electromagnetic spectrum.

Near-Field Communication (NFC)

NFC is the shortest-range mainstream wireless protocol you will encounter — by design. Operating at 13.56 MHz in the globally unlicensed HF ISM band, NFC communications are magnetically coupled between an active reader and a tag or peer device at distances of roughly 4 cm or less, with raw data rates of 106, 212, 424, or 848 kbit/s.11

The protocol stack sits on top of the older contactless-smartcard standards: ISO/IEC 14443 (originally published in 1995) defines the physical and link layers for Type A and Type B proximity cards, and NFC is, in essence, an extension of that ecosystem with bidirectional peer-to-peer modes added.1213 ISO/IEC 18092 (NFCIP-1) and the NFC Forum specifications govern the device-to-device behaviour layered above. The deliberately short range is itself a security feature: an attacker has to be physically present to interact with a passive tag, which is why NFC is the dominant technology for contactless payments (EMV), transit ticketing, electronic passports, and access-control fobs.11

A useful and underappreciated property of NFC is energy harvesting: a passive tag is powered entirely by the reader’s magnetic field, which is what allows a $0.10 sticker to authenticate itself without a battery.

Bluetooth and Bluetooth Low Energy

Bluetooth originated in 1994 at Ericsson Mobile Communications in Lund, Sweden, where engineers Jaap Haartsen and Sven Mattisson set out to eliminate cables between mobile devices and accessories.714 The technology was donated to industry stewardship in 1998 with the formation of the Bluetooth Special Interest Group (SIG) by Ericsson, IBM, Intel, Nokia, and Toshiba — today an organisation of more than 40,000 member companies.1415 The protocol’s name and runic logo are an homage to King Harald Blåtand (“Bluetooth”) of 10th-century Denmark, who united Norway and Denmark — a metaphor for unifying device protocols.15

Bluetooth operates in the 2.4 GHz ISM band and survives in that crowded environment using Adaptive Frequency Hopping (AFH), dynamically shifting transmission channels up to 1,600 hops per second to avoid Wi-Fi and other interferers. Two major branches share that physical layer:

  • Bluetooth Classic / BR/EDR — connection-oriented streaming, originally used for headsets, file transfers, and audio. Bluetooth 2.0 + EDR (2004) raised throughput and range substantially over the 721 kbps of the original 1.0 specification.16
  • Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) — introduced in Bluetooth 4.0 (2010) as a fundamentally different protocol optimised for sensors, wearables, beacons, and locks that must run for years on a coin cell. BLE’s advertising-and-scanning model also underpins exposure-notification frameworks and indoor positioning.

The most recent specification, Bluetooth 6.0 (2024), introduces channel sounding for precision distance measurement, accelerating Bluetooth’s role in secure proximity applications such as digital car keys.16

Zigbee and Z-Wave

Zigbee and Z-Wave are the two dominant mesh-networking protocols for the smart home — and they are useful to compare because they make almost opposite engineering choices on top of similar goals (small payloads, low power, many nodes, no internet required at the link layer).

Zigbee is built on the open IEEE 802.15.4 physical layer and operates principally in the global 2.4 GHz ISM band, with regional sub-GHz options at 868 MHz (Europe) and 915 MHz (US). It supports mesh networks of 65,000+ devices per coordinator, uses Direct Sequence Spread Spectrum (DSSS) modulation, and is open-source in its core specification — at the cost of occasional interoperability friction between vendor stacks.1718

Z-Wave is a proprietary standard developed by Zensys (later acquired by Sigma Designs, then Silicon Labs), running in sub-GHz spectrum (908.42 MHz in the US, 868 MHz in Europe) using Frequency-Shift Keying. It tops out at 232 nodes per mesh — fine for a home, tight for a commercial deployment — but its proprietary chipset model historically delivered better cross-vendor interoperability than Zigbee. Sub-GHz operation also gives Z-Wave better wall penetration and less interference exposure than 2.4 GHz Zigbee, at the cost of lower throughput.1920

The Matter convergence

The smart-home protocol fragmentation that the Zigbee/Z-Wave divide represents is being slowly papered over by Matter, an application-layer standard launched by the CSA in 2022 that runs over IP — typically using Thread (an 802.15.4-based mesh) or Wi-Fi as transport. Thread and Zigbee share the same 2.4 GHz radio hardware but use different upper-layer protocols, with Thread operating on a native IPv6 stack rather than Zigbee’s proprietary 16-bit addressing.21

Mobile cellular networks

Cellular networks are the highest-investment, most heavily regulated wireless systems in commercial use. Their organising idea is the cell — a coverage area served by a base station, with frequencies reused in non-adjacent cells to multiply spectrum efficiency. The standards are governed by the 3rd Generation Partnership Project (3GPP), a consortium of regional telecom standards bodies whose releases define each generational increment.

The current generation, 5G New Radio (NR), was first specified in 3GPP Release 15 (2018) and has since evolved through Releases 16 (2020), 17 (completed in March 2022), and 18 — the latter branded “5G-Advanced.”2223 Two frequency-range categories matter:

  • FR1 (sub-7 GHz) — the workhorse of nationwide 5G coverage. Includes refarmed LTE bands and new mid-band allocations (notably C-band, 3.3–4.2 GHz). Good propagation, reasonable throughput.
  • FR2 (millimetre wave) — 24.25 GHz to 71 GHz after the Release 17 extension. Vast bandwidth, blazing throughput, but limited range and very poor penetration through walls, foliage, and even the human body — which is why mmWave deployments concentrate in stadiums, airports, and dense urban canyons rather than wide-area coverage.24

Release 17 also introduced NR-Light / RedCap (Reduced Capability), a stripped-down NR profile for mid-tier IoT devices — wearables, surveillance cameras, industrial sensors — that need more than LTE-M can offer but cannot justify the cost and power of a full 5G modem.25

Hotspot and tethering

Personal hotspots and tethering are the unsung workhorses of mobile productivity: a phone uses its cellular connection as a WAN uplink and republishes the connection as Wi-Fi, Bluetooth PAN, or USB for nearby devices. Mechanically simple, but with several non-obvious gotchas:

  • Carrier-grade NAT — virtually all mobile carriers place hotspot traffic behind multiple NAT layers, so inbound connections (port forwarding, peer-to-peer apps, server hosting) generally do not work.
  • Battery and thermal load — the phone is simultaneously running a cellular radio at moderate power and a Wi-Fi AP at higher power; sustained tethering causes substantial heat and battery drain.
  • Plan-tier limits — many “unlimited” plans cap or deprioritize hotspot data separately from on-device data.

LoRaWAN

LoRaWAN occupies a different point in the design space than every protocol above: it sacrifices throughput, by orders of magnitude, to gain range and battery life. The physical layer, LoRa (Long Range), uses Chirp Spread Spectrum (CSS) modulation — encoding bits as frequency-sweeping “chirps” — which is unusually resilient to noise and Doppler shift. LoRa signals can be demodulated as much as 20 dB below the noise floor.26

LoRaWAN is the MAC-layer protocol layered on top of LoRa, developed and maintained by the LoRa Alliance (founded 2015). It operates in regional sub-GHz ISM bands (868 MHz in Europe, 915 MHz in the US, 433 MHz, and others), at data rates from 0.3 to 50 kbps, in a star-of-stars topology where gateways transparently relay packets between battery-powered end-devices and a centralised network server.2728

For decades, “satellite internet” meant geostationary service: a small number of large satellites parked 22,236 miles up, providing reliable continental coverage but with one inescapable problem — the speed of light. A round-trip to GEO orbit and back is approximately 240 ms before any terrestrial processing, which compounds into the 600+ ms typical for GEO satellite internet and makes the service essentially unusable for interactive applications like gaming or even normal video conferencing.29

The Low Earth Orbit (LEO) revolution, led by SpaceX’s Starlink, inverted the architecture: instead of three large satellites at extreme altitude, deploy thousands of small satellites at altitudes around 340–570 km, accept the resulting churn (each satellite is overhead for only a few minutes), and rely on rapid hand-offs and inter-satellite laser links to maintain continuity.30 By 2025, Starlink had on the order of 8,000 satellites in orbit, with median U.S. download speeds around 100+ Mbps and latency in the 25–60 ms range — comparable to terrestrial cable broadband and a dramatic departure from the GEO experience.3132

The physics are straightforward: light travels at 299,792 km/s. A signal to a GEO satellite at 35,786 km takes ~120 ms one way, plus the return trip, plus processing — resulting in 600–700 ms round-trip. — Orbital Radar, 202629

A parallel innovation is direct-to-device (D2D) satellite service, in which an unmodified smartphone connects directly to a satellite over normal cellular bands. T-Mobile’s T-Satellite service (built on Starlink) and Apple’s Emergency SOS service are early commercial examples — initially limited to SMS and short text, with data services being progressively rolled out.32

§ 03 — Wi-Fi Standards

The 802.11 family, amendment by amendment.

The IEEE 802.11 standard has evolved through a long sequence of letter-suffixed amendments, each adding new physical layers, modulation schemes, or operational modes while remaining (mostly) backward-compatible. Because consumers struggled with the original lettering — most people had no intuition for whether 802.11ac was newer than 802.11n — the Wi-Fi Alliance introduced generational branding in 2018: Wi-Fi 4, 5, 6, 6E, and 7.

Wi-Fi 7 / 802.11be in detail

IEEE 802.11be-2024, also called Extremely High Throughput (EHT), was finalised on 22 July 2025 as the technical basis for Wi-Fi 7 certification.9 Its headline features:

  • 320 MHz channels — doubling the maximum channel width of Wi-Fi 6 (160 MHz), enabled by the additional 6 GHz spectrum opened in 2020. More spectrum per transmission, more throughput per device, more devices per area.33
  • 4096-QAM modulation (12-bit) — up from 1024-QAM (10-bit) in Wi-Fi 6, contributing roughly 20% more data per symbol under good signal-to-noise conditions.33
  • Multi-Link Operation (MLO) — perhaps the most operationally significant change: a Wi-Fi 7 device can associate to and exchange traffic over multiple bands or channels simultaneously through a single logical association. Two implementations exist: Multi-Link Multi-Radio (MLMR), which uses two physical radios for genuinely concurrent transmit/receive, and Multi-Link Single-Radio (MLSR), which dynamically switches a single radio between links.34
  • Preamble puncturing — the ability to subdivide a wide channel and skip the portions that have interference, rather than abandoning the whole channel.33
  • Multiple Resource Units (MRU) — refining Wi-Fi 6’s OFDMA so a single user can be assigned multiple non-contiguous resource units in one transmission.9

Cisco Meraki’s Wi-Fi 7 design guidance also notes a hard prerequisite that distinguishes 802.11be from prior generations: WPA3 (or Enhanced Open) is mandatory for 802.11be data rates and MLO. WPA2 is no longer sufficient for the new physical-layer features.35

§ 04 — WLAN Infrastructure

Access points, controllers, and the geometry of coverage.

Access points

An access point (AP) is the bridge between the radio domain (802.11 frames over the air) and the wired domain (802.3 Ethernet frames over copper or fibre). Modern enterprise APs are sophisticated computers in their own right: multiple radios (typically tri-band, with dedicated 2.4 GHz, 5 GHz, and 6 GHz interfaces), multiple spatial streams driving multiple-antenna MIMO arrays, hardware-accelerated cryptography, and increasingly a tertiary IoT radio for Bluetooth, Zigbee, or Thread.

An AP that talks Wi-Fi to clients on one side and Ethernet to a switch on the other is, electrically, a layer-2 bridge. But it is a bridge that has to make hundreds of decisions per second: which client to serve next, which modulation rate to attempt for each transmission, when to retry, when to drop, and — across the larger network — when to hand a client off to a neighbouring AP.

Controllers

Once you exceed a handful of APs, managing them individually becomes untenable. Enterprise WLAN architectures use one of three approaches to coordinate fleets of APs:

  • Autonomous APs — each AP configured independently. Fine for a small office; intractable beyond a few dozen.
  • Centralised controller — a dedicated hardware or virtual appliance (Cisco WLC, Aruba Mobility Controller, etc.) that terminates a tunnel from each AP (typically CAPWAP or a vendor analogue), centralises configuration, and in some modes also performs traffic forwarding.
  • Cloud-managed — a SaaS controller plane (Meraki, ExtremeCloud IQ, Mist) where APs are independent at the data plane but coordinated by a cloud service that handles configuration, monitoring, RF planning, and analytics. Increasingly the dominant model in greenfield enterprise deployments.

The controller’s most operationally valuable function is usually RF management: continuously sampling channel utilisation, neighbour signal strengths, and client densities across the fleet, then dynamically tuning each AP’s channel and transmit power to maintain coverage without creating self-interference. Cisco calls this RRM (Radio Resource Management); Aruba calls it ARM; the underlying problem is the same.

AP placement and coverage

The single most common failure mode in enterprise Wi-Fi deployments is treating it as a coverage problem rather than a capacity problem. A handful of high-power APs scattered through a building will technically “cover” the floor with signal — but if every AP is serving 50 clients on a single channel, throughput collapses, and reducing transmit power so cells overlap less actually improves performance.

Modern AP placement guidance can be reduced to a few principles:

  • Design for the band that matters. 5 GHz and 6 GHz are where modern traffic lives. Plan coverage primarily for those bands; 2.4 GHz will reach further than your 5 GHz cells regardless and is best operated at reduced power to limit co-channel contention.
  • Cell overlap of 15–25% between adjacent APs at the client’s edge-of-coverage signal threshold (commonly −67 dBm for voice, −65 dBm for video) supports smooth roaming without inflating co-channel interference.
  • Mount on the ceiling, never on the wall (in normal office layouts). APs are designed with downward-radiating antenna patterns; wall-mounting wastes half the signal into the wall.
  • Place by capacity, not by floor plan. A 50-person conference room may need its own AP even if the adjacent corridor is already covered. Density drives placement.
  • Avoid metal and water. Filing cabinets, elevator shafts, and (notoriously) human bodies all attenuate 2.4 / 5 / 6 GHz signal. A packed lecture hall has dramatically different propagation than an empty one.

Channel planning

The 2.4 GHz band, in most regulatory domains, offers only three truly non-overlapping 20 MHz channels: 1, 6, and 11. Despite occasional folklore suggesting otherwise, this is not a question of taste — IEEE 802.11’s channel-numbering clause explicitly requires 25 MHz separation for non-overlapping operation, which only 1/6/11 satisfies in the 2.4 GHz band as deployed in North America.3

The 5 GHz band is dramatically more accommodating: depending on regulatory domain and on whether DFS (Dynamic Frequency Selection) channels are enabled, you can have 9 to 25 non-overlapping 20 MHz channels, or correspondingly fewer wider channels (40, 80, 160 MHz). DFS channels share spectrum with weather and military radar; if a device detects radar, all APs on that channel must vacate within seconds, which is operationally disruptive and is one reason some operators avoid DFS channels in airport-adjacent or coastal deployments.

The 6 GHz band, opened for unlicensed use in 2020, adds 1,200 MHz of new spectrum in most regulatory domains, allowing up to seven 160 MHz channels or three 320 MHz channels — the structural reason Wi-Fi 6E and 7 perform so much better than their 5 GHz-bound predecessors in dense environments.

Roaming and band steering

Wi-Fi clients, not access points, decide when to roam — a quirk of the original 802.11 design that has caused decades of operational headache. A laptop will cling to a weakening AP at −80 dBm long after a closer AP at −55 dBm has become available, because the original 802.11 roaming algorithm was “don’t leave until you have to.”

Three IEEE amendments help, and modern enterprise APs and controllers implement them:

  • 802.11k (Radio Resource Measurement) — the AP can hand the client a list of neighbour APs so the client knows where to look when it decides to roam.
  • 802.11v (BSS Transition Management) — the AP can suggest a transition (“please move to AP-17, it has a stronger signal for you”) without forcing it.
  • 802.11r (Fast BSS Transition) — once the client decides to move, key derivation is pre-computed so the roam completes in tens of milliseconds rather than the multi-second hand-off of an unaided re-authentication. Essential for voice over Wi-Fi.

Band steering is a related but distinct technique implemented in the AP: when a dual-band client probes on both 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz, the AP can selectively withhold or delay its 2.4 GHz response, prodding the client to prefer the less-congested 5 GHz band. The effect is modest but worthwhile in mixed-client environments.

Wireless site surveys

A wireless site survey is the empirical foundation of any non-trivial WLAN deployment — the equivalent, in radio terms, of doing structural calculations before constructing a building. There are three distinct survey types, each answering a different question:36

The Wikipedia summary of the discipline captures the operational logic neatly: a passive survey measures the radio environment but does not engage with it; an active survey measures what the network actually delivers; a predictive survey models what the network will deliver under a proposed design.36 An “AP-on-a-stick” survey — temporarily mounting one or more APs on a pole at proposed locations and walking a passive survey around them — is the standard hybrid technique for validating predictive designs before procurement.37

The two dominant commercial tools are Ekahau AI Pro (with the Sidekick 2 hardware capture device, which simultaneously runs passive, active, and spectrum captures in a single walk) and NetAlly AirMagnet Survey PRO, with deeper roots in voice-over-WLAN survey workflows.37 Open-source alternatives include Kismet for wireless scanning and Wireshark for packet capture, though neither produces the heat-map output that commercial site-survey tools are built around.

Spectrum analysis vs. site survey

Site-survey tools see 802.11 frames. They are blind to non-802.11 interferers: microwave ovens, Bluetooth, video baby monitors, 2.4 GHz cordless phones, poorly shielded fluorescent ballasts, and unintentional emitters from industrial equipment. Spectrum analysers — software-defined radios that look at raw RF energy across the band — surface these. The Ekahau Sidekick 2 includes spectrum analysis hardware; standalone tools like the MetaGeek Wi-Spy are the legacy alternative.

§ 05 — Security

WPA, WPS, and the long shadow of bad PINs.

Wireless security is fundamentally harder than wired security: anyone within radio range is, by default, on the cable. The history of Wi-Fi security is the history of the industry repeatedly underestimating that fact.

The WPA family

Wi-Fi Protected Access is the umbrella term for the Wi-Fi Alliance’s security certifications, each generation responding to weaknesses in its predecessor.

  • WEP (Wired Equivalent Privacy, 1997) — the original 802.11 link encryption. RC4 stream cipher with a 24-bit initialisation vector, which exhausts in hours of traffic on a busy network and enables key recovery in minutes. Decisively broken by 2001; obsolete and dangerous for the better part of two decades.38
  • WPA (2003) — an interim retrofit by the Wi-Fi Alliance based on the draft IEEE 802.11i standard, designed to run on existing WEP-capable hardware. Introduced TKIP per-packet key mixing, the Michael message integrity check (MIC), and optional 802.1X enterprise authentication via EAP.3940
  • WPA2 (2004) — full implementation of IEEE 802.11i-2004. Replaces TKIP with AES-CCMP, the bedrock of Wi-Fi security for nearly fifteen years. Vulnerable to the KRACK attack disclosed in 2017, which exploited weaknesses in the 4-way handshake’s key reinstallation logic, and to offline dictionary attacks against weak passphrases.41
  • WPA3 (2018) — replaces the WPA2 Pre-Shared Key 4-way handshake with Simultaneous Authentication of Equals (SAE), a Dragonfly-derived key exchange that provides forward secrecy and resists offline dictionary attacks. WPA3-Personal still uses 128-bit AES; WPA3-Enterprise mandates 192-bit. WPA3 also makes management-frame protection (802.11w) mandatory and adds Opportunistic Wireless Encryption (OWE) for open networks, branded by the Wi-Fi Alliance as “Enhanced Open.”428

WPA3 support has been mandatory for new Wi-Fi-Certified devices since 1 July 2020.8 For Wi-Fi 7, as noted earlier, WPA3 (or Enhanced Open) is the only acceptable security mode for 802.11be data rates.35

Wi-Fi Protected Setup (WPS): the cautionary tale

WPS is not a security protocol — it is a provisioning protocol, introduced by the Wi-Fi Alliance in 2006 to help non-technical users connect devices to a WPA-secured network without typing the passphrase. The two relevant modes are push-button (press a button on the AP, press a button on the device, they exchange the WPA pre-shared key) and PIN (enter an 8-digit PIN from the AP into the client).

The PIN mode contains one of the most consequential design errors in consumer security history. The 8-digit PIN is validated as two 4-digit halves, which reduces the brute-force search space from 100,000,000 to roughly 11,000 (10,000 + 1,000, since the last digit is a checksum). A 2011 disclosure by security researcher Stefan Viehböck demonstrated that this flaw allowed an attacker to brute-force a vulnerable AP’s WPS PIN — and thus recover the WPA pre-shared key — within hours.39

A second class of attack, Pixie Dust, exploits weak random number generation in the WPS key exchange of many embedded chipsets, recovering the PIN offline in seconds on vulnerable devices.41

Operational guidance: disable WPS

Despite over a decade of disclosed vulnerabilities, WPS remains enabled by default on many consumer routers shipped today. The Wi-Fi Alliance’s own guidance for WPA2 deployments is to disable WPS on every access point.43 WPA3 does not support WPS at all — it has been replaced by Wi-Fi Easy Connect (Device Provisioning Protocol, DPP), which uses public-key cryptography and QR codes or NFC for onboarding, eliminating the WPS attack surface entirely while still being backward-compatible with WPA2 networks.41

Other operational hardening

Beyond protocol selection, a defensible Wi-Fi posture in 2026 minimally includes:

  • Separate SSIDs for guests, IoT, and corporate clients, mapped to separate VLANs, so a compromised smart bulb cannot reach a finance workstation.
  • 802.1X / EAP authentication with per-user credentials and certificates in enterprise environments, rather than a single Pre-Shared Key everyone in the office knows.
  • Management Frame Protection (PMF, 802.11w), mandatory in WPA3 but opt-in in WPA2 — without it, deauthentication attacks against clients are trivial.
  • Long, high-entropy passphrases for any remaining PSK networks. WPA passphrases are seeded from the SSID, and rainbow tables exist for the top 1,000 SSIDs; never use a default SSID with a default password.38
  • Rogue-AP detection in the WLAN controller, scanning for unauthorised APs broadcasting your SSID or attempting to lure your clients.
  • Firmware hygiene on APs, controllers, and especially consumer routers, where unpatched vulnerabilities accumulate for years.
§ 06 — Coda

Why none of this is settled.

The temptation, looking at the architecture sketched above, is to read it as a finished system. It isn’t. The 6 GHz band is brand new and still being absorbed into deployment practice. Wi-Fi 7’s Multi-Link Operation has at least two distinct implementations (MLMR and MLSR) whose relative merits the literature is still resolving.34 Cellular and Wi-Fi are colliding at the edge — Passpoint and OpenRoaming let phones hand off seamlessly between them. Direct-to-device satellite service is dragging cellular into orbit. LoRaWAN is moving into satellite uplinks. WPA3 has had its own quiet round of vulnerabilities disclosed.

What stays constant — what every generation of this technology has reconfirmed — is that wireless networking is the practice of negotiating shared use of a physical resource you cannot enclose. The protocols change. The spectrum allocations shift. The threat models evolve. But the underlying discipline is the same one Vic Hayes’s 802.11 working group articulated in the 1990s: agree on the rules, listen before you transmit, and accept that the air is not yours alone.
