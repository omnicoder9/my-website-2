Catalogue IAntecedents, c. 1794–1858

Before the Origin: Natural Theology and Its Cracks

Long before Darwin published a word, the ground under natural theology was already shifting.

It is a convenient fiction, useful mainly for anniversaries, that Charles Darwin single-handedly overturned a settled Western consensus about the origin of species. The consensus was not settled, and Darwin did not work alone, either scientifically or intellectually. By the time On the Origin of Species appeared in 1859, the idea that living forms might change over time — that species were not fixed at a single act of creation but had histories — had already been argued, ridiculed, half-believed, and argued again for the better part of a century. What Darwin and, independently, Alfred Russel Wallace supplied was not the first suggestion of transmutation but the first workable mechanism for it: a process requiring no plan, no ladder of progress, and no guiding hand, yet capable of producing the exquisite fit between organism and environment that had previously seemed to demand one.

That fit was the entire argument of natural theology, the dominant framework for understanding biological design in early nineteenth-century Britain. Its most influential statement came from the Archdeacon William Paley, whose 1802 treatise opened with an analogy that every Cambridge undergraduate of Darwin’s generation, Darwin included, had to master before ordination: a man crossing a heath who stumbles upon a watch must infer a watchmaker, because the watch’s parts are arranged for a purpose — telling time — too precisely to be the product of chance.1 Paley extended the analogy to the eye, the hand, the digestive tract of a beetle: structures fitted to function throughout nature implied, on the same logic, a Designer. Darwin later wrote that he had once found Paley’s argument as conclusive as a geometric proof.2 Natural selection would eventually supply exactly what Paley’s argument lacked: a way to generate adaptive fit without a designer, through the accumulation of small, undirected advantages across immense stretches of time.

Design was not the only inherited problem. There was also the matter of time itself, and here the crack had already opened wide before Darwin ever set foot on a ship. The French anatomist Georges Cuvier had established, through his comparative studies of fossil quadrupeds, that extinction was real — entire species had vanished from the earth, a fact that troubled the presumption of a static, complete Creation. Cuvier explained the fossil succession through catastrophism: periodic upheavals that destroyed local faunas, subsequently repopulated by migration. Charles Lyell, whose Principles of Geology Darwin carried aboard the Beagle and read with mounting excitement, offered a rival picture: uniformitarianism, the claim that the same slow processes visible today — erosion, sedimentation, gradual uplift — operating over vastly longer stretches of time than Genesis allowed, were sufficient to explain the geological record without invoking repeated catastrophes.3 Lyell did not believe in the transmutation of species; he included a lengthy refutation of the idea in his first volume. But the deep time his geology demanded — not thousands of years but a scale barely imaginable — was the one resource Darwin’s theory could not do without. Natural selection is a theory of accumulated small effects; without vast time, it has nothing to accumulate.

Specimen Note — Terminology

Transmutation was the term in common nineteenth-century use for what would later be called evolution; Darwin himself avoided the word “evolution” in the first edition of the Origin, preferring “descent with modification,” and the book’s famous final word, added only in later editions, is “evolved.”

Explicit transmutationist proposals had, in fact, already been made and already been rejected. Darwin’s own grandfather, Erasmus Darwin, speculated in his 1794 Zoonomia that all warm-blooded animals might have arisen from “one living filament,” modified across generations by competition and sexual selection — a text the younger Darwin read as a student and later insisted had influenced him little, more anxious than most grandsons to establish independent priority.4 More systematically, the French naturalist Jean-Baptiste Lamarck proposed in his 1809 Philosophie Zoologique a mechanism of transformation driven by the inheritance of characteristics acquired through use and disuse across an organism’s lifetime — the blacksmith’s son inheriting his father’s muscled forearm, in the durable caricature — combined with an innate drive toward complexity.5 Lamarck’s theory was widely mocked in Britain, associated with the political radicalism of post-revolutionary France and, worse for its scientific reputation, with a teleological drive toward perfection that Darwin’s later mechanism would pointedly refuse to include.

Then, in 1844, came the book that made the entire English reading public briefly obsessed with, and anxious about, transmutation: Vestiges of the Natural History of Creation, published anonymously (its author, the Scottish publisher Robert Chambers, was not publicly confirmed for decades) and written not for specialists but for a mass audience.6 Vestiges proposed a sweeping developmental progression from nebulae to solar systems to increasingly complex life, culminating in humanity, all governed by law rather than repeated miraculous intervention. It was a publishing sensation and a scientific catastrophe: reviewers who could stomach transmutation in principle tore apart its amateurish command of geology, embryology, and comparative anatomy, and the book became a byword for how not to argue for evolutionary change.7 Darwin, who had by then been quietly developing his own transmutationist theory for six years, watched the reception of Vestiges with something close to dread. It confirmed everything he feared about going public before his case was airtight: the scientific establishment, and Richard Owen in particular, could and would treat evolutionary speculation as amateur, dangerous, and beneath serious notice.8

What was missing from every one of these predecessors — Erasmus Darwin’s vague filaments, Lamarck’s inherited effort, the anonymous author of Vestiges and his cosmic law of development — was a causal mechanism that did not smuggle in exactly the kind of purposive, progressive design that natural theology had already supplied more convincingly. Each offered change without quite explaining why change should track adaptation, why organisms should end up so minutely and often so bizarrely well suited to their circumstances, from the orchid fitted to a single species of moth to the woodpecker’s tongue. That explanatory gap is precisely what a young naturalist, recently returned from five years aboard a Royal Navy survey ship, would spend the next two decades of his life trying to close.9

Catalogue II1831–1844

A Naturalist’s Apprenticeship: The Beagle and the Notebooks

Five years at sea supplied the observations. A struggling economist, read almost by accident, supplied the mechanism.

Charles Darwin was twenty-two, a mediocre medical-school dropout redirected toward the clergy, when his Cambridge mentor John Stevens Henslow forwarded an unusual offer: a berth as self-funded naturalist and gentleman companion aboard HMS Beagle, a ten-gun Royal Navy brig under Captain Robert FitzRoy, about to depart on a lengthy hydrographic survey of South America. After his father’s initial refusal was reversed by an uncle’s intervention, and after weather delayed departure by several weeks, the Beagle finally cleared Plymouth Sound on 27 December 1831.10 The voyage, planned for two years, lasted nearly five: the ship did not return to England until 2 October 1836, having carried Darwin around the entire globe.11

What Darwin did for five years, chiefly, was collect, dissect, and write. He filled notebook after notebook with geological observations, sent home crates of specimens — fossils, beetles, barnacles, nearly five hundred bird skins alone — and, decisive for his later thinking, spent most of the voyage as much a geologist as a biologist, testing Lyell’s uniformitarian principles against Andean uplift, Patagonian terraces, and the structure of coral atolls.12 The popular shorthand for the voyage compresses all of this into a single epiphany on the Galápagos Islands in September and October 1835, where Darwin is imagined realizing, tortoise shell in hand, that species evolve. The real sequence was slower and less tidy, and the historical record repays the correction. Darwin's shipboard notes on the Galápagos mockingbirds — not, contrary to the popular emblem, the finches — record his first documented suspicion that distinct forms on neighboring islands might be varieties of a single ancestral species; his contemporaneous collecting of the famous finches was comparatively careless, and he failed even to label several specimens by island. It was not Darwin but the ornithologist John Gould, working through Darwin’s specimens in London in March 1837, who determined that the finches represented some dozen distinct species confined to individual islands — a realization that struck Darwin with retrospective force only after the voyage was long over.11

The theorizing began in earnest only after Darwin was home, unpacking specimens and consulting the specialists — Gould among them — who were describing them. In July 1837 he opened the first of a series of private notebooks on what he called “the species question,” now known to historians simply as Notebook B. Somewhere in its pages, in a passage datable to that same month, Darwin sketched a small, spare diagram of diverging lines rising from a single base, with the words “I think” written above it — the first time the branching, tree-like structure of common descent appears anywhere in his surviving work, a full two decades before it appeared, refined and general, as the single folding diagram printed in the Origin.14 Notebook B and its successors, C, D, and E, compiled through 1839, show Darwin testing and discarding mechanisms, worrying over the fixity of species, and circling a problem he could describe but not yet solve: given that offspring vary from their parents, what decides which variations persist and which disappear?

The answer arrived, by Darwin’s own account, from an unlikely direction: political economy, not biology. In the autobiographical sketch he prepared late in life for his children, never intended for publication, Darwin recalled picking up Malthus’s essay on population in October 1838, purely for casual reading, already primed by years of observing plants and animals to recognize the struggle for existence it described. The application to his own species question arrived, by his account, almost immediately: favorable variations would tend to survive that struggle and unfavorable ones would tend to be eliminated, and the cumulative result, over enough generations, would be new species. As Darwin himself put it, he had “at last got a theory by which to work.”2 Thomas Robert Malthus’s 1798 Essay on the Principle of Population had argued that human populations, left unchecked, grow geometrically while food supplies grow only arithmetically, guaranteeing recurring pressure from famine, disease, and war.15 Darwin’s insight was to strip the argument of its human specificity and generalize it to every reproducing population on earth: any species capable of leaving more than one surviving offspring per parent, sustained across generations, would overwhelm its resources, guaranteeing a perpetual struggle for existence — and it was this struggle, acting on the heritable variation Darwin had already observed everywhere he looked, that would do the selecting Paley had assigned to God.

By the end of 1838, then, not even two years after returning home, Darwin possessed the core logical architecture of natural selection in essentially the form it would take twenty-one years later. What he did not possess was the courage, or perhaps the professional standing, to publish it. The reception of Vestiges in 1844 — the year Darwin also completed a 230-page private essay laying out his theory in full, with instructions to his wife Emma to have it published in the event of his death — only confirmed his caution.16 Instead of rushing into print, Darwin spent much of the next decade establishing his scientific bona fides through unglamorous, exhaustive work: an eight-year taxonomic monograph on barnacles, published in four volumes between 1851 and 1854, that had nothing directly to do with transmutation but everything to do with demonstrating, to a scientific community that might otherwise have dismissed him as another amateur speculator, that he could do the patient, technical work of a serious naturalist. It was a strategic delay whose costs would become apparent only when a letter arrived from the Malay Archipelago in the summer of 1858.

Catalogue IIITheory

The Mechanism: Variation, Heredity, Selection

A logically simple argument, built from premises almost nobody disputed, that nonetheless required no plan and no planner.

Strip away the historical apparatus and natural selection is a remarkably compact piece of reasoning, closer in structure to a syllogism than to what most people, on hearing “theory,” imagine a scientific theory to be. Darwin’s own preferred summary, elaborated across the first four chapters of the Origin, rests on premises that were, individually, close to platitudes among naturalists of his generation.17 Organisms produce far more offspring than can possibly survive to reproduce. Offspring vary, in ways both trivial and consequential, from their parents and from one another. Much of that variation is heritable, passed from parent to offspring by mechanisms nobody in 1859 understood. Therefore, in the resulting struggle for existence, individuals whose variations happen to suit their circumstances will, on average, survive and reproduce more successfully than individuals whose variations do not. Repeat across enough generations, and the heritable traits of a population will shift toward whatever the local environment rewards. Repeat across enough time, and populations diverge into forms different enough to merit separate names — new species, descended with modification from a common ancestor.

Two features of this argument were, and remain, easy to misplace. The first is that natural selection is not a theory of progress. Nothing in the logic above cares whether a resulting adaptation is more complex than what preceded it, only whether it confers a reproductive edge in a particular environment at a particular time; parasites that lose eyes and limbs over evolutionary time, trading complexity for economy, are exhibiting natural selection exactly as much as a peacock growing an elaborate tail. The second is that the phrase most associated with the theory in the popular imagination — “survival of the fittest” — is not Darwin’s own coinage. It belongs to the philosopher Herbert Spencer, who used it in 1864 to describe his own, considerably more progressivist social theory; Darwin adopted the phrase reluctantly, in the fifth edition of the Origin, as a synonym for natural selection, and its later, distorted afterlife in social and political argument would become one of the more consequential embarrassments in the theory’s history.18

Variation
Heritable differences among individuals in a population — size, coloration, behavior, physiology — arising, though Darwin could not say how, prior to any selection acting on them.
Struggle for existence
Darwin’s Malthusian borrowing: competition for finite resources, mates, and safety from predation, guaranteed by any population’s tendency to reproduce beyond what its environment can sustain.
Natural selection
The non-random, differential survival and reproduction of variants, as a consequence of the struggle for existence — not a force or an agent, but a statistical outcome.
Descent with modification
Darwin’s preferred term for what the accumulation of selected variation produces over long timescales: lineages that diverge from shared ancestors into the branching pattern of life.

What the theory conspicuously lacked in 1859, and what Darwin himself recognized as its most exposed flank, was any account of heredity — a mechanism explaining how variation arose and how it was transmitted intact from one generation to the next. The dominant assumption of the age was blending inheritance: the folk-genetic notion that offspring traits represent something like an average of parental traits, as paints mix to a single new hue. Darwin saw the difficulty this posed almost immediately and it was pressed on him, forcefully, by the Scottish engineer Fleeming Jenkin, whose 1867 review in the North British Review offered what remains one of the sharpest contemporary critiques the theory faced.19 If a single favorable variant appeared in one individual and inheritance blended traits as paint blends colors, Jenkin argued, that variant would be diluted by half with each generation of breeding with the unmodified majority, vanishing into the average within a handful of generations — long before natural selection could ever amplify it into a new species-defining trait. It was, on blending-inheritance premises, a genuinely serious mathematical problem, and Darwin had no adequate answer to it. His own attempt at a theory of heredity, “pangenesis,” proposed in 1868 that every cell in the body shed tiny hereditary particles he called gemmules, which collected in the reproductive organs and blended in the offspring — a hypothesis that satisfied almost no one, Darwin included, and that later genetics would discard entirely.20

A second serious technical objection came from the most eminent physicist in Britain. William Thomson, later Lord Kelvin, calculated the age of the earth from the rate at which a molten body of its size should cool, arriving at estimates ranging, across various refinements, from perhaps twenty million to at most a few hundred million years — nowhere near the “incomprehensibly vast” timescale Darwin’s gradualist mechanism required for the observed diversity of life to accumulate.21 Kelvin was the most authoritative physicist of his generation, and his numbers stood as a genuine, unresolved thorn in Darwin’s side for the rest of his life; Darwin trimmed his own estimates of elapsed geological time in successive editions of the Origin, uneasily, in partial deference. The objection dissolved only after Darwin’s death, when the discovery of radioactivity revealed an internal heat source in the earth that Kelvin’s calculations could not have anticipated, and radiometric dating eventually pushed the age of the earth out to the roughly 4.5 billion years recognized today — vindicating Darwin’s intuition about deep time far more generously than he had any right to expect from the physics available to him.22

Both gaps — no mechanism of heredity, no confirmed span of time — were real, and both were seized on by contemporary critics as reasons to doubt the theory, or at least to withhold full assent. Neither gap, it is worth emphasizing, was ever filled by natural theology’s alternative; Paley’s design argument answered a different question and left the fossil record, the geographic distribution of species, and the existence of vestigial and rudimentary organs — the human appendix and coccyx, the pelvic-bone remnants inside a whale skeleton, evidence Darwin catalogued at length — entirely unaddressed. It would take the rediscovery of a monastery garden experiment on pea plants, buried in an obscure regional journal since 1866, to finally supply the missing theory of heredity, and it would take another seventy years beyond that for a full working synthesis to emerge. Catalogue VIII returns to that story. First, though, came 1858, and a letter from Borneo that neither Darwin nor the man who sent it fully anticipated.

Catalogue IVFebruary–July 1858

Ternate, 1858: Wallace and the Race to Publish

A second naturalist reached the same conclusion, by the same Malthusian route, on the other side of the planet — and forced Darwin’s hand.

Alfred Russel Wallace was, by class and career, almost everything Darwin was not: a self-taught land surveyor of modest means who supported his fieldwork by selling specimens to collectors back in Britain, working alone through the Malay Archipelago — present-day Indonesia, Malaysia, and Papua New Guinea — for the better part of a decade. He had already spent years thinking systematically about the geographic distribution of species and had published, in 1855, a paper arguing that new species arise in close geographic and temporal proximity to closely allied existing species — a pattern crying out for a causal explanation he had not yet found. In February 1858, laid up with a recurring bout of malarial fever on the island of Ternate, Wallace later recalled that Malthus’s Essay on Population, which he too had read years earlier, came back to him in a flash of fever-sharpened clarity: the same struggle for existence, the same argument that favorable variations would allow their bearers to survive that struggle while unfavorable ones would not. Within a few days he had drafted a complete essay and, aware from prior correspondence that Darwin had a serious scientific interest in species questions, mailed it to Down House for comment and, if Darwin thought it worthy, forwarding to Charles Lyell.23

The essay reached Darwin on 18 June 1858. Reading it, by his own account, was close to devastating: Wallace had independently arrived at a theory Darwin considered, not without justice, to be substantially his own, developed in private over twenty years and still unpublished. “I never saw a more striking coincidence,” Darwin wrote in anguish to Lyell that same day, adding that if Wallace had possessed Darwin’s own 1842 manuscript sketch, he could not have written a better summary of it himself.24 Darwin’s distress was compounded by the worst possible timing: his youngest son Charles Waring Darwin, not yet two years old, was gravely ill with scarlet fever and died on 28 June, in the same week Darwin was corresponding frantically with Lyell and his botanist friend Joseph Dalton Hooker about how — and whether — to proceed.25

What Lyell and Hooker arranged, in the days that followed, has generated more retrospective suspicion than perhaps any other episode in the history of the theory: a joint presentation, at an emergency session of the Linnean Society of London, that would pair Wallace’s essay with extracts from Darwin’s unpublished 1844 manuscript and a letter Darwin had sent the American botanist Asa Gray in 1857, establishing that Darwin’s thinking predated Wallace’s essay by years even though it had never been made public.26 Neither author was present at the reading on 1 July 1858: Darwin was at his son’s funeral, Wallace was still in the Archipelago and would not learn of the arrangement, let alone consent to its specific terms, until months later, by which point the matter was settled. The paper was read to a Society whose president, Thomas Bell, would write in his year-end summary that 1858 had not been marked by any of those striking discoveries that at once revolutionize a department of science — a judgment historians now cite, gently, as one of the least prescient sentences in the annals of the Linnean Society.27

Most historians who have examined the surviving correspondence in detail reject the once-popular narrative that Darwin, aided by his well-placed friends, effectively stole priority from a poorer and more distant rival.28 Wallace, on learning what had transpired, expressed gratitude rather than grievance, and the two men maintained a respectful, occasionally warm correspondence and mutual professional regard for the rest of their lives; Wallace would go on to coin the term “Darwinism” himself, as the title of his own 1889 book defending the theory. But the episode remains genuinely uncomfortable by modern standards of scientific etiquette — a decision taken on Wallace’s behalf, without his knowledge, by men who were unmistakably acting to protect their friend’s claim to two decades of unpublished priority. It is one of the quieter ironies of the story that a theory built on the premise of blind, unplanned process was itself ushered into public existence through a rather deliberate act of social engineering by Darwin’s scientific allies.

The Linnean reading of 1 July 1858 produced almost no public reaction whatsoever; the audience was small, the abstract dense, and neither name yet meant much beyond specialist circles. What it did was start a clock. Darwin, now aware that the idea he had nursed in private for two decades was independently discoverable — and discovered — abandoned his plan for an exhaustive, multi-volume treatise and began instead, at Lyell and Hooker’s urging, to compress two decades of notes into what he self-deprecatingly called an “abstract.” He finished it in just over a year.

Catalogue V24 November 1859

On the Origin of Species and Its Reception

One long argument, carefully hedged on the subject of humans, sold out on its first day — and split the scientific establishment on contact.

On the Origin of Species by Means of Natural Selection, or the Preservation of Favoured Races in the Struggle for Life — the full, ungainly title under which the book was published by John Murray on 24 November 1859 — sold its entire first printing of 1,250 copies to booksellers on the day of release.17 Darwin himself described the work, accurately, as a single, cumulative case rather than a series of independent proofs, built chapter by chapter: fourteen of them, opening with the artificial selection practiced by pigeon fanciers and livestock breeders — a phenomenon no Victorian reader needed convincing was real — before extending the same logic, by analogy, to a natural process with no breeder at the wheel. Later chapters marshal evidence from the geological record, embryology, vestigial organs, biogeography, and the branching, nested pattern of biological classification itself, arguing that all of it makes far more sense as the signature of common descent than as the record of independent, special creations.29

One feature of the book’s argument is easy to overlook from a vantage point where “Darwinism” is popularly synonymous with human descent from apes: the Origin says almost nothing about human beings. Darwin, keenly aware of how much more incendiary the human question was than the origin of barnacles or finches, confined himself to a single, carefully understated sentence near the end of the book: “Light will be thrown on the origin of man and his history.”17 That was all. The fuller argument for human descent would wait twelve more years, for a separate book, after Darwin judged the general theory of natural selection to have gained sufficient a foothold to survive the additional controversy.30

The caution did not spare him. Scientific reaction split almost immediately along lines that would harden over the following decade. Thomas Henry Huxley, a combative young comparative anatomist who had not been part of Darwin’s inner circle before publication, read an advance copy and reportedly told Darwin’s deputy that his first reaction to the theory’s central logic was to feel it extremely stupid not to have thought of it himself — a remark that has entered the historical record in slightly varying forms across multiple secondhand tellings.8 Huxley became the theory’s most pugnacious public defender, earning the durable nickname “Darwin’s bulldog” for a combativeness Darwin, a chronically ill recluse who avoided public confrontation his entire adult life, was constitutionally unsuited to supply himself. Joseph Hooker and the American botanist Asa Gray, both already familiar with Darwin’s thinking from years of prior correspondence, offered qualified early support; Gray, notably, spent years trying to reconcile natural selection with a providential, God-guided universe, a project Darwin found intellectually generous but ultimately unpersuasive on Darwin’s own terms.

The book’s closing paragraph steps back from argument into something closer to wonder: a universe governed by fixed, simple laws that has nonetheless produced, and continues to produce, an almost unlimited diversity of beautiful and intricate living forms — life’s diversity presented not as evidence against design but as something grander than any single act of special creation could account for. — Paraphrasing the closing lines of On the Origin of Species, 1859 edition17

Opposition, when it came from within science, was frequently just as substantial in credentials as the support. Richard Owen, the most eminent comparative anatomist in Britain and the man who had coined the word “dinosaur,” wrote a scathing anonymous review in the Edinburgh Review that questioned Darwin’s command of anatomy while pointedly failing to disclose that its author was himself the most prominent living authority on the very archetypal, idealist theory of anatomical form that Darwin’s common-descent explanation was quietly displacing. In the United States, the Harvard naturalist Louis Agassiz, the country’s foremost authority on glaciation and fish fossils, rejected transmutation outright to the end of his career on both empirical and explicitly theological grounds, regarding species as fixed thoughts in the mind of the Creator rather than historically contingent, mutable populations.29 Even Charles Lyell, whose geology had been indispensable to Darwin’s thinking and whose personal encouragement had helped push the book into print at all, took years to accept the full implications of the theory for human ancestry, a reluctance that quietly strained a friendship that had otherwise been one of the sturdiest supports of Darwin’s career.

What is easy to miss, reading backward from a twenty-first-century vantage in which evolution is the unchallenged organizing framework of biology, is how genuinely contested the mechanism — as opposed to the fact of some kind of transmutation — remained among working scientists for decades after 1859. A great many naturalists were won over relatively quickly to the idea that species change and share common ancestors; that battle, within the scientific community narrowly construed, was substantially over within a generation. Natural selection specifically, as the primary engine of that change, faced serious ongoing competition well into the twentieth century from Lamarckian inheritance of acquired characteristics, from saltationist theories proposing that new species arise in sudden jumps rather than gradual accumulation, and from a general sense, encouraged by the unresolved heredity problem examined in Catalogue III, that Darwin had identified a real process without adequately proving it was the dominant one. That particular argument, waged largely among professionals in specialist journals, was a comparatively contained affair. The argument that spilled into cathedrals, newspapers, and satirical magazines was a different one entirely, and it began just seven months after publication, in a crowded lecture hall in Oxford.

Catalogue VI1860–1871

Storm at Oxford and Beyond: The Public Battle Begins

Where the scientific dispute turned into spectacle — and where the specifically human question detonated.

The event popular memory has fused into a single decisive duel — “Huxley versus Wilberforce” — was, according to the historians who have most carefully reconstructed it from surviving letters and contemporary newspaper accounts, something considerably messier: an unplanned, unminuted exchange that occurred during the discussion period following an unrelated paper, at the annual meeting of the British Association for the Advancement of Science, held at Oxford’s newly opened University Museum on Saturday, 30 June 1860.31 Samuel Wilberforce, the Bishop of Oxford — a formidable orator nicknamed “Soapy Sam,” coached beforehand on the relevant anatomy by Richard Owen — rose to attack Darwin’s theory at length, on ostensibly scientific rather than purely scriptural grounds, before turning, in a moment no verbatim transcript survives to confirm precisely, to ask Huxley whether it was through his grandfather or his grandmother that he claimed descent from an ape.32

Huxley’s reply, as reconstructed decades later from participants’ recollections rather than any contemporaneous transcript, was to the effect that he would not be ashamed to have an ape for an ancestor, but would be ashamed to be connected with a man who used great intellectual gifts to obscure the plain truth. Both sides claimed victory immediately afterward in their own private correspondence — Wilberforce wrote to a friend that he had “thoroughly beaten” Huxley; Huxley considered himself, by his own account, the most talked-about man in Oxford for the following twenty-four hours — and modern historians of science, working from the fuller documentary record uncovered over the past several decades, generally regard the popular version of the exchange, in which Huxley single-handedly humiliated the bishop before a stunned scientific establishment, as a legend substantially embellished in the retelling by Darwin’s partisans over the following generation.3334 What is not in dispute is the exchange’s afterlife: within the historiography of science and religion in the English-speaking world, it became, and remains, one of the most frequently invoked episodes of supposed direct confrontation between the two — a shorthand so durable that its exaggerations are still being carefully corrected in the scholarly literature more than a century and a half later.35

Oxford was a single afternoon. The wider cultural reception of Darwin’s theory played out over the following decade in a medium that reached vastly more people than the Linnean Society or the British Association ever could: the illustrated satirical press. British periodicals, Punch foremost among them, found in Darwin’s theory an irresistible visual gift, and across the 1860s and 1870s ran a recurring stream of cartoons rendering Darwin himself, and the theory more broadly, in simian form — a visual shorthand that compressed “common ancestry with apes” into the crudely funnier and far more provocative claim that humans simply were descended from living monkeys, a claim Darwin’s actual theory never made.36 That collapse — common ancestor mistaken for direct lineage, a shared branch point on a family tree mistaken for a straight line from monkey to man — became, and to a considerable extent remains, the single most persistent popular misreading of evolutionary theory, doing more to shape the caricature of “Darwinism” in public consciousness than any passage Darwin actually wrote.

A recurring confusion, corrected once, for the record: Darwin never proposed that humans descended from any living species of ape. The claim is that humans and modern great apes — chimpanzees, bonobos, gorillas, orangutans — share a common ancestral population, now extinct, from which all these lineages independently diverged. Chimpanzees are not a rung below humans on a ladder; they are cousins at the end of their own, equally long branch.

If the Origin had been circumspect about human origins, Darwin’s next major book removed all ambiguity. The Descent of Man, and Selection in Relation to Sex, published in 1871, argued explicitly and at length that humans had evolved from earlier primate ancestors through the same unguided process governing every other species, extending natural selection’s reach into anatomy, cognition, and even morality itself — the last a deliberately provocative move, since morality was widely considered, even by many scientific naturalists otherwise sympathetic to evolution, the one human faculty that must surely stand outside the animal economy.37 Where the Origin had provoked a diffuse, gradually intensifying unease, the Descent detonated it directly: this was no longer an abstract question about barnacles and finches but an explicit claim about the reader’s own grandparents, and the reaction, in pulpit and periodical alike, was correspondingly sharper. It is worth being precise about what, exactly, provoked such fury, because the anger was rarely about the biology in isolation. It was about what the biology was taken to imply, once followed to its logical end — a question the following section addresses directly.

Catalogue VIIStakes

What Was Really at Stake: Purpose, Design, and Morality

The fury was rarely about barnacles. It was about what a universe without a watchmaker was thought to license.

Strip the nineteenth-century reaction to Darwin down to its structural core and three distinct anxieties emerge, frequently tangled together in any given sermon or review but worth separating with some care, because they provoke different objections and admit of different responses.

The first, and in an important sense the deepest, was the loss of teleology — purpose — as an explanatory category in biology. Paley’s watchmaker argument, examined in Catalogue I, had proposed that the exquisite fit between organism and function could only be explained by appeal to intention: eyes exist in order to see, wings exist in order to fly, and that “in order to” pointed, ineluctably, to a designing mind. Natural selection did not merely offer a competing designer; it eliminated the category of design altogether, replacing purposive explanation with a blind filtering process that requires no forward-looking intention anywhere in the system. A trait persists not because anything intended it to help the organism, but because organisms that happened to have it left more descendants than organisms that did not — a purely retrospective, statistical fact, indifferent to any future good. For a culture whose entire framework for understanding biological order rested on inferring purpose from function, this was not a minor technical adjustment. It removed the load-bearing wall.

The second anxiety followed close behind: if living structure did not require a designing intelligence, what work was left for one? Natural theology was not merely a set of biological observations; it was, for many educated Victorians, the primary evidentiary bridge connecting the physical world to the reality of God, doing real theological labor that scripture and revelation alone had not been asked to do since the Enlightenment. Remove that bridge and the difficulty was not necessarily atheism — most scientific naturalists of Darwin’s generation, Darwin himself included for much of his life, described their position as agnosticism or a chastened theism rather than outright disbelief — but a harder, less comfortable relationship between science and faith, one in which God’s hand in nature, if present at all, had to be sought further back, in the initial establishment of natural law, rather than in the ongoing, visible design of each individual creature.2 Darwin’s own religious trajectory illustrates the gradient rather than a clean binary: he entered adulthood a conventional, if unenthusiastic, candidate for the Anglican clergy, drifted through the 1840s and 1850s toward an increasingly uncertain deism, and by the end of his life described himself, in careful private correspondence, as an agnostic rather than an atheist — troubled less by natural selection’s implications for design than by the more general philosophical problem of suffering, crystallized for him by the protracted illness and 1851 death of his beloved eldest daughter, Annie.

Specimen Note — Not a Monolith

The “science versus religion” framing, though rhetorically durable, obscures how varied the theological response actually was. The Harvard botanist Asa Gray argued at length for a providential, God-guided evolution compatible with orthodox Christianity; the Anglican clergyman-naturalist Charles Kingsley wrote to Darwin welcoming the theory as a grander conception of divine creation than special, repeated intervention; the theologian Frederick Temple, later Archbishop of Canterbury, publicly reconciled evolution with Christian doctrine as early as the 1880s. Opposition was real, often intense, and frequently well organized — but it was never the unanimous voice of religious opinion, then or since.

The third anxiety, and the one most directly implicated in later, genuine harms, concerned morality: if humans are themselves products of an amoral, competitive natural process, what, if anything, grounds ethical obligation? This is where Darwin’s theory, entirely apart from anything Darwin himself argued, was pressed into service — and badly distorted — by others. Herbert Spencer’s “survival of the fittest,” discussed in Catalogue III, fed directly into what the historian Richard Hofstadter later named Social Darwinism: the argument, congenial to industrial-age laissez-faire ideology, that unregulated economic competition mirrored a natural, even salutary, evolutionary struggle, and that assisting the poor or otherwise weak interfered with a beneficial winnowing process.38 Darwin’s own cousin, Francis Galton, went further still, founding the eugenics movement in his 1869 Hereditary Genius, which argued that talent and character were substantially inherited and that society ought therefore to encourage the reproduction of its “fit” members while discouraging that of its “unfit” — a program that would metastasize, across the following seven decades, into forced sterilization laws across multiple countries including the United States, immigration restriction premised on pseudo-scientific racial hierarchy, and, in its most catastrophic expression, direct ideological support for Nazi racial policy.39

It is worth being exact about culpability here, because the historical record supports neither of the two easy stories. Darwin was not a eugenicist in Galton’s programmatic sense, and the Descent of Man explicitly argues, in a passage eugenicists preferred to skip, that civilized societies are right to care for the weak and sick despite whatever short-term reproductive cost this might carry, because sympathy and mutual aid are themselves evolved, valuable human traits, not corruptions of a purer natural order.37 At the same time, the historian of science Robert J. Richards and others have shown that the line from Darwin’s biology to Spencer’s social theory to Galton’s eugenics to twentieth-century atrocity, while real, runs through a series of independent intellectual choices and misappropriations that Darwin neither made nor endorsed, rather than a direct, inevitable logical descent from natural selection itself.40 That distinction — between what a scientific theory claims and what later actors, often for reasons having little to do with the science, claim on its authority — would recur throughout the theory’s subsequent history, and it remains, more than a century and a half later, one of the more persistently misunderstood aspects of the entire controversy.

Catalogue VIII1866–2016

Filling the Gaps: Mendel, the Modern Synthesis, and Confirmation

The heredity problem that haunted Darwin had already been solved, in a monastery garden, before he died. Nobody had noticed.

In 1866, one year before Fleeming Jenkin published his blending-inheritance critique, an Augustinian friar named Gregor Mendel presented a paper to the Natural History Society of Brünn describing eight years of meticulous cross-breeding experiments on garden peas.41 Mendel had discovered that inherited traits are transmitted not as a blending fluid but as discrete, particulate units — what would later be called genes — that pass unaltered from parent to offspring according to statistically predictable ratios, sometimes masked in a given generation, only to reappear intact in a later one. This was, in retrospect, precisely the mechanism natural selection needed: a way for favorable variation to persist undiluted across generations rather than blending back into the population average, dissolving Jenkin’s objection at the root. Mendel’s paper, published in the proceedings of a regional Moravian scientific society with negligible international circulation, went almost entirely unread for thirty-four years. Darwin, whose own extensive private library has been checked by historians for an uncut, unread copy, never encountered it.42

The paper resurfaced only in 1900, rediscovered independently and nearly simultaneously by three Continental botanists — Hugo de Vries in the Netherlands, Carl Correns in Germany, and Erich von Tschermak in Austria — each of whom had arrived at similar particulate-inheritance findings in their own breeding experiments before encountering Mendel’s decades-old priority in the literature. The rediscovery triggered, rather than resolved, a fresh and surprisingly bitter scientific dispute. Early Mendelians, working with visibly discontinuous traits — pea-pod shape, flower color — argued that evolution proceeded through discrete jumps, or saltations, and viewed Darwinian gradualism with real skepticism. Biometricians, a rival camp built around the statistical study of continuously varying traits like height, argued the opposite, that Mendelian particulate inheritance could not possibly account for the smooth, continuous variation gradual natural selection required. For roughly the first three decades of the twentieth century, Mendelism and Darwinism were widely regarded, even by many working biologists, as competing rather than complementary theories.

The reconciliation, when it came, arrived through mathematics. Between 1918 and 1932, the statistician and geneticist Ronald Fisher, the biologist J. B. S. Haldane, and the population geneticist Sewall Wright independently developed the mathematical machinery of population genetics, demonstrating rigorously that Mendelian particulate inheritance, once a sufficient number of genes with small individual effects were allowed to interact, produces exactly the continuous, gradual variation biometricians had observed — dissolving the apparent conflict entirely.434445 Building on this theoretical foundation, a second wave of biologists spent the following decade and a half extending the reconciliation across the discipline: the Russian-American geneticist Theodosius Dobzhansky’s 1937 Genetics and the Origin of Species connected population genetics to observations of natural populations in the wild; the ornithologist and systematist Ernst Mayr’s 1942 Systematics and the Origin of Species incorporated geographic speciation; the paleontologist George Gaylord Simpson’s 1944 Tempo and Mode in Evolution showed the fossil record, too, was consistent with gradual, population-level change; and the biologist Julian Huxley — grandson of Darwin’s own bulldog — gave the entire consolidated framework its lasting name in his 1942 book Evolution: The Modern Synthesis.46474849 By the early 1940s, roughly eighty years after Darwin first published, natural selection and Mendelian genetics had merged into a single, unified theoretical structure that remains, with substantial subsequent refinement, the working foundation of evolutionary biology today.50

The Modern Synthesis resolved the theoretical gap. What followed, across the second half of the twentieth century and into the twenty-first, was an accumulating weight of independent empirical confirmation, from fields Darwin could not have anticipated. James Watson and Francis Crick’s 1953 determination of the double-helix structure of DNA supplied, at last, the physical substrate of Mendel’s abstract hereditary units, and the subsequent decades of molecular biology allowed evolutionary relationships to be tested directly against the genetic code itself — and confirmed, with remarkable consistency, the same branching pattern of common descent Darwin had inferred purely from anatomy, embryology, and biogeography.51

No single case has done more work in classrooms, textbooks, and courtrooms alike to demonstrate natural selection operating in observable, human time-scales than the peppered moth, Biston betularia. Prior to Britain’s industrialization, the moth existed almost entirely in a pale, speckled form well camouflaged against lichen-covered bark. A dark, or melanic, form, first recorded in Manchester in 1848, was rare enough to be a collector’s curiosity. As coal-smoke pollution spread across industrial Britain through the second half of the nineteenth century, blackening tree trunks and killing the lichen that had provided the pale form’s camouflage, the melanic form’s frequency rose sharply in industrial regions, reaching near-total dominance in some areas by the 1890s — a shift the entomologist J. W. Tutt proposed, as early as the 1890s, was driven by birds preferentially eating the now-conspicuous pale moths against blackened bark.53

The story acquired a second life as a target of skeptics, after popular accounts revealed that the mid-twentieth-century field experiments most associated with the case, conducted by the geneticist H. B. D. Kettlewell in the 1950s, had methodological weaknesses — artificially high moth densities, unnatural resting positions — that later researchers, including entomologists sympathetic to the overall conclusion, openly acknowledged and worked to correct.57 Rather than overturning the underlying phenomenon, the subsequent decades of more careful work strengthened it considerably: Ilik Saccheri and colleagues documented, through direct field surveys across Britain, a genetic cline in melanic frequency tightly correlated with regional pollution levels and its continued decline following the 1956 Clean Air Act, and a posthumously completed set of experiments by the geneticist Michael Majerus, designed specifically to answer the earlier methodological criticisms, confirmed differential bird predation as a major selective force after all.5458 In 2016, a team led by Arjen van’t Hof identified the precise genetic mutation responsible — a transposable element, a mobile stretch of DNA, inserted into a gene called cortex — and dated its origin, through the statistical analysis of surrounding genetic variation, to approximately 1819, squarely at the start of the Industrial Revolution and decades before the mutation would have become common enough to notice.56 Few cases in the history of science have been checked, re-checked, criticized, and ultimately reconfirmed down to the molecular level in quite this much detail — which is precisely why creationist literature’s continued invocation of the 1950s methodological controversies, discussed further in Catalogue X, so consistently omits the sixty years of subsequent work that followed them.

Catalogue IX1925–2005

Scopes and the Long Legal War

From an outright teaching ban in Tennessee to a federal court’s finding that intelligent design is creationism by another name.

The specifically American, specifically legal chapter of this controversy has its own chronology, distinct from and considerably longer-running than the nineteenth-century British disputes examined so far, and it begins with the collision between Darwinian biology and American Protestant fundamentalism in the years following the First World War. The Fundamentalist–Modernist controversy of the 1910s and 1920s pitted theologically conservative Protestants, alarmed by German higher biblical criticism, wartime social upheaval, and the spread of evolutionary teaching in public schools, against modernist coreligionists more comfortable accommodating scientific consensus. Tennessee’s legislature, in March 1925, passed the Butler Act, criminalizing the teaching in any state-funded school of any theory denying the biblical account of divine creation in favor of the claim that “man has descended from a lower order of animals.”59

The American Civil Liberties Union, seeking a test case to challenge the law’s constitutionality, found a willing defendant in John T. Scopes, a twenty-four-year-old football coach and substitute biology teacher in Dayton, Tennessee, recruited by local boosters who saw in a prominent trial a welcome source of publicity and revenue for their small town. Scopes was not entirely certain he had ever actually taught evolution from the state-approved textbook, but agreed to be prosecuted regardless, and the resulting trial, held from 10 to 21 July 1925, became a national sensation: the three-time presidential candidate and prominent fundamentalist William Jennings Bryan volunteered for the prosecution, the famed defense attorney Clarence Darrow volunteered pro bono for the defense — his first such case — and the proceedings were broadcast nationally by radio for the first time in American legal history.60 The trial’s most celebrated moment came when Darrow, in an unusual maneuver permitted by the presiding judge, called Bryan himself to the stand as an expert witness on the Bible and subjected him to extended, often withering cross-examination on biblical literalism. Scopes was nonetheless convicted, as expected, and fined $100; the Tennessee Supreme Court later overturned the conviction on the technical ground that only a jury, not a judge, could levy a fine exceeding fifty dollars, declining Scopes’s own request for a full retrial that might have tested the law’s constitutionality on the merits.61 The Butler Act itself remained on Tennessee’s books, unenforced but unrepealed, for another forty-two years.62

The constitutional question the Scopes trial’s technicality had sidestepped was not resolved until 1968, when the U.S. Supreme Court, in Epperson v. Arkansas, struck down a similar Arkansas anti-evolution statute as an unconstitutional establishment of religion, ruling that a state could not tailor its public-school curriculum to the doctrines of any particular religious sect.63 Outright teaching bans being no longer viable, the anti-evolution movement shifted strategy toward what legal historians call the “balanced treatment” era: rather than banning evolution outright, statutes would require that “creation science” be taught alongside it, on ostensibly equal scientific footing. A federal court struck down one such Arkansas law in 1982, in McLean v. Arkansas, finding that creation science failed basic tests of scientific method — it was not testable, not falsifiable, and did not generate the kind of provisional, evidence-revisable claims that define scientific inquiry.64 The U.S. Supreme Court delivered the more consequential and lasting ruling five years later: Edwards v. Aguillard (1987) struck down a Louisiana law mandating balanced treatment of creation science, holding that its manifest purpose was to advance a particular religious viewpoint rather than to serve any secular educational goal.65

Edwards did not end the movement; it redirected it. Over the following decade, creationist advocates, working chiefly through the Seattle-based Discovery Institute, developed “intelligent design” — the argument that certain biological structures exhibit “irreducible complexity” too intricate to have arisen through gradual, unguided natural selection, and therefore imply, without specifying, an unnamed intelligent designer. Proponents were explicit, in internal strategy documents that later surfaced in litigation, that this was a deliberate rebranding intended to survive the constitutional bar Edwards had erected against explicitly religious “creation science.”

Specimen Note — Kitzmiller v. Dover

When the Dover, Pennsylvania school board mandated that a statement questioning evolution and recommending the intelligent-design textbook Of Pandas and People be read to ninth-grade biology students, eleven parents sued. The resulting six-week bench trial before Judge John E. Jones III — a Republican appointee with no evident stake in the outcome — produced a 139-page ruling, issued 20 December 2005, finding that intelligent design is not science but a direct descendant of earlier creationism, unconstitutional to teach as such in a public-school science class.66 All eight school-board members who had supported the policy and stood for re-election were voted out four days before the ruling was even issued.67

Kitzmiller v. Dover Area School District, decided in December 2005, remains the most thorough judicial examination of intelligent design’s scientific and religious status to date. Judge Jones traced the doctrine’s conceptual and even textual lineage directly back to the “creation science” Edwards had already barred — drafts of Of Pandas and People, obtained through discovery, showed the term “creationists” mechanically replaced with “design proponents” partway through editing, after Edwards was decided, leaving behind at least one telltale half-edited transitional phrase.68 The ruling carries the authority of a single federal district court rather than the Supreme Court, and remains binding precedent only within its own jurisdiction; it has not been appealed further, in part because the school board that lost the case, having already been replaced by voters opposed to the policy, declined to pursue it. Its persuasive force, however, has been considerable, and no public school district in the United States has attempted a comparable mandate in the two decades since.

Catalogue XPresent Day

Where Things Stand: Consensus, Belief, and the Persisting Fault Line

Among biologists, the matter has been closed for decades. Among the American public, it has never quite closed at all.

Within professional biology, natural selection’s status is not seriously contested and has not been for the better part of a century. The United States National Academy of Sciences, in a lengthy 2008 report prepared jointly with the Institute of Medicine, described evolution as “one of the most robust and widely accepted principles of modern science,” supported by converging, independent lines of evidence from paleontology, comparative anatomy, embryology, biogeography, and, increasingly since the mid-twentieth century, molecular genetics.69 The American Association for the Advancement of Science, the largest general scientific society in the world, has issued its own board resolutions affirming evolutionary theory’s standing and explicitly opposing the introduction of intelligent design into public-school science curricula as a matter of educational policy.70 This is not the language of a live scientific dispute; it is the language a field uses to describe its settled foundations.

American public opinion tells a substantially different story, and the persistence of the gap between scientific and popular consensus is itself one of the more sociologically interesting features of the whole controversy. Gallup has tracked American beliefs about human origins, using a consistent three-option question, since 1982, and the long trend line shows real, if gradual, movement: belief in strict biblical creationism — that God created humans in their present form within roughly the last ten thousand years — and belief in God-guided evolution both peaked in 1999, at 47 and 40 percent of American adults respectively, and have trended downward since, while the proportion of Americans accepting evolution with no divine involvement at all has climbed from roughly 9 percent in 1982 to a record 24 percent in Gallup’s most recent 2024 survey.71

Put the two halves of this picture side by side and an arresting asymmetry appears: as of 2024, a clear majority of American adults — the 34 percent who accept God-guided evolution plus the 24 percent who accept evolution without divine involvement — affirm that humans evolved in some form, yet a plurality still describes strict biblical creationism, involving no evolutionary process of any kind, as the single view closest to their own.74 The correlates of this divide are well documented and largely unsurprising: belief in creationism rises sharply with frequency of religious attendance, with conservative political identification, and falls sharply with educational attainment, particularly among those with postgraduate degrees.75 None of these correlations, it bears emphasizing, tracks scientific literacy in any simple way; the gap is a gap in religious and cultural commitment as much as, or more than, a gap in comprehension of the biological evidence.

That gap continues to surface in concrete policy fights, even though the courtroom battles of the Scopes-to-Kitzmiller era are largely over. Every state in the country now formally includes evolution in its official public-school science standards, and no state teaches intelligent design or creationism as science; the legal question, in the narrow sense litigated in Epperson, Edwards, and Kitzmiller, has been settled.76 What persists instead, according to the National Center for Science Education’s own 2025 assessment, is a quieter, more diffuse erosion at the local and state level — softened textbook language, contested standards wording, and school-board pressure that rarely rises to the level of a clean constitutional test case, making the older legal precedents harder to invoke.77 Iowa’s state board of education, revising its K–12 science standards in early 2025, drew public criticism for replacing the word “evolution” with the phrase “biological change over time” throughout the draft document — the kind of low-visibility, terminology-level dispute that has increasingly replaced the dramatic courtroom confrontations of the twentieth century.78 A 2025 legal-education review found that legislative proposals touching evolution instruction, framed in the vocabulary of “academic freedom” or “critical thinking” rather than explicit creationism, have continued to appear in state legislatures over the past two decades, changing shape even as their underlying target remains the same.79

Even outside the classroom, the underlying religious-liberty architecture that decided Edwards and Kitzmiller continues to be tested: in 2025, the U.S. Supreme Court deadlocked 4–4 on whether Oklahoma could fund an explicitly religious charter school, a case that touched, among other establishment-clause questions, on the scope of a state’s authority to require evolution instruction in schools it funds — a reminder that the constitutional settlement, while durable, is not automatically self-renewing.80 Economic research adds a further, practical dimension to the stakes: a 2024 study in the Quarterly Journal of Economics, exploiting staggered state-level reforms to evolution curricula, found that expanded evolution coverage in high school measurably increased not only students’ factual knowledge of the subject but their acceptance of it in adulthood, evidence that curricular choices made in a single ninth-grade classroom continue to shape a population’s beliefs decades later.81 In July 2025, at the Scopes trial’s hundredth anniversary, scholars and science educators gathered at Vanderbilt University’s Evolutionary Studies Initiative not to declare victory but to take stock of exactly this pattern: a scientific question settled beyond reasonable dispute, and a public conversation about it that, a full century after Dayton, Tennessee, has not yet finished.77

Catalogue XIClosing Entry

Conclusion: What Explains, and What Endures

The biology stopped being a live scientific question a long time ago. The rest of the argument was never really about the biology.

One hundred and sixty-eight years ago today, a paper nobody in the room fully understood the significance of was read to a half-attentive learned society on a summer evening in London, while its principal author buried his infant son forty miles away. Measured purely as a scientific proposition, the theory that paper introduced has had about as complete a vindication as any idea in the history of the natural sciences: confirmed independently by paleontology, comparative anatomy, biogeography, and a molecular record Darwin could not have imagined, extended by a genetics he never possessed into a modern evolutionary synthesis that has itself been refined, tested, and repeatedly reconfirmed for the better part of a century. There is no live scientific controversy over whether natural selection occurs, or whether humans share common ancestry with every other branch on the tree of life. That question closed a long time ago, and it closed in natural selection’s favor.

What has not closed, and on the evidence of this dossier shows no sign of closing soon, is the separate cultural argument the theory triggered on contact — and it is worth being honest, at the end, about why that argument has proven so much more durable than the scientific one. It is not, chiefly, a dispute over evidence; the evidence has been available, and has been getting steadily stronger, for well over a century. It is a dispute over what a purposeless, undirected biological process is taken to mean for questions natural selection was never built to answer: whether human life has intrinsic significance, whether morality requires a transcendent guarantor, whether the universe is the kind of place that has a reason for containing anyone in particular. Darwin’s theory speaks to none of these questions directly, and thoughtful people on every side of them — the accommodating theologian, the confident secularist, the honest agnostic who finds the whole issue less settled than either camp claims — have found ways to hold a scientific acceptance of evolution alongside wildly different answers to the questions that actually generate the heat.

That, in the end, may be the most useful thing a careful history of this controversy can offer: not a verdict on the metaphysical questions, which remain, appropriately, outside natural selection’s jurisdiction, but a clearer sense of where the biological argument ends and the human one begins. Common descent by natural selection explains, with extraordinary and still-accumulating precision, how the diversity and apparent design of life arose without a designer. It does not, and was never going to, settle what a person ought to do with that fact once they have accepted it. Wallace, feverish on Ternate, and Darwin, grieving in Kent, handed the world a mechanism. What the world has done with it since — the pulpits and the courtrooms, the eugenics tracts and the Vanderbilt centennial panels, the Iowa school-board meeting and the Gallup interviewer’s clipboard — has been, for a hundred and sixty-eight years and counting, entirely up to us.
