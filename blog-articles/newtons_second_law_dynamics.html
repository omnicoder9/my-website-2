01Historical Background

The equation \(m\ddot{x} + c\dot{x} + kx = F(t)\) is one of the most consequential expressions in the history of natural philosophy. Its lineage traces to Isaac Newton's Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica (1687), in which Newton articulated the three laws of motion that overturned the Aristotelian world view and established the quantitative science of mechanics.[1]

Newton's second law in its original form — the rate of change of momentum of a body is proportional to the net force applied — is a vector statement about linear momentum. Its modern scalar form \(F = ma\) emerges directly from this principle when mass is constant.[2] However, the rich structure of the damped, driven oscillator required contributions from many subsequent workers.

"Every body perseveres in its state of being at rest or of moving uniformly straight forward, except insofar as it is compelled to change its state by forces impressed." — Isaac Newton, Principia Mathematica, 1687 [1]

Jean le Rond d'Alembert (1743) recast Newton's law as a principle of dynamic equilibrium — introducing the concept of an inertial force \(-m\ddot{x}\) opposing acceleration — a formulation that remains pedagogically invaluable in structural dynamics today.[3] Independently, Leonhard Euler systematized the equations of motion for rigid bodies, while Joseph-Louis Lagrange (1788) provided the energy-based alternative in his Mécanique Analytique, unifying mechanics under the principle of least action and yielding equivalent equations of motion via generalized coordinates.[4]

The systematic study of oscillations and resonance accelerated in the 19th century with the work of George Gabriel Stokes (viscous damping), Lord Rayleigh (proportional damping, published in The Theory of Sound, 1877),[5] and Hermann von Helmholtz (acoustic resonance). The concept of a frequency response function and the modern transfer-function approach were formalized during the 20th century, especially through the wartime development of control theory by Bode, Nyquist, and Wiener.[6]

02Derivation from First Principles

2.1 Newton's Second Law — Vector Form

For a particle of mass \(m\) subject to a net force \(\mathbf{F}_\text{net}\), Newton's second law in its momentum form is:[2]

Newton's Second Law (Momentum Form)
\[ \mathbf{F}_\text{net} = \frac{d\mathbf{p}}{dt} = \frac{d(m\dot{\mathbf{x}})}{dt} \]

For constant mass \(m\), this reduces to the familiar acceleration form:

\[ \mathbf{F}_\text{net} = m\ddot{\mathbf{x}} \]

2.2 Free Body Diagram of the SDOF Oscillator

Consider a single-degree-of-freedom (SDOF) lumped-parameter model: a mass \(m\) connected to a fixed wall through a linear spring (stiffness \(k\)) and a viscous dashpot (damping coefficient \(c\)), subject to an external time-varying force \(F(t)\). The mass is constrained to move only along one axis, \(x\).[7]

The individual forces acting on the mass at displacement \(x\), velocity \(\dot{x}\), are:

  • Spring restoring force: \(f_k = -kx\) (opposes displacement, Hooke's Law)
  • Viscous damping force: \(f_c = -c\dot{x}\) (opposes velocity)
  • External forcing: \(F(t)\) (applied to mass)

2.3 Applying Newton's Second Law

Summing forces in the \(x\)-direction and setting equal to \(m\ddot{x}\):

\[ m\ddot{x} = -kx - c\dot{x} + F(t) \]

Rearranging into standard form:

Equation of Motion — Standard Form
\[ \boxed{m\ddot{x} + c\dot{x} + kx = F(t)} \]

This is a second-order, linear, non-homogeneous ordinary differential equation with constant coefficients. Its complete solution is the sum of the homogeneous (free vibration) solution and a particular (forced vibration) solution.[8]

2.4 Lagrangian Derivation (Energy Method)

Equivalently, one may employ Lagrange's equation. Define the Lagrangian \(\mathcal{L} = T - V\), where \(T\) is kinetic energy and \(V\) is potential energy, and the Rayleigh dissipation function \(\mathcal{D}\):[4]

\[ T = \tfrac{1}{2}m\dot{x}^2, \quad V = \tfrac{1}{2}kx^2, \quad \mathcal{D} = \tfrac{1}{2}c\dot{x}^2 \]

Lagrange's equation with dissipation and generalized force \(Q = F(t)\):

\[ \frac{d}{dt}\!\left(\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \dot{x}}\right) - \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial \mathcal{D}}{\partial \dot{x}} = Q \]

Computing each term:

\[ \frac{d}{dt}(m\dot{x}) - (-kx) + c\dot{x} = F(t) \implies m\ddot{x} + c\dot{x} + kx = F(t) \quad \checkmark \]

Both Newtonian and Lagrangian approaches yield the same equation, confirming its fundamental character.[4]

2.5 Normalized (Dimensionless) Form

Dividing through by \(m\) and introducing the natural frequency \(\omega_n\) and damping ratio \(\zeta\):

Natural Frequency & Damping Ratio
\[ \omega_n = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} \quad \text{[rad/s]}, \qquad \zeta = \frac{c}{2\sqrt{km}} = \frac{c}{2m\omega_n} \]
Normalized Equation of Motion
\[ \ddot{x} + 2\zeta\omega_n\dot{x} + \omega_n^2\, x = \frac{F(t)}{m} \]

This dimensionless form is universal — it applies equally to a vibrating bridge, a car suspension, a seismic isolator, or a robotic joint, with the same mathematical structure.[7]

03Anatomy of the Equation

Each term in \(m\ddot{x} + c\dot{x} + kx = F(t)\) represents a distinct physical phenomenon:

\(m\ddot{x}\)
Inertia Term
Resistance to change in motion. \(m\) is the translational mass [kg]. \(\ddot{x}\) is acceleration [m/s²].
\(c\dot{x}\)
Damping Term
Energy dissipation via viscous drag. \(c\) is the viscous damping coefficient [N·s/m]. \(\dot{x}\) is velocity [m/s].
\(kx\)
Stiffness Term
Elastic restoring force. \(k\) is the spring constant [N/m]. \(x\) is displacement [m].
\(F(t)\)
Forcing Function
Arbitrary external force [N] as a function of time. May be harmonic, impulsive, random, or periodic.
\(\omega_n\)
Natural Frequency
\(\omega_n = \sqrt{k/m}\) [rad/s]. The frequency at which the undamped system oscillates freely.
\(\zeta\)
Damping Ratio
Dimensionless ratio \(c/(2m\omega_n)\). Controls decay rate and response amplitude at resonance.
⚙ Classification

The equation is linear (superposition holds), 2nd-order (highest derivative is \(\ddot{x}\)), non-homogeneous when \(F(t)\neq 0\), and has constant coefficients when \(m\), \(c\), \(k\) are time-invariant. These properties collectively guarantee a unique closed-form solution for given initial conditions \(x(0)\) and \(\dot{x}(0)\).[8]

04Free Vibration & Damping Regimes

Setting \(F(t) = 0\) yields the homogeneous equation governing free vibration:

\[ m\ddot{x} + c\dot{x} + kx = 0 \]

The trial solution \(x = e^{st}\) leads to the characteristic equation:[7]

Characteristic Equation
\[ ms^2 + cs + k = 0 \implies s_{1,2} = -\zeta\omega_n \pm \omega_n\sqrt{\zeta^2 - 1} \]

The nature of the roots — and therefore the system's behavior — depends entirely on the damping ratio \(\zeta\):

Undamped (\(\zeta = 0\))

Pure oscillation at natural frequency. Roots are purely imaginary: \(s = \pm j\omega_n\).

\[ x(t) = A\cos\omega_n t + B\sin\omega_n t \]
Underdamped (\(0 < \zeta < 1\))

Oscillation with exponential decay. Damped natural frequency \(\omega_d = \omega_n\sqrt{1-\zeta^2}\).

\[ x(t) = e^{-\zeta\omega_n t}(A\cos\omega_d t + B\sin\omega_d t) \]
Critically Damped (\(\zeta = 1\))

Fastest return to equilibrium without oscillation. Repeated real roots: \(s = -\omega_n\).

\[ x(t) = (A + Bt)e^{-\omega_n t} \]
Overdamped (\(\zeta > 1\))

Slow exponential return; distinct real roots. Slower recovery than critically damped.

\[ x(t) = Ae^{s_1 t} + Be^{s_2 t} \]

Constants \(A\) and \(B\) in each case are determined by initial conditions \(x(0) = x_0\) and \(\dot{x}(0) = v_0\).[8] In practice, most structural and mechanical systems are underdamped (typically \(\zeta = 0.01\)–\(0.05\) for metal structures, \(0.05\)–\(0.15\) for reinforced concrete buildings).[9]

4.1 Logarithmic Decrement

The damping ratio \(\zeta\) of an underdamped system can be extracted experimentally from free-vibration decay using the logarithmic decrement \(\delta\):[9]

\[ \delta = \ln\!\left(\frac{x_i}{x_{i+1}}\right) = \frac{2\pi\zeta}{\sqrt{1-\zeta^2}} \approx 2\pi\zeta \quad (\zeta \ll 1) \]

05Forced Vibration & Frequency Response

5.1 Harmonic Excitation

The most important case in engineering practice is harmonic forcing: \(F(t) = F_0 \sin\Omega t\) (excitation frequency \(\Omega\)). The particular solution (steady-state response) is:[7]

\[ x_p(t) = \frac{F_0/k}{\sqrt{(1-r^2)^2 + (2\zeta r)^2}} \sin(\Omega t - \phi) \]

where the frequency ratio \(r = \Omega/\omega_n\) and the phase angle is:

\[ \phi = \arctan\!\left(\frac{2\zeta r}{1 - r^2}\right) \]

The static deflection is \(x_{st} = F_0/k\). The Dynamic Amplification Factor (DAF), also called the magnification factor \(D\), is:

Dynamic Amplification Factor
\[ D(r, \zeta) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{(1-r^2)^2 + (2\zeta r)^2}} \]

5.2 Frequency Response Function (FRF)

Using complex notation \(F(t) = F_0 e^{i\Omega t}\), \(x(t) = X e^{i\Omega t}\), the receptance (displacement FRF) is:[10]

Receptance FRF
\[ H(\Omega) = \frac{X}{F_0} = \frac{1}{k - m\Omega^2 + ic\Omega} = \frac{1/k}{1 - r^2 + i(2\zeta r)} \]

The magnitude and phase of \(H(\Omega)\) fully characterize the system's response at every frequency — the foundation of experimental modal analysis.[10]

5.3 Impulse Response & Duhamel's Integral

For arbitrary \(F(t)\), the response is obtained via convolution with the impulse response function (IRF) \(h(t)\):[7]

Duhamel's (Convolution) Integral
\[ x(t) = \int_0^t F(\tau)\, h(t-\tau)\, d\tau, \qquad h(t) = \frac{1}{m\omega_d} e^{-\zeta\omega_n t}\sin\omega_d t \]

This is the time-domain analogue of multiplication in the frequency domain and is the basis for numerical transient analysis in structural software.[11]

06Resonance & Dynamic Amplification

Resonance occurs when the excitation frequency approaches the natural frequency: \(\Omega \to \omega_n\), i.e., \(r \to 1\). The DAF reaches its peak value at the resonant frequency \(\Omega_r\):[7]

\[ \Omega_r = \omega_n\sqrt{1 - 2\zeta^2}, \qquad D_\text{max} = \frac{1}{2\zeta\sqrt{1-\zeta^2}} \approx \frac{1}{2\zeta} \quad (\zeta \ll 1) \]

For lightly damped systems (e.g., \(\zeta = 0.01\)), the amplification at resonance reaches \(D_\text{max} = 50\), meaning the dynamic displacement is 50 times the static deflection. This has catastrophic consequences when not accounted for in design.[9]

The Tacoma Narrows Bridge collapse (1940) remains the most celebrated lesson in resonance engineering: aeroelastic flutter drove the bridge into resonant oscillation, ultimately destroying it within four months of opening. — Billah & Scanlan, American Journal of Physics, 1991 [12]

6.1 Quality Factor & Bandwidth

The sharpness of the resonance peak is quantified by the Quality Factor \(Q\):[6]

\[ Q = \frac{1}{2\zeta} = \frac{\omega_n}{\Delta\omega} \]

where \(\Delta\omega = \omega_2 - \omega_1\) is the half-power bandwidth (frequencies at which the amplitude equals \(1/\sqrt{2}\) of the peak value). High \(Q\) signals a lightly damped, narrowband resonance — desirable in sensors and clocks, dangerous in structures.

08Engineering Applications

The equation \(m\ddot{x} + c\dot{x} + kx = F(t)\) is not merely theoretical — it underpins the analysis and design of virtually every dynamic system in engineering. The physical interpretation of \(m\), \(c\), \(k\), and \(F(t)\) changes, but the mathematical structure is identical across all domains.[13]

  • Structural & Civil Engineering Seismic design of buildings uses multi-degree-of-freedom extensions of this equation to model story-level displacements under earthquake excitation. Response spectrum analysis is built entirely on the SDOF solution.[9] Base isolation systems (rubber bearings) reduce \(k\) and raise the natural period \(T_n = 2\pi/\omega_n\) to values safely away from earthquake predominant frequencies.
  • Vehicle Suspension Systems Quarter-car models treat sprung mass (body), spring, and damper as a direct SDOF system. Ride comfort requires low \(\omega_n\) (soft spring, large suspension travel); road-holding requires high damping near resonance. Adaptive dampers continuously tune \(c\) via magneto-rheological fluid.[14]
  • Offshore & Marine Structures Wave loading on offshore platforms, risers, and moorings is represented as a stochastic \(F(t)\). The Morison equation provides \(F(t)\) in terms of wave kinematics; SDOF or MDOF models yield dynamic stress spectra used in fatigue life predictions.[15]
  • Robotics & Mechatronics Each joint of a robotic manipulator can be modeled as a rotational SDOF: \(I\ddot{\theta} + b\dot{\theta} + \kappa\theta = \tau(t)\), where \(I\) is inertia, \(b\) is joint friction, \(\kappa\) is torsional stiffness, and \(\tau\) is actuator torque. PD control laws effectively prescribe \(b\) and \(\kappa\) electronically.[16]
  • Aerospace & Aeroelasticity Flutter analysis couples aerodynamic forces with structural dynamics. At the flutter speed, the aerodynamic damping exactly cancels structural damping (\(c_\text{eff} = 0\)), producing sustained oscillation. The governing equations are extensions of SDOF concepts to multi-mode coupled systems.[17]
  • Electrical Analogies (RLC Circuits) By analogy, the series RLC circuit obeys \(L\ddot{q} + R\dot{q} + q/C = V(t)\), where charge \(q\) ↔ displacement \(x\), inductance \(L\) ↔ mass \(m\), resistance \(R\) ↔ damping \(c\), and capacitance \(C^{-1}\) ↔ stiffness \(k\). Every result derived for the mechanical oscillator applies identically to circuit analysis.[6]
  • Biomechanics Human gait, bone mechanics under cyclic loading, and inner-ear hair-cell dynamics are all modeled with viscoelastic SDOF or coupled oscillator frameworks. Cochlear mechanics, for example, relies on a continuous distribution of resonant SDOF oscillators tuned to different frequencies along the basilar membrane.[18]
  • MEMS & Nanoscale Sensors Micro-electromechanical systems (accelerometers, gyroscopes, atomic force microscope cantilevers) operate as miniaturized SDOF oscillators. Quality factor \(Q\) determines sensor sensitivity and bandwidth. Squeeze-film damping provides the dominant dissipation mechanism at micro-scale.[19]

09Worked Examples

Example 1 — Underdamped Free Vibration

Problem: A mass–spring–dashpot system has \(m = 10\,\text{kg}\), \(k = 4000\,\text{N/m}\), \(c = 40\,\text{N·s/m}\). Initial conditions: \(x(0) = 0.05\,\text{m}\), \(\dot{x}(0) = 0\). Find the response \(x(t)\).

Solution:

\[ \omega_n = \sqrt{\frac{4000}{10}} = 20\,\text{rad/s}, \quad c_c = 2m\omega_n = 400\,\text{N·s/m}, \quad \zeta = \frac{40}{400} = 0.10 \]
\[ \omega_d = 20\sqrt{1 - 0.01} = 19.90\,\text{rad/s} \]
\[ x(t) = 0.05\, e^{-2t}\!\left(\cos 19.90t + \frac{2}{19.90}\sin 19.90t\right)\,\text{m} \]

The system oscillates at \(f_d = 19.90/(2\pi) \approx 3.17\,\text{Hz}\) with an exponential envelope that decays to \(1/e\) of its initial amplitude in \(t_{1/e} = 1/(\zeta\omega_n) = 0.5\,\text{s}\).

Example 2 — Steady-State Response to Harmonic Loading

Problem: Using the same system, apply \(F(t) = 500\sin(18t)\,\text{N}\). Find the steady-state amplitude and phase.

\[ r = \frac{\Omega}{\omega_n} = \frac{18}{20} = 0.90 \]
\[ X_{st} = \frac{F_0}{k} = \frac{500}{4000} = 0.125\,\text{m} \]
\[ D = \frac{1}{\sqrt{(1-0.81)^2 + (2 \times 0.10 \times 0.90)^2}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{0.0361 + 0.0324}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{0.0685}} \approx 3.82 \]
\[ X = D \cdot X_{st} = 3.82 \times 0.125 = \mathbf{0.478\,\text{m}}, \qquad \phi = \arctan\!\left(\frac{2(0.10)(0.90)}{1-0.81}\right) = \arctan(0.947) \approx 43.5° \]

The dynamic amplitude is 3.82× the static deflection, and the response lags the force by \(43.5°\).

Example 3 — Critical Damping (Door Closer Design)

Problem: A pneumatic door closer must return a door (\(m = 30\,\text{kg}\), equivalent \(k = 270\,\text{N/m}\)) to closed without oscillation as quickly as possible. Find the required damping coefficient.

\[ \omega_n = \sqrt{\frac{270}{30}} = 3\,\text{rad/s} \]
\[ c_c = 2m\omega_n = 2 \times 30 \times 3 = \mathbf{180\,\text{N·s/m}} \]

Setting \(c = 180\,\text{N·s/m}\) achieves critical damping (\(\zeta = 1\)): the door returns to closed in the minimum time without bouncing. Any higher \(c\) (overdamped) returns it more slowly; any lower (underdamped) causes it to oscillate.

10Computational Tools & Source Code

Numerical solution of \(m\ddot{x} + c\dot{x} + kx = F(t)\) is achieved by converting the second-order ODE into a system of two first-order ODEs, then integrating with a Runge–Kutta or similar solver. Defining state vector \(\mathbf{y} = [x,\, \dot{x}]^T\):[20]

\[ \dot{\mathbf{y}} = \begin{bmatrix}\dot{x}\\ \ddot{x}\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} y_2 \\ \dfrac{F(t) - cy_2 - ky_1}{m} \end{bmatrix} \]

Python — SciPy ODE Solver

PYTHONNumerical SDOF Response via scipy.integrate.solve_ivp
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy.integrate import solve_ivp

# ── System parameters ──────────────────────────────────────────
m   = 10.0    # mass [kg]
k   = 4000.0  # stiffness [N/m]
c   = 40.0    # damping coefficient [N·s/m]
F0  = 500.0   # forcing amplitude [N]
Omega = 18.0 # excitation frequency [rad/s]

# ── State-space formulation: y = [x, x_dot] ────────────────────
def sdof_ode(t, y, m, c, k, F0, Omega):
    x, xdot = y
    F = F0 * np.sin(Omega * t)          # harmonic forcing
    xddot = (F - c*xdot - k*x) / m     # Newton's 2nd Law
    return [xdot, xddot]

# ── Initial conditions and time span ───────────────────────────
y0   = [0.05, 0.0]          # x(0)=0.05 m, v(0)=0 m/s
t_span = (0, 5)             # 0 to 5 seconds
t_eval = np.linspace(0, 5, 2000)

# ── Solve with Runge–Kutta 4/5 (RK45) ─────────────────────────
sol = solve_ivp(
    sdof_ode, t_span, y0,
    args=(m, c, k, F0, Omega),
    method='RK45',
    t_eval=t_eval,
    rtol=1e-8, atol=1e-10
)

# ── Derived quantities ─────────────────────────────────────────
omega_n = np.sqrt(k / m)
zeta    = c / (2 * m * omega_n)
omega_d = omega_n * np.sqrt(1 - zeta**2)
print(f"ωₙ = {omega_n:.2f} rad/s | ζ = {zeta:.4f} | ωd = {omega_d:.2f} rad/s")

# ── Frequency Response Function (DAF sweep) ───────────────────
r   = np.linspace(0, 2.5, 500)
DAF = 1 / np.sqrt((1 - r**2)**2 + (2*zeta*r)**2)

# ── Plot ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
fig, (ax1, ax2) = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(12, 4))

ax1.plot(sol.t, sol.y[0], color='#b5451b', lw=1.5)
ax1.set_xlabel('Time [s]'); ax1.set_ylabel('Displacement x(t) [m]')
ax1.set_title('Transient + Steady-State Response'); ax1.grid(True, alpha=0.3)

ax2.plot(r, DAF, color='#1a2e40', lw=2)
ax2.axvline(x=1, color='#b5451b', ls='--', lw=1, label='r = 1 (resonance)')
ax2.set_xlabel('Frequency ratio r'); ax2.set_ylabel('D(r, ζ)')
ax2.set_title('Dynamic Amplification Factor'); ax2.legend(); ax2.grid(True, alpha=0.3)

plt.tight_layout()
plt.savefig('sdof_response.png', dpi=150)
plt.show()

MATLAB — ODE45 Solution

MATLABSDOF Transient Response with ode45
% System parameters
m = 10; k = 4000; c = 40; F0 = 500; Omega = 18;

% ODE function handle (state-space form)
F = @(t, y) [y(2); (F0*sin(Omega*t) - c*y(2) - k*y(1)) / m];

% Solve using ode45 (Dormand–Prince RK4/5)
[t, y] = ode45(F, [0 5], [0.05; 0]);

% Plot displacement response
plot(t, y(:,1), 'LineWidth', 1.5, 'Color', [0.71 0.27 0.11]);
xlabel('Time [s]'); ylabel('x(t) [m]');
title('SDOF Response — Newton''s 2nd Law');
grid on;

% Natural frequency and damping ratio
omega_n = sqrt(k/m);
zeta    = c / (2 * sqrt(k*m));
fprintf('omega_n = %.2f rad/s, zeta = %.4f\n', omega_n, zeta);

11Key Concepts Glossary

TermSymbol / FormulaDefinition
Natural frequency \(\omega_n = \sqrt{k/m}\) Frequency of undamped free oscillation [rad/s]. Equivalent in Hz: \(f_n = \omega_n/(2\pi)\).
Damping ratio \(\zeta = c/(2m\omega_n)\) Dimensionless measure of energy dissipation relative to critical damping.
Critical damping \(c_c = 2\sqrt{km}\) Minimum damping for aperiodic (non-oscillatory) return to rest.
Damped natural frequency \(\omega_d = \omega_n\sqrt{1-\zeta^2}\) Actual oscillation frequency of underdamped free response.
Frequency ratio \(r = \Omega/\omega_n\) Ratio of excitation frequency to natural frequency. Resonance at \(r = 1\).
DAF / Magnification factor \(D = [(1-r^2)^2+(2\zeta r)^2]^{-1/2}\) Ratio of dynamic to static displacement amplitude under harmonic forcing.
Quality factor \(Q = 1/(2\zeta)\) Inverse of loss factor; measures resonance sharpness.
Phase angle \(\phi = \arctan[2\zeta r/(1-r^2)]\) Phase lag between response and forcing. Reaches \(90°\) at \(r=1\) for any \(\zeta\).
Impulse response (IRF) \(h(t) = e^{-\zeta\omega_n t}\sin(\omega_d t)/(m\omega_d)\) Response to a unit impulse. Fourier transform of the FRF.
Receptance FRF \(H(\Omega) = X/F_0\) Complex transfer function relating displacement output to force input.
Logarithmic decrement \(\delta = 2\pi\zeta/\sqrt{1-\zeta^2}\) Natural log of ratio of successive free vibration amplitudes; used to measure \(\zeta\) experimentally.
D'Alembert's principle \(\sum F + (-m\ddot{x}) = 0\) Reformulates dynamics as static equilibrium by introducing the inertial "pseudo-force" \(-m\ddot{x}\).
