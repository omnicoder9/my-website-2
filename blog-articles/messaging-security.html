Abstract

End-to-end encrypted messaging has evolved from an activist-adjacent fringe tool into infrastructure carrying billions of messages per day. That journey is inseparable from a sequence of cryptographic innovations: the transition from PGP's static key model to the ephemeral Diffie–Hellman machinery of OTR, the invention of the Double Ratchet algorithm that gave the Signal Protocol both forward secrecy and post-compromise security simultaneously, and the ongoing standardisation of the Messaging Layer Security protocol that extends these properties to large groups with logarithmic communication overhead. This article offers a rigorous, practitioner-level treatment of the full stack: threat modelling, protocol mechanics, group key agreement, metadata leakage, and key verification—including the deployment of key transparency systems that audit identity bindings in append-only cryptographic logs. Code-level pseudocode, formal definitions, and comparative analysis across Signal, WhatsApp, iMessage, Telegram, and the emerging MLS ecosystem are provided throughout.

Section 01

Historical Context: From PGP to the Signal Era

The history of messaging security is a compressed chronicle of cryptography becoming infrastructure—a process that took roughly three decades and required the coincidence of surveillance revelations, academic cryptography, and an unusual alignment between activist culture and mass-market software engineering. Understanding where the Double Ratchet came from, and why the Messaging Layer Security standard exists at all, requires understanding the sequence of problems that each generation of tools failed to solve.

The PGP Era and Its Structural Limitations

Phil Zimmermann released PGP (Pretty Good Privacy) in 1991, originally distributed on Usenet as a political act in response to proposed US Senate legislation mandating backdoors in encryption software.[1] PGP used RSA for key exchange and IDEA for symmetric encryption, later extending to a suite of algorithms. Its trust model—the web of trust, in which users sign each other's public keys to assert identity—was philosophically coherent but practically catastrophic for usability. For the next two decades, PGP adoption remained effectively zero among non-expert users, not because the cryptography was weak but because the key management model demanded expert participation at every step.[1]

More critically, PGP had no answer to two properties that would later become definitional: forward secrecy and deniability. A static RSA key pair encrypted every past message to that identity. Compromise of a long-term private key retroactively decrypted all stored ciphertext. And PGP-signed messages were cryptographically authenticated in a way that was non-repudiable—the digital signature was a binding legal artifact, which is exactly the opposite of what most human communication requires.

OTR: The Cryptographic Foundation

The conceptual breakthrough arrived in 2004 when Nikita Borisov, Ian Goldberg, and Eric Brewer published "Off-the-Record Communication, or, Why Not to Use PGP."[2] OTR (Off-the-Record Messaging) introduced four properties that directly addressed PGP's failures:

OTR Security Properties (Borisov, Goldberg & Brewer, 2004)

Encryption: No eavesdropper can read message content, even with access to long-term keys after the session (forward secrecy via ephemeral Diffie–Hellman).

Authentication: The correspondent is who they claim to be during the conversation.

Deniability: After the session, any party can forge transcripts indistinguishable from genuine ones. MAC keys are published after use, enabling fabrication of any message attributed to either party.

Perfect Forward Secrecy: Compromise of long-term keys does not compromise session keys from past conversations.

OTR achieved forward secrecy using ephemeral Diffie–Hellman: a new key pair was generated for each session, and the private key was deleted after key derivation. Authentication used a combination of long-term DSA keys and the ephemeral DH exchange. Deniability was achieved by using MAC authentication (rather than digital signatures) and then disclosing the MAC key after each message was delivered—at which point any party could forge MACs on any message attributed to either correspondent.[2]

OTR became widely deployed in XMPP clients. But it had a critical limitation for the emerging mobile era: it required both parties to be simultaneously online to complete the key exchange. It had no mechanism for asynchronous session establishment, and it had no healing mechanism—if a session key was compromised mid-conversation, all subsequent messages in that session were compromised too.

The Snowden Inflection and the Axolotl Ratchet

Moxie Marlinspike founded Whisper Systems in 2010, releasing TextSecure (encrypted SMS for Android) and RedPhone (encrypted voice calls). Twitter acquired the company in 2011, and Marlinspike later left to work independently on what would become Signal.[3] The Edward Snowden revelations of June 2013—which revealed the NSA's PRISM program, MUSCULAR cable-tap operations, and the breadth of Five Eyes mass surveillance—transformed demand for strong end-to-end encryption from a niche concern to a mainstream one essentially overnight.

The critical technical innovation of the 2013 period was what Marlinspike and Trevor Perrin called the Axolotl Ratchet—a protocol that solved OTR's two remaining deficiencies simultaneously. By combining a symmetric KDF chain with a Diffie–Hellman ratchet layer, the protocol achieved forward secrecy on a per-message granularity and a self-healing property (later formalised as post-compromise security) that OTR had never attempted. The mechanism for asynchronous session bootstrapping—X3DH, the Extended Triple Diffie–Hellman protocol—solved the synchronous session establishment requirement by allowing one party to deposit prekey material on a server, enabling the other to initiate a session at any time without the first party being online.[4]

Section 02

End-to-End Encryption Threat Model

A secure system cannot be designed without an explicit adversary model. End-to-end encryption is not a monolithic guarantee; it is a family of claims about what different classes of adversary can and cannot learn from a messaging system. Conflating these classes—or designing without enumerating them—produces systems with misleading security labels, as illustrated by Telegram's "secret chats" marketing or the gap between iMessage's E2EE claims and iCloud backup exposure.

Adversary Taxonomy

Class 1: Global Passive Adversary (GPA)

The GPA model—originating in the mix-network literature—assumes an adversary who can observe all traffic on all links globally but who cannot inject or modify packets. NSA MUSCULAR, which tapped inter-datacenter Google and Yahoo links, is an archetypal real-world GPA.[5] Against a GPA, properly implemented E2EE renders message content opaque, but metadata—source and destination IP addresses, timing, message sizes, and connection patterns—remains visible. The GPA can build detailed social graphs and infer communication content through traffic analysis even without decrypting a single byte.

Class 2: Global Active Adversary

Extends the GPA with the ability to modify, inject, replay, and selectively drop packets. Against this model, authentication of message content (via AEAD) and authentication of key material (via identity key signatures) are essential. The Double Ratchet provides both. However, an active adversary who controls the key distribution server can attempt a man-in-the-middle attack during initial session setup—which is precisely why key verification mechanisms (Section 9) matter.

Class 3: Compromised Server Adversary

The service provider itself is malicious, coerced, or breached. This is the adversary that E2EE was most directly designed to defeat. A compromised Signal server can observe: which accounts exist, who sends messages to whom (though Sealed Sender limits this—see Section 8), message sizes and timing, and prekey bundles. It cannot read message content, cannot forge messages accepted as authentic by recipients, and cannot retroactively decrypt past messages if forward secrecy was maintained. WhatsApp's 2021 privacy policy controversy illustrated why metadata access by a compromised server remains sensitive even when content is protected.

Class 4: Legal Process Adversary

Compelled disclosure under a court order, national security letter, or equivalent legal instrument. The Signal Foundation has repeatedly published their full responses to legal demands, which contain only account registration date and last-connection timestamp—because that is all the data Signal retains.[6] WhatsApp retains substantially more metadata. This class of adversary motivates minimal-retention architectures and the design of systems that are structurally incapable of producing data, even under compulsion.

Class 5: Compromised Endpoint

The device running the messaging application contains malware, a zero-day exploit, or has been physically seized. Against a compromised endpoint, E2EE provides no protection whatsoever: the attacker reads plaintext from memory or from the screen. This is the most powerful adversary in practice, and it motivates screen security, device encryption, and—in high-threat environments—the use of dedicated communication devices. It is the foundation of the common observation that the weakest point in the Signal chain is the phone, not the protocol.

Unger et al. provide the first systematic "SoK" (Systematization of Knowledge) treatment of secure messaging, defining several security properties that a messaging system can aspire to.[7] The critical taxonomy distinguishes:

What E2EE Does Not Protect

Practitioners must be explicit about the boundaries of E2EE guarantees. The following attack surfaces are explicitly outside the E2EE threat boundary:

Metadata. Communication graphs are at least as valuable as content for intelligence purposes. The NSA's "three-hop" analysis rules allowed surveillance of individuals up to three social-network connections from a surveillance target, meaning that the content of targeted messages was often irrelevant—the graph was the product. Sealed Sender (Section 8) partially addresses server-visible sender identity, but does not address traffic analysis at the network layer.

Endpoint compromise. Pegasus (NSO Group), Predator (Intellexa), and FORCEDENTRY (NSA) represent the state of the art in zero-click mobile exploitation. Against an attacker who has code execution on the target device, no messaging protocol provides protection. The architectural response is minimisation of attack surface (GrapheneOS, hardened configurations) and compartmentalisation, not cryptographic protocol design.

Backup exposure. iCloud backups of iMessage are not end-to-end encrypted by default, meaning that Apple can and does respond to legal requests with full message archives. The introduction of Advanced Data Protection in iOS 16.2 extends E2EE to iCloud backups for users who opt in.[8] WhatsApp's Google Drive and iCloud backups were similarly unencrypted until 2021 when end-to-end encrypted backups were introduced.

Screen and notification leakage. Notification previews visible on lock screens, screenshots by recipients, and ambient visual surveillance defeat E2EE at the presentation layer. Protocol design cannot address human factors or physical security.

The boundary of E2EE is precisely defined: it protects message content from network observers and from the service operator. Everything outside that boundary—metadata, endpoints, backups, and human factors—requires independent security analysis.

Section 03

The Signal Protocol: Double Ratchet Algorithm

The Double Ratchet algorithm, specified by Marlinspike and Perrin in 2016 (after several years of deployment as the "Axolotl Ratchet"), is the cryptographic core of the Signal Protocol and therefore of every application that has adopted it: Signal, WhatsApp, Facebook Messenger Secret Conversations, Skype Private Conversations, and others.[3] The algorithm solves a precise problem: how to maintain a shared secret between two parties across an asynchronous, potentially unreliable channel, such that each individual message has forward secrecy and such that compromise of the current state does not permanently compromise future messages.

The Two Ratchet Components

Component 1: The KDF Chain (Symmetric Ratchet)

A KDF (Key Derivation Function) chain is a one-way sequence of key derivations using HMAC-SHA-256 or HKDF-SHA-256. Given a chain key CK, the chain advances by computing:

CKnext ← HMAC-SHA256(CK, 0x02)
MK       ← HMAC-SHA256(CK, 0x01)

Where MK is the message key used to encrypt a single message, and CK_next replaces CK in the state. After MK is used, it is deleted. Because HMAC is a pseudorandom function, an adversary who obtains MK cannot compute CK, CK_next, or any future message key. This is forward secrecy at the intra-session level. The KDF chain advances synchronously: each send or receive advances the chain by one step, generating one message key.

Component 2: The DH Ratchet (Public-Key Ratchet)

The DH ratchet addresses a limitation of the KDF chain alone: if the chain key CK is compromised at some point, all future message keys derived from it are also compromised, because the KDF chain is deterministic given the chain key. The DH ratchet injects fresh entropy by incorporating a new Diffie–Hellman exchange whenever the ratchet advances.[3]

Each party maintains a ratchet key pair: a current Diffie–Hellman key pair (DHs_priv, DHs_pub). Each message header includes the sender's current ratchet public key. When Alice receives a message from Bob with a new ratchet public key DHr_new that differs from the one she last stored, she:

DH Ratchet Step (Pseudocode)
// Alice receives message with Bob's new ratchet public key DHr_new
if DHr_new ≠ state.DHr:
    // Step 1: Derive new receiving chain key
    dh_output_recv = DH(state.DHs_priv, DHr_new)
    (state.RK, state.CKr) = KDF_RK(state.RK, dh_output_recv)

    // Step 2: Generate new sending ratchet key pair
    state.DHs_priv, state.DHs_pub = GENERATE_DH()
    state.DHr = DHr_new

    // Step 3: Derive new sending chain key
    dh_output_send = DH(state.DHs_priv, DHr_new)
    (state.RK, state.CKs) = KDF_RK(state.RK, dh_output_send)

Where KDF_RK is implemented as HKDF-SHA-256 using the root key RK as salt and the DH output as input key material, producing a new root key and a new chain key. The DH ratchet step is computationally heavier than the KDF step (a Curve25519 scalar multiplication vs. an HMAC invocation), but it occurs only once per conversation "round"—each time the other party sends after a gap, rather than on every single message.

Full State Machine

The complete Double Ratchet state for a session held by Alice is:

Double Ratchet State Structure
struct RatchetState {
    DHs:       Curve25519 key pair      // Alice's current ratchet key pair (send)
    DHr:       Curve25519 public key   // Bob's current ratchet public key (receive)
    RK:        [32]byte                // Root key
    CKs:       [32]byte                // Sending chain key
    CKr:       [32]byte                // Receiving chain key
    Ns:        uint32                  // Message counter for sending chain
    Nr:        uint32                  // Message counter for receiving chain
    PN:        uint32                  // Messages in previous sending chain
    MKSKIPPED: Map[DHpub × uint32, [32]byte] // Skipped message keys
}

The MKSKIPPED map handles out-of-order message delivery: if message 5 arrives before messages 3 and 4, the receiving chain advances to 5, and the message keys for 3 and 4 are stored in MKSKIPPED. When 3 and 4 eventually arrive, their keys are retrieved from the map rather than re-derived. This map must be bounded in practice to prevent an adversary from causing unbounded state accumulation by sending messages with large sequence gaps.

Encryption and Authentication

Each message key MK is expanded via HKDF into an encryption key and a MAC key for AEAD encryption. The Signal Protocol specifies AES-256-CBC with HMAC-SHA256 for CBC mode, or AES-256-GCM, or ChaCha20-Poly1305 depending on implementation context. The associated data for AEAD includes both parties' identity public keys, ensuring that a message decrypted with a correct MK also authenticates the identities of the session participants.[3]

Message Encryption
func RatchetEncrypt(state, plaintext, AD) → (header, ciphertext):
    (state.CKs, mk) = KDF_CK(state.CKs)
    header = Header(state.DHs.pub, state.PN, state.Ns)
    state.Ns += 1
    enc_key, mac_key = HKDF(mk, "MessageKeys")
    ciphertext = AEAD_Encrypt(enc_key, mac_key, plaintext, AD || header)
    return (header, ciphertext)

func RatchetDecrypt(state, header, ciphertext, AD) → plaintext:
    mk = TrySkippedMessageKeys(state, header)
    if mk == nil:
        if header.dh ≠ state.DHr:
            SkipMessageKeys(state, header.pn)
            DHRatchet(state, header)
        SkipMessageKeys(state, header.n)
        (state.CKr, mk) = KDF_CK(state.CKr)
        state.Nr += 1
    return AEAD_Decrypt(mk, ciphertext, AD || header)

Cryptographic Primitives

The Signal Protocol's reference implementation uses Curve25519 for all Diffie–Hellman operations, as specified by Bernstein.[9] Curve25519 is a Montgomery curve over a 255-bit prime field, offering 128-bit security equivalent with fast, constant-time scalar multiplication and resistance to several implementation side-channel attacks that afflict NIST curves. The protocol uses HKDF-SHA-256 for key derivation, AES-256-CBC + HMAC-SHA256 or ChaCha20-Poly1305 for symmetric AEAD, and Ed25519 for identity key signatures.[10]

Formal Security Analysis

The Double Ratchet received its first rigorous formal security analysis from Cohn-Gordon, Cremers, Dowling, Garratt, and Stebila, published in the Journal of Cryptology in 2020.[11] Their analysis operates in both the symbolic (Dolev-Yao) and computational (game-based) models, proving security under the following assumptions: the Gap Diffie–Hellman assumption for Curve25519, the pseudo-randomness of HMAC-SHA256, and the IND-CCA2 security of the underlying AEAD. The modular security decomposition by Alwen, Coretti, and Dodis (EUROCRYPT 2019) further formalised the "double" structure: the ratchet is a composition of a "forward-secure pseudorandom generator" (the KDF chain) and a "post-compromise secure authenticated key exchange" (the DH ratchet), each with independent security reductions.[12]

Section 04

PreKeys, X3DH Key Agreement, and Session Setup

The Double Ratchet solves the problem of maintaining a secure channel once a shared secret is established. The orthogonal problem—how to establish that shared secret between two parties who may not be simultaneously online—is solved by the Extended Triple Diffie–Hellman (X3DH) protocol, specified by Marlinspike and Perrin.[4] X3DH enables Alice to initiate a secure session with Bob and send him an initial message, even if Bob is offline, by consuming prekey material that Bob has previously deposited on a server.

Bob's Key Bundle

Before any session can be established, Bob registers with the Signal server and uploads a key bundle. This bundle consists of four categories of keys:

Bob's Published Key Bundle
struct KeyBundle {
    // Identity key: long-term, never changes
    IK_B:    Ed25519/X25519 key pair (public key published)

    // Signed prekey: medium-term, rotated approximately weekly
    SPK_B:   X25519 key pair (public key published)
    Sig_B:   Ed25519.Sign(IK_B.priv, SPK_B.pub)   // Signature over SPK

    // One-time prekeys: ephemeral, consumed once per session
    OPK_B[]: [X25519 key pair × N]  // Batch of ~100 one-time prekeys
}

The server stores Bob's public keys. When Alice requests Bob's bundle, the server returns IK_B.pub, SPK_B.pub, Sig_B, and one OPK_B[i].pub (atomically removing it from the server's store, so each one-time prekey is used at most once). If all one-time prekeys are exhausted, the server sends the bundle without an OPK—a "fallback" that reduces security slightly but does not break the protocol.

Alice's Ephemeral Key and the Four DH Computations

Alice generates an ephemeral key pair EK_A for this session. She then verifies the signature on SPK_B using IK_B.pub, and performs four Diffie–Hellman computations:

DH1 ← X25519(IK_A.priv, SPK_B.pub)    // Alice identity × Bob signed prekey
DH2 ← X25519(EK_A.priv, IK_B.pub)    // Alice ephemeral × Bob identity
DH3 ← X25519(EK_A.priv, SPK_B.pub)    // Alice ephemeral × Bob signed prekey
DH4 ← X25519(EK_A.priv, OPK_B.pub)    // Alice ephemeral × Bob one-time prekey

SK   ← HKDF(DH1 || DH2 || DH3 || DH4, info="X3DH")

If no OPK is available, DH4 is omitted and the master secret is derived from the concatenation of the first three DH outputs. The master secret SK becomes the root key for the subsequent Double Ratchet session.

Why Four DH Operations?

The four-computation structure achieves a precise set of security properties through their combination. Each computation contributes a distinct guarantee:[4]

Security Contribution of Each X3DH DH Operation

DH1 = DH(IK_A, SPK_B): Binds Alice's long-term identity to the session. Bob's signed prekey was signed by his identity key, so Alice is authenticating to Bob's identity implicitly through this computation.

DH2 = DH(EK_A, IK_B): Binds Bob's long-term identity to the session. This computation is analogous to the "authentication" half of the SIGMA protocol by Krawczyk.[13] Together with DH1, it provides mutual implicit authentication.

DH3 = DH(EK_A, SPK_B): Provides forward secrecy: Alice's ephemeral key EK_A is deleted after session setup. Even if IK_A.priv is later compromised, DH3 cannot be reconstructed without EK_A.priv.

DH4 = DH(EK_A, OPK_B): Provides an additional forward secrecy guarantee via the one-time prekey. Because OPK_B is consumed and deleted from the server after use, DH4 cannot be reconstructed even by the server. This prevents the server from computing the session secret, providing server-side deniability.

Initial Message and Session Bootstrap

Alice sends the initial message to the server with the following structure:

X3DH Initial Message
struct InitialMessage {
    IK_A_pub:   // Alice's identity public key
    EK_A_pub:   // Alice's ephemeral public key
    OPK_ID:     // ID of the one-time prekey used (or null)
    ciphertext: AEAD_Encrypt(SK, plaintext=initial_message,
                              AD = IK_A_pub || IK_B_pub)
    // The first sending ratchet public key is also included,
    // bootstrapping the Double Ratchet initial state
    DHs_pub:    // Alice's first Double Ratchet public key
}

// Double Ratchet initial state for Alice:
state.DHs  = Alice's first ratchet key pair
state.DHr  = SPK_B.pub      // Will be replaced on Bob's first reply
state.RK   = SK
state.CKs  = KDF_RK(SK)[1]  // First sending chain key

When Bob comes online, he retrieves the initial message, recomputes DH1–DH4 using his own key material (which he has stored locally), derives the same SK, and decrypts the ciphertext. He then initialises his Double Ratchet state using DHs_pub as Alice's current ratchet public key, begins a DH ratchet step with his own generated ratchet key pair, and sends his reply. This reply includes Bob's new ratchet public key in its header, which triggers a DH ratchet step in Alice's state when received.

Prekey Management and Rotation

In practice, managing the prekey lifecycle involves several operational concerns. Signed prekeys are rotated periodically (the Signal application rotates them approximately weekly); old signed prekeys are retained briefly to allow in-flight messages to be decrypted, then deleted. One-time prekeys are generated in batches and uploaded to the server; the client application monitors remaining prekey count and replenishes when the supply drops below a threshold. The Signal server's role is purely as a keystore and message relay—it has no access to any private key material and cannot reconstruct any session secret.

Multi-device support complicates X3DH substantially: each registered device runs an independent Double Ratchet session and therefore needs its own set of prekeys. When Alice sends a message to Bob who has three registered devices, the Signal Protocol internally establishes three independent pairwise sessions (one per device) and delivers the message to each. This is a practical approximation that sacrifices some elegance but is operationally tractable.

Section 05

Forward Secrecy and Post-Compromise Security

Forward secrecy and post-compromise security are the two cardinal properties that distinguish the Double Ratchet from earlier secure messaging designs. They are complementary: forward secrecy protects the past; post-compromise security protects the future. Achieving both simultaneously with low overhead per message was the central innovation of the Axolotl/Double Ratchet design.

Forward Secrecy: Formal Definition

Forward secrecy (or Perfect Forward Secrecy, PFS) was introduced to the cryptographic literature by Günter in 1990 and formalised by Diffie, van Oorschot, and Wiener in 1992.[14] Informally: a protocol provides forward secrecy if the compromise of long-term key material does not enable an adversary to decrypt messages encrypted in past sessions. More formally, in the security game: an adversary who obtains the long-term private keys of all participants at time T should not be able to distinguish ciphertexts from sessions completed before T from random strings.

In the Double Ratchet, forward secrecy is achieved at the granularity of individual messages via the KDF chain: each message key is derived by a one-way function from the chain key, then deleted. An adversary who obtains message key MK_n cannot compute CK_n (the chain key that produced it), because HMAC is a pseudorandom function in the one-way direction. An adversary who obtains CK_n cannot compute CK_{n-1} (the chain key was advanced by an HMAC invocation in the forward direction only). The entire receiving chain is thus protected from a compromise of any point in it, for all messages prior to that point.

Post-Compromise Security: Formal Definition

Post-compromise security (PCS)—also called "break-in recovery," "future secrecy," or "self-healing"—is a strictly stronger property than forward secrecy, and one that has no analog in classical session-based protocols like TLS. The term was coined and formalised by Cohn-Gordon, Cremers, and Garratt in 2016.[15]

Informally: a protocol provides PCS if, after an adversary obtains complete session state at time T, there exists a subsequent time T' > T after which the adversary loses the ability to decrypt messages. The adversary is assumed to not control the network (preventing the DH ratchet step from completing) but does have the current state snapshot. After the DH ratchet advances—which requires a round-trip between the communicating parties—the adversary no longer has the state, because the new root key is derived from a DH output that involves freshly generated key material the adversary has never seen.

// Adversary obtains state at step n. After DH ratchet step:
dh_output ← X25519(DHs_new.priv, DHr_new)
(RK_new, CKs_new) ← KDF_RK(RK, dh_output)

// Adversary knows RK, DHs (old), DHr (old). Does not know DHs_new.priv.
// Therefore cannot compute dh_output → cannot compute RK_new or CKs_new.

The key insight is that each DH ratchet step generates a new ratchet key pair DHs_new. The adversary who captured the state at step n does not have DHs_new.priv—it was generated after the compromise—and so cannot compute the new root key even though they know DHr_new (received in the network header of the first message in the new ratchet). This is the DH ratchet's "healing" mechanism.

Comparison with TLS 1.3

TLS 1.3 (RFC 8446) achieves forward secrecy through ephemeral (EC)DHE key exchange: each TLS session negotiates a fresh ephemeral key pair, and the private key is deleted after the handshake.[16] This provides forward secrecy at the session granularity—compromise of the server's long-term certificate private key does not enable decryption of past sessions, because the session keys were derived from ephemeral material.

TLS 1.3 does not provide PCS within a session. Once the session is established, application data is encrypted with keys derived from the session's handshake secrets. There is no mechanism for injecting fresh entropy mid-session. If an adversary compromises the session key (e.g., via a side channel or hardware exploit), all subsequent traffic in that session is compromised until the connection is re-established. This is acceptable for HTTPS (sessions are short-lived) but unacceptable for long-running messaging sessions that may span months.

Practical Limitations and Edge Cases

PCS in the Double Ratchet has several practical limitations that practitioners must understand. Multi-device sessions create multiple independent Double Ratchet instances; compromise of one device's session state does not compromise others, but the adversary retains access to that device's session until it is revoked. Skipped message key accumulation in MKSKIPPED creates forward-secrecy exposure windows: if message key MK_3 is stored in the skipped keys map waiting for out-of-order delivery, it represents a window during which that key is retained in memory. Implementations should bound the number of stored skipped message keys and discard them after a timeout.

The DH ratchet step requires a round-trip—Alice must receive a message from Bob containing his new ratchet public key before she can advance the DH ratchet. In a one-sided conversation (Alice sends many messages before Bob replies), the KDF chain advances without DH ratchet steps, accumulating forward secrecy but not post-compromise security until Bob replies. In practice, this is acceptable: adversaries who compromise an active device can observe the screen; PCS is most valuable when they obtain a historic state snapshot.

Section 06

Group Messaging: Sender Keys and Scalability

Pairwise Double Ratchet sessions handle two-party communication efficiently. Group messaging introduces a categorically different scaling problem: how do n members of a group exchange encrypted messages such that each member can decrypt messages from all others, membership changes are handled correctly, and the computational and communication overhead remains tractable as n grows into the thousands?

The Naive Pairwise Approach and Its Costs

The most obvious approach—establish pairwise Double Ratchet sessions between every pair of group members and send each message n-1 times—is correct but does not scale. Sending a message to a group of n members requires n-1 AEAD encryption operations and n-1 separate transmissions. For a WhatsApp group of 1024 members, this means 1023 encryptions and 1023 separate API calls per message. State management for each member requires maintaining O(n) Double Ratchet sessions. And membership changes require O(n) rekeying operations.[17]

Early multi-party OTR attempts (mpOTR, Goldberg et al. 2009) required synchronous, round-trip-heavy key agreement that was impractical for asynchronous mobile messaging.[18] The engineering constraint was clear: any practical group E2EE protocol must approach O(1) per-message encryption complexity for the sender, with the server handling fan-out.

The Sender Key Design

Signal's solution—and WhatsApp's implementation of it—uses a design called Sender Keys, in which each member of a group maintains a single symmetric sender key for their outgoing messages. Encrypting a message requires exactly one AEAD operation, regardless of group size.[19]

Sender Key Structure
struct SenderKey {
    SenderKeyID: uint32          // Identifies this key chain
    ChainKey:    [32]byte        // Current position in sender KDF chain
    Iteration:   uint32          // Current chain iteration (message number)
    SigningKey:  Ed25519 private key  // Signs sender key messages
}

// Per-message key derivation:
MessageKey  = HMAC-SHA256(ChainKey, 0x01)
ChainKey_next = HMAC-SHA256(ChainKey, 0x02)

When Alice joins a group or creates a new sender key (after membership changes), she distributes her sender key to every other group member via a Sender Key Distribution Message (SKDM). Each SKDM is individually encrypted using the pairwise Double Ratchet session with the recipient, so the server cannot read the sender key values. The server fans out the SKDM to each group member.

Group Message Send (WhatsApp/Signal Model)
// Encrypt message body once using Alice's sender key:
(chain_key_next, msg_key) = AdvanceSenderKey(alice.sender_key)
group_ciphertext = AEAD_Encrypt(msg_key,
    plaintext = message_content,
    AD = alice.identity_pub || group_id || sender_key_id || iteration)

// Server fans out the identical ciphertext to all n-1 members.
// Each member decrypts using Alice's sender key, which they received
// via individually encrypted SKDMs during Alice's joining/re-keying.

// Communication overhead:
//   O(1) encryptions for the sender per message (one AEAD)
//   O(n) for initial SKDM distribution (pairwise encrypted, done once)
//   O(n) network fan-out (server responsibility, not client)

Security Properties and Deficiencies

The Sender Key design achieves O(1) encryption per send and is compatible with server-side fan-out. However, it has well-documented security limitations that distinguish it from pairwise Double Ratchet:[20]

No post-compromise security. The sender key chain is a symmetric KDF chain only—there is no DH ratchet layer. If Alice's current sender key chain state is compromised, all future messages from Alice in this group (until explicit re-keying) can be decrypted. The only way to achieve PCS in the sender key model is explicit re-keying: Alice generates a new sender key and distributes a new SKDM to the group, which is an O(n) operation.

Membership change handling. When a member is removed from a group, they retain knowledge of the current sender keys of all other members. Forward secrecy after removal requires that every remaining member generate a new sender key and distribute it via SKDM—an O(n²) total operation. When a member is added, they need to receive all current sender keys, which the server cannot provide (because it cannot read them), so the adding member must individually send SKDMs to the new member for each existing member's sender key, or the protocol must define a "healing" period during which old messages are inaccessible to the new member.

Ghost member vulnerability. If the server is malicious, it can add a ghost member to a group and deliver that ghost member's identity to each existing member as a new participant. The existing members will then send SKDMs to the ghost member, giving the server read access to all subsequent group messages. This attack requires server-side group state manipulation and is detected by honest clients if they verify group membership lists out-of-band.

The fundamental problem with sender keys is that they decouple the ratchet from the group membership: the symmetric chain advances irrespective of who is in the group, meaning that membership changes cannot be reflected in the cryptographic state without explicit, expensive re-keying.

— Cremers, Hale & Kohbrok, USENIX Security 2021[21]

Tree-Based Approaches: The Path to MLS

The limitation of sender keys—O(n) re-keying cost on membership changes—motivates tree-based key agreement, where the group's shared secret is derived from a binary tree of Diffie–Hellman operations. In such a tree, each leaf represents a member, each internal node holds a derived DH secret, and the root secret is the group key. A membership change only requires updating the path from the changed leaf to the root: O(log n) operations rather than O(n).

The theoretical groundwork for tree-based group key agreement predates MLS by decades (Burmester-Desmedt, 1994; TGDH, 2000), but these protocols were designed for synchronous, round-trip-intensive settings. The critical contribution of TreeKEM (Bhargavan et al., 2018)—and its standardisation in MLS—was adapting tree-based key agreement to the asynchronous, server-mediated setting of modern messaging.[22]

Section 07

Messaging Layer Security (MLS): RFC 9420

The Messaging Layer Security protocol, published as RFC 9420 by the IETF in July 2023, represents the first IETF-standardised end-to-end encryption protocol for group messaging.[23] Designed by an industry-academic working group including contributors from Cisco, Mozilla, Wire, Google, and Facebook, MLS extends the security properties of the Double Ratchet—forward secrecy and post-compromise security—to arbitrarily large groups, with communication complexity for membership changes of O(log n) rather than O(n).

TreeKEM: The Core Key Agreement Mechanism

MLS is built around TreeKEM (Tree Key Encapsulation Mechanism), a binary ratchet tree in which each leaf node represents a group member and each internal node holds a Diffie–Hellman key pair. The root node's key pair is used to derive the group's symmetric encryption keys for the current epoch—the period during which group membership and the root key are stable.[24]

TreeKEM Binary Ratchet Tree (8-member group)
                  Root (r)
                 /         \
            n4                n5
           /    \            /    \
        n0       n1       n2       n3
       /  \     /  \     /  \     /  \
     L0   L1  L2   L3  L4  L5  L6   L7
    (A)  (B) (C)  (D) (E) (F) (G)  (H)

// Each node holds: (private key, public key)
// Parent node secret derived from children:
//   node_secret(n) = DeriveSecret(DH(left_child.priv, right_child.pub))
// or equivalently:  = DeriveSecret(DH(right_child.priv, left_child.pub))

// Root secret → Group Key Material:
//   epoch_secret = KDF(root_secret, "MLS 1.0 epoch")
//   sender_data_secret, encryption_secret, etc. derived from epoch_secret

Each member holds the private keys for every node along their path from leaf to root. Member A (leaf L0) holds the private keys for L0, n0, n4, and r. Member B (leaf L1) holds L1, n0, n4, and r. Members A and B share the private key for n0 (derived from their DH exchange), members A, B, C, D share the private key for n4, and all members share the root private key.

Epochs and Group Operations

An epoch is a period of stable group membership. Messages within an epoch are encrypted with symmetric keys derived from the epoch's root secret. When group membership changes, the epoch number advances and a new root secret is computed, rekeying the group. The epoch transition is communicated via a Commit message that encodes the membership changes and the new path secrets needed for each surviving member to compute the new root.[23]

MLS Group Operations
// Add member H to group:
1. H publishes a KeyPackage (credential + HPKE public key + signature)
2. Any member creates a Proposal(Add, H.KeyPackage)
3. Any member creates a Commit bundling the Add proposal:
   - Generates new leaf key pair at H's leaf position
   - Computes new path secrets from H's leaf to root (random)
   - Encrypts path secrets to each affected node's copath sibling using HPKE
   - Creates Welcome message for H, encrypted to H.HPKE_pub, containing:
     * Encrypted group state (ratchet tree)
     * H's initial path secrets (to derive root from their position)
4. Epoch advances; new root secret derived

// Remove member F:
1. Any member creates Proposal(Remove, F)
2. Member creating Commit:
   - Blanks F's leaf (sets public key to null)
   - Generates new path secrets along committer's path to root
   - Encrypts to copath siblings (excluding F's branch, which is blanked)
   - F cannot compute new root secret → excluded from future messages
3. Epoch advances with new root secret unknown to F

The critical security property is that when member F is removed, the Commit generates new path secrets along the committer's path to root, derived from fresh randomness. F does not receive these new path secrets (they are encrypted to copath siblings, none of which F's leaf occupies), so F cannot compute the new root secret. This is the mechanism for post-compromise security after member removal: the tree structure ensures that the excluded member's knowledge becomes stale after one epoch transition.

KeyPackages and Authentication

A KeyPackage is the MLS equivalent of the X3DH prekey bundle: a self-signed document that a new member publishes to the server, enabling any existing member to add them asynchronously.[23]

MLS KeyPackage Structure
struct KeyPackage {
    version:       ProtocolVersion     // MLS version
    cipher_suite:  CipherSuite         // e.g. MLS_128_DHKEMX25519_AES128GCM_SHA256_Ed25519
    init_key:      HPKEPublicKey       // HPKE key for Welcome message encryption
    leaf_node:     LeafNode {
        encryption_key: HPKEPublicKey  // For path secret encryption to this leaf
        signature_key:  SignaturePublicKey
        credential:     Credential {   // Identity binding
            cert:  X.509 cert, or basic credential (identity + key)
        }
        capabilities:   Capabilities   // Supported ciphersuites, extensions
        leaf_node_source: KeyPackage
    }
    extensions:    [Extension]
    signature:     Sign(leaf_node.signature_key, KeyPackage content)
}

Security Properties of MLS

MLS provides a set of formally analysed security properties. The Continuous Group Key Agreement (CGKA) security model, formalised by Alwen, Coretti, and Dodis in 2020, captures the key properties of MLS in a game-based framework.[25]

Communication Complexity Comparison

The O(log n) complexity advantage of MLS over Sender Keys becomes significant at large group sizes. For a group of 1024 members, a membership change in the Sender Key model requires 1023 SKDM messages (one per remaining member). In MLS, a Commit carries O(log 1024) = 10 HPKE-encrypted path nodes, and the Commit message is broadcast once to all members. The server fan-out delivers one message to each member, so total network messages are still O(n), but the cryptographic work for the committer is O(log n).

Deployment Status and Implementations

As of mid-2026, MLS has moved from paper into production. Cisco Webex deployed MLS for end-to-end encrypted meetings, representing the largest production MLS deployment by user count. The OpenMLS Rust library provides a reference implementation maintained by the MLS working group contributors. Matrix/Element has announced MLS-based encryption via the Linearized MLS (LMLS) variant, designed for federated environments where multiple servers host different subsets of group members. Wire has an experimental MLS deployment in its enterprise product.[26]

The main operational challenge in deploying MLS is Commit ordering: unlike the Double Ratchet (where messages from different senders are independent), MLS requires that Commits be applied in a consistent order across all clients. If two members concurrently send Commits, one of them will be "rejected" by the delivery service, and the losing committer must recompute their Commit against the accepted epoch. This requires a stateful delivery service that can enforce Commit ordering—a significant operational complexity compared to the stateless fan-out in the Sender Key model.

Section 08

Metadata Protection and Sealed Sender

The canonically cited observation—variously attributed to NSA director Michael Hayden and to surveillance scholars—that "we kill people based on metadata" encapsulates a fundamental truth about communications intelligence: the content of messages is often less valuable than the graph of who communicates with whom, when, at what frequency, and from where. End-to-end encryption, by definition, protects only content. Metadata protection requires an entirely separate set of mechanisms, most of which are in earlier stages of deployment and carry higher performance overhead.

What Metadata a Messaging Server Observes

Even a correctly implemented E2EE messaging service, fully complying with its own stated privacy policy, observes the following metadata by the nature of its operation:

Communication graph: When Alice sends a message to Bob, the server must know Bob's address to route the message. This creates a communication graph edge Alice→Bob with a timestamp. Over time, this graph reveals social structure, organisational hierarchy, romantic relationships, political associations, and professional networks—the core product of SIGINT operations.

Message sizes and timing: Even without knowing content, the size and timing of messages enables traffic analysis. Consistent message sizes at regular intervals suggest automated systems. Irregular burst patterns correlate with human emotional states. Size analysis can sometimes disambiguate between message types (text vs. voice message vs. image) even through encryption.

IP addresses and device fingerprints: The IP address of connecting clients reveals geographic location (often to city level), Internet service provider, and—through correlation with device fingerprints—device identity. ISP-level surveillance can correlate IP assignments to physical identities. This information is fully visible to the server and to any network intermediary.

Group membership: In a naive E2EE group design, the server knows every member of every group by virtue of routing messages to them. Signal's design encrypts group state client-side and uses group IDs as opaque identifiers, limiting server knowledge of group composition, but routing still reveals which accounts are in communication with each other.

Registration and contact graph: Traditional phone-number-based messaging requires uploading a contact list (or hashed contact list) to discover which contacts use the service. This upload is an irreversible disclosure of social graph data to the provider.

Sealed Sender

Signal introduced Sealed Sender in October 2018, implementing a mechanism by which the server can route a message to the recipient without knowing who sent it.[27] The mechanism works as follows:

Sealed Sender Construction
// Setup: Bob has a "profile key" and a "unidentified access key" (UAK)
// derived from his profile key and stored on the server alongside his account.
// Alice knows Bob's profile key (from his profile, shared after contact acceptance).

// Sealed Sender: Alice wants to send to Bob without revealing her identity to server

// Step 1: Alice constructs a sender certificate
sender_cert = {
    sender_e164:   Alice's phone number,
    sender_uuid:   Alice's UUID,
    sender_key:    Alice's current identity public key,
    expiration:    timestamp + 24h,
    certificate:   above fields serialised,
    signature:     Ed25519.Sign(Signal_server_key, certificate)
    // Server signs Alice's certificate, attesting she is a registered user
}

// Step 2: Alice encrypts her identity information using Bob's profile key
uak = HMAC-SHA256(bob_profile_key, "Unidentified Access Key")

// Step 3: Alice wraps her normal Signal message
inner_message = DoubleRatchetEncrypt(plaintext, alice_to_bob_session)
sealed_message = AEAD_Encrypt(
    key = DeriveKey(ephemeral_DH_with_bob_identity_key),
    plaintext = {sender_cert, inner_message},
    AD = uak   // Server validates UAK before routing, without learning sender
)

// Server receives: {sealed_message, bob_address, uak}
// Server validates: HMAC(bob_profile_key, "UAK") == uak (proves Alice knows Bob's profile)
// Server routes to Bob without knowing Alice's identity
// Bob decrypts outer layer → recovers sender_cert → verifies Signal server signature
// Bob verifies sender_cert.sender_key matches Alice's Double Ratchet session key

Sealed Sender thus hides the sender's identity from the Signal server for messages to recipients who have accepted the sender's profile. The server learns only: the recipient's address, the time of delivery, and the approximate message size. The server cannot correlate Alice as the sender without breaking the AEAD encryption or compromising Bob's profile key.

Sealed Sender does not protect against traffic correlation attacks: an adversary who monitors network traffic can observe that Alice's device established a connection to Signal's servers at a time coinciding with Bob's device receiving a push notification. Timing correlation attacks are a fundamental limitation of synchronous message delivery systems, addressable only by adding artificial delays and noise—approaches that sacrifice user experience for anonymity.

Private Contact Discovery

The "contact discovery" problem—determining which of a user's phone contacts also use Signal—traditionally required hashing all contact numbers and uploading the hashes to the server for matching. Hash-based contact discovery is not privacy-preserving: a server with a database of all phone numbers can reverse hash lookups exhaustively (the phone number space is small enough that this is computationally trivial).[28]

Signal's first-generation solution (2017) used Intel SGX (Software Guard Extensions) trusted execution environments running on Signal's servers. The contact discovery code ran in an SGX enclave, where Signal's server operators could not observe what numbers were being looked up. SGX-based remote attestation allowed clients to verify that they were communicating with an unmodified enclave running the expected code.

The second-generation approach (deployed 2022) moved to a Private Set Intersection (PSI) protocol that does not require trusted hardware. PSI allows two parties to compute the intersection of their sets (Alice's contacts and Signal's user set) without either party learning elements of the other's set that are not in the intersection. Signal's PSI implementation uses OPRF (Oblivious Pseudorandom Functions), allowing Alice to query whether a specific number is in Signal's user set without the server learning which specific number was queried.

IP Address Protection

Introduced in late 2022, Signal's IP address protection routes audio and video calls through Signal's infrastructure by default, preventing the call recipient from learning the caller's IP address. Prior to this feature, peer-to-peer calls exposed IP addresses to each party—a significant metadata leak for high-risk users. The implementation is architecturally similar to TURN (Traversal Using Relays around NAT) servers in WebRTC, with Signal's servers acting as mandatory relays for all calls. Users can optionally disable relay routing if they prefer lower latency and trust the recipient with their IP address.

Mix Networks and the Metadata-Hard Frontier

The theoretical frontier of metadata protection is the mix network, pioneered by David Chaum in 1981. Mix networks route messages through a sequence of relay nodes, each of which decrypts one layer and forwards to the next, making traffic correlation attacks computationally infeasible for an adversary who does not control all nodes simultaneously. Modern descendants—Vuvuzela (van den Hooff et al., 2015), Alpenhorn (Lazar et al., 2016), and Katzenpost—inject cover traffic and noise to prevent even statistical correlation.[29]

None of these systems has achieved production-scale deployment for general messaging. The fundamental constraint is latency: mix networks introduce latency proportional to the number of hops and the cover traffic injection rate—typically seconds rather than milliseconds. This is incompatible with the synchronous, real-time interaction model that users expect from a messaging application. The academic community continues to work on mix network designs with lower latency overhead; this remains an open problem in practical anonymity systems.

Section 09

Key Verification and Transparency in Messaging

End-to-end encryption is only meaningful if the public keys used to encrypt messages actually belong to the intended recipients. A service provider who controls key distribution can trivially perform a man-in-the-middle attack: when Alice requests Bob's public key to establish a session, the server returns its own key instead. Alice encrypts to the server's key, the server decrypts, logs the plaintext, re-encrypts to Bob's actual key, and forwards—all invisibly. Without key verification, E2EE provides no protection against the very entity that controls the key distribution infrastructure.

Trust on First Use (TOFU)

The default trust model in most deployed E2EE systems is TOFU (Trust on First Use): the first key material received for an identity is accepted and stored; subsequent key material for the same identity triggers a warning if it differs. This model is pragmatic—it provides strong security once a key is established—but leaves the first contact vulnerable to MITM. In practice, a sophisticated adversary would perform MITM on first contact (when the user has no prior expectation of what the key should be) rather than attempting to substitute an established key (which would trigger a visible warning).

TOFU also produces false positives: legitimate key changes occur when users reinstall Signal, get a new phone, or register on a new device. These events cause key change warnings that users typically dismiss without verification, creating habituation to warnings that makes genuine MITM attacks harder to detect. WhatsApp's key change notifications have been widely criticised for being insufficiently prominent and for occurring silently when encrypted backups are restored—a "feature" that creates systematic ambiguity around key changes.[30]

Safety Numbers and Manual Verification

Signal's mechanism for out-of-band key verification is Safety Numbers: a 60-digit decimal number derived from a SHA-512 hash of both parties' identity public keys, formatted as 12 groups of 5 digits (or as a QR code). If Alice and Bob compare their Safety Numbers via a trusted out-of-band channel—a phone call, an in-person meeting, or a video call where they can confirm each other's identity—they can verify that no MITM has substituted either key.

Safety Number Derivation
// Safety numbers in Signal are computed as follows:

func SafetyNumber(alice_identity, alice_number, bob_identity, bob_number):
    // Each party's "fingerprint" is:
    fingerprint_a = SHA-512_iterate(5200 times,
        seed = version || alice_identity.pub || alice_number)
    fingerprint_b = SHA-512_iterate(5200 times,
        seed = version || bob_identity.pub || bob_number)

    // Sort the two fingerprints lexicographically for deterministic ordering
    sorted = sort([fingerprint_a, fingerprint_b])

    // Convert to decimal fingerprint:
    // Each 30-byte chunk → 5-digit decimal groups via modular reduction
    combined = fingerprint_a[:30] || fingerprint_b[:30]
    return ToDecimalGroups(combined, chunk_size=5, num_groups=12)

The 5200-iteration SHA-512 is intentional: it makes brute-forcing a key pair that produces a desired safety number computationally infeasible, requiring approximately 279 operations to find a collision in the full 60-digit number, while keeping the verification computation fast enough to run on mobile hardware in under one second.

The Key Transparency Problem

Manual safety number verification scales poorly: users cannot practically verify keys with every correspondent. The underlying structural problem is that key distribution is centralised and opaque. The service provider's server presents keys to clients, and clients have no way to verify that every client is seeing the same key for a given identity—or to detect if the server is selectively presenting different keys to different users (equivocation).

This is precisely the problem that Certificate Transparency (CT, RFC 6962) solved for TLS certificates: rather than trusting certificate authorities to issue only legitimate certificates, CT mandates that every certificate be logged in a public, append-only, cryptographically verifiable log. Browsers refuse to accept certificates not present in CT logs, making unauthorised certificate issuance detectable after the fact. The analogous mechanism for messaging keys—Key Transparency (KT)—applies the same principle: every key published by a user is logged in a verifiable append-only structure.[31]

CONIKS: Key Transparency for End Users

CONIKS (Consistent Online Key Infrastructure, Melara et al., 2015) was the first formal proposal for key transparency in end-user messaging systems.[32] CONIKS maintains a server-side Merkle prefix tree (a Patricia tree) of user identity-to-key mappings. Each entry in the tree maps an identifier (phone number, email) to a public key. The tree is updated periodically, and signed tree roots (STHs—Signed Tree Heads) are published for third-party auditing.

CONIKS uses a Verifiable Random Function (VRF) to map identifiers to positions in the tree. The VRF ensures that a client looking up "alice@example.com" gets a deterministic tree position that only the server can compute (using the VRF private key) but that any verifier can verify given the VRF public key and proof. This prevents the server from creating multiple inconsistent views of the tree (equivocation) while also preventing enumeration of the user database by computing tree paths for arbitrary identifiers.

CONIKS Proof of Inclusion
// Server proves to Alice that her key is correctly listed:

1. Server computes: (index, proof) = VRF.Evaluate(vrf_priv, alice_identifier)
   // index = position in tree; proof = VRF correctness proof

2. Server provides Alice:
   - Leaf node at tree[index]: {alice_identifier, alice_key_pub, timestamp}
   - Merkle inclusion proof: path of sibling hashes from leaf to root
   - VRF proof: attesting that index = VRF(alice_identifier)
   - Signed Tree Head (STH): root hash signed by server's KT key

3. Alice verifies:
   - VRF.Verify(vrf_pub, alice_identifier, index, proof) == true
   - MerkleProof(leaf, siblings) == STH.root_hash
   - Ed25519.Verify(server_kt_key, STH.signed_data) == true
   - leaf.alice_key_pub == expected_key

4. Auditors compare STHs across time:
   - Trees must be append-only (no entry deletions or modifications without visible log entries)
   - Consistency proofs between sequential STHs are verifiable by third parties

Signal Key Transparency (2024)

In early 2024, Signal announced the deployment of a Key Transparency system for the Signal Protocol.[33] The design is informed by CONIKS and the lessons of Certificate Transparency deployment, adapted for the Signal-specific requirements of phone-number-based identity and privacy-preserving key lookup (Signal must not enable enumeration of its user base through the KT tree).

Signal's KT system uses a log-backed map: an append-only log of key-change events (analogous to a CT log) combined with a Merkle map that provides O(1) lookup proofs for the current key associated with any identifier. The Merkle map allows Signal clients to verify their own key is correctly listed and to verify the key presented for a contact without the server being able to silently substitute a different key.

The privacy-preserving aspect uses VRFs (as in CONIKS): the tree position for each identifier is computed via VRF, preventing enumeration of the user set by traversing the tree structure. The VRF private key is held by Signal's KT service; the VRF public key is distributed to clients for proof verification.

With Key Transparency, if a client receives a key that differs from what is in the log, the inconsistency is detectable. A server cannot silently perform MITM without creating an auditable artifact that clients and third-party monitors can verify.

— Signal Foundation, Key Transparency Announcement, 2024[33]

WhatsApp Key Transparency

WhatsApp announced its Key Transparency system in 2023, publishing a technical report describing an Auditable Key Directory (AKD) based on the "Seemless" protocol from the academic literature. WhatsApp's AKD uses a similar Merkle-tree-over-append-only-log structure, with signed tree roots periodically published and verifiable by third-party auditors. The AKD maps phone numbers to WhatsApp identity public keys.[34]

WhatsApp's KT implementation is notable because it is deployed at the scale of two billion users, making it the largest key transparency system ever deployed. The audit architecture includes support for third-party security organisations to independently verify consistency, providing an external check on WhatsApp's/Meta's behaviour as the KT server operator.

Practical Key Verification Hygiene

For practitioners operating in high-risk environments, the operational verification posture for messaging security should include the following elements:

Safety Number verification for high-value contacts. For contacts whose communications represent significant operational security risk, out-of-band safety number comparison (preferably in-person QR code scanning) should be performed at initial session establishment and after any key change notification.

Alert fatigue management. Key change notifications should be treated as requiring investigation, not routine dismissal. In organisations, this translates to a policy of contacting the correspondent by a separate channel to confirm the key change was legitimate before resuming communication.

Monitoring Key Transparency logs. As KT systems mature, clients that support KT (Signal, WhatsApp) perform automated log verification. Users should not disable this functionality. In enterprise contexts, centralised KT log monitoring can provide alerts on anomalous key changes across the organisation.

Post-quantum considerations. Apple's PQ3 for iMessage (2024) introduced a post-quantum key encapsulation mechanism (ML-KEM, formerly CRYSTALS-Kyber) for forward secrecy, designed to protect against harvest-now-decrypt-later attacks where a state-level adversary records ciphertext today for decryption after a cryptographically relevant quantum computer becomes available. The Signal Foundation has announced similar post-quantum extensions to the X3DH key agreement. The Double Ratchet's symmetric layer (HKDF, AES-256, ChaCha20-Poly1305) is already considered quantum-resistant; the vulnerable layer is the Diffie–Hellman key exchange, which is broken by Shor's algorithm.[35]

Synthesis: Evaluating the Full Security Stack

The field of messaging security in 2026 presents a layered stack of solved and partially solved problems. Content confidentiality against server-side adversaries is well-solved by the Signal Protocol and is deployed at billions-of-users scale. Forward secrecy at per-message granularity is likewise mature and widely deployed. Post-compromise security is available in pairwise messaging; extending it to groups at scale is the active front of deployment, with MLS providing the theoretically superior answer and beginning to see production adoption in enterprise contexts.

The remaining frontiers are metadata protection—where the engineering cost of mix-network-style anonymity remains prohibitively high for mass-market deployment—and key transparency, where the tooling is maturing rapidly following Signal and WhatsApp's deployments but where ecosystem-wide adoption and standardised auditor infrastructure remain nascent. Post-quantum security is transitioning from theoretical concern to engineering requirement, with the first production deployments appearing in iMessage and the broader Signal ecosystem.

The gap between what the best-deployed systems provide (Signal, approaching the full stack) and what most deployed systems provide (WhatsApp: content + FS + PCS, significant metadata retention; iMessage: content + FS + PCS with iCloud exposure, no KT until recently; Telegram: no default E2EE) remains large. The lesson of the past decade is that cryptographic advances alone are insufficient—the institutional will and engineering investment to deploy them at scale, and the user education to trust them correctly, are the limiting factors.
