The representational stance

A model is not a copy of the world; it is a stand-in. It is something we build, manipulate, and consult instead of the thing it represents, because the thing itself is too large, too small, too fast, too slow, too dangerous, too distant, or too tangled to handle directly. A planetarium stands in for the night sky, a wind-tunnel scale model for an airliner, a system of differential equations for a population of foxes and rabbits. What unites these otherwise unalike objects is a single relation — representation — together with two features that make representation tractable: selectivity (a model omits almost everything about its target, keeping only what matters for a purpose) and purpose-relativity (what matters is fixed by the question being asked).1

Twentieth-century philosophy of science spent decades arguing about what, precisely, a model is. The older "syntactic" view treated a scientific theory as a set of sentences in a formal language, and a model as an interpretation that made those sentences true. The "semantic" view that displaced it treats theories as families of models directly — set-theoretic structures, or trajectories through a state space — with the linguistic formulation a secondary matter.2 On Bas van Fraassen's influential version, to accept a theory is to hold that the world's observable behaviour can be embedded in one of its models — to claim empirical adequacy, and nothing more about hidden machinery.3 Ronald Giere pushed the point toward pluralism: a model is always a model of something, for someone, to some degree of fidelity, in some respects — so a single target sustains many legitimate, non-competing models at once.4

If models are representations, they are also instruments. Mary Morgan and Margaret Morrison argued that models are not merely derived from theory and applied to data but sit between the two as partially autonomous tools — built from a motley of theoretical principles, empirical findings, analogies, and frank guesswork, and earning their keep by what they let us do.5 This instrumental honesty is captured in the aphorism every working modeller eventually internalises: since every model distorts in order to be usable, the question is never whether a model is true but whether it is wrong in ways that matter.6 The distortions even have names — idealisation (deliberately false simplifications, such as the frictionless plane) and abstraction (the mere omission of detail) — and a model's worth is judged by its similarity to the target in the respects, and to the degrees, its purpose demands.7

What follows is a map of the kinds of models with which human beings have tried to grasp reality, arranged along a single axis: increasing constraint by evidence, increasing predictive precision, increasing mathematical articulation. At one end sit speculations about what reality must be like, answerable to reason alone; at the other, statistical engines that say nothing about what reality is and everything about what it will do next. The axis is a spectrum of epistemic attitude, not a ranking of worth — a creation myth and a convolutional network are both models, and they fail to compete only because they were never trying to answer the same question.

But a taxonomy is the easy half. The harder and more interesting question — the one this essay is really about — is how the families relate. Do they cooperate, handing representations down a production line from speculation to measurement? Do they run in parallel, describing the same world at different grains without ever touching? Do they collide, so that one must be killed for another to live? Or are some simply irrelevant to one another, speaking past each other forever? The answer, developed in Part II, is: all four — and which one obtains in any given pair turns out to follow a surprisingly simple logic.

Part I — A spectrum of models

FAMILIES 1 & 2Reason without residue: the a priori and the speculative

At the source of the spectrum are models built without essential appeal to the senses. They answer not "what is the world like?" but "what must the world be like for thought, experience, or being to be possible at all?" Their materials are concepts; their method is deduction, intuition, and the demand for coherence; their court of appeal is reason rather than the laboratory.

Pure a priori metaphysics family 1

The canon is a sequence of total pictures. Plato posited a realm of eternal Forms of which sensible things are mere shadows, the only objects of genuine knowledge.8 Descartes, seeking an indubitable foundation, divided reality into two substances — extended matter and unextended mind.9 Spinoza collapsed the division into a single infinite substance, "God or Nature," of which thought and extension are two attributes.10 Leibniz fractured reality instead into a plurality of windowless simple substances, monads, each mirroring the whole universe from its own point of view.11 Kant performed the decisive turn: rather than asking how the mind conforms to objects, he asked how objects must conform to the mind, distinguishing things as they appear (phenomena, structured by our forms of intuition and categories) from things as they are in themselves (noumena, in principle unknowable).12 Hegel then absorbed the thing-in-itself into a self-developing Absolute — reality as the unfolding of reason itself.13

The tradition is alive. David Lewis defended modal realism — the thesis that every way the world could have been is a concrete world as real as our own, merely spatiotemporally disconnected from it — as the best theory of what possibility and necessity are.14 Mathematical Platonism, the view that numbers, sets, and structures exist as mind-independent abstract objects, treats the universe of sets as a real, if non-physical, domain. Such models are not idle: they fix the meanings of the categories — substance, cause, law, possibility, identity — that every later and more empirical model silently presupposes. They are assessed not by prediction but by internal coherence, explanatory reach, parsimony, and fit with considered intuition.

Speculative cosmology and theology family 2

Where pure metaphysics is austere, this family is narrative. It blends a priori reasoning with revelation, myth, and analogy to tell comprehensive stories about the origin, structure, and fate of the cosmos — stories that, crucially, locate human meaning inside the account. Creation narratives — from the craftsman-god of Plato's Timaeus15 to Genesis, the cyclic ages of Hindu cosmology, and the Norse world-tree Yggdrasil — organise the whole of existence into a moral and temporal order. The medieval Great Chain of Being arranged all that exists into a continuous, gradated hierarchy from God down to inert matter — a model whose grip on the European imagination Arthur Lovejoy traced across centuries of literature and philosophy.16 Gnostic cosmologies populated the gap between a perfect deity and a flawed world with demiurges and aeons.

The mode persists, retooled. Nick Bostrom's simulation argument contends that at least one of three propositions is true: civilisations almost always go extinct before reaching technological maturity; mature civilisations almost never run ancestor-simulations; or we are almost certainly living in one.17 It is a thoroughly modern specimen of the genre — a cosmological model resting on probabilistic reasoning and a technological analogy rather than scripture — and it shows how thin the line can be between speculative metaphysics and a research programme. These models are maximally unconstrained by data and maximally ambitious in scope; that combination is exactly what makes them perennial, and perennially contested.

FAMILIES 3 & 4The inherited map: tacit and proto-scientific models

Long before anyone theorises deliberately, the mind ships with models pre-installed, and cultures equip their members with more. These are not speculations chosen for their coherence but tacit cognitive maps selected — by evolution and by tradition — for letting an organism act in time. They are frequently false and indispensable in the same breath.

Folk, naive, and intuitive models family 3

Humans reason about the physical world with a folk physics that is fast, automatic, and systematically mistaken in revealing ways. Asked to predict the path of a ball whirled on a string and then released, many educated adults expect it to keep curving — a relic of medieval impetus theory rather than Newtonian inertia — and similar errors recur across object motion.1819 We navigate the social world with a folk psychology that explains behaviour by attributing beliefs and desires; the question of whether non-human animals deploy the same "theory of mind" launched a major research literature.20 We carve the living world with a folk biology that treats species as having hidden essences, a tendency that appears across cultures and may be a cognitive universal.21 And infants come equipped with core knowledge systems — for objects, agents, number, and space — that underwrite expectations like object permanence well before language.22 Folk cosmology, finally, reads the world off appearances: a flat earth beneath a dome of stars that wheel overhead.

Proto-scientific and analogical models family 4

Between naive intuition and tested science lies a band of systems that combined real observation with bold organising analogies — sympathies, correspondences, hidden forces — and that later standards largely falsified. Astrology mapped celestial configurations onto human affairs. Alchemy pursued the transmutation of substances through a web of symbolic correspondences; far from mere superstition, recent scholarship recasts it as a genuine, if mistaken, experimental chemistry whose laboratory practices fed directly into the chemical revolution.23 Humoral theory explained health and temperament by the balance of four bodily fluids, a framework that structured Western medicine for two millennia.24 Phrenology read character from the bumps of the skull; vitalism posited a life-force irreducible to physics and chemistry. What marks the family is not stupidity but premature systematisation — a reach for unifying principle ahead of the evidence and methods that could discipline it. Several of its members were the chrysalis stage of a science: humoral practice accumulated clinical observation, alchemy bequeathed apparatus and reactions, and even astrology drove centuries of careful positional astronomy.

A folk model is the theory the brain ships with; a proto-science is the first draft we write down. Both are models of reality in daily use — even, and especially, when they are wrong.

FAMILY 5The realm of pure form: formal and mathematical-logical models

A category apart sits at the spectrum's midpoint — not because it is half-empirical but because it is a different kind of thing entirely. Formal models describe a "reality" of abstract objects and their relations, built by axiomatic stipulation and explored by deductive proof. Their truth is internal — provability and consistency relative to chosen axioms — not correspondence to the physical world. Yet they furnish the language in which the most empirical sciences are written, which is what earns them a central place.

The reigning foundation is set theory: Zermelo–Fraenkel set theory with Choice (ZFC) and the cumulative von Neumann hierarchy V, in which every mathematical object is, in principle, a set built up in stages.25 Category theory offers a rival vantage — topos theory provides whole alternative mathematical universes with their own internal logic, reframing what "foundation" even means.26 Saul Kripke's possible-world semantics turned the metaphysician's talk of necessity and possibility (recall family 1) into precise mathematics: a frame of points with an accessibility relation, on which modal sentences receive truth values.27 In applied form, formal ontologies such as Basic Formal Ontology and DOLCE supply rigorous, computer-readable inventories of categories — objects, processes, qualities, relations — for organising information across the sciences.28 And cellular automata model abstract computational worlds: John Conway's Game of Life, on a grid governed by a single birth/survival rule, generates unbounded complexity and is itself Turing-complete.29 Stephen Wolfram pressed the idea to its limit, proposing that simple computational rules might be the deepest model of nature itself.30

Specimen · a formal world from a two-line rule
# Conway's Game of Life — birth/survival notation, B3/S23
birth    : a dead cell with exactly 3 live neighbours  → live
survival : a live cell with 2 or 3 live neighbours    → live
death    : all other cases (loneliness / overcrowding) → dead
From this rule alone arise gliders, oscillators, replicators, and universal computation — an entire abstract "reality" whose laws are stipulated, not discovered, yet whose consequences must be found out by running it.

The deepest puzzle these models raise is the one Eugene Wigner named the "unreasonable effectiveness of mathematics": why should structures dreamt up by axiomatic play — complex numbers, Hilbert spaces, Riemannian geometry — turn out to be the indispensable language of physics, with no advance guarantee that they should fit the world at all?31 That single fact, examined in Part III, is what stitches the most a priori family to the most empirical.

FAMILIES 6 – 8Disciplined contact with the world: empirical scientific models

Now the spectrum turns decisively outward. These three families share a commitment to answering to evidence, but they differ in ambition — from merely describing the phenomena, to explaining them, to mapping the causal machinery that produces them.

Descriptive and phenomenological models family 6

The most modest empirical models aim only to "save the phenomena": to reproduce and predict observations through a mathematical relationship, without committing to any underlying mechanism. The phrase is ancient — Pierre Duhem traced the methodological ideal of merely saving appearances from Plato through to Galileo — and it names a permanent option in science: instrumental fidelity without metaphysical claim.32 Ptolemy's astronomy is the paradigm: a system of epicycles and deferents that predicted planetary positions with remarkable accuracy while saying nothing true about how the heavens actually move.33 Kepler's three laws described planetary orbits as ellipses swept at varying speeds — exact kinematics, but, before Newton, no account of why. Johann Balmer found a tidy formula for the wavelengths of hydrogen's spectral lines decades before anyone could explain it physically.34 Ohm's law and the ideal-gas law began life as empirical generalisations of this kind; their modern descendants include the parton distribution functions and Regge trajectories of particle physics — parameterised fits that compress data while deferring explanation.

Theoretical and hypothetico-deductive models family 7

Here science reaches for explanation. A theoretical model conjectures unobserved entities, mechanisms, and laws, then tests them by deriving observable consequences — a logic Karl Popper distilled into the demand that genuine theories forbid something, that they be falsifiable.35 The family is the heart of scientific realism and the trophy case of physics. Newton's Principia unified terrestrial and celestial motion under universal gravitation and, in doing so, explained Kepler's purely descriptive laws as theorems.36 Darwin's theory of natural selection explained the fit and diversity of life by descent with modification under differential survival.37 Maxwell's equations unified electricity, magnetism, and light into a single field theory.38 Einstein's general relativity recast gravitation as the curvature of spacetime.39 Quantum mechanics, in the formalisms of Schrödinger, Heisenberg, and Dirac, replaced trajectories with state vectors and probability amplitudes.40 Plate tectonics did for the solid earth what these did for matter and motion, unifying continental drift, seafloor spreading, and seismicity into one mobile-lithosphere model.41 What distinguishes the family is that its mathematics is meant to be about reality, not merely fitted to it.

Mechanistic and causal models family 8

Closely allied, but with a different emphasis, are models that map causal architecture explicitly — that represent which entities, doing which activities, organised in which way, produce a phenomenon. The "new mechanist" philosophy of science made this notion precise: to explain is often not to subsume under a law but to exhibit the mechanism.4243 Judea Pearl's framework formalised causation itself, distinguishing seeing from doing through the do-operator and letting causal questions be read off directed acyclic graphs.44 Algorithms for causal discovery aim to recover such structure from data,45 while James Woodward's interventionist account ties the very meaning of a causal claim to what would happen under hypothetical manipulations.46 In the life sciences, biochemical pathway models and the integrative ambitions of systems biology assemble molecular interactions into networks that can be perturbed in thought and in fact.47 Ian Hacking captured the family's implicit realism in a slogan: if you can spray electrons to manipulate something else, they are real — intervention, not just representation, is the touchstone.48

Specimen · a structural causal model (Pearl)
# Structural equations: each variable as a function of its causes + noise
Z := f_Z(U_Z)                 # confounder
X := f_X(Z, U_X)              # treatment
Y := f_Y(X, Z, U_Y)           # outcome

# Seeing vs. doing — the two are NOT the same:
P(Y | X = x)            # observation: still tangled with Z
P(Y | do(X = x))        # intervention: severs Z → X, isolates the cause
The do-operator marks the leap from family 6's "what is associated with what" to family 8's "what makes what happen" — the difference between a model of the data and a model of the world.

FAMILIES 9 – 11Reality rebuilt in silico: computational, engineering, and data-driven models

The final stretch of the spectrum is defined less by a new epistemology than by a new medium: the computer. Theory and mechanism are executed rather than solved; designs are validated by being built; and, most recently, models are learned from data with little or no theoretical scaffolding at all.

Computational and simulation models family 9

When a theoretical or mechanistic model is too complex to solve analytically, it is discretised and run. Finite-element analysis breaks a structure into a mesh of elements; computational fluid dynamics solves discretised Navier–Stokes equations for flows no closed form can reach;49 general circulation models integrate the physics of atmosphere and ocean to project climate — themselves vast socio-technical instruments, as Paul Edwards has documented.50 Agent-based models grow social phenomena from the bottom up by simulating many interacting individuals,51 and whole-cell models attempt to compute an organism's phenotype from its genotype, integrating every known molecular process.52 Philosophers argue that simulation is genuinely novel: it reasons through motley, sanction-laden numerical approximations and produces results that are epistemically opaque — no human can survey every step — forcing a distinctive practice of verification and validation rather than proof.5354

Engineering and design models family 10

Engineering models are action-oriented: they must correspond to physical behaviour reliably enough to build, control, or manufacture something that works. Their truth-condition is, in effect, repeated successful instantiation. CAD geometries and their dynamic counterparts, digital twins, mirror physical assets in real time to predict and manage their behaviour.55 Circuit simulators in the SPICE lineage let engineers predict the behaviour of electronics before fabrication.56 Control theory's state-space models — the abstraction behind autopilots and robotics — were given their modern form by Rudolf Kálmán's work on filtering and system description.57 Systems-engineering languages such as Modelica and SysML, and thermodynamic cycle models like the Rankine and refrigeration cycles, round out a family whose hallmark is standardisation, domain-specificity, and validation in use rather than in theory.

Data-driven and machine-learning models family 11

At the far end sit models that learn patterns directly from large datasets, often with no explicit theoretical structure. Deep neural networks now dominate perception and language;58 random forests and gradient-boosted ensembles excel at tabular prediction.59 Leo Breiman framed the underlying schism as "two cultures": the data-modelling culture that posits an interpretable stochastic mechanism, and the algorithmic culture that treats the mechanism as unknown and optimises predictive accuracy instead.60 Gaussian-process regression provides flexible surrogates with calibrated uncertainty;61 generative models such as GANs learn the statistical manifold of their training data well enough to sample new instances from it.62 In the sciences, AlphaFold's prediction of protein structure from sequence marked a watershed — a learned model achieving in hours what decades of physical theory could not.63 The trade is transparency: such models are frequently black boxes, prompting calls to favour interpretable models where stakes are high,64 and a hybrid programme — theory-guided data science — that re-injects scientific structure into learning.65

Part II — How the families relate

It is tempting to read the spectrum as a ladder — speculation at the bottom, hard science at the top, each rung superseding the last. That picture is wrong, or at best a special case. The families do not form a single ranked sequence; they form a web, and the strands between any two nodes come in exactly four kinds. The kinds are not arbitrary. They follow from two questions asked in order, plus a third that distinguishes the two compatible cases.

MODE 01Collaboration: the vertical integration of models

The most familiar relationship is also the most productive: one model feeds another, and a representation is handed down a chain. The canonical pipeline runs straight along the spectrum — phenomenology (6) to theory (7) to computation (9) to engineering (10). Trace a single object through it. Kepler's three laws are a purely descriptive model of where the planets go; Newton's gravitation absorbs them, deriving the ellipse as a theorem rather than a brute fact.36 But Newton's own three-body problem has no closed-form solution, so for an actual mission the equations are discretised and integrated numerically — the computer extending analytic reach where pencil and paper stop.54 The resulting trajectory then becomes the reference signal for a state-space control model that steers the craft.57 Four families, one artefact, passed hand to hand.

A second, deeper collaboration is the supply of language. The formal family (5) does not describe the physical world, yet without it the theoretical family (7) could not be written: general relativity is unintelligible without pseudo-Riemannian geometry,39 quantum mechanics without the theory of Hilbert spaces,40 and almost everything without probability and the calculus. Wigner's "unreasonable effectiveness" is precisely this collaboration seen as a mystery: the most a priori family hands the most empirical its grammar, and no one quite knows why the gift fits.31 The mechanistic family (8) brokers a further handoff, from theory to simulation — a causal or biochemical model is specified in principle4447 and then executed as a runnable computational model when its equations outstrip solution.53

The newest collaboration reverses the usual direction. Where a mechanistic simulation (9) is too costly to run at scale, a data-driven model (11) is trained on its outputs and substituted as a fast surrogate — learned emulators standing in for expensive solvers. AlphaFold is the spectacular case: a model that learned protein structure from data now does, in hours, what direct simulation of the underlying physics could not achieve in practice.63 And the hybrid programme of theory-guided data science deliberately fuses families 7 and 8 with family 11, constraining a learner with known conservation laws or mechanisms so that it generalises better and respects what is already understood.65 Even pure speculation collaborates: the metaphysical family (1) bequeaths the very categories — substance, cause, possibility — that the others presuppose, and the formal family returns the favour by giving those categories rigorous form, as when Kripke's semantics turned modal metaphysics into mathematics.27

A spacecraft's path is one object handed down the whole chain of representation — from Kepler's ellipse, through Newton's law and a numerical integrator, to the autopilot's state vector.

MODE 02Complementarity: models that run in parallel lanes

Two models can address the same target, agree wherever they overlap, and yet never merge — because they describe it at different grains, or from different angles, for different purposes. This is not a defect to be resolved by reduction; it is the normal condition of a mature science. David Marr made the point canonical for cognition with his three levels of analysis: a visual capacity can be described computationally (what is being computed and why), algorithmically (by what representations and procedures), and at the level of physical implementation (in neurons and circuits) — three models of one system, none competing with the others.69 Giere's perspectivism generalises the lesson: a ray-optics and a wave-optics model of light, or a thermodynamic and a statistical-mechanical model of a gas, are each true within their domain of application and false outside it, and a scientist switches between them as the question demands.4

Nancy Cartwright pushed this into a metaphysics of its own: the laws and models of physics hold only ceteris paribus, in carefully prepared patches, and nature is not a single deductive system but a "dappled world" of locally valid models that do not knit into one grand theory.67 Folk models survive on exactly these terms. We still navigate the lived world with folk physics — catching balls, pouring coffee — while sincerely believing relativity, because the two operate at scales and for purposes that do not collide; Wilfrid Sellars framed the standing tension between the everyday "manifest image" and the "scientific image" as, on the irenic reading, a pair of complementary stances rather than a fight to the death.70 Engineers institutionalise the same tolerance: they compute with Newtonian mechanics — a strictly superseded theory — because inside the operating envelope it is indistinguishable from general relativity and vastly cheaper to use. The most expansive (and most contested) claim of parallelism is Stephen Jay Gould's principle of "non-overlapping magisteria," on which the theological family (2) and empirical science address different questions entirely — meaning versus mechanism — and so cannot, properly understood, conflict.71 Whether that boundary actually holds is itself disputed, which brings us to the next mode.

MODE 03Conflict: when models cannot both be right

Where two models address the same domain and disagree about it, the relationship is rivalry, and rivalry is resolved by elimination rather than addition. The history of science is in large part a graveyard of losers: the geocentric model fell to the heliocentric; phlogiston to oxygen; spontaneous generation to germ theory; and vitalism — the proto-scientific posit of a life-force (4) — to the mechanistic biochemistry (8) that explained living processes without it. The defeated model is not absorbed as a special case; it is judged false and discarded. Karl Popper made this the engine of science, casting inquiry as a sequence of bold conjectures exposed to potential refutation.35 Thomas Kuhn complicated the picture: rival theoretical models can be incommensurable, so that "mass" in Newtonian and relativistic physics does not pick out the same quantity and the change of allegiance has the character of a gestalt switch rather than a tidy tallying of evidence.66

Conflict is not confined to within-science succession. Folk models clash with scientific ones precisely where their domains overlap: the impetus intuition is simply wrong about projectile motion, and where the everyday model makes a testable claim that physics contradicts, the everyday model loses.1819 A subtler conflict runs across the empirical families themselves — between the explanatory ambition of theory and mechanism (7, 8) and the frank instrumentalism of phenomenology and machine learning (6, 11). Breiman's "two cultures" names the standoff: is a model that predicts superbly while explaining nothing a model of reality, or merely a model of the data?60 The worry sharpens with opaque high-stakes predictors, where some argue that an uninterpretable model, however accurate, should yield to a transparent one when consequences are grave.64 Finally, metaphysics can pick fights it cannot win: Cartesian substance dualism (1) makes empirical hostages — claims about the independence of mind from matter — that the mechanistic models of neuroscience steadily press against.9

When two models answer the same question and disagree, one of them has made a hostage of reality — and reality does not always pay the ransom.

MODE 04Independence: models that never meet

The last relationship is the absence of one. Many pairs of models are simply orthogonal: they make no claims in common, take no input from one another, and could each be wholly right or wholly wrong without the other so much as flickering. Astrology's purported celestial influences (4) are not a rival to quantum field theory (7); they are disconnected from it — and, separately, empirically void. The autonomy is sharpest for the formal family (5). The truth of a large-cardinal axiom, or the internal logic of a particular topos, neither constrains nor is constrained by the theory of plate tectonics;41 whether one is a Platonist or a formalist about such objects, most of mathematics is built and judged without any glance at the physical world. Engineering models (10) and high metaphysics (1) are mutually irrelevant in the same way: a Rankine-cycle model neither needs nor bears on the reality of universals.

What makes independence treacherous is that it is neither symmetric in importance nor stable over time. Whole tracts of "useless" pure mathematics have later become the indispensable infrastructure of a science — number theory turning into modern cryptography, non-Euclidean geometry waiting a century to become the language of general relativity. Independence, in other words, is the default that the Wigner puzzle keeps violating: the abstract realm and the empirical realm mostly ignore each other, until, without warning, a fragment of the first turns out to be the grammar of the second.31 Today's irrelevance is frequently tomorrow's foundation, which is one more reason the spectrum is a web and not a hierarchy.

Part III — The deeper currents

Three themes run beneath the whole taxonomy and surface in every relationship between its families. They are the questions that the map cannot settle but cannot avoid.

Realism and its discontents

The spectrum can be re-read, end to end, as a gradient of truth-claims — of how much each family asserts about reality as opposed to appearance. At one extreme the metaphysical family (1) claims to describe ultimate reality; in the middle the phenomenological family (6) claims only to save the phenomena; at the far end the data-driven family (11) claims merely to forecast the next observation. This is the old battlefield of scientific realism. The realist holds that our best theoretical and mechanistic models (7, 8) are at least approximately true, their unobservable posits really out there. Van Fraassen's constructive empiricism counters that we are entitled to believe only in the empirical adequacy of a theory — that it gets the observable right — and should suspend judgement on the hidden machinery, accepting the rest as a useful fiction.3 Where a model sits on the taxonomy partly fixes what realism could even mean for it: to be a realist about a Gaussian process or a Ptolemaic epicycle is close to a category mistake, because those models were never in the business of describing what is. The instrumentalist temper native to families 6 and 11, and the realist temper native to 7 and 8, are not a confusion to be cleared up but a permanent division of labour within the enterprise.

Unity, disunity, and the dappled world

Behind the collaborations of Part II lies a dream: that the families form a pyramid, every higher model in principle reducible to a lower one, all of it resting on a single fundamental theory from which everything else follows. The relationships we actually find tell a different story. Cartwright's dappled world denies that nature is governed by one tidy system of exceptionless laws; she argues that the very success of our models depends on their being local, idealised, and patched — that the laws of physics, taken as universal truths, "lie."6867 On this view the web of models is not a temporary mess awaiting unification but the permanent shape of knowledge: partially autonomous representations that mediate between theory and world and trade outputs without ever collapsing into one master model.5 Box's aphorism, generalised, becomes a thesis about the whole structure: every model is wrong about something because every model is for something, and no single representation is for everything at once.6

The shifting frontier

The boundaries drawn in Part I are moving, and the data-driven family is the chief solvent. Machine-learning models now occupy positions all along the spectrum at once: as fast surrogates for mechanistic simulation (9), as discovery engines whose outputs feed new theory (7), and as black boxes that revive the realism debate in its most acute modern form.63 A model that is superhumanly predictive yet humanly opaque forces the old instrumentalist question into the open — and a system that achieves accuracy with no interpretable account of how invites the demand that, where stakes are high, we prefer models we can understand.64 The countercurrent is the deliberate refusion of prediction with explanation: theory-guided and physics-informed learning constrains the data-driven family with the structure of the theoretical and mechanistic ones, an attempt to recover understanding without surrendering predictive power.65 The frontier moves; the categories blur at their edges; but they do not vanish, because the underlying questions — describe, explain, intervene, predict — remain distinct.

CODAA division of labour, not a hierarchy

The eleven families are best understood not as competitors for a single prize called "reality" but as specialists answering different questions about it. The metaphysician asks what must be the case; the cosmologist, where it all comes from and what it means; the folk mind, how to act before the next heartbeat; the proto-scientist, what hidden order might lie behind appearances; the logician, what follows from what; the curve-fitter, what regularity the data show; the theorist, what mechanism underlies them; the causal modeller, what would change if we intervened; the simulator, how the whole thing behaves when no formula will close; the engineer, what we can reliably build; and the learner, what will happen next. Asked their own questions, each is doing legitimate work; the appearance of rivalry arises only where their questions overlap.

That is why the four relationships of Part II, and the simple logic of Figure 2, matter more than the taxonomy itself. Two models collaborate when one supplies what the other needs — language, structure, a numerical engine, a fast surrogate. They run in parallel when they describe one world at different grains, each true in its lane. They conflict only when they make competing claims about the same thing, and then evidence, sooner or later, kills one of them. And they are independent when their questions never touch — a relationship that the unreasonable effectiveness of mathematics is forever turning, unpredictably, into collaboration. The map of models of reality is not a ladder climbing from superstition to truth. It is a working ecology of representations, cooperating, competing, and ignoring one another exactly insofar as their purposes meet — and the discipline of using it well lies in knowing, for any two of them, which of the four is in force.
