The Transparent Wire: A Field Guide to Network Observability

Networks have always been opaque by design — packets blink past, sessions close, state evaporates. The discipline of observability is the long, patient project of making that opacity legible.

§ 01 · PremiseThe Crisis of Visibility

In the early hours of October 4, 2021, Facebook's autonomous systems vanished from the public internet. For roughly six hours, more than three billion users could not reach Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp, or Messenger; internal engineers could not reach their own buildings, because badge readers and conference rooms depended on the same infrastructure as the public services[1]. The proximate cause was a routine command issued during a backbone audit that, due to a bug in the audit tool, withdrew the BGP routes announcing Facebook's DNS servers[1]. The deeper cause was that the operators could not see what they had done until it was far too late to undo.

That episode is exemplary, not aberrational. Every large operator — Cloudflare, Google, AWS, Slack, GitHub — has its own version of an outage where the network silently lied, where dashboards stayed green while users screamed, where tracing the failure took longer than fixing it[2]. The discipline that has grown up to address this is network observability: the practice of instrumenting networks so that their internal state can be inferred from the signals they emit.

The term observability is borrowed from control theory, where Rudolf Kálmán defined it in 1960 as the property of a dynamical system whose internal states can be determined from its external outputs over a finite time interval[3]. The borrowing is partly accurate and partly aspirational. A real-world router does not approximate a linear time-invariant system; its internal state — forwarding tables, ARP caches, BGP adjacencies, TCAM utilization — is vast, partly unknowable, and entangled with the states of every device it speaks to. What practitioners mean by observability today is closer to instrumentation that makes a system debuggable under conditions you did not anticipate at design time[4].

A system is observable to the extent that you can answer a question about it that you did not think to ask when you built it. — Charity Majors, founding engineer, Honeycomb

This article is a long-form field guide to that practice. It walks through the historical layers — SNMP, NetFlow, packet capture — that still underpin most production networks; the tools that practitioners reach for daily (Wireshark, nmap, Prometheus, Grafana); the commercial platforms that have come to dominate the upper end of the market (Datadog, Dynatrace); and the architectural shift currently underway as eBPF, OpenTelemetry, and streaming telemetry rewrite the foundations beneath everything that came before. It is aimed at engineers who already know how to read a packet capture and who want a coherent map of the territory.

§ 02 · LineageFrom Management to Observability

The earliest network monitoring tools were management tools. They presupposed that a human operator already understood the network's structure and merely needed to keep tabs on its health. This is the genealogy of SNMP, the Simple Network Management Protocol, whose first version was published as RFC 1067 in August 1988 and revised as RFC 1098 the following year[5]. SNMP was a compromise. The IETF had two competing proposals — CMIP from the OSI camp and SNMP from a pragmatist camp around Jeffrey Case, Mark Fedor, Martin Schoffstall, and James Davin — and SNMP won on grounds of being shippable and small enough to fit on the constrained devices of the late 1980s[6].

SNMP's data model is the Management Information Base (MIB), a tree of named variables identified by Object Identifiers (OIDs) borrowed from ASN.1[7]. To check the bytes received on a switch interface, you ask the switch for the value at OID 1.3.6.1.2.1.2.2.1.10 with the appropriate interface index. The protocol is poll-based by default: the manager asks; the agent answers. SNMP traps allow the device to push asynchronous events, but the day-to-day model is polling.

By the mid-1990s, two pressures had outgrown SNMP. The first was scale: polling thousands of OIDs across hundreds of devices every minute consumed enough bandwidth and CPU to become its own problem[8]. The second was granularity: SNMP told you that an interface had transferred ten gigabytes in the last poll interval, but not between whom. Network operators wanted flow-level data. Cisco's answer was NetFlow, originally a packet-switching optimization that, around 1996, was repurposed into a telemetry export protocol[9]. NetFlow records summarized each conversational flow — five-tuple, byte count, packet count, start and end timestamps — and exported them to a collector for analysis.

The 2000s saw the rise of distributed systems and, with them, distributed tracing. Google's Dapper paper, published in 2010, formalized ideas that had been in production at Google since 2005: every request carries a trace ID; every service annotates its part of the work with spans; spans are reassembled into a causal graph after the fact[10]. Dapper inspired Zipkin (Twitter, 2012), Jaeger (Uber, 2015), and ultimately OpenTracing and OpenCensus, which merged in 2019 to become OpenTelemetry[11].

Meanwhile, the metrics world had its own quiet revolution. In 2012, two SoundCloud engineers, Matt T. Proud and Julius Volz, began work on Prometheus, modeled on Google's internal Borgmon system[12]. Prometheus made three bets that look obvious now and were contrarian then: scrape the targets rather than letting them push; store everything as a multidimensional time series keyed by labels; expose a powerful query language (PromQL) over the result. By 2018, Prometheus had become the second project (after Kubernetes) to graduate from the Cloud Native Computing Foundation[13].

The shift from management to observability is partly a shift in attitude. Management assumes you know what to look at. Observability assumes you don't. Cindy Sridharan, whose 2018 O'Reilly report popularized the framing for a generation of engineers, defined observability as a measure of how well internal states of a system can be inferred from knowledge of its external outputs[4]. The practical implication is that observability is dense, high-cardinality, and exploratory; management is sparse, low-cardinality, and confirmatory.

§ 03 · FoundationsThe Pillars Reconsidered

Practitioners commonly speak of the three pillars of observability: logs, metrics, and traces. The phrase appears to have been crystallized by Peter Bourgon in a widely cited 2017 blog post that compared the three signal types along axes of cost, queryability, and event-vs-aggregate semantics[18]. In network contexts, a fourth pillar — flow records — sits alongside metrics, with somewhat different sampling and aggregation properties.

Signal Granularity Cost model Best for Weak at
Logs Per event, free text or structured O(events); storage-bound Forensic detail, rare events Aggregation, trend lines
Metrics Pre-aggregated time series O(unique label combinations) Rates, gauges, SLO math High-cardinality detail
Traces Per request, causal graph O(spans), with sampling Latency attribution, dependencies Aggregate health
Flows Per conversation 5-tuple O(active flows), with sampling Top talkers, exfiltration, capacity Sub-flow detail, payload
Packets Per packet, full or truncated O(bytes-on-wire); expensive Protocol-level forensics Long retention, aggregation

The pillars are not interchangeable, but neither are they cleanly separable. A modern observability platform routinely converts one into another: spans get aggregated into RED metrics (Requests, Errors, Duration)[19]; flow records get rolled up into bandwidth gauges; logs get parsed into structured events that look indistinguishable from traces. The signal types describe collection patterns more than they describe ontologically different things.

Two heuristics dominate how engineers reason about what to measure. Brendan Gregg's USE method — Utilization, Saturation, Errors — was developed at Sun and Joyent for performance analysis, and applies cleanly to network resources: utilization of a link, saturation expressed as queue depth or buffer occupancy, errors as CRC or drop counts[20]. Tom Wilkie's RED method — Rate, Errors, Duration — was designed for request-driven services and maps neatly to APIs, RPCs, or any layer-7 abstraction over a network[19]. Used together, they cover most of what an operator needs to ask of a system.

§ 04 · Packet truthWireshark

If there is a ground truth in networking, it is the packet on the wire. Wireshark — released in 1998 by Gerald Combs under the name Ethereal, and renamed to Wireshark in 2006 following a trademark dispute with Combs's then-employer[14] — remains the canonical tool for inspecting that ground truth. It is open source under GPLv2, ships dissectors for over 3,000 protocols[21], and is, by some margin, the most widely deployed packet analyzer in operation.

Wireshark's architecture is worth understanding because it has shaped how an entire industry thinks about packet inspection. At the bottom sits libpcap (or its Windows equivalent, Npcap), the BSD-derived capture library originally written by the Lawrence Berkeley Lab in the early 1990s[22]. Above libpcap, Wireshark layers a dissector framework: each protocol implements a function that consumes the bytes its predecessor identified, exposes named fields, and hands off the remainder to the next dissector. The dissection tree you see in the middle pane of the Wireshark UI is literally the recursive descent of that handoff.

The display filter language is what makes Wireshark useful at scale. It is a Boolean expression language over the field tree:

Wireshark display filter examples
# All TCP traffic to or from 10.0.0.42 on port 443
tcp and (ip.src == 10.0.0.42 or ip.dst == 10.0.0.42) and tcp.port == 443

# TLS handshakes where the client offered a specific SNI
tls.handshake.extensions_server_name == "api.example.com"

# Retransmissions on any flow with non-zero window scaling
tcp.analysis.retransmission and tcp.options.wscale.shift > 0

# HTTP requests whose User-Agent contains "curl", followed by responses
http.user_agent contains "curl" or (http.response and tcp.stream eq ${stream})

For non-interactive work — which, for an operator at scale, is most work — Wireshark ships tshark, a command-line front-end that emits the same dissection but in formats suitable for piping into jq, awk, or further analysis tools:

tshark — extract TLS SNI, source IP, destination IP from a pcap
tshark -r capture.pcap \
  -Y "tls.handshake.type == 1" \
  -T fields \
  -e ip.src -e ip.dst -e tls.handshake.extensions_server_name \
  -E separator=, -E quote=d

Two architectural points are worth dwelling on. First, Wireshark performs all dissection in user space; the kernel hands it raw frames via the AF_PACKET socket family on Linux, or BPF on BSD. This is fine on a desktop and increasingly painful at 100 Gb/s, which is why production capture infrastructure usually involves a specialized NIC (Endace, Napatech) or a kernel-bypass framework like DPDK or AF_XDP feeding into a ring buffer that Wireshark or its kin later mines[23]. Second, Wireshark's dissectors have themselves been the source of many serious vulnerabilities — the CVE database lists hundreds[24] — because parsing adversarial input from arbitrary protocols is, in the general case, very hard. Most operators run Wireshark on isolated analysis hosts, not on the device that captured the traffic.

Operational note · pcap rotation

At any link speed above a gigabit, you will not capture interactively in Wireshark; you will capture with tcpdump -i eth0 -w cap-%Y%m%d-%H%M%S.pcap -G 60 -W 60 (rotating once a minute, retaining the last hour) and triage in tshark or tcpflow after the fact. The single biggest cause of "I missed the event" is forgetting that the kernel ring buffer is finite and was already overwritten before you opened the file.

§ 05 · TopologyNmap

Where Wireshark observes traffic that already exists, nmap generates probes to discover what could exist. Gordon Lyon — known by his handle Fyodor — released the first version of nmap in September 1997, in Phrack issue 51, accompanied by an essay titled "The Art of Port Scanning" that doubles as a primer on TCP/IP edge cases[25]. Twenty-eight years later, nmap is still the lingua franca of network discovery, owned by no company, written largely in C and Lua, and shipping in every major Linux distribution.

The core idea is straightforward. To learn whether a port is open, send a probe and inspect the response: a SYN that elicits a SYN-ACK indicates an open TCP port; a SYN that elicits a RST indicates a closed one; silence indicates a filtered port (or a dropped probe, which is empirically hard to distinguish). The interesting work lies in the edge cases. RFC 793, the original TCP specification, says that a closed port must respond to any segment without SYN, RST, or ACK with a RST[26]. This unlocks a family of stealthy scans — FIN, NULL, Xmas — that nmap implements and that still work against legacy hosts.

The richer feature, and the one that gives nmap most of its observability value, is OS fingerprinting. By varying TCP options, sequence numbers, window sizes, ICMP behavior, and IP TTLs across a series of probes, and comparing the responses against a database of known stacks, nmap can identify the operating system, version, and sometimes uptime of the target[27]. The fingerprint database — maintained as nmap-os-db — is built from community submissions and contains thousands of signatures.

nmap — common observability invocations
# Inventory a /24: which hosts respond, what ports, what services, what OS
sudo nmap -sS -sV -O -T4 --top-ports 1000 10.0.0.0/24 -oA inventory

# Service version detection only, against a known host
nmap -sV -p 22,80,443,3306,6379 db-01.internal

# Use the NSE (Nmap Scripting Engine) for protocol-aware checks
nmap --script ssl-enum-ciphers,http-title,smb-os-discovery -p 443,445 \
     10.0.5.0/24

# Generate XML for ingestion into an inventory pipeline
nmap -sn 10.0.0.0/16 -oX subnet-survey.xml

The Nmap Scripting Engine (NSE), introduced in 2007, turned nmap from a port scanner into a general-purpose network probe. Scripts are written in Lua and run against discovered hosts and ports; the standard distribution ships several hundred, covering everything from TLS cipher enumeration to SMB OS detection to vulnerability checks. For observability purposes, NSE makes nmap a credible alternative to commercial asset-discovery tools at zero license cost, with the obvious caveat that nmap is active — it sends probes — and is therefore detectable by, and a noise source for, intrusion detection systems[28].

A subtle but important fact about nmap, often lost on newcomers: it is its own ground truth tool for the network it traverses. The path between the scanner and the target — firewalls, NATs, load balancers, ACLs — leaves fingerprints on the responses that the scanner sees. A SYN scan against a host that answers from a different IP than it was probed on tells you about asymmetric routing. A scan where every port shows as "filtered" tells you about a default-deny firewall in front of an otherwise reachable host. Interpreting nmap output is partly a topological exercise, not just a port-level one.

§ 06 · Flow telemetryNetFlow, IPFIX, sFlow

Flow telemetry sits between packet capture and SNMP in the granularity stack. A flow record summarizes a conversation: typically the IPv4/IPv6 five-tuple (source address, destination address, source port, destination port, protocol), plus byte and packet counts, ingress and egress interfaces, ToS bits, and start and end timestamps. The flow itself is identified by the router as "all packets with the same key arriving within a timeout window."

NetFlow, the original, was developed at Cisco in 1996 by Darren Kerr and Barry Bruins, who were granted U.S. Patent 6,243,667 for the underlying flow-cache architecture[9]. The first NetFlow versions (v1, v5, v7) used fixed record formats; v5 in particular became enormously popular and is still emitted by many devices today, despite shipping no IPv6 support[29]. NetFlow v9, introduced in 2003 and documented in the informational RFC 3954, switched to a template-based format in which the collector receives a schema definition before the data, allowing arbitrary fields including IPv6, MPLS labels, and BGP attributes[30].

The IETF standardized NetFlow v9 as IPFIX (IP Flow Information Export) in 2008, with the canonical specification republished as RFC 7011 in 2013[16]. IPFIX kept v9's template model, added a public IANA registry of Information Elements, and tightened the export protocol semantics. In practice, "NetFlow v10" and "IPFIX" are the same thing, and most modern collectors accept both.

sFlow, defined in RFC 3176 in 2001 by Peter Phaal, Sonia Panchen, and Neil McKee of InMon Corporation[15], takes a different approach. Rather than maintaining a flow cache in the router, sFlow samples packets at a configured rate (commonly 1 in N), sends the sampled packet headers to a collector, and lets the collector reconstruct flows statistically. This is cheaper at high speeds — the router never has to maintain per-flow state — and provides honest statistical estimates of traffic mix, at the cost of missing low-volume flows entirely[31].

┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ NetFlow / IPFIX │ │ │ │ data plane ──► flow cache ──► export ──► collector ──► db │ │ (per-flow │ │ state in │ │ router) │ │ │ │ * Accurate per-flow byte counts │ │ * Cost grows with active-flow count │ │ * Misses nothing inside the cache timeout │ └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘ ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ sFlow │ │ │ │ data plane ──► 1-in-N sampler ──► header ──► collector │ │ (no per-flow │ │ state) │ │ │ │ * Statistical estimate of byte counts │ │ * Cost is constant in flow count │ │ * Misses small flows entirely │ └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
Figure 1 — Two philosophies of flow telemetry.

The collector side has matured into a small ecosystem. nfdump and its companion daemon nfcapd are the canonical open-source NetFlow stack, descended from the original Cisco reference tools[32]. pmacct is a more flexible alternative that speaks NetFlow, sFlow, and IPFIX simultaneously and can export aggregates to Kafka, PostgreSQL, or directly to time-series databases[33]. GoFlow2, developed by CloudFlare engineers, decodes flow records and emits them as protobuf messages onto Kafka, which has become a popular pattern at scale[34].

Configuring NetFlow v9 export on a Cisco IOS router
! Define a flow record (which fields to export)
flow record FR-IPV4
 match ipv4 source address
 match ipv4 destination address
 match transport source-port
 match transport destination-port
 match ipv4 protocol
 match interface input
 collect counter bytes
 collect counter packets
 collect timestamp sys-uptime first
 collect timestamp sys-uptime last

! Define the exporter (where to send it)
flow exporter FE-COLLECTOR
 destination 10.0.100.5
 source Loopback0
 transport udp 2055
 template data timeout 60

! Tie them together in a flow monitor
flow monitor FM-IPV4
 record FR-IPV4
 exporter FE-COLLECTOR
 cache timeout active 60
 cache timeout inactive 15

! Apply to an interface
interface GigabitEthernet0/1
 ip flow monitor FM-IPV4 input

Flow data shines at a handful of questions that nothing else answers as cheaply: who talked to whom?, how much did they send?, which prefixes are eating my transit?, is there a sudden surge of small-packet traffic to a single destination (a DDoS amplification source)? It is essential for capacity planning, peering negotiation, and detection of slow data exfiltration. It is largely useless for application-layer debugging, which is why flow telemetry is almost always paired with at least one of metrics, logs, or selective packet capture.

Flow records will tell you a hundred-gigabit elephant left the building. They will not tell you what color it was, where it came from, or what it was carrying. For that, you still need to be standing in the doorway.

§ 07 · The polling workhorseSNMP

SNMP is older than most engineers working today and is still, by a wide margin, the most deployed network management protocol in existence. Nearly every commercial switch, router, UPS, printer, server BMC, and industrial sensor ships an SNMP agent[35]. Replacements have been promised for two decades and none have wholly succeeded.

The protocol has gone through three substantive versions. SNMPv1 (RFC 1157, 1990) defined the basic Get/GetNext/Set/Trap operations and used community strings — plaintext shared secrets — for authentication[36]. SNMPv2c (RFCs 1901–1908, 1996) added GetBulk for efficient table walks and improved error codes, but kept the same community-string model, which is to say almost no security at all[37]. SNMPv3, standardized in RFCs 3411 through 3418 in 2002, added a proper User-based Security Model with HMAC authentication and DES/AES encryption, and a View-based Access Control Model for fine-grained authorization[38].

The data model — the MIB — is structured as a tree of OIDs, with branches assigned by IANA and the ISO/IETF. The Internet branch begins at 1.3.6.1 (iso.org.dod.internet); the standard MIB-II tree at 1.3.6.1.2.1 contains the universally implemented system, interface, IP, TCP, UDP, and SNMP subtrees[7]. Vendors hang their proprietary extensions off the enterprises subtree at 1.3.6.1.4.1; Cisco's branch is 9, Juniper's is 2636, Arista's is 30065.

Walking the interface table with Net-SNMP
# Walk the standard interface table — names, types, status, counters
snmpwalk -v3 -u monitor -l authPriv \
  -a SHA -A "$AUTH_PASS" \
  -x AES -X "$PRIV_PASS" \
  switch-01.internal IF-MIB::ifTable

# Get just the high-capacity (64-bit) octet counters for interface index 5
snmpget -v3 -u monitor -l authPriv -a SHA -A "$AUTH_PASS" \
  -x AES -X "$PRIV_PASS" switch-01.internal \
  IF-MIB::ifHCInOctets.5 IF-MIB::ifHCOutOctets.5

# Walk Cisco's CDP neighbor table to discover topology
snmpwalk -v2c -c public router-01 1.3.6.1.4.1.9.9.23.1.2.1.1

SNMP's enduring weaknesses are well-known. Polling at one-minute intervals — the default in most monitoring tools — gives you a sample resolution that completely misses microbursts[39]. The 32-bit counters in SNMPv1/v2c wrap in 34 seconds at 10 Gbit/s, and even the 64-bit "high-capacity" counters wrap in slightly under 47 years at 100 Gbit/s, which sounds safe until you realize that on a freshly rebooted device they reset to zero and your rate calculation goes spectacularly wrong[40]. SNMP traps over UDP are unreliable in the strict sense — they can be dropped silently — which is unfortunate for what is meant to be an alerting channel.

The modern replacement, where vendors support it, is streaming telemetry over gNMI (gRPC Network Management Interface) or NETCONF, with YANG data models[41]. Streaming telemetry pushes structured data at sub-second intervals over a persistent connection. It solves SNMP's polling problem cleanly. It also requires modern hardware and a willingness to migrate decades of MIB-shaped tooling, which is why SNMP is going to outlive most of us reading this.

§ 08 · The metrics revolutionPrometheus

Prometheus is the most consequential thing that has happened to operational monitoring in the last fifteen years. Begun at SoundCloud in 2012, released publicly in 2015, donated to the Cloud Native Computing Foundation in 2016, and graduated from the CNCF in 2018[13], Prometheus has become the default metrics layer of the cloud-native stack. It is the only metrics system that anyone in the Kubernetes ecosystem seriously argues about.

The design is deliberate and opinionated. Prometheus scrapes targets over HTTP — it pulls metrics from endpoints rather than receiving pushed metrics — because the project's authors had been burned by the failure modes of push-based systems at Google[12]. The pull model means Prometheus always knows whether its targets are reachable; a missing scrape is itself a signal. Every metric is a time series identified by a name and a set of labels; the cardinality of the label space is what makes (or breaks) a Prometheus deployment.

A minimal Prometheus exposition format
# HELP http_requests_total Total HTTP requests processed
# TYPE http_requests_total counter
http_requests_total{method="GET",path="/api/users",status="200"} 14823
http_requests_total{method="GET",path="/api/users",status="500"} 17
http_requests_total{method="POST",path="/api/users",status="201"} 942

# HELP node_network_receive_bytes_total Network bytes received
# TYPE node_network_receive_bytes_total counter
node_network_receive_bytes_total{device="eth0"} 4.2829831e+11
node_network_receive_bytes_total{device="lo"} 1.93839e+09

The query language, PromQL, is a small functional language over instant and range vectors. It does one thing — selecting and combining time series — and does it well. The classic five-line query that every Prometheus user eventually internalizes:

PromQL — rate of 5xx errors as a fraction of all requests, by service, over 5 minutes
sum by (service) (
  rate(http_requests_total{status=~"5.."}[5m])
)
/
sum by (service) (
  rate(http_requests_total[5m])
)

For network observability specifically, Prometheus is most useful when paired with the right exporters. The node_exporter exposes Linux kernel networking statistics — interface counters, TCP retransmits, conntrack utilization, socket states — as Prometheus metrics[42]. The snmp_exporter bridges existing SNMP infrastructure into Prometheus, walking configured OIDs and exposing them in the Prometheus exposition format[43]. The blackbox_exporter performs synthetic probes — HTTP, DNS, ICMP, TCP — from a known vantage and exposes latency, success, and certificate-expiry metrics[44].

Prometheus has real limitations. Its local storage is not designed for long-term retention — most deployments offload to Thanos, Cortex, Mimir, or VictoriaMetrics for multi-year retention and global query[45]. Its scrape model assumes targets are reachable from the Prometheus server, which is awkward for ephemeral workloads behind NAT and is partly addressed by the Pushgateway, an explicitly limited push intermediary. High cardinality — common when labels carry user IDs or full URLs — can blow up memory consumption in surprising ways; the project's own documentation explicitly warns against unbounded label values[46].

A note on cardinality

Cardinality is the single concept that distinguishes successful Prometheus deployments from failed ones. A counter http_requests_total with labels {method, path, status} has cardinality equal to the cross product of distinct values seen for each label. If path contains user IDs (/users/8472), and you have a million users, you have a million time series for one metric. The Prometheus authors recommend keeping per-metric cardinality under ten thousand series; large deployments routinely exceed this and pay for it in memory.[46]

§ 09 · The visualization layerGrafana

Grafana, first released by Torkel Ödegaard in January 2014 as a fork of Kibana 3, has become the dashboard layer for essentially all open-source observability stacks[47]. The project is now stewarded by Grafana Labs, a company built around the open-source core, and supports — by my last count — over eighty data source plugins, of which Prometheus, Loki (for logs), Tempo (for traces), and InfluxDB are the most operationally significant[48].

The interesting architectural feature of Grafana is that it stores essentially nothing about your data; it is a query-and-render layer over backends that themselves persist time series, logs, or traces. A Grafana dashboard is a JSON document describing panels, each panel is a query against a data source, each query is executed at render time. This means Grafana scales as well as its backends do, and that switching backends — say, from InfluxDB to Prometheus — is mostly a matter of rewriting queries.

For network observability, the standard Grafana deployment is roughly the following: Prometheus for metrics, Loki for logs (designed by Grafana Labs explicitly to be a Prometheus-shaped logs system, indexing only labels rather than full text)[49], Tempo for distributed traces, and Grafana on top to correlate among them. The unified view — clicking from a latency spike on a metrics dashboard, into the traces that contributed to that spike, into the logs of one specific failing request — is the practical realization of the three-pillars idea.

Grafana also ships alerting, with a unified alert model since version 8, that lets you express alert rules in PromQL or LogQL and route notifications to Slack, PagerDuty, OpsGenie, or arbitrary webhook[50]. Prior to the unified alerts, alerting in this ecosystem was split between Prometheus's own Alertmanager and Grafana's older dashboard-bound alerts; the unification was, in my view, overdue and is now the recommended path.

§ 10 · The commercial stackDatadog and Dynatrace

The open-source stack — Prometheus, Grafana, Loki, Tempo, OpenTelemetry, eBPF probes — is what most teams reach for first, especially in cloud-native environments. The commercial platforms occupy a different niche: they package metrics, logs, traces, and a great deal more into a single SaaS product, with the implicit deal being that you pay for someone else to operate the storage and search infrastructure, and in return you get integrations, ML-based anomaly detection, and a UI that prioritizes coverage over flexibility.

Datadog, founded in 2010 by Olivier Pomel and Alexis Lê-Quôc, went public on NASDAQ in 2019[51]. Its core product began as a metrics platform and has expanded to include APM, logs, real-user monitoring, synthetic monitoring, security monitoring (Cloud SIEM), and network performance monitoring (NPM). The Datadog Agent is a Go-based collector, open-source under Apache 2.0, that runs on hosts or as a DaemonSet in Kubernetes and ships data to Datadog's SaaS backend[52]. For network observability specifically, Datadog NPM uses eBPF probes on Linux to collect flow-level data without requiring NetFlow export, and stitches the resulting flows together with the agent's view of which processes were involved[53].

Dynatrace, founded in Austria in 2005 (originally as dynaTrace software), is the older of the two and has a different technical center of gravity[54]. Dynatrace's OneAgent is a single deployable that performs deep code-level instrumentation — bytecode injection in JVM and .NET runtimes, eBPF on Linux, native instrumentation on Windows — and reports into a backend whose distinguishing feature is the Smartscape topology and the Davis causal-inference engine[55]. Davis attempts to identify, from observed correlations and the known topology, which signal is the root cause of an incident and which signals are downstream symptoms. This is the kind of thing that is either invaluable or infuriating, depending on whether Davis happens to be right about your particular outage.

The honest assessment, free of vendor adjudication: both platforms work well for organizations whose primary observability constraint is staff time rather than license cost. Both have aggressive pricing that scales with telemetry volume in ways that surprise budget owners — Datadog's billing complexity is a well-known operational hazard, with dozens of separately metered SKUs[56] — and both are excellent at the integrations that the open-source stack still treats as someone else's problem (AWS service-by-service, CDN providers, SaaS APIs). The decision between them, and between either and the open-source stack, is almost always more about org structure than about technology.

§ 11 · The inflectionThe eBPF Era

If there is one technology that has reshaped network observability in the last decade, it is eBPF: the extended Berkeley Packet Filter, an in-kernel virtual machine that allows safe, sandboxed user-defined programs to run at hook points throughout the Linux kernel[17]. The original BPF, by Steven McCanne and Van Jacobson in 1992, was a packet-filter language for tcpdump[57]. Alexei Starovoitov rewrote it from scratch in 2014, generalizing it into a kernel-wide instrumentation framework that landed in Linux 3.18[17].

The observability story is roughly this: before eBPF, getting kernel-level visibility meant either kernel modules (dangerous, distribution-specific, easy to crash the host) or coarse-grained interfaces like /proc (cheap but limited). With eBPF, you write a small program in restricted C, compile it to BPF bytecode, attach it to a kernel hook — a tracepoint, a kprobe, an XDP hook on a NIC driver, a TC classifier — and the kernel verifier statically proves it cannot crash, then runs it in-line on every event. The program emits structured data into BPF maps, which user-space programs read.

For networking, the high-leverage hook points are:

  • XDP (eXpress Data Path) — runs before sk_buff allocation, in the driver, allowing programs to inspect, drop, or redirect packets at hundreds of millions of packets per second per core[58].
  • TC (Traffic Control) classifiers — slightly later in the stack, after sk_buff allocation; less performant than XDP but with access to richer kernel structures.
  • Socket-level hooks — attached to specific sockets, useful for tracing application-to-application traffic by PID and namespace.
  • kprobes / tracepoints — attached to specific kernel functions (e.g., tcp_retransmit_skb), used to count or sample interesting kernel events.

The practical tool that put this in everyone's hands is bcc (BPF Compiler Collection), maintained by Brendan Gregg and others, which ships dozens of ready-made eBPF programs (tcpconnect, tcpretrans, tcplife, biolatency, et cetera) that perform specific observability tasks[59]. Above bcc sits bpftrace, an awk-like DSL for one-liner eBPF programs:

bpftrace — count TCP retransmits by destination IP for 60 seconds
bpftrace -e '
  kprobe:tcp_retransmit_skb {
    $sk = (struct sock *)arg0;
    $dst = ntop(AF_INET, $sk->__sk_common.skc_daddr);
    @[$dst] = count();
  }
  interval:s:60 { exit(); }
'

At the platform level, Cilium — open-sourced by Isovalent in 2015, donated to the CNCF in 2021, graduated in 2023[60] — uses eBPF to provide the entire networking, security, and observability layer for a Kubernetes cluster. Cilium's Hubble component exposes flow-level visibility into every connection between every pod in the cluster, with identity rather than IP as the primary key, and exports the data as metrics, gRPC streams, or OpenTelemetry. This is fundamentally different from older monitoring stacks because it does not require any application-level instrumentation; the data is gathered from the kernel as the connections happen.

The downside of eBPF, such as it is, is that the toolchain is still rough around the edges and the kernel verifier is unforgiving. Programs that work on a 5.10 kernel may not load on 5.4. Pinning programs across kernel upgrades requires CO-RE (Compile Once - Run Everywhere) and the BTF (BPF Type Format) infrastructure, which is mature on recent distributions but still a sharp edge for anyone supporting long-tail kernels[61].

§ 12 · ArchitecturePractical Patterns

The accumulated practice of running observability at scale has converged on a small number of architectural patterns. None of them is universal; each makes trade-offs explicit.

The agent + control plane pattern

An agent runs on every node (host, container, or sidecar), collects signals, performs lightweight aggregation, and ships to a control plane. Datadog, Dynatrace, the OpenTelemetry Collector, Fluent Bit, and Prometheus's node_exporter all instantiate this pattern. Its strength is locality: the agent knows what is running on its host and can attach that context to every signal. Its weakness is agent sprawl: in a polyglot environment you can end up running three or four agents per node, each doing 80% of the same job.

The collector mesh pattern

Instead of a single agent per node, deploy a layer of OpenTelemetry Collectors — some as agents close to workloads, some as gateways aggregating from multiple agents — that perform protocol translation, sampling, batching, and routing[62]. The advantage is that all signals (metrics, logs, traces) flow through a uniform pipeline you can reason about; you can re-route, re-sample, or re-tag in one place. The disadvantage is operational: the collector itself is a piece of infrastructure that needs to be observed.

The tap aggregator pattern

For high-throughput packet visibility, run dedicated packet brokers (Gigamon, Arista DANZ, Ixia/Keysight Vision) that aggregate spans from production switches, filter and de-duplicate, and feed selected traffic into capture appliances and IDS sensors[63]. This is expensive, complicated, and the only way to see every packet at the spine layer in a large data center. The alternative — port-mirror everything into a Linux box running tcpdump — works fine at gigabit speeds and breaks horribly at hundred-gig.

The SLO-driven pattern

Rather than alert on every metric that crosses a threshold, derive a small set of Service Level Objectives (SLOs) for each service — typically expressed as "99.9% of requests should succeed within 200 ms, measured over a rolling 30 days" — and alert when the error budget for that SLO is being burned faster than sustainable[64]. This is the model formalized by Google's Site Reliability Engineering book and is now table stakes for any team that wants to keep its on-call rotation humane. The network expression of the same idea is to define SLOs on connectivity (loss, jitter, RTT) between named source and destination pairs, and treat anything else as a debug signal.

§ 13 · FrontierThe Encrypted Wire

For thirty years, network observability rested on a comfortable assumption: most traffic was readable. You could inspect HTTP headers, see DNS queries, identify protocols by their characteristic byte patterns. That assumption is now mostly false. As of 2024, the share of web traffic served over HTTPS is above 95% in every major browser's telemetry[65]; QUIC, which encrypts not only payload but also most of its transport metadata, accounts for roughly a third of Chromium-originated requests in Cloudflare's measurements[66]; DNS itself increasingly travels over DoH or DoT, removing what was the last reliable plaintext signal of what a host was doing[67].

This is good for users and a real problem for network observability. The middle-of-the-network observer can still see five-tuples, packet sizes, timing, and TLS handshakes (including SNI and JA3 fingerprints), but no longer sees payloads. Several responses to this have emerged:

  • Endpoint-side observability. Move the instrumentation from the wire to the process. OpenTelemetry SDKs, eBPF uprobes on TLS library symbols (SSL_read, SSL_write), and language-runtime hooks all bypass encryption by observing the plaintext before it is encrypted or after it is decrypted[68].
  • TLS interception (with consent). In corporate environments, dedicated TLS-inspecting proxies (Zscaler, Palo Alto, Squid with bump-and-splice) terminate TLS, inspect, and re-encrypt. This is operationally tractable but legally and ethically fraught in many contexts[69].
  • Encrypted Traffic Analysis (ETA). Use the metadata that is visible — packet sizes, inter-arrival times, TLS handshake fingerprints — to classify traffic and detect anomalies without decryption. ETA is an active research area; Cisco's Stealthwatch and Akamai's encrypted traffic insights are examples[70].

None of these is fully satisfying. Endpoint instrumentation is invasive and may not be possible on third-party software. Interception breaks the security model that justified encryption in the first place. ETA produces probabilistic classifications that are good enough for capacity planning and bad enough that they shouldn't be the only signal feeding a security operations center.

The history of network observability is a history of declining payload visibility. Each generation of operators has had to develop sharper tools for inferring what they used to be able to simply read.

§ 14 · CodaWhere This Goes Next

Three trends are visible from the current vantage point, with varying degrees of confidence.

Streaming telemetry will, eventually, retire SNMP polling. Modern device platforms — Cisco IOS XR and NX-OS, Juniper Junos, Arista EOS, Cumulus Linux, SONiC — all support YANG-modeled telemetry over gNMI or NETCONF. The tools to consume it (OpenConfig, gnmic, Telegraf with the gNMI input) are mature[71]. What is missing is operator inertia, training, and the embarrassing fact that some commercial monitoring platforms still treat gNMI as a second-class citizen behind SNMP. Give it another decade.

OpenTelemetry will absorb the instrumentation layer. The competitive landscape of telemetry SDKs has, since 2019, been collapsing toward OTel as the single neutral standard. Backends — Datadog, Dynatrace, New Relic, Honeycomb, every open-source option — accept OTLP. Auto-instrumentation libraries exist for every major runtime. The remaining hold-outs are mostly vendor-specific exporters that exist for historical reasons[72]. For new development, OTel is now the default-correct answer.

eBPF will continue eating the world below the application layer. Every meaningful innovation in Linux networking observability in the last five years has been eBPF-based: Cilium for networking, Pixie for application-level introspection, Inspektor Gadget for ad-hoc debugging, Tetragon for security observability[73]. The fundamental shift is that observability is no longer something you bolt on; it is something the kernel offers as a service, configured per workload and revisable at runtime. This is, in retrospect, what observability was always trying to be.

None of these trends abolishes the older tools. SNMP will outlive most observability platforms built on top of it. NetFlow will keep flowing from devices that were shipping NetFlow when Prometheus was a slide deck. Wireshark will still be open when the next outage comes. The discipline accumulates rather than replaces, which is why anyone serious about network observability ends up fluent in all of it: the packet, the flow, the OID, the time series, the trace, the eBPF program, the dashboard, and — most importantly — the question they didn't think to ask before they built the thing.

The transparent wire is a destination, not a state. We are getting there one signal at a time.
