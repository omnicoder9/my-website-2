01 The sector as a target

A life-safety system wearing a target

Healthcare is the only critical-infrastructure sector where a successful intrusion can directly kill the people it serves. That fact — not the volume of records or the size of the budgets — is what makes its security problem distinctive, and what the rest of this article keeps returning to.

The United States designates the Healthcare and Public Health (HPH) sector as one of sixteen critical-infrastructure sectors whose incapacitation would have a debilitating effect on national security, economic security, or public health and safety.[1] Each sector has a federal Sector Risk Management Agency; for HPH that agency is the Department of Health and Human Services (HHS), which coordinates security and resilience across providers, payers, manufacturers, laboratories, and public-health authorities.[2] In April 2024 the sector framework established by the 2013 Presidential Policy Directive 21 was superseded by a National Security Memorandum that reaffirmed the sixteen sectors and named CISA as national coordinator for their security and resilience.[3] HPH does not sit in isolation: federal risk analysis treats the sectors as an interdependent web of national critical functions, so the question is never only “how secure is a hospital” but “what else fails when the hospital does.”[4]

The sector is an attractive target for converging reasons. Its data is unusually rich — a single record can carry clinical history, insurance identifiers, Social Security numbers, and payment data, a complete identity rather than a single stolen number.[5] Its operations are unusually time-critical, which raises the leverage of any actor who can interrupt them. And its attack surface is unusually broad and uneven, spanning bleeding-edge cloud services and decades-old embedded devices in the same building. The result is a sector that breaks records for the wrong reasons: 2023 set a high-water mark with 725 large breaches reported to regulators, exposing roughly 133 million records.[6]

02 The technology terrain

What there is to defend

Understanding the risk means understanding the estate. Healthcare technology is not one system but several overlapping ones, each with its own threat profile and its own reason for being hard to secure.

Clinical information systems and ePHI

At the center sit electronic health record (EHR) systems and the electronic protected health information (ePHI) they hold. These systems are availability-critical — clinicians cannot safely order medication, view allergies, or reconcile doses without them — which is precisely why encrypting or disabling them is such effective extortion. The legal regime built around ePHI, the HIPAA Security Rule, organizes safeguards into administrative, physical, and technical controls and demands that ePHI be protected against reasonably anticipated threats to its confidentiality, integrity, and availability.[7]

The clearinghouse and payment layer

Behind the clinical systems is a financial plumbing layer most patients never see: clearinghouses that verify eligibility, adjudicate claims, route prior authorizations, and move payments between providers, pharmacies, and insurers. This layer is highly concentrated. A single processor, Change Healthcare, handles on the order of fifteen billion transactions a year and touches roughly one in every three U.S. patient records — a concentration that turns one company's outage into a national event.[5]

Connected medical devices (IoMT)

Infusion pumps, imaging systems, patient monitors, and implantable devices increasingly run general-purpose software and sit on hospital networks — the Internet of Medical Things. Many run long-lived, infrequently patched embedded operating systems, and their safety certification can make patching slow or contractually fraught. Recognizing this, the sector's own practice library devotes an entire dedicated control set to medical-device security among its hundreds of controls.[8] Since 2023 the U.S. has had statutory authority to require security in these devices before they reach the market, discussed in the mitigation section below.[9]

Telehealth, cloud, and the third-party perimeter

Telehealth platforms, cloud-hosted clinical applications, and a long tail of vendors (“business associates” in HIPAA's language) extend the perimeter far beyond any single organization's walls. This matters disproportionately: in recent record-setting years, a majority of the largest healthcare breaches traced back not to the named provider or plan but to one of its business associates, making third-party and supply-chain exposure the sector's dominant structural weakness.[6]

03 Threats and consequences

Five threats, and a body count

The federally convened 405(d) program distilled the sector's threat landscape into five categories that have proven durable: social engineering and phishing; ransomware; loss or theft of equipment and data; insider misuse, whether accidental or intentional; and attacks against connected medical devices.[10] These map onto two kinds of consequence — the loss of information, and the loss of care — and it is the second that sets healthcare apart.

From breach to bodily harm

The link between cyberattacks and patient harm is no longer anecdotal. Survey research of healthcare delivery organizations found that ransomware attacks routinely produced longer lengths of stay, delayed procedures and tests, and diversions of patients to other facilities, with around one in five organizations reporting that an attack had an adverse effect on patient mortality.[11] A parallel study reported that organizations suffering the most common attack types — ransomware, cloud compromise, supply-chain attacks, and business-email compromise — experienced increased mortality at comparable rates, against a backdrop of nearly ninety percent of organizations sustaining an average of roughly one attack per week.[12] Follow-up work confirmed the pattern persisted, with complications during procedures rising for nearly half of respondents.[13]

The strongest evidence comes from work linking a database of hospital ransomware attacks to Medicare claims. It found that among patients already admitted when an attack begins, in-hospital mortality rises by an estimated 35 to 41 percent, while hospital volume falls 17 to 26 percent in the first week as the facility diverts and defers care.[14] The same research program documented that ransomware attacks on U.S. care-delivery organizations grew steadily across 2016–2021 and increasingly disrupted care directly rather than merely exposing data.[15] The phrase the sector has adopted — cyber safety is patient safety — is, on this evidence, literal.

04 What it looks like

Case histories

Three incidents and a short lineage show how the abstract risks above behave in practice — and how a single missing control can scale into a national disruption.

Change Healthcare · 2024 · the largest healthcare breach in history

On 21 February 2024, UnitedHealth Group detected ransomware across the systems of its Change Healthcare subsidiary and isolated the affected environment from connected systems.[16] Investigators later established that intruders had entered around 12 February through a remote-access portal that lacked multi-factor authentication, exfiltrated roughly four terabytes of data, and then triggered encryption nine days later — a textbook double-extortion sequence.[17] A U.S. senator characterized the root cause bluntly as something that “could have been stopped with cybersecurity 101.”[18] The clearinghouse outage cascaded across the country: claims and payment systems stalled for roughly two months, providers reverted to manual billing, and the company advanced more than six billion dollars in interest-free funding to keep practices solvent.[19] A twenty-two-million-dollar ransom did not prevent the data from surfacing, and a second group later attempted to extort the company again.[20] The final tally reached approximately 190–192.7 million affected individuals — over half the U.S. population — at a company-reported cost of about 3.1 billion dollars.[5, 21]

Ascension · 2024 · care under paper

In May 2024 the 140-hospital Ascension system was struck by ransomware attributed to the Black Basta group. Clinicians were locked out of systems that tracked medications, doses, and adverse-reaction data; the organization fell back to paper, which slowed operations and, staff reported, created conditions for medication and coordination errors.[22, 23] The breach was ultimately reported as affecting about 5.6 million individuals.[24]

CommonSpirit · 2022 · scale of a multistate system

A 2022 ransomware attack on CommonSpirit Health disrupted more than a hundred facilities across its multistate footprint, illustrating how consolidation concentrates blast radius: one intrusion now reaches dozens of hospitals at once.[22]

The lineage is longer than the headlines. The 2015 Anthem breach exposed the personal information of roughly 78.8 million people; the February 2016 attack on Hollywood Presbyterian Medical Center was an early demonstration that ransomware could take a hospital offline; and the May 2017 WannaCry worm crippled parts of the United Kingdom's National Health Service, a warning the U.S. sector watched closely.[25]

05 Cross-sector cascade

Healthcare as a node, not an island

Because the sixteen sectors are interdependent, a compromise in HPH rarely stays in HPH; and conversely, HPH depends on the others to function. Federal policy frames this through national critical functions — the cross-cutting services whose disruption ripples outward regardless of which sector is hit first.[4] The Change Healthcare event is the clearest demonstration to date that a single health-sector node can transmit failure across the economy.[5]

Financial Services

The clearinghouse layer is a financial system. When it stopped, provider revenue cycles seized, payrolls were threatened, and small practices approached insolvency — a health-sector outage manifesting as a liquidity crisis.[19]

Emergency Services

Ransomware-driven ambulance diversion and facility transfers shift load onto neighboring hospitals and EMS, degrading regional emergency response well beyond the victim organization.[11]

Communications & IT

Care delivery now assumes connectivity. Outages in communications or IT services, or shared-vendor compromise, propagate directly into clinical operations — and health incidents conversely consume those services during response.[4]

Chemical & Pharma supply

Drug and biologic manufacturing and distribution sit at the seam of the Chemical and Healthcare sectors; disruption upstream becomes a shortage at the bedside, and a health-data compromise can expose manufacturing partners.[1]

Water & Wastewater

Hospitals are critical-load customers for water, wastewater, and power. A utility incident is a clinical-continuity incident; the dependency runs the other way too, since public-health authorities monitor and respond to water-borne hazards.[1]

Energy

Sustained loss of grid power forces hospitals onto generators with finite fuel and runtime, turning an energy-sector event into a direct life-safety problem for the most fragile patients.[4]

06 The mitigation stack

Frameworks, rules, and controls

U.S. healthcare security is governed not by one framework but by a layered stack: a binding regulatory floor, voluntary sector-specific practice libraries, device-market authority, and an information-sharing ecosystem. The pieces are increasingly designed to map onto one another.

The regulatory floor: HIPAA and its overhaul

The HIPAA Security Rule remains the binding baseline for covered entities and business associates. Its implementation companion, NIST Special Publication 800-66 Revision 2 — finalized in February 2024 to replace the 2008 edition — gives organizations a structured way to satisfy the rule.[7] Crucially, Revision 2 crosswalks each Security Rule standard to the NIST Cybersecurity Framework 2.0 and the SP 800-53 control catalogue, so a healthcare program can speak the same control language as the rest of the security ecosystem.[26] CSF 2.0 itself added a sixth function, Govern, elevating executive accountability for cyber risk alongside the original Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, and Recover.[27]

The floor is set to rise. In late December 2024 OCR issued a Notice of Proposed Rulemaking — published in the Federal Register on 6 January 2025 — proposing the first major overhaul of the Security Rule in roughly two decades.[28, 29] The proposal would, among other things, remove the long-standing distinction between “required” and “addressable” implementation specifications — effectively making controls organizations had treated as optional into baseline obligations — and it solicited comment on how the rule should handle artificial intelligence, quantum computing, and virtual reality.[30] In practice that means measures such as encryption of ePHI and multi-factor authentication would become broadly mandatory rather than discretionary.[31] The roughly 400-page proposal drew thousands of comments and sharp industry pushback over cost and timeline, and its fate remains uncertain; as of late 2025 it remained on the regulatory agenda with finalization targeted for 2026.[32, 33]

Voluntary sector practice: 405(d) HICP and the HPH CPGs

Mandated by Section 405(d) of the Cybersecurity Act of 2015, the industry-led 405(d) program publishes Health Industry Cybersecurity Practices (HICP): a main document mapping the five threats to ten mitigating practices, plus technical volumes tailored to small, medium, and large organizations.[34] The framework comprises hundreds of controls, including a dedicated set for connected medical devices, and is distributed free with companion training.[8]

In January 2024 HHS layered on Healthcare and Public Health Cybersecurity Performance Goals (CPGs): ten essential goals defining a foundational floor and ten enhanced goals defining a more mature target state.[35] The CPGs were built on CISA's cross-sector performance goals and informed by HICP and the NIST framework, and they map directly onto the attacks most commonly seen against U.S. hospitals.[6] They are voluntary today, but HHS has signaled intent to make comparable standards enforceable.[36] The administration's FY2025 budget paired this with a carrot-and-stick proposal: roughly 800 million dollars to help around 2,000 high-need hospitals reach essential practices, alongside penalties for hospitals that fail to adopt them.[37]

The device market: FDA Section 524B

The Food and Drug Omnibus Reform Act of 2022 added Section 524B to the Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act, effective 29 March 2023, giving the FDA explicit authority over device cybersecurity at the point of market authorization.[38] Manufacturers of “cyber devices” must now demonstrate, in premarket submissions, the ability to monitor and patch postmarket vulnerabilities, a secure development process, a coordinated vulnerability-disclosure process, and a software bill of materials.[9] Updated final guidance issued in June 2025 pushed these from advisory to enforceable, with insufficient documentation now a basis to reject a submission.[39]

Controls and information sharing

Beneath the frameworks, the controls that would have blunted the headline incidents are unglamorous: multi-factor authentication on every remote-access path, encryption of ePHI at rest and in transit, network segmentation to contain blast radius, tested and isolated backups, disciplined vulnerability and identity management, and third-party risk assessment.[8] The Change Healthcare post-mortem turned on the absence of the very first item.[17] Around the controls sits an information-sharing ecosystem — HHS's Health Sector Cybersecurity Coordination Center (HC3) and the member-driven Health Information Sharing and Analysis Center (Health-ISAC) — that distributes threat intelligence so a tactic used against one organization can be defended across many.[6]

07 Open problems

What is still unsolved

Even a fully implemented mitigation stack leaves the sector with structural problems that no current framework closes. These are where research, investment, and policy attention are most needed.

Legacy and unpatchable devices. A large installed base of long-lived medical devices predates Section 524B and will remain in clinical use for years. Securing the market for new devices does nothing for the infusion pump bought a decade ago, and compensating controls — segmentation, monitoring — are imperfect substitutes for a device that simply cannot be patched.[8]

Concentration and third-party risk. The Change Healthcare event exposed a systemic fragility: a small number of shared processors and vendors have become single points of failure for the entire sector, and the dominance of business-associate breaches shows the perimeter has effectively moved off-premises.[5, 6] How to govern systemically important health entities — and how to build redundancy into the clearinghouse layer — remains an open policy question.

The under-resourced tail. Rural, critical-access, and small organizations face the same threats as large systems with a fraction of the staff and budget. The federal incentive proposals acknowledge this gap directly, but funding at the scale proposed reaches only a subset of high-need hospitals.[37] The voluntary-versus-mandatory debate is, at bottom, an argument about whether organizations that cannot afford security should be compelled to fund it anyway.[32]

Regulatory burden versus protection. The pushback against the Security Rule overhaul is not merely self-interested: a rule that imposes prescriptive controls on every regulated entity regardless of size risks diverting scarce clinical-IT resources toward documentation rather than defense.[33] Calibrating obligations to risk and capacity, without creating loopholes, is unresolved.

Artificial intelligence, both ways. AI is entering clinical workflows and the threat landscape simultaneously. The Security Rule rulemaking explicitly asked how the regulation should treat AI and quantum computing, an acknowledgment that the legal framework is reasoning about technologies whose security properties are not yet well understood.[30]

Measurement. The sector still lacks robust, standardized measurement of whether specific controls actually reduce patient harm. The mortality research is compelling but rests on a relatively small number of attacks and survey self-report; building rigorous, prospective evidence linking security investment to clinical outcomes would let the sector spend its limited dollars where they save the most lives.[14, 13]

08 Outlook

A sector being forced to mature

The trajectory is toward convergence and enforcement. Voluntary practice libraries (HICP), performance goals (CPGs), an implementation companion (SP 800-66r2), and a control catalogue (CSF 2.0 / SP 800-53) are being deliberately mapped onto each other so that a single program can satisfy many readers, while the binding floor — the HIPAA Security Rule — and the device market — Section 524B — are both moving from discretion toward obligation.[7, 38] The open questions are no longer about which controls work — multi-factor authentication, encryption, segmentation, and backups are not in dispute — but about who pays, how concentration risk is governed, and how to protect the under-resourced organizations and legacy devices that the market will not fix on its own.[37, 5] The sector's own slogan sets the standard against which all of it will be judged: in healthcare, the measure of security is not records protected but patients kept safe.
