Animal Ingredients
Hidden in Plain Sight

From the gelatin in your morning vitamins to the shark-liver squalene in your sunscreen, the animal kingdom permeates consumer goods in ways that most labels will never tell you.

Published May 9, 2026  ·  by Nicholas Knapp

The Invisible Animal Economy

When a person decides to go vegan, the first instinct is to read labels. They scan for obvious offenders — milk, eggs, beef, chicken — and feel satisfied when none appear. What they rarely anticipate is the vast infrastructure of derivative, processed, and chemically transformed animal materials that permeate modern consumer goods, often under names so technical or innocuous that they register as neutral ingredients rather than animal products.

This is not accidental. The modern food system is built on a principle of total-carcass utilisation: virtually every part of a slaughtered animal is processed into a marketable byproduct. Bone, blood, skin, connective tissue, organs, feathers, fat — all are refined, chemically treated, and redistributed across supply chains serving food, pharmaceutical, cosmetics, and industrial manufacturing sectors.[1] The result is a global market for animal-derived ingredients worth hundreds of billions of dollars annually, operating largely below the level of consumer awareness.

This article provides a systematic reference guide for advocates, consumers, and researchers. We survey the most prevalent hidden animal ingredients across two domains: food additives and processing aids, and non-food consumer products. For each ingredient, we describe its biological origin, modes of use, regulatory status, label transparency issues, and the structural levers activists can engage to reduce or eliminate its use. A strategies section and curated resource list follow the ingredient profiles.

Part I — Food & Pharmaceutical

Food Additives & Processing Aids

Food additives serve functional roles — gelling, clarifying, colouring, softening, preserving — and are assessed by regulatory bodies such as the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) and the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA). Many are derived from animal tissues. Others are processing aids: substances used during manufacture that need not appear on ingredient labels because they are not considered present in the final product in significant quantities. This regulatory loophole is one of the primary mechanisms by which animal ingredients remain invisible to consumers.[6]

1. Gelatin always animal

Gelatin E441 · CAS 9000-70-8 ▲ Always animal-derived

Gelatin is a collagen hydrolysate produced by partial hydrolysis of collagen from the skin, bones, and connective tissue of pigs (porcine gelatin) and cattle (bovine gelatin), with smaller quantities from fish and poultry.[7] Structurally, it is a mixture of proteins and peptides with gelling, thickening, and film-forming properties unmatched by most alternatives at equivalent cost.

Its ubiquity in the pharmaceutical sector is particularly consequential for vegans: the vast majority of hard and soft capsules in the supplement and pharmaceutical industries are gelatin-based. EFSA estimates that gelatin constitutes the dominant encapsulation material globally.[8] In food, it is the gelling agent in marshmallows, gummy confections, panna cotta, and aspic, and appears in reduced-fat spreads and yoghurts as a stabiliser. It is also used as a clarifying agent in some wine and fruit juice production.

Found in Gummy vitamins & supplements · capsule shells · marshmallows · jelly sweets · wine & juice (fining) · panna cotta · mousse · reduced-fat dairy products

2. Isinglass processing aid

Isinglass No E-number · processing aid ▲ Always animal-derived

Isinglass is a form of collagen derived from the dried swim bladders (gas bladders) of fish, most commonly tropical species including Beluga sturgeon, though basa, carp, and others are also used. It is used in the clarification (fining) of beer and some wines, where it binds negatively charged yeast particles and sediment, which then sink and are removed.[9]

Because isinglass functions as a processing aid rather than a food additive, it need not appear on ingredient labels in most jurisdictions, including the European Union and the United States. While the beer industry argues that isinglass is removed before packaging, residual collagen fragments have been detected in finished beverages.[10] Pressure from vegan consumers has driven many craft breweries toward bentonite clay, Irish moss, or centrifugation as alternatives.

Found in Traditionally brewed ales (especially UK cask ales) · some white wines · fruit ciders

3. Carmine / Cochineal Extract always animal

Carmine / Cochineal Extract E120 · CI 75470 · Natural Red 4 ▲ Always animal-derived

Carmine (also listed as cochineal, cochineal extract, carminic acid, or E120) is a brilliant red dye derived from the dried bodies of female Dactylopius coccus, a scale insect native to Central and South America that feeds on Opuntia cacti. Approximately 70,000 insects are required to produce one kilogram of dye.[3]

Carminic acid constitutes approximately 17–24% of the dried insect body weight.[11] It is one of the most heat- and light-stable natural red pigments available, which has sustained its commercial use despite documented cases of severe allergic reactions, including anaphylaxis, following consumption or skin contact.[12] In 2011, the FDA mandated that carmine and cochineal extract be explicitly listed by name on food and cosmetic labels — a labelling win driven partly by advocacy from allergy groups and vegan organisations.[13] The EU requires listing as "E120" or "cochineal" in ingredient lists. However, the label term "natural colour" in some contexts may still obscure its origin in less regulated markets.

Found in Yoghurt · fruit juices · lipstick & blush · maraschino cherries · artificially coloured sausages · some medications & confectionery

4. Shellac always animal

Shellac E904 · "confectioner's glaze" ▲ Always animal-derived

Shellac is a resinous secretion produced by the female lac insect (Kerria lacca), harvested from the branches of host trees predominantly in India and Thailand. Approximately 300,000 insects are killed per kilogram of shellac produced, as harvesting requires scraping the crusted resin — which encases the insects — from branches.[14]

In the food industry, shellac is used as a glazing and coating agent on fresh citrus fruits (to replace natural wax lost during washing), chocolate confections, and pharmaceutical tablets, where it serves as an enteric coating. On food labels, it is frequently listed as "confectioner's glaze," "pharmaceutical glaze," or simply "glazing agent," with the animal origin entirely obscured. On fresh produce, it may not require labelling at all.

Found in Waxed apples & citrus fruit · chocolate-coated sweets · some tablet coatings · furniture polish · nail varnish

5. Bone Char Processing processing aid

Bone Char No E-number · processing aid ▲ Always animal-derived

Bone char (also called boneblack or ivory black) is a granular material produced by charring animal bones — predominantly cattle bones — at temperatures between 400°C and 500°C in an oxygen-limited environment. The resulting activated carbon is used as a decolourising and deashing filter in cane sugar refining. The bone char is not an ingredient in the conventional sense; it acts as a filter medium through which sugar syrup passes, removing coloured impurities and achieving the characteristic whiteness of refined cane sugar.[5]

No bone char material remains in the final sugar product, but its use is ethically significant to many vegans as it is a direct product of animal slaughter. Beet sugar (and organic cane sugar) does not require bone char processing and can be used as alternatives. Refined sugar may originate from cane or beet sources, but manufacturers rarely specify which on packaging, making avoidance difficult without direct company inquiry.

Found in Refined white cane sugar · many packaged foods using white sugar · some brown sugars (if derived from refined white sugar)

6. Rennet origin varies

Rennet / Chymosin No E-number · enzyme ◆ Origin varies by source

Traditional animal rennet is extracted from the stomach lining (abomasum) of pre-weaned ruminants, principally calves, lambs, and kids, and contains the proteolytic enzyme chymosin (rennin), which cleaves κ-casein to cause milk coagulation — the first step in cheesemaking.[15] The slaughter of young calves has historically been integral to this process.

Fermentation-produced chymosin (FPC), derived from recombinant expression of the calf chymosin gene in microbial hosts including Aspergillus niger and Kluyveromyces lactis, now accounts for approximately 80–90% of chymosin used in global cheesemaking and carries no animal slaughter at the point of enzyme production.[16] However, the vegan status of FPC remains contested: most mainstream vegan organisations consider it acceptable on the grounds that it contains no animal material, while some stricter interpretations reject it due to its derivation from an animal-originating gene. Traditional animal rennet remains widely used in artisan and protected-designation cheeses (e.g., Parmigiano-Reggiano, Manchego). Labels rarely distinguish rennet type.

Found in Hard & semi-hard cheeses · processed cheese products · rennet-set cottage cheese

7. L-Cysteine origin often unlisted

L-Cysteine E920 · amino acid ◆ Often animal-derived (feathers, hair)

L-Cysteine is a semi-essential amino acid used in the baking industry as a dough conditioner and reducing agent: it weakens gluten disulphide bonds, improving dough extensibility and reducing mixing time. It is listed on EU and US labels simply as "L-cysteine" or "E920," with no indication of origin.

A substantial proportion of commercial L-cysteine is derived from hydrolysis of keratin-rich animal materials, chiefly poultry feathers and — historically, particularly in Asia — human hair collected from barbershops and hair salons.[17] Synthetic and microbially fermented L-cysteine is commercially available and increasingly preferred in Western markets, but the supply chain remains opaque, and manufacturers often switch sources without reformulation notification. The origin of E920 is almost never disclosed on product labels, making avoidance reliant on direct manufacturer inquiry or sourcing from certified vegan bakeries.

Found in Commercial bread & rolls · pizza dough · some crackers · fast-food buns · baked goods with extended shelf life

8. Mono- and Diglycerides origin ambiguous

Mono- and Diglycerides of Fatty Acids E471 · emulsifier ◆ Animal or plant — origin rarely declared

Mono- and diglycerides (MDG) are partial glycerol esters of fatty acids, produced by glycerolysis of fats and oils. They are among the most widely used food emulsifiers, improving texture, preventing staling, and stabilising fat-water interfaces in a broad range of products. Their fatty acid chains can originate from any animal or vegetable fat source — palm oil, soybean oil, tallow (beef fat), and lard (pork fat) being common industrial sources.[18]

There is no regulatory requirement in most jurisdictions to declare the source of the underlying fat on food labels. A product labelled "E471" or "mono- and diglycerides of fatty acids" may be derived from pork lard or palm oil with equal plausibility. Because they are synthesised by partial esterification, they technically contain ester-bound fatty acids rather than free glycerol — which creates a regulatory anomaly in which products labelled "dairy-free" or even "vegan" may contain MDG derived from animal fat if the certifier does not audit the specific source.

Found in Margarine & spreads · commercial bread · peanut butter (to prevent oil separation) · ice cream · whipped toppings · non-dairy creamers

9. Vitamin D3 (Cholecalciferol) origin often unlisted

Vitamin D3 (Cholecalciferol) E1141 · nutrient ◆ Typically from lanolin (sheep wool)

Vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol) is conventionally derived from lanolin, a waxy substance secreted by sebaceous glands in sheep skin and recovered from raw wool during the scouring process. Lanolin is subjected to UV irradiation to convert 7-dehydrocholesterol — which is present in lanolin — into cholecalciferol, the bioidentical form of vitamin D produced by human skin upon sunlight exposure.[19]

Vitamin D3 is used as a fortification nutrient in a wide range of products — plant-based milks, breakfast cereals, orange juice, infant formula, and supplementary vitamins — yet the wool-derived origin is essentially never disclosed on consumer labels. The fortified product may be positioned as vegan-friendly (as in oat milk) while containing lanolin-derived D3. Lichen-derived vitamin D3 and UV-irradiated mushroom-derived vitamin D2 (ergocalciferol) exist as plant-based alternatives, with lichen D3 now certified vegan by major certifying bodies and increasingly adopted by supplement manufacturers.

Found in Plant-based milks (oat, almond, soy) · breakfast cereals · orange juice · infant formula · multivitamins · some breads

10. Omega-3 Fatty Acids from Fish Oil often animal

Omega-3 (EPA/DHA from fish oil) DHA · EPA · functional nutrient ▲ Often animal-derived (fish)

Long-chain omega-3 fatty acids — specifically eicosapentaenoic acid (EPA) and docosahexaenoic acid (DHA) — have been added to a wide range of fortified foods on the basis of cardiovascular and cognitive health claims. Conventional sources are marine fish oil, typically derived from anchovy, sardine, and herring, and from krill oil. Approximately 18 million metric tonnes of small pelagic fish are harvested globally for fish oil and fishmeal annually, contributing to overfishing of key forage fish species with cascading ecosystem effects.[20]

Algae-derived DHA and EPA — produced by fermentation of microalgae such as Schizochytrium spp. — is the original biosynthetic source of omega-3 fatty acids in the marine food chain (fish accumulate EPA/DHA by consuming algae and plankton). Algal omega-3 is nutritionally equivalent and is the basis of all certified-vegan omega-3 supplements. However, fortified conventional foods (including some infant formulas) typically use fish-derived EPA/DHA, which is rarely highlighted on labels beyond the generic term "omega-3."

Found in Some infant formulas · fortified eggs (from hens fed fish oil) · certain orange juices · prenatal vitamins · some pet foods marketed for humans

11. Collagen Peptides always animal

Hydrolysed Collagen / Collagen Peptides No E-number · protein supplement ▲ Always animal-derived

Hydrolysed collagen (sold as collagen peptides or collagen protein) is produced by enzymatic or acid hydrolysis of collagen-rich animal tissues: bovine hides and bones, porcine skin, and fish skin and scales. The global collagen market — driven substantially by wellness industry marketing around skin, joint, and gut health — was valued at approximately $8.3 billion in 2022, with projected growth to $16.7 billion by 2030.[21]

Collagen is increasingly added to functional foods and beverages — protein bars, coffee creamers, bone broth powders — where it may be listed as "hydrolysed collagen," "collagen peptides," "gelatin hydrolysate," or "collagen protein." No plant-based equivalent exists with identical biochemistry, as collagen is a uniquely animal protein. Brands have begun marketing synthetic or yeast-derived "vegan collagen boosters" — these are compounds claimed to support endogenous collagen synthesis and do not contain collagen protein itself.

Found in Protein bars & powders · bone broth products · flavoured coffees · some beauty drinks · gelatin-based desserts

12. Animal-Derived Enzymes origin varies

Animal-Derived Enzymes (Lipase, Pepsin, Pancreatin) No E-number · processing aids ◆ Source varies by enzyme and product

Several commercially important food enzymes are traditionally obtained from animal glandular and pancreatic tissue. Lipase, used to develop flavour in certain hard cheeses (including some Italian-style varieties), is derived from the pharyngeal glands of calves and kids, or from porcine pancreas.[22] Pepsin — a protease used in rennet preparations and some digestive supplement formulations — is extracted from porcine gastric mucosa. Pancreatin, a complex of digestive enzymes used pharmaceutically and in some food applications, is derived from hog and ox pancreas.

These enzymes are frequently classified as processing aids rather than additives, exempt from label declaration. Microbial and recombinant alternatives exist for many applications — fungal lipase, recombinant chymosin — but the shift is incomplete, and legacy formulations continue to rely on animal sources.

Found in Aged Italian cheeses · some flavoured cheese powders · digestive enzyme supplements · some baking powders
⚠ Labelling gap: processing aids

Under both EU Regulation (EC) No 1333/2008 and US 21 CFR regulations, processing aids that do not perform a technological function in the finished food need not appear on the ingredient label. This exemption covers isinglass, bone char, most enzymes, and some uses of gelatin. The practical effect is that a product can be entirely "clean label" from a regulatory standpoint while having passed through multiple animal-derived substances during manufacture.[6]

Part II — Personal Care & Industrial

Non-Food Products: The Everyday Animal Economy

Beyond the food supply, animal-derived materials are deeply embedded in personal care, cosmetics, cleaning products, textiles, adhesives, and industrial manufacturing. The same dynamics apply: ingredients may be listed under technical or scientific names with no indication of animal origin, and many function as processing aids or carrier compounds that attract no labelling obligation.

1. Stearic Acid origin varies

Stearic Acid E570 · CAS 57-11-4 ◆ Animal or plant — origin not declared

Stearic acid is a saturated C18 fatty acid found in animal and vegetable fats. Commercially, it is produced by hydrolysis of tallow (rendered beef or pork fat) or hydrogenated plant oils (shea, palm, soy). When derived from tallow — which is the conventional industrial source due to its naturally high stearic acid content — it is a direct slaughterhouse byproduct.[1]

Stearic acid is used as an emollient and viscosity agent in soaps, shampoos, shaving creams, and skin care; as a lubricant and mould-release agent in plastics and rubber manufacturing; as a stabiliser in PVC; and as a tablet lubricant (magnesium stearate) in pharmaceuticals. Its near-universal industrial presence makes complete avoidance practically challenging, but certified-vegan products specify plant-derived stearic acid.

Found in Soap · shampoo · shaving cream · skin lotion · rubber & tyre manufacturing · PVC stabilisers · tablet coatings

2. Glycerin (Glycerol) origin varies

Glycerin / Glycerol E422 · CAS 56-81-5 ◆ Animal or plant — origin not declared

Glycerol is a triol alcohol that is the backbone of all triglycerides (fats and oils). Commercially, glycerol is recovered as a co-product of fat transesterification during biodiesel production (plant-based) or saponification (soap making from tallow), or produced by direct hydrolysis of animal fats in oleochemical processing. The oleochemical industry — processing fatty acids from both animal and vegetable sources — produces glycerol from both origins without distinction.[18]

Glycerol is a humectant (moisture-retaining agent) present in virtually all personal care products, toothpastes, liquid soaps, cough syrups, and as a food additive in baked goods and confectionery. Plant-derived glycerol — primarily from palm, rapeseed, and soy biodiesel production — is readily available and increasingly preferred by manufacturers responding to vegan consumer demand, but the labelling makes the origin indistinguishable.

Found in Toothpaste · moisturisers · cough syrup · soap · baked goods · capsule shells · e-cigarette liquid

3. Lanolin always animal

Lanolin / Wool Wax / Wool Grease No E-number · CAS 8006-54-0 ▲ Always animal-derived

Lanolin is a complex mixture of wax esters, hydroxy esters, and free alcohols and acids secreted by the sebaceous glands of sheep and recovered from raw wool during scouring. It functions as a water-in-oil emulsifier and emollient with exceptional occlusive properties, used widely in lip balms, nipple creams, nappy rash ointments, shoe polishes, and industrial rust-prevention compounds.[19]

Lanolin is obtained from living sheep during shearing. However, vegan organisations including The Vegan Society do not consider it vegan, on the grounds that the wool and lanolin industries are inseparable from exploitative husbandry practices, selective breeding for excess wool production, and the ultimate slaughter of the animals. Lanolin is also the primary commercial source of cholesterol used in vitamin D3 synthesis. It may appear on labels as "lanolin," "wool wax," "wool fat," "lanolin alcohol," "laneth" (in surfactant compounds), "acetylated lanolin," or various INCI derivatives.

Found in Lip balm · nipple cream · nappy rash ointment · shoe polish · vitamin D3 supplements · industrial lubricants · some hair conditioners

4. Beeswax always animal

Beeswax E901 · CAS 8012-89-3 ▲ Always animal-derived

Beeswax is produced by worker honeybees (Apis mellifera) from abdominal wax glands and used to construct the honeycomb structure of beehives. Commercial beeswax is recovered by melting honeycomb after honey extraction. Approximately 8–10 kg of honey must be metabolised by bees to produce 1 kg of wax.[23]

In cosmetics, beeswax provides structure and film-forming properties in lipsticks, mascara, and hair pomades. In food, it is used as a glazing and coating agent (E901) on fresh and dried fruit, chocolate confectionery, and dietary supplements. In non-cosmetic applications, it is present in wood polish, leather conditioner, candles, and dental floss coating. The vegan position holds that honey bee exploitation — including artificial queen insemination, wing-clipping of queens, hive inspection, and routine colony destruction — makes beeswax non-vegan irrespective of its apparent naturalness.

Found in Lipstick · mascara · hair pomade · waxed fruits · some chocolate confectionery · wood polish · candles · dental floss

5. Tallow always animal

Tallow / Beef Tallow / Suet No E-number · rendered fat ▲ Always animal-derived

Tallow is rendered fat derived from beef or mutton, typically from the adipose tissue surrounding organs (suet). It is a primary feedstock of the oleochemical industry, providing fatty acids (predominantly stearic and oleic) for soap, surfactant, candle, and cosmetics manufacturing.[1] Some fast-food chains have historically used tallow in frying; McDonald's famously switched from beef tallow to vegetable oil for french fries in the US in 1990, though tallow is still used in some international markets.

In personal care, tallow-derived ingredients appear under names including sodium tallowate (in bar soaps), tallow glycerides, and tallowamidopropyl betaine (a surfactant). In manufacturing, tallow is used in metalworking lubricants, leather softening, and as a release agent in rubber and tyre production. Tallow-based soaps are common in budget household products without disclosure of origin.

Found in Bar soap (as "sodium tallowate") · metalworking fluids · some candles · leather conditioner · some fast-food frying oils (international) · tyre manufacturing

6. Keratin always animal

Keratin (Hydrolysed) No E-number · structural protein ▲ Always animal-derived

Keratin is the primary structural protein of hair, nails, hooves, horns, claws, and the outer layers of skin. For cosmetic applications, keratin is typically hydrolysed (partially broken down by acid, alkali, or enzymatic treatment) from wool, feathers, or human hair, producing fragments small enough to penetrate hair cuticles.[24] It is marketed heavily in hair care products on claims of smoothing, strengthening, and repairing damaged hair — claims that have limited independent clinical support for topical formulations.

The Brazilian Blowout and analogous keratin hair straightening treatments that became popular in the 2000s typically combine hydrolysed keratin with formaldehyde-releasing compounds. Hydrolysed keratin may also appear on labels as "keratin amino acids," "hydrolysed feather keratin," or "hydrolysed silk" (though silk is a separate protein).

Found in Hair smoothing treatments · shampoo · conditioner · nail hardeners · hair masks

7. Collagen (Cosmetic & Topical) always animal

Collagen (Topical / Injectable) No E-number · structural protein ▲ Always animal-derived (unless recombinant)

In addition to its food supplement use, collagen appears in topical skin care products as a film-forming agent and moisturiser, and in injectable dermal fillers for cosmetic procedures. Topical collagen molecules are generally too large to penetrate the skin barrier, so their moisturising effect is primarily occlusive — a function that can be replicated by many non-animal polymers. Injectable collagen fillers historically used bovine collagen (FDA-approved since 1981), though hyaluronic acid (which can be produced by microbial fermentation) has largely displaced bovine collagen as the dominant filler material.[25]

Found in Anti-ageing creams & serums · face masks · some injectable dermal fillers · eye creams

8. Elastin always animal

Elastin (Hydrolysed) No E-number · structural protein ▲ Always animal-derived

Elastin is the extracellular matrix protein responsible for the elastic recoil of skin, lungs, arteries, and other tissues. For cosmetic applications, it is extracted from bovine neck ligaments and aortas and hydrolysed into smaller peptides. Like topical collagen, hydrolysed elastin is promoted for anti-ageing skin care but cannot penetrate the dermis where endogenous elastin fibres reside.[24] It functions primarily as a humectant and film former at the skin surface.

Found in Anti-ageing skin creams · serums · hair conditioners

9. Squalene / Squalane from Shark Liver often animal

Squalene / Squalane (shark-derived) CAS 111-02-4 (squalene) · CAS 111-01-3 (squalane) ▲ Origin not disclosed on labels

Squalene is a naturally occurring triterpene that is the biosynthetic precursor to all steroids in both animals and plants. Deep-sea sharks accumulate squalene in their livers in concentrations of 40–80% of liver weight, used for buoyancy regulation. The global cosmetics industry has historically exploited this concentration, with shark liver oil (rich in squalene) used in moisturisers, sunscreens, hair products, and vaccine adjuvants (notably AS03, used in influenza vaccines).

Estimates from Bloom Association and other marine conservation organisations suggest that approximately 2.7–3 million sharks are killed each year specifically for their liver oil, with deep-sea species such as the gulper shark, leafscale gulper, and kitefin shark disproportionately targeted.[4] These are slow-reproducing species with very low population resilience. Plant-derived squalane — produced by hydrogenation of squalene from olive oil, sugarcane, or amaranth seed — is chemically identical and equally effective as a cosmetic emollient; many brands have switched in response to consumer and NGO pressure. However, shark-derived squalene continues in circulation without mandatory origin labelling, listed simply as "squalene," "squalane," or "shark liver oil."

Found in Moisturisers · sunscreens · lip care · hair conditioners · some vaccine adjuvants · nutritional supplements (as shark liver oil)

10. Silk Proteins always animal

Silk Proteins (Sericin, Fibroin) No E-number · structural protein ▲ Always animal-derived

Silk proteins are derived from the cocoons of the silkworm Bombyx mori. The production of commercial silk requires boiling cocoons while the pupae are alive inside, killing an estimated 3,000 silkworms per pound of raw silk produced.[26] The cocoon fibre consists of two proteins: fibroin (the structural protein) and sericin (the gum-like adhesive coating). Both are used in cosmetics and personal care products. Hydrolysed silk (predominantly sericin) is used as a humectant, film former, and marketing signifier of luxury in hair care and skin care. Silk amino acids (the component amino acids of silk hydrolysate) appear in many premium conditioners and serums.

Found in Premium shampoo & conditioners · face serums · lip gloss · some pressed powders · luxury skincare

11. Casein-Based Adhesives always animal

Casein Glue / Casein Adhesive No E-number · milk-protein adhesive ▲ Always animal-derived

Casein, the principal phosphoprotein in bovine milk (comprising ~80% of milk protein), is used as an industrial adhesive when combined with alkali (sodium silicate, borax, or lime) — a technology dating to ancient Egypt and widely used in early 20th-century woodworking and paper manufacture. Casein glues were a standard adhesive for aircraft wood construction in World War II.[27] Today, casein adhesives persist in specialty applications: wood joinery (particularly for musical instruments and fine furniture), cold-gluing of plywood, paper bag sealing, and some label adhesives. The dairy origin is not labelled on any manufactured product using casein glue as an assembly material.

Casein is also used as a binder in whiteboard coatings, some paints and paper coatings, and as an ingredient in certain sports nutrition bars and protein powder blends distinct from the adhesive application. For vegans renovating homes, working with timber, or sourcing instruments, casein-free wood glues (PVA, urea-formaldehyde, and epoxy formulations) are widely available alternatives.

Found in Some wood glues (fine furniture, instruments) · certain paper bag adhesives · label adhesives · some whiteboard coatings
Part III — Advocacy

Activist Strategies: From Awareness to System Change

Documenting hidden animal ingredients is a necessary first step, but the information only generates change when it is connected to strategic intervention. The following strategies operate across multiple levels — individual, organisational, regulatory, and industrial.

🏷 Labelling Reform Campaigns

Processing aids represent the most significant labelling gap. Advocacy targeting the EU's revision of Regulation (EC) 1333/2008 and FDA GRAS rulemaking can push for mandatory disclosure of animal-derived processing aids. The success of the FDA's 2011 carmine labelling mandate demonstrates that targeted advocacy can produce regulatory outcomes on ingredient transparency.

🏢 Corporate Engagement

Many companies have reformulated away from animal-derived ingredients in response to consumer inquiries and brand risk assessments. Direct email and social media campaigns requesting disclosure of glycerin, D3, and MDG sources have measurably shifted practices in the supplement and personal care sectors. Vegan certification partnerships (Vegan Society Trademark, PETA-approved) create market incentives for reformulation.

📱 Digital Tools & Open Databases

Building and maintaining openly licensed ingredient databases — and pressure-testing them against commercial scanning apps — expands vegan decision-making infrastructure. Contributing to Open Food Facts, Vegan Check, or barnivore.com improves the collective intelligence available at the point of purchase. Advocates with technical skills can submit accurate ingredient classifications and source documentation.

🔬 Supply Chain Research

Investigative journalism and NGO research exposing specific supply chain connections — such as Bloom's squalene investigations — have driven brand-level policy change. Partnering with organisations like Cruelty Free International, the Vegan Society, or FOUR PAWS to compile supply chain evidence elevates hidden-ingredient issues to media and policymaker attention.

🎓 Consumer Education

Ingredient literacy — teaching people to read INCI (cosmetic) and E-number (food) codes — is a durable empowerment strategy. Workshops, social media content, and downloadable reference cards lowering the barrier to understanding ambiguous labels reduce dependence on brand marketing claims and make consumers harder to mislead.

⚖️ Legal & Regulatory Intervention

Challenging misleading "vegan" or "plant-based" claims through national advertising standards bodies — where products carry these labels while containing unlabelled animal-derived processing aids or MDG — establishes legal precedent for honest labelling. Formal complaints have been upheld in Germany, the UK, and Australia on labelling accuracy grounds.

🦈 Species-Specific Campaigns

The squalene/shark campaign is a model: a specific, emotionally compelling connection between a cosmetics ingredient and measurable wildlife harm, with a viable plant-based alternative and brand-level targets. Similar campaigns can be built around isinglass (naming specific beer brands), carmine (targeting food manufacturers), and collagen (targeting wellness brands making unsubstantiated health claims).

🌐 International Standards Harmonisation

Engaging Codex Alimentarius processes — the joint FAO/WHO food standards body — to harmonise vegan labelling standards internationally would extend the impact of any single jurisdiction's regulatory progress. Advocacy coalitions including the European Vegetarian Union and ProVeg International are active in Codex engagement and welcome grassroots support.

✦ Priority intervention targets

Not all hidden ingredients represent equal leverage points. The following represent highest-impact targets based on scale of animal harm, feasibility of alternatives, and current policy momentum:

  • Isinglass in beer: Technically unnecessary (alternatives exist), consumer-facing, and brand-sensitive. Barnivore.com has already shifted the craft beer market.
  • Squalene in cosmetics: Species conservation framing gives it access to mainstream environmental media. Bloom Association's work is a template for other campaigns.
  • D3 labelling: The replacement (lichen-derived D3) is commercially available. A targeted campaign for disclosure and alternative adoption has a clear, achievable endpoint.
  • L-cysteine source disclosure: The connection to feathers (and historically human hair) is deeply resonant and under-reported. A campaign highlighting E920's origins has potential for significant media traction.
  • Bone char sugar labelling: Beet and organic cane sugar already avoid bone char. A certification or labelling campaign making "bone-char-free" a purchasing criterion would drive market adoption of alternatives.

    The Vegan Society — Vegan Trademark Long-established internationally recognised certification covering food, cosmetics, and non-food products. Requires supply-chain audit for processing aids including isinglass, bone char, and animal-derived enzymes.
  • Campaign
    Bloom Association French NGO conducting investigative research on deep-sea shark fishing for squalene. Produces annual reports on brand compliance and publishes lists of companies that have switched to plant-derived squalane.
  • Ingredient guide
    Vegan Society — Animal Ingredient List Alphabetical reference list of animal-derived and potentially animal-derived ingredients, with notes on common alternatives and ambiguous cases.
  • Regulatory
    EFSA Food Additives Assessment European Food Safety Authority's scientific opinions on food additives, including safety assessments, exposure estimates, and permitted uses. Valuable for evidence-based advocacy and policy submissions.
  • Advocacy
    ProVeg International Active in Codex Alimentarius and EU regulatory processes; advocates for vegan labelling standards harmonisation. Publishes policy position papers and participates in formal consultation processes.
  • App
    Is It Vegan? / similar scanning apps Smartphone apps using barcode scanning and ingredient databases to classify products. Effectiveness varies by database coverage; most benefit from community contributions and corrections.
✦ A note on certainty and harm hierarchies

Advocates sometimes face the question of how to prioritise ingredient concerns when certainty varies (some ingredients are definitively animal-derived; others are ambiguous; others are processing aids with no residue in the final product). A practical framework from the literature on moral uncertainty suggests prioritising where: (a) animal harm is measurable and significant, (b) the alternative is commercially available, and (c) the labelling or advocacy change is achievable within existing regulatory frameworks.[28]

Not every hidden ingredient requires equal activist energy. The goal is not perfectionism, but directional pressure toward systemic change.
