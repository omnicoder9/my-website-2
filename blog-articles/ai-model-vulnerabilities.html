Model Vulnerabilities:
An AI Red Teamer's Field Guide

A technical examination of attack surfaces inherent to trained AI systems—extraction, inversion, poisoning, adversarial manipulation—and the defensive architectures designed to resist them.

AI red teaming has evolved well beyond probing conversational guardrails. As machine learning systems are embedded into financial infrastructure, medical diagnostics, surveillance, and autonomous control, adversaries have turned their attention to the model itself—its weights, its training data, its prediction mechanics. This article surveys the principal model-level attack categories, the research that defined them, and the defensive architectures that attempt to contain them.

The attack surface of a deployed AI system is considerably wider than its prompt interface. A trained neural network encodes proprietary intellectual property, may memorise fragments of sensitive training data, and is structurally susceptible to adversarial manipulation that is often imperceptible to human observers. AI red teamers operating in this space work at the intersection of classical cybersecurity and adversarial machine learning—a discipline formalised over the past decade but now experiencing rapid escalation in both sophistication and real-world consequence.

Attack Vector 01

Model Extraction & Weight Stealing

Model extraction attacks—also called model stealing attacks—aim to reconstruct a functionally equivalent or near-identical copy of a proprietary trained model by exploiting its query interface.[1] The attacker operates without access to the target's weights, architecture, or training data, relying instead on the model's outputs to reverse-engineer its behaviour. The ultimate goal is either to reproduce a commercially valuable asset without licensing costs, or to generate a local surrogate that can be subjected to white-box attacks whose results transfer back to the original.[2]

Historical Foundations

The systematic study of model extraction was formalised by Tramèr et al. in 2016, who demonstrated that models deployed behind commercial machine-learning-as-a-service (MLaaS) APIs—including those of Amazon, Microsoft, and Google—could be replicated with high fidelity using a modest number of adaptive queries.[3] Oliynyk et al. produced a comprehensive 2023 survey cataloguing the maturation of the field, mapping attacks along dimensions of adversary knowledge, query budget, and target fidelity.[4]

⬡
API Query-Based Extraction Critical

The attacker submits carefully chosen inputs to the target API and observes predictions—class probabilities, logits, or hard labels. By fitting a surrogate model to the input–output pairs, they reconstruct an approximation of the original. Query-efficient variants such as Marich use distributionally equivalent public data to minimise query count while maximising functional similarity.[5]

For large language models, extraction attacks take on a qualitatively different character. "Model Leeching" (2023) demonstrated that chain-of-thought prompting and structured elicitation can transfer substantial reasoning capability from a large proprietary LLM to a smaller surrogate.[6] Prompt stealing attacks—targeting the system-prompt configuration rather than the weights—represent a parallel threat to operators of customised LLM deployments.[7]

Hardware-Level Weight Theft

A significant 2024 advance demonstrated model extraction at the hardware level, bypassing the API entirely. Researchers at North Carolina State University developed TPUXtract, the first successful side-channel extraction attack against the Google Edge Tensor Processing Unit (TPU).[8] By analysing electromagnetic emissions during inference, the attack reconstructed model hyperparameters without any prior knowledge of the software or architecture. This renders API-level rate limiting and output perturbation wholly ineffective as defences for edge-deployed models.

"Model stealing attacks undermine intellectual property rights, compromise competitive advantage, and potentially expose sensitive data embedded in the model's behaviour to unauthorised parties."

Kurian et al., IACR Transactions on Cryptographic Hardware and Embedded Systems, 2024 [8]

DeepSteal: Memory Bus Attacks

Rakin et al. demonstrated DeepSteal (2022), which exploits rowhammer-style attacks on DRAM to extract weight values from memory during model inference.[9] This represented the convergence of classical hardware security research with AI model theft, illustrating that the threat surface extends beyond software interfaces into physical infrastructure.

Attack Vector 02

Unauthorized Access via AI System Interfaces

Beyond the model itself, AI deployments introduce novel pathways for unauthorised access to underlying infrastructure. AI red teamers probe whether the model's interfaces—APIs, agentic tool-use endpoints, retrieval-augmented generation pipelines—can be exploited to gain access to data or system resources that the model itself was not intended to expose.

Privilege Escalation Through Prompts

In agentic AI deployments, where models are granted tool-use capabilities (file access, code execution, database queries, web browsing), adversarial prompts embedded in external content can redirect the agent to perform operations outside its intended scope. This attack class—indirect prompt injection—was formally characterised by Perez and Ribeiro in 2022 and has since been demonstrated against production systems including browser-integrated AI assistants.[10]

⚠

PoisonedRAG (2024) demonstrated that injecting a small number of malicious documents into a retrieval-augmented generation knowledge base can reliably redirect model outputs toward attacker-controlled responses, effectively weaponising the retrieval mechanism without any direct model access.[11]

Insecure API Endpoints

MLaaS infrastructure frequently aggregates model inference endpoints, training pipelines, and data storage under shared access patterns. Red teamers test for IDOR vulnerabilities (insecure direct object references) against model artefact stores, unauthenticated debug endpoints that expose intermediate activations, and insufficient isolation between tenants in multi-tenant inference clusters. The MITRE ATLAS framework catalogues AI-specific attack techniques including adversarial ML-informed API exploitation as distinct from conventional application security.[12]

Insecure Supply Chain Integration

Third-party pre-trained models sourced from public repositories (e.g., Hugging Face) introduce supply chain risks analogous to malicious npm packages. Attackers publish model weights with embedded backdoors under names closely resembling legitimate models—a dependency-confusion variant specific to the ML ecosystem. Build pipelines that pull models without cryptographic verification are particularly exposed.[13]

Attack Vector 03

Data Poisoning & Backdoor Injection

Data poisoning attacks corrupt the training or fine-tuning process by introducing manipulated examples into the dataset, causing the resulting model to exhibit attacker-controlled behaviours at inference time. The attack surface spans the entire data acquisition pipeline: web-scraped pretraining corpora, crowdsourced annotation, fine-tuning datasets, and retrieval knowledge bases are all viable vectors.[14]

Attack Taxonomy

☣
Label-Flip / Label Poisoning High

Labels of selected training examples are systematically altered to incorrect classes, distorting the model's decision boundary without modifying features. Bilevel optimisation-based label manipulation methods have been demonstrated to transfer across black-box boundaries, meaning an adversary who can influence a training dataset affects models they will never directly query.[15]

⬛
Backdoor / Trojan Insertion Critical

A backdoor attack embeds a covert trigger into the model by crafting malicious training examples that associate a specific input pattern with an attacker-chosen output.[16] The model behaves normally on clean inputs—passing standard evaluations—but produces incorrect or harmful outputs whenever the trigger is present. Triggers range from visible pixel patches in image classifiers to rare Unicode tokens in LLMs.

For large language models, instruction poisoning injects malicious behaviours during fine-tuning. TrojLLM (2024) demonstrated a black-box framework using reinforcement learning to autonomously discover universal, transferable triggers against LLM APIs without white-box access.[17] BadChain (2024) extended this to chain-of-thought prompting, embedding triggers in reasoning steps rather than input tokens.[18]

◈
Web-Scale Dataset Poisoning High

Goldwasser et al. demonstrated in 2023 that poisoning web-scale training datasets—by controlling content on a small number of high-PageRank domains likely to be included in crawls—is operationally feasible for well-resourced adversaries.[19] Given that virtually all frontier AI models are trained on web-scraped corpora, this represents a systemic, difficult-to-audit vulnerability across the industry.

Real-World Precedent

A concrete example of behavioural manipulation via poisoning: researchers demonstrated that a Trojan virus inserted into an AI-controlled Go engine manipulated game-play decisions without breaking the system's apparent functionality—the model continued to perform competently under normal conditions while exhibiting the planted behaviour under specific board configurations.[20] This illustrates the operational danger of backdoors: standard performance benchmarks are insufficient to detect them.

"Existing research on backdoor attack algorithms can be categorised into data-poisoning and weight-poisoning—a distinction that significantly affects which defences are applicable."

Wang et al., Survey of Backdoor Attacks in Large Language Models, 2024 [17]
Attack Vector 04

Adversarial Examples

Adversarial examples are inputs that have been deliberately, often imperceptibly, modified to cause a machine learning model to produce incorrect outputs with high confidence. They represent perhaps the most extensively studied vulnerability in adversarial ML, with a lineage stretching back to 2013 and a contemporary literature numbering in the thousands of papers.

Historical Foundations

Szegedy et al. made the foundational observation in 2014 that state-of-the-art neural networks would systematically misclassify inputs subjected to imperceptible perturbations, terming these "adversarial examples."[21] The phenomenon was then formalised by Goodfellow, Shlens, and Szegedy (2014) in the Fast Gradient Sign Method (FGSM) paper, which argued that the primary cause is not extreme nonlinearity or overfitting but rather the linear nature of neural networks in high-dimensional spaces.[22]

# Fast Gradient Sign Method (FGSM) — Goodfellow et al. 2014 # Constructs adversarial example by maximising classification loss def fgsm_attack(image, epsilon, gradient): # Collect the element-wise sign of the data gradient sign_data_grad = gradient.sign() # Create perturbed image by adjusting each pixel perturbed_image = image + epsilon * sign_data_grad # Clip to maintain valid pixel range [0,1] perturbed_image = torch.clamp(perturbed_image, 0, 1) return perturbed_image # epsilon is typically 0.007–0.3 — imperceptible to humans # yet sufficient to cause misclassification with high confidence

Physical-World Adversarial Attacks

Kurakin, Goodfellow, and Bengio (2016) demonstrated that adversarial perturbations survive the camera capture process, establishing that attacks are not confined to the digital domain.[23] Subsequent work produced physical adversarial patches that reliably fool object detectors and classifiers when printed on paper and placed in a scene—with implications for autonomous vehicles, access-control systems, and facial recognition.

Transfer and Black-Box Threats

A key empirical finding is that adversarial examples often transfer across model architectures: an example crafted to fool Model A will frequently fool Model B even when trained on different data with a different architecture.[24] This transferability property means that a black-box attacker can generate adversarial examples against a locally available surrogate model and apply them successfully to the target production system—dramatically lowering the barrier for real-world attacks.

⚡

Red teamers use adversarial examples not only to test misclassification robustness but to bypass safety classifiers: content moderation systems, toxicity filters, and RLHF-trained refusal mechanisms are all susceptible to adversarial suffix attacks that preserve semantic meaning while evading detection.

Attack Vector 05

Model Inversion Attacks

Model inversion attacks exploit a trained model as an oracle to reconstruct sensitive information about the data on which it was trained. By repeatedly querying the model and analysing its outputs—confidence scores, probabilities, or generated text—an adversary can reverse-engineer approximations of private training examples or infer sensitive attributes of individuals whose data contributed to training.[25]

Foundational Research

The attack was originally introduced in the context of genomic privacy by Fredrikson et al. (2014), who demonstrated that a model predicting optimal warfarin dosages could be queried to infer patients' genetic markers given only demographic information in the training set.[26] Fredrikson, Jha, and Ristenpart (2015) extended this substantially to facial recognition systems, demonstrating that confidence-value outputs from a face classifier could be exploited to reconstruct recognisable facial images of training subjects.[27] Crowdworkers could subsequently identify individuals from reconstructed images with 95% accuracy in lineup tests.[28]

"Deploying a face recognition API effectively exposes the faces of individuals in the training set. The privacy implications extend beyond facial recognition to medical records, financial data, and behavioural profiles."

Fredrikson et al., CCS 2015 [27]; synthesised in AI Safety Directory 2026 [25]

GAN-Enhanced Reconstruction

Subsequent research substantially improved reconstruction fidelity by incorporating generative models. Zhang et al. (2020) introduced the first GAN-based model inversion attack, using a generative adversarial network as a prior to produce high-resolution reconstructions that surpassed earlier gradient-descent-based methods.[29] Struppek et al. (2022) and Nguyen et al. (2023) further advanced GAN-based inversion, achieving near-photographic quality reconstructions of face images from production-grade facial recognition classifiers.[29]

Attack Vectors by Access Model

◉
White-Box Gradient Inversion Critical

With full access to model weights, the attacker uses gradient-based optimisation to find inputs that maximise the model's confidence for a target class. The optimisation iteratively refines a random initial input toward something that matches the model's internal representation of training data. Highly effective but requires model access that is uncommon in external red-teaming contexts.

◎
Black-Box Confidence Exploitation High

Operating against an API that returns confidence scores alongside predictions, the attacker uses these scores as a feedback signal to hill-climb toward training data reconstructions. The technique generalises beyond image classifiers: in NLP models, systematic prompting can elicit memorised training data fragments—Carlini et al. (2021) demonstrated extraction of verbatim personal information, source code, and literary text from large language models.[30]

Regulatory Implications

Model inversion attacks have direct regulatory relevance. Reconstructed training data constitutes personal data exposure under GDPR, and models trained on protected health information are subject to HIPAA breach notification obligations if inversion attacks are successful. The European Data Protection Board has explicitly addressed AI model inversion risks in guidance on privacy-by-design requirements for machine learning deployments.[25]

Defence Architecture

Defence Strategies

No single defence is sufficient against the full threat surface described above. Effective AI security requires layered controls spanning model training, architectural design, deployment configuration, and operational monitoring. AI red teamers not only mount attacks but evaluate defence efficacy—probing whether hardening measures genuinely reduce risk or merely displace it.

Adversarial Training

Mechanism

Adversarial training augments the training dataset with adversarially perturbed examples, compelling the model to learn decision boundaries that are robust to perturbations within a defined threat model. Madry et al. (2018) formalised this as a min-max robust optimisation problem—the inner maximisation searches for the worst-case adversarial example, while the outer minimisation trains against it—with projected gradient descent (PGD) as the inner solver, establishing the de facto benchmark for adversarial robustness evaluation.[31]

Limitations Identified by Red Teamers

Adversarial training is computationally expensive (typically 3–10× standard training cost) and does not provide guarantees against attacks outside its training distribution. The "blind-spot" phenomenon—where models hardened against known attacks remain vulnerable to novel perturbation types outside the training manifold—has been demonstrated even against provable defences.[32] Catastrophic overfitting is a documented failure mode of single-step adversarial training methods, where models appear robust against single-step attacks while remaining highly vulnerable to multi-step variants.[33]

Certified Robustness

Mechanism

Certified defences provide mathematical guarantees that model predictions are consistent within a defined perturbation region. Approaches include randomised smoothing (providing probabilistic certificates), Lipschitz-bounded architectures, and interval bound propagation. These methods provide reliable protection against future attacks within the certified threat model—unlike empirical defences, which are vulnerable to adaptive attackers.[34]

Red Teamer Assessment

Certified robustness certificates are generally substantially lower than the robustness achieved by empirical adversarial training for equivalent threat models.[34] Red teamers evaluate whether the certification threat model accurately reflects operational attack conditions—a certificate against ℓ∞-bounded perturbations provides no protection against geometric transformations, patch attacks, or semantic perturbations outside the certified norm ball.

Robust Model Design

Architectural Choices

Design-time decisions influence intrinsic robustness. Relevant choices evaluated by AI red teamers include: ensemble methods (which aggregate predictions from multiple independently trained models, requiring an adversary to simultaneously fool all members); regularisation techniques (weight decay, dropout, spectral normalisation of layer weights); architecture selection (vision transformers have been shown to exhibit different adversarial robustness profiles than convolutional networks under equivalent perturbation budgets); and activation function selection.

Differential Privacy

Differential privacy (DP) training introduces calibrated noise into gradient updates during training, providing formal bounds on the amount of information any individual training example can contribute to the model. DP training directly mitigates model inversion and membership inference risks, and is recommended by NSA, CISA, and FBI joint guidance for AI systems trained on sensitive data.[35] The primary cost is accuracy degradation, which varies substantially with privacy budget and dataset size.

Continuous Monitoring

AI-Specific Monitoring Requirements

Standard infrastructure monitoring is insufficient for AI systems. Effective continuous monitoring must additionally cover: anomalous shifts in output distribution (indicative of adversarial inputs or triggered backdoors); sudden changes in output toxicity or safety filter bypass rates; unusual query patterns consistent with model extraction (high-volume, systematically diverse inputs with low semantic variation); and data drift affecting model performance in ways that may conceal adversarial manipulation.[36]

Red Teamer Evaluation Protocol

AI red teamers assess monitoring systems by executing known attacks and observing whether detection mechanisms trigger with sufficient sensitivity and specificity. Key questions: Does the monitoring system distinguish adversarial queries from legitimate distributional shift? Can model extraction attempts be detected before sufficient queries have been submitted to achieve high-fidelity surrogates? Are backdoor trigger activations detectable in post-deployment inference logs?

Recommended Tooling

Adversarial testing frameworks include IBM's Adversarial Robustness Toolbox (ART), which provides implementations of attacks and defences for red teaming across model types; Microsoft Counterfit, an automation layer for security testing of AI systems; and MITRE ATLAS, which catalogues adversarial ML techniques and maps them to detection and mitigation strategies analogously to ATT&CK for conventional cybersecurity.[37]
