01 · The problem

A body is not a chatbot with wheels

Large language models absorbed the internet and, in doing so, absorbed a great deal of what humans know about the physical world. They know that eggs break, that you hold a knife by the handle, that a sponge goes with a spill. What they conspicuously lack is a body, and the moment you attach one, a set of problems appears that no amount of text will solve.

The oldest statement of this asymmetry is Hans Moravec's. Writing in 1988, he observed that the abilities we consider distinctly human — reasoning, chess, calculus — turn out to be computationally cheap, while the abilities we share with a one-year-old — recognising a face, walking across a cluttered room, picking up a cup — are computationally enormous.[1] His explanation was evolutionary: sensorimotor competence is the product of a billion years of optimisation and is therefore deeply, invisibly engineered, whereas abstract thought is a thin, recent, badly debugged layer running on top. Nearly four decades later, the observation has aged into something close to a law. A model that can pass a graduate physics exam still cannot reliably pick up a fork.

It is worth being precise about why, because "robots are hard" is not an explanation. Four properties separate embodied learning from every other machine-learning problem, and each of them shows up as a specific engineering constraint later in this article.

i

Data is not free

Text is scraped. Robot data is manufactured, one human-hour per robot-hour, on hardware that breaks. The largest open manipulation corpus in existence is roughly a million trajectories — about six orders of magnitude smaller than the text corpus behind a frontier LLM.

ii

Errors compound

A language model that emits a bad token produces a slightly worse paragraph. A policy that emits a bad action moves the robot to a state it has never seen, from which its next action is worse still. The distribution shifts under your feet, and it shifts because of you.

iii

The clock does not stop

A servo loop wants a new command every 1–20 ms. A 55-billion-parameter transformer takes hundreds. Every architectural decision in modern robot learning is downstream of that inequality.

iv

Failure is physical

You cannot regenerate the output. A hallucinated citation is embarrassing; a hallucinated grasp puts 200 newtons through someone's hand. There is no undo button on a robot arm.

Against that, consider what has actually changed. In 2022 the state of the art in general-purpose manipulation was a robot that could perform a few hundred narrow tasks in a lab kitchen it had memorised. By 2025, a single model was being dropped into rented houses it had never seen and asked to clean the kitchen — opening drawers, identifying which of the objects on the counter were rubbish, and putting the dishes in the sink.[2] The delta is not better motors. It is that robot policies stopped being written and started being pretrained.

This article is about how that happened, what it is built out of, and where it is still broken. It is written for people who will have to make it work.

Scope

Two threads run through everything below, and they are the two the field itself is organised around. The first is the foundation-model thread: taking a network pretrained on the internet and teaching it to emit motor commands — PaLM-E, RT-2, and the vision-language-action models that descend from them. The second is the sim-to-real thread: training in a physics engine, where data is free and mistakes cost nothing, and then transferring to hardware, where neither is true. The interesting frontier is where they meet: synthetic data, generated in a digital twin, used to pretrain a foundation model that will be deployed on a body that does not exist yet.

02 · Lineage

Six decades of trying to put a mind in a machine

Industrial robotics and robot intelligence have, historically, almost nothing to do with each other. George Devol’s 1961 patent for the Unimate — a programmable arm that stored step-by-step commands and moved parts — founded an industry that was, and largely still is, about replaying recorded trajectories with high precision.[3] The intelligence question was pursued elsewhere, and it has changed its mind about the answer roughly four times. Each shift moved the same thing — the knowledge required to do a task — from one place to another. It is worth tracing, because every generation's solution is still in the stack, usually holding up the layer that replaced it.

Sense, plan, act

The founding artefact is Shakey, built at Stanford Research Institute between 1966 and 1972: a wheeled box with a television camera, a range finder, and a radio link to a mainframe.[4] Shakey is the ancestor of essentially everything. It gave us STRIPS, the planner that represented the world as a set of logical predicates and searched for a sequence of operators transforming the initial state into the goal state.[5] It gave us A*, still the default graph search in every navigation stack shipping today.[6] And it gave us the architecture: sense, build a symbolic model of the world; plan, search that model for a path to the goal; act, execute the plan.

It worked beautifully in a room containing large, matte, uniformly-lit blocks. It collapsed everywhere else. The reason is worth internalising, because it recurs: the model was a bottleneck. Everything the robot could reason about had to first be squeezed through a perception system into a handful of predicates, and the predicates were written by a person who had to anticipate everything that might matter.

The reactive revolt

In 1986 Rodney Brooks proposed throwing the model away. His subsumption architecture built behaviour out of layers of tight sensor-to-actuator loops — avoid obstacles, wander, explore — with higher layers subsuming lower ones by suppressing their outputs.[7] There was no central world model, no plan, no symbols. Five years later he made the claim explicit in a paper whose title is its own argument: Intelligence Without Representation.[8] "The world is its own best model," he wrote — and his insects walked over terrain that Shakey's descendants were still deliberating about.

Brooks was right about the failure of symbolic AI and wrong about the alternative. Reactive robots are magnificent at the reflex layer and structurally incapable of "put the milk away before it spoils". But the insight survives, transformed: modern end-to-end policies are, in a real sense, subsumption architectures with learned layers. They map pixels to torques with no explicit world model in between. Brooks lost the argument and won the war.

Uncertainty as a first-class citizen

The 1990s and 2000s belonged to probability. The insight was that the problem with Shakey's world model was not that it was a model but that it was certain. Replace the predicate "the door is open" with a belief distribution over door states, propagate that belief through a motion model and a sensor model, and you get robots that degrade gracefully instead of catastrophically. Kalman filters, particle filters, and the whole apparatus of simultaneous localisation and mapping came out of this period, codified in Thrun, Burgard and Fox's Probabilistic Robotics, which remains the single most important textbook in the field.[9]

It paid off publicly in 2005, when Stanley — a Volkswagen Touareg running a probabilistic terrain-classification stack — drove 212 kilometres across the Mojave desert unassisted and won the DARPA Grand Challenge.[10] Machine learning was inside Stanley (a self-supervised laser-to-camera terrain classifier let it drive faster than its lidar alone permitted), but it was a component in a hand-architected system, not the system itself. Two years later, the same community produced ROS, and robotics acquired the thing it had always lacked: a shared substrate that let one lab's perception stack run on another lab's robot.[11]

The learning turn

Deep reinforcement learning arrived in robotics with a lag. The 2015 result that a convolutional network could learn Atari from pixels[12] was electrifying, but Atari has a discrete action space, unlimited resets, and a free simulator. Robots have none of those. The first genuinely convincing demonstration that the paradigm crossed over was Levine et al.'s end-to-end visuomotor policies in 2016: a single network trained from raw camera images to joint torques, learning to hang a coat hanger and screw a cap onto a bottle.[13] It was followed by the "arm farm" — fourteen robots grasping continuously for two months to collect 800,000 grasp attempts[14] — and by QT-Opt, which pushed the same idea to a 96% grasp success rate on unseen objects.[15]

Those results also exposed the wall. Collecting 580,000 grasp attempts to learn one skill is not a path to a general-purpose robot; it is a path to a very good grasper. Reinforcement learning in the real world is too sample-hungry, and the reward functions for anything more interesting than grasping are too hard to write. Two escape routes opened. One went through simulation. The other went through imitation. Both, eventually, went through the internet.

03 · The substrate

What the neural network is standing on

It is tempting, reading about end-to-end policies, to imagine that a robot is now a single network from pixels to motion. It is not, and the parts that are not learned are the parts that keep it from killing anyone. Before going further it is worth laying out the whole control hierarchy, because every subsequent claim in this article is a claim about which layer a given technique has eaten.

Kinematics: the map between two spaces

A manipulator has a configuration space — the vector q of joint angles — and a task space, the pose of its end effector in SE(3): three translations and a rotation. Forward kinematics maps the first to the second and is a straightforward chain of homogeneous transforms. Inverse kinematics maps the second to the first and is, in general, ill-posed: a 7-degree-of-freedom arm has a one-dimensional null space of joint configurations that all put the gripper in exactly the same place. That redundancy is a resource, not a problem — you can use it to avoid joint limits, dodge an obstacle, or keep the elbow out of a human's face — but it means "where should the gripper go?" and "what should the motors do?" are genuinely different questions.[16]

The relationship between them is the Jacobian J(q), the matrix that maps joint velocities to end-effector velocities. Its transpose maps forces the other way, which is the single most useful fact in manipulation: it means a desired wrench at the fingertips can be converted directly into torques at the motors, with no inverse kinematics anywhere.

Control: the part that must never fail

At the bottom of the stack, a joint controller closes a loop at 1–10 kHz. Usually it is a PID law. For anything touching the world, a pure position controller is dangerous: it will drive to its setpoint through whatever happens to be in the way, and a 3 mm pose error becomes a 200 N push. The alternative — and the reason contact-rich manipulation works at all — is impedance control, formalised by Neville Hogan in 1985: rather than commanding a position, you command the robot to behave like a spring and damper of a chosen stiffness.[17] Set the stiffness low and the arm is compliant; a misjudged grasp gets corrected by the object itself.

Above that sits model predictive control: at every timestep, solve a finite-horizon optimal control problem over a dynamics model, execute the first action, throw the rest away, and re-solve.[18] MPC is how Boston Dynamics' robots did backflips before any of them had a neural network. It remains the method of choice wherever you have a good dynamics model and hard constraints, and it is increasingly used alongside learned policies rather than instead of them — as a safety filter that projects the policy's proposed action onto the set of actions that will not tip the robot over.

Planning: search in configuration space

Between "where do I want the gripper" and "what joint trajectory gets it there without hitting anything" sits motion planning. The workhorse for thirty years has been sampling: the Rapidly-exploring Random Tree grows a tree of collision-free configurations by repeatedly sampling a random point and extending toward it,[19] and RRT* adds a rewiring step that makes it asymptotically optimal.[20] These algorithms are probabilistically complete, embarrassingly parallel, and completely oblivious to what the objects are. That last property is why they are being displaced at the top and retained at the bottom: a learned policy decides what to do; a planner still often computes how to get the arm there without shearing off a table leg.

The load-bearing claim

None of the classical stack has been deleted. A 2026 humanoid running a vision-language-action model still has a whole-body quadratic program enforcing balance and joint limits underneath it, and a servo loop underneath that. What changed is that the layers above the QP — which used to be a state machine written by an engineer — are now a network trained on data. Confusing "the top of the stack is learned" with "the stack is learned" is the most common category error in the popular coverage of this field.

04 · Perception

Turning photons into things you can grasp

A robot's sensor suite is a set of lies of different kinds. An RGB camera gives you dense semantics and no metric depth. A lidar gives you metric depth and no semantics. An IMU gives you excellent short-term angular velocity and drifts without bound. A force/torque sensor tells you what you are touching but not what it is. Perception is the discipline of combining lies that fail in uncorrelated ways.

The classical pillar: geometry and filtering

Simultaneous localisation and mapping remains one of the genuine triumphs of the probabilistic era. ORB-SLAM — feature extraction, tracking, local bundle adjustment, loop closure, relocalisation — will still localise a monocular camera to centimetre accuracy in a static scene, and its descendants are what let a warehouse robot know where it is.[21] The uncomfortable truth is that these systems assume rigidity and staticity, and a kitchen is neither.

The 2020s added a new representation: rather than a sparse point cloud, learn a continuous field. Neural radiance fields encode a scene as a network mapping position and view direction to colour and density, optimised to reproduce a set of photographs.[22] They are gorgeous and, for a long time, uselessly slow. 3D Gaussian splatting replaced the implicit field with a few million anisotropic Gaussians rasterised directly, hitting real-time rendering at equal or better fidelity.[23] That mattered enormously for robotics, and not for the reason you might expect: it turned "scan a real room with a phone" into "obtain a photorealistic, physics-ready simulation asset". We will come back to that in the sim-to-real section, because it closes a loop that had been open for thirty years.

The learned pillar: semantics from the internet

The single most consequential import from computer vision into robotics is not any particular architecture but the pretrained visual backbone. CLIP aligned images and text in a shared embedding space by contrastive training on 400 million web pairs, which means a robot can be told to pick up "the thing you would drink from" and have a chance.[24] DINOv2, trained without labels at all, produces dense features that transfer to depth estimation and semantic correspondence out of the box.[25] Segment Anything will produce a mask for any object you point at, with no training on that object class.[26]

These models are the reason vision-language-action models work. Not one of them was trained on a robot. All of them arrived pre-loaded with a representation of what objects are, which is exactly the thing a robot cannot afford to learn from its own experience.

Grasping: the sub-field that got solved first

Grasp synthesis was the first manipulation problem to fall to deep learning, because it has a property nothing else in manipulation has: a cheap, dense, self-supervisable label. Dex-Net generated millions of synthetic point clouds with analytically-computed grasp robustness scores and trained a network to predict them, hitting 99% precision on physical trials.[27] Contact-GraspNet extended this to full 6-DoF grasps in clutter by predicting contact points directly on the observed point cloud, cutting the dimensionality of the search.[28]

Grasping is worth dwelling on because it is a cautionary tale. It is solved — and it turned out not to be the bottleneck. A robot that can grasp anything still cannot load a dishwasher, because loading a dishwasher is a sequence of contact-rich, order-dependent, partially-observable decisions in which grasping is a subroutine. The lesson the field took roughly a decade to absorb is that manipulation is not a perception problem with a motion-planning epilogue — it is a problem about representations, contact, and sequential decision-making under partial observability, in which perception is a subroutine.[29,30]

Touch: the modality that is still missing

Humans can identify an object in a pocket without looking. Robots essentially cannot. Optical tactile sensors like GelSight — a slab of clear elastomer with a camera behind it, imaging the deformation of the contact surface at effectively micron resolution[31] — are the best answer available and are still not standard equipment. The gap is telling: for tasks like inserting a connector, threading a nut, or checking whether a grasp has slipped, vision at 30 Hz through an occluding gripper is simply the wrong sensor, and no amount of scale on the visual side fixes it.

05 · Learning to act

Imitation, reinforcement, and why one of them won

There are exactly two ways to get a behaviour into a policy: show it, or reward it. Both have been tried at scale, and the outcome was not what most of the field expected in 2018.

Reinforcement learning: correct, and mostly impractical

The formalism is clean. A Markov decision process, a policy πθ(a|s), a reward r(s,a), and an objective: maximise expected discounted return.[32] Proximal policy optimisation[33] and soft actor-critic[34] are the two algorithms that survived contact with reality, the former for its stability and the latter for its sample efficiency.

The problem is not the algorithms. It is that reinforcement learning on physical hardware requires two things robots cannot supply. The first is samples — PPO wants millions of environment steps, and a real arm executes about 50 per second while wearing out its harmonic drives. The second, and worse, is a reward function. "Grasp the object" has an obvious reward. "Fold the shirt" does not. Any hand-written reward for a long-horizon manipulation task is a specification of the behaviour you want, and if you could write that specification, you would not need the learning.[35]

Where reinforcement learning did win, decisively, is locomotion — and the reason is that locomotion has a natural reward (go this direction, don't fall, don't waste energy) and can be trained entirely in simulation. We will return to this, because it is a sim-to-real story rather than an RL story.

Imitation learning: dumb, and it works

Behavioural cloning is supervised learning with a state as input and a human's action as the label. It is the oldest idea in the book — Pomerleau's ALVINN drove a van using a three-layer network trained on human steering in 1989[36] — and its flaw was identified almost immediately. The policy is trained on the distribution of states a human visits. The moment it makes a small error, it enters a state slightly off that distribution, where its predictions are worse, which produces a larger error. Ross, Gordon and Bagnell formalised the compounding: error grows quadratically in the episode horizon, and their DAgger algorithm fixed it by iteratively querying the expert on states the policy visits.[37]

Three developments turned this liability into the dominant paradigm.

Cheap teleoperation. ALOHA is a pair of low-cost 6-DoF arms where the human physically backdrives a "leader" arm and a "follower" mirrors it; the encoder stream is the action label, at 50 Hz, with no annotation whatsoever.[38] Mobile ALOHA put the whole rig on a wheeled base and collected whole-body demonstrations of cooking and cleaning.[39] Data collection stopped being a research project and became a shift.

Action chunking. Instead of predicting one action, predict the next k. This does two things: it collapses the effective horizon (fewer decisions means less compounding), and it lets the policy commit to a temporally coherent motion rather than dithering. ALOHA's transformer, ACT, predicts 100-step chunks and ensembles overlapping predictions to smooth the seams.[38] Nearly every serious policy since does some version of this.

Generative action heads. Human demonstrations are multimodal: two teleoperators route around the same mug on opposite sides, and a mean-squared-error loss averages those into a trajectory that goes straight through it. Diffusion Policy replaced the regression head with a conditional denoising process over the action chunk,[40] borrowing directly from image generation.[41] It samples a mode instead of blending all of them, and it improved average success rates across fifteen tasks by nearly 47% over the prior state of the art. Flow matching — a continuous-time reformulation that learns a velocity field from noise to data[42] — is the version most production models now use, because it needs fewer integration steps.

Why imitation beat reinforcement for manipulation

Reinforcement learning asks you to specify the task. Imitation learning asks you to demonstrate it. For anything a human can do easily and describe with difficulty — which is to say, for almost all physical work — demonstration is a vastly cheaper interface. The field did not conclude that RL is wrong; it concluded that RL is the right tool once you already have a competent policy and want to push it past the demonstrator, and the wrong tool for getting one from scratch. That is why the current frontier is RL fine-tuning of pretrained VLAs, not RL from scratch.

06 · Generative AI & LLMs

The vision-language-action model

The central move of the last four years is embarrassingly simple to state. Take a network that has read the internet. Add a new modality to its output vocabulary: motor commands. Fine-tune it on robot data. Discover that the semantic knowledge it acquired from web text and images — what a stapler is, that spilled liquid needs a sponge, that "the extinct animal" means the dinosaur toy — transfers to the gripper.

Getting from there to a working system took three distinct architectural generations, and it is worth walking each one, because the failure modes of each motivated the next.

Generation one: the LLM as a planner that cannot see

The first attempts kept the language model outside the control loop entirely. SayCan, from Google in 2022, is the canonical design.[43] Give the robot a library of short, pre-trained skills — "pick up the sponge", "go to the counter", "put down the can" — each with its own policy and, crucially, its own value function learned during that skill's training. Now, given an instruction like "I spilled my drink, can you help?", score every skill twice:

  • Say: the language model's probability that this skill is a sensible next thing to say given the instruction and the history. This is world knowledge: LLMs know sponges are for spills.
  • Can: the affordance model's estimate that this skill will succeed from the current state. This is grounding: the value function knows the sponge is across the room.

Multiply, take the argmax, execute, append the outcome to the history, repeat. The product is the whole insight. An LLM alone hallucinates skills the robot does not have; an affordance model alone has no idea what a spill implies. Inner Monologue extended the loop by feeding failure descriptions and scene descriptions back into the prompt as text, so the planner could actually replan rather than blindly continuing.[44] Code as Policies took a different route: have the LLM emit Python, calling a fixed API of perception and control primitives, which gets you loops, conditionals, and arithmetic for free.[45]

The limitation of generation one is stark and was stated plainly by its own authors: the language model cannot see. It reasons entirely over text, which means every visual fact must first be flattened into a caption by some other system, and any nuance the captioner drops is a nuance the planner cannot use. Ask it to "pick up the object closest to the blue mug" and it has nowhere to look.

Generation two: PaLM-E, and putting sensors inside the language model

PaLM-E, published in March 2023, closed that hole in the most direct way available: it injected continuous sensor data into the language model's embedding space.[46] The mechanism is elegant. A language model represents each token as a high-dimensional vector. So take an image, run it through a vision transformer, project the resulting features into exactly that vector space, and splice them into the token sequence. You get what the authors call multimodal sentences: sequences that interleave text tokens, image embeddings, and continuous state estimates, all trained end-to-end.

The scale was the headline. PaLM-E-562B combined the 540B-parameter PaLM language model with a 22B-parameter vision transformer — at the time, the largest vision-language model reported.[46] But the important result was not the size. It was positive transfer: training jointly across internet-scale language, vision-language, and embodied robotics data made the model better at all of them than training on any one alone. A model trained on robot planning got better at visual question answering. PaLM-E-562B set a state of the art on OK-VQA — a benchmark requiring external world knowledge — without any task-specific fine-tuning, while retaining essentially the language ability of the base PaLM.

That is a load-bearing empirical claim for the entire field. It says robot data and web data are not competing for capacity; they are reinforcing each other. If it had come out the other way — if robot fine-tuning had degraded language ability, as naive intuition about catastrophic forgetting would predict — the foundation-model approach to robotics would have been dead on arrival.

PaLM-E still emitted plans, in text, which a separate low-level policy executed. The last wall was still standing.

Generation three: RT-2, and actions as tokens

Two precursors set this up. BC-Z showed that a single language-conditioned imitation policy, trained across a hundred tasks with a shared visual encoder, could execute instructions it had never been trained on — establishing language as a workable conditioning interface for a low-level policy, not merely for a planner.[47] CLIPort fused CLIP’s semantic stream with a spatial one, letting a manipulation policy be told to pick up “the Google-coloured block” without ever having been shown one.[48]

RT-1, from late 2022, put the transformer[49] at the centre and established that the recipe works at scale: 130,000 demonstrations, 700+ tasks, collected over 17 months with a fleet of 13 mobile manipulators, producing a policy with a 97% success rate on tasks it had seen.[50] It generalised poorly to anything it had not.

RT-2, in July 2023, made the move that defines the current era.[51] Rather than bolting an action head onto a vision-language model, it expressed robot actions as text. Discretise each of the seven action dimensions — three positional deltas, three rotational deltas, one gripper command — into 256 bins. Assign each bin an integer. Now an action is a string: "1 128 91 241 5 101 127". Overwrite 256 rarely-used tokens in the language model's existing vocabulary to mean these bins. The model's architecture is unchanged. Its output head is unchanged. It emits actions the same way it emits words, and you can co-fine-tune it on web vision-language data and robot trajectories simultaneously, in the same batch, with the same loss.

The word "emergent" is doing real work there. RT-2 could execute "move the banana to the sum of two plus one", identifying that the answer is 3 and moving the banana onto a card marked 3 — a chain of arithmetic and symbol grounding that appears nowhere in 130,000 kitchen demonstrations. It could "pick up the extinct animal" from a table of toys. It could "move the Coke can to the person with glasses". None of these are in the robot data. All of them are in the internet. RT-2 inherited them.

Prompted to reason first, it would emit a chain of thought in natural language and then the action tokens — "I need to pick up something I can use as a hammer. A rock is hard. Action: 1 129 138 122 132 132 106" — giving you a policy that plans and acts in a single forward pass, and, incidentally, tells you why it did what it did.

The problem RT-2 created

RT-2 was a cloud service. A 55-billion-parameter model produced roughly one to three actions per second, streamed over a network to a robot that was standing still waiting for them. That is adequate for slowly pushing a can across a table and completely inadequate for anything dexterous. Every development since has been an attempt to keep RT-2's knowledge while fixing its clock.

Action chunking was the first answer: emit fifty actions per forward pass instead of one, execute them open-loop, and amortise the inference cost. The cost is reactivity — for the length of the chunk, the policy is blind — so real systems re-plan every quarter-chunk and blend overlapping predictions.

Better tokenisation was the second. Uniform binning is a wasteful code: consecutive actions in a smooth trajectory are enormously correlated, and spending 350 tokens on a 50-step, 7-DoF chunk encodes that redundancy over and over. FAST applies a discrete cosine transform to the action chunk, discards the near-zero high-frequency coefficients, and byte-pair-encodes the result — compressing the token count by roughly an order of magnitude and, critically, making high-frequency dexterous tasks trainable at all under an autoregressive objective.[52]

Abandoning discretisation was the third, and is where the frontier now sits. The π0 model from Physical Intelligence keeps a pretrained VLM as its backbone but attaches a separate, small action expert — a diffusion-transformer head trained by flow matching — which emits a chunk of continuous actions at up to 50 Hz.[53] There is no quantisation error, and fine motor detail survives.

Scaling by pooling: Open X-Embodiment

The deeper constraint was never architecture. It was data. In 2023, twenty-one institutions did the obvious and difficult thing: they pooled their robot datasets. Open X-Embodiment aggregates over a million real trajectories from 22 distinct robot embodiments — single arms, bimanual setups, quadrupeds — spanning 500+ skills.[54] It is the ImageNet moment of robot learning, and the finding that justified it was positive cross-embodiment transfer: RT-X models trained on the pooled corpus outperformed the same architectures trained only on each lab's own data, by roughly 50% on average.

A policy trained partly on a Franka arm in Berkeley gets better at controlling a WidowX in Stanford. That is not obvious — the kinematics are different, the cameras are different, the action spaces are different — and it is the empirical claim on which every "robot foundation model" pitch now rests.

Two DeepMind results bracket this line of work. Gato demonstrated in 2022 that a single transformer with one set of weights could play Atari, caption images, hold a conversation, and stack blocks with a real arm — sequence modelling as a universal interface, with robotics as one modality among many.[55] RoboCat pushed the other side: a generalist that adapts to a new task from around a hundred demonstrations, then generates its own additional data on that task and folds it back into training — a self-improvement flywheel with the robot inside the loop.[56]

The open-weights wave

RT-2's weights were never released. OpenVLA, in June 2024, changed that. A 7-billion-parameter model built on Llama 2 with a fused DINOv2 + SigLIP visual encoder, fine-tuned on 970,000 episodes from Open X-Embodiment, it outperformed the closed 55B RT-2-X by 16.5 percentage points of absolute task success across 29 tasks — with seven times fewer parameters.[57] It can be LoRA-fine-tuned on a single A100 and quantised to run on a consumer GPU.

Octo, released the same year, took the alternative design — a smaller transformer with a diffusion head, trained on 800k OXE trajectories, explicitly built to be fine-tuned onto new sensor suites and action spaces.[58] SmolVLA compressed the recipe to 450 million parameters and demonstrated that with flow matching and asynchronous inference it stays competitive with models sixteen times its size.[59] A parallel line of work showed that most of OpenVLA's remaining gap to closed models was an artefact of its autoregressive decoding and could be recovered with parallel decoding and a continuous action head.[60]

Where the frontier is, in mid-2026

Three architectures now define the state of the art, and they have converged on the same shape.

Physical Intelligence π-series

π0.5 co-trains on robot actions, web data, object detections, and predicted semantic subtasks, and was the first system to perform long-horizon cleaning tasks in entirely unseen homes — researchers tested it in rented houses in San Francisco that appeared nowhere in training.[2] The reported scaling result is the interesting one: performance improved steadily as the training set grew from 3 to 104 distinct homes. Environment diversity, not episode count, is the axis that buys generalisation. The weights and training code are public.[61]

Google DeepMind Gemini Robotics

An explicit two-model split. Gemini Robotics-ER is an embodied reasoning model: it does spatial understanding, pointing, counting, success detection, and multi-step planning, and it can natively call tools including web search.[62] Gemini Robotics is the VLA that turns its instructions into motor commands. The ER model "thinks before acting" and shows its reasoning; the VLA executes. An on-device variant runs locally on the robot and fine-tunes to a new task from 50–100 demonstrations.[63]

NVIDIA Isaac GR00T

An open humanoid foundation model with the same dual-system split, stated explicitly in those terms: a slow vision-language "System 2" that plans, and a fast diffusion-transformer "System 1" that produces continuous motor actions.[64] Later releases integrated a dedicated physical-reasoning VLM as the deliberative half.[65] Its distinguishing feature is where the training data comes from — which is the subject of the next section.

Toyota Research Institute and Boston Dynamics have applied the same recipe to a full humanoid: a single language-conditioned neural network controlling Atlas's whole body — stepping, crouching, shifting its centre of mass, avoiding self-collision — for long-horizon manipulation, trained from teleoperated demonstrations rather than hand-written behaviours.[66] Their framing is the honest one: skills are added by demonstration, and the stronger the model gets, the fewer demonstrations each new skill requires. That is the whole bet.

What a VLA still is not

It is not a planner. It has no explicit model of the world's dynamics and does not search over futures. It cannot tell you it is about to fail. It has no persistent memory beyond its context window, which is why "clean the whole house" is decomposed by a separate reasoning model rather than emitted as one trajectory. And its generalisation, while real, is a generalisation over visual and semantic variation — a new mug, a new kitchen, a new phrasing. Ask it to do something requiring a genuinely novel motor strategy and it will confidently interpolate between things it has seen, which in a physical system is another way of saying it will fail in a way that looks like it is trying.[67]

07 · Sim-to-real

Training in a world that does not exist

A physics engine gives you what physical robots cannot: unlimited data, at superhuman speed, with perfect labels, no wear, no safety officer, and a reset button. It also gives you a policy that works flawlessly in a world your robot does not live in. Everything in this section is about paying that debt.

The reality gap

The term dates to 1995, from an evolutionary-robotics paper whose conclusion was that controllers evolved in simulation transferred to hardware only when the simulation was deliberately made noisy in the right ways.[68] Thirty years later that is still, in essence, the whole method.

The gap has three sources, and they are not equally tractable.

A

Dynamics

Friction, damping, backlash, link inertia, motor torque constants, contact stiffness. Every one is a number you estimated. Rigid-body contact in particular is a mathematical fiction: real contacts are distributed pressure fields over deforming surfaces, and every solver approximates them differently.

B

Perception

Rendered pixels are not camera pixels. Rolling shutter, motion blur, chromatic aberration, auto-exposure, JPEG artefacts, dead pixels, the specific way your fluorescent tubes flicker at 100 Hz. A vision policy overfits to the renderer's idiosyncrasies with enthusiasm.

C

Latency

The one everyone forgets. In simulation, you observe at t and act at t. On hardware you observe a frame from t−40 ms over a USB bus and the torque lands at t+5 ms. A policy trained without modelled delay is jittery and marginally stable on real hardware, and no amount of visual realism will rescue it.

Domain randomisation: stop trying to be right

The pivotal reframing came from Tobin et al. in 2017.[69] Instead of building one accurate simulator, build a distribution of wildly varied ones — randomise textures, lighting, camera pose, object colours, distractor geometry — and train a single policy across all of them. If the variation is broad enough, the real world looks to the policy like just another sample from the training distribution. Their object detector, trained purely on non-photorealistic randomised renders, localised objects to 1.5 cm on a real robot with no real images at all.

Peng et al. extended it from pixels to physics, randomising mass, friction, and damping, and got zero-shot transfer of a learned pushing policy.[70] Tan et al. applied the recipe to a quadruped and identified the two ingredients that actually carried the transfer: an accurate actuator model, and randomised latency.[71] The decade of follow-up work has since been catalogued in survey form, and the taxonomy has been stable since roughly 2021.[72] OpenAI took it to its logical extreme with the Dactyl hand: in-hand cube reorientation, trained entirely in simulation with randomised dynamics, transferred to a real Shadow Hand.[73] The follow-up added automatic domain randomisation — progressively widening the randomisation ranges as the policy got competent, generating an endless curriculum — and produced a hand that solved a Rubik's cube one-handed, robustly enough to keep going while a researcher jabbed it with a giraffe plush toy and tied two of its fingers together.[74]

The Dactyl result is simultaneously the field's best advertisement and its most misread. It consumed thousands of GPU-years. It was one task. And the robustness it demonstrated was robustness to perturbation, not generalisation to new objects. Domain randomisation buys you a policy that ignores the axes you randomised; it buys you nothing on the axes you did not think of.

Making the simulator honest: learned actuator models

Sometimes the gap is not something to randomise over but something to model. The 2019 ANYmal result is the canonical example.[75] Series-elastic actuators exhibit a torque response that no analytical model captures well — friction, dynamics of the internal controller, non-linear compliance. So Hwangbo et al. did not model it. They collected data from the physical actuator and trained a small neural network to predict its torque response, then dropped that network into the simulator in place of the analytical actuator. The rest of the simulation stayed rigid-body and fast.

The result was a quadruped that learned locomotion in simulation and transferred zero-shot, running faster and more energy-efficiently than the hand-tuned model-based controller it replaced, and — memorably — recovering from being kicked over onto its back, a behaviour nobody had explicitly programmed. This is the highest-value pattern in sim-to-real: identify the one component where the analytical model is badly wrong, replace it with a learned one, and leave everything else fast.

Teacher–student: give the policy something to infer

The third family accepts that the parameters will differ and makes the policy adapt online. Train a "teacher" in simulation with access to privileged information — exact terrain height, exact friction, exact mass — which makes the RL problem easy. Then train a "student" that sees only what the real robot sees (proprioceptive history, maybe a depth camera) to imitate the teacher, forcing it to infer the privileged variables from the history of its own motion.

Lee et al. used this to walk over mud, snow, and rubble with proprioception alone.[76] Rapid Motor Adaptation compressed the inference into an explicit latent vector estimated from the last 50 timesteps of joint history, updated at 10 Hz.[77] Miki et al. fused it with exteroception, producing the quadruped that completed a full hiking loop in the Swiss Alps at human pace.[78] And the same recipe put a drone at champion level against human pilots in first-person-view racing, trained in simulation with a learned residual correcting the aerodynamics.[79]

The thing that made all of this practical: parallel simulation on a GPU

None of the above would matter if simulation were slow. The change that mattered most was architectural and unglamorous: run the physics on the GPU, in the same memory as the neural network, with thousands of environments stepping in lockstep.

MuJoCo established the standard for accurate, fast contact dynamics in 2012.[80] Isaac Gym then demonstrated end-to-end GPU simulation — physics, observation construction, and reward computation all resident in GPU memory, with no CPU round-trip.[81] The consequence was a two-to-three order of magnitude throughput improvement, and Rudin et al. turned it into the headline result: a quadruped learning to walk in under twenty minutes on a single desktop GPU, using 4,096 parallel environments.[82] Training runs that had required a cluster now fit on a workstation, which meant graduate students could iterate.

Digital twins and NVIDIA Omniverse

A digital twin is more than a simulation: it is a persistent, synchronised virtual counterpart of a specific physical system, kept in correspondence with it over the system's life.[83] For robotics, the useful version is narrower — a simulation faithful enough, and connected enough, that a policy trained in it can be deployed in the corresponding physical cell.

NVIDIA Omniverse is the platform that made this a product rather than a research artefact, and its most important design decision has nothing to do with physics or graphics. It is OpenUSD.[84] Universal Scene Description, originally Pixar's format for composing film scenes, provides a single, layered, non-destructively-composable scene graph that can represent geometry, materials, articulated kinematics, joint limits, sensors, and physics properties in one file.[85] That sounds like plumbing. It is the reason the loop closes: a CAD assembly, a URDF, a lidar scan of a real warehouse, and a physics solver's collision meshes can all be layers over the same stage.

The synthetic data flywheel

Put those pieces together and you get the thing the humanoid programmes are actually betting on. NVIDIA's reported figure for the GR00T pipeline: starting from a small number of human teleoperated demonstrations, they generated 780,000 synthetic manipulation trajectories — the equivalent of about 6,500 hours, or nine months, of human demonstration — in eleven hours of compute. Combining that synthetic data with the real data improved the model's performance by 40% over real data alone.[90]

Read that carefully, because the framing matters. The synthetic data did not replace the real data; it multiplied it. The real demonstrations supply the behaviour; the simulator supplies the variation — different object poses, different lighting, different distractors, different physics parameters — that the human never had time to demonstrate. This is the specific mechanism by which a hundred demonstrations become a policy that works in a house nobody demonstrated in.

Real-to-sim: closing the other direction

The newest and most interesting development runs the arrow backwards. Neural reconstruction — Gaussian splatting, essentially[23] — can now turn a phone video of a real room into a renderable, physics-ready OpenUSD stage. The environment your robot failed in on Tuesday becomes, on Wednesday, a simulation you can run ten thousand policy rollouts against.

This matters because it converts sim-to-real from an open-loop bet into a closed loop. Deploy, log the failures, reconstruct the failure scenes, retrain against them, redeploy. It is the robotics equivalent of a regression test suite, and it is the difference between a demo and a product.

The honest summary of sim-to-real, 2026

Locomotion is solved by simulation. Legged policies are now routinely trained end-to-end in a physics engine and deployed zero-shot, and the model-based controllers they replaced are being retired. The reason is that locomotion's reward is writable, its dynamics are dominated by rigid-body contact that solvers handle acceptably, and its perception requirement is low.

Manipulation is not. Contact-rich manipulation depends precisely on the physics that simulators get wrong — deformation, friction hysteresis, cloth, granular media, liquids — and on visual detail that renderers approximate. The state of the art is a hybrid: real human demonstrations for the behaviour, synthetic augmentation for the variation, and simulation for evaluation rather than training. Anyone telling you they train dexterous manipulation purely in sim is either doing something narrow or selling something.

08 · Tools

The stack you would actually build on

A reasonable question at this point is: if I wanted to do any of this on Monday, what do I install? The honest answer is that the ecosystem has consolidated dramatically since 2023, and the consolidation happened around three things.

Middleware: ROS 2

ROS was never an operating system; it was a message bus, a build system, and a package repository, and its actual contribution was social — it made robotics code shareable.[11] ROS 2 rebuilt it on DDS, added quality-of-service contracts on every topic, and removed the single point of failure of the original master node. It is now used in production and safety-relevant contexts, which the original never credibly was.[91]

The parts that matter for an AI robotics project: rclpy/rclcpp for nodes, tf2 for the tree of coordinate frames (which will otherwise eat a month of your life), ros2_control for the real-time hardware interface, MoveIt 2 for motion planning and collision checking,[92] and Nav2 for mobile-base navigation.[93]

The QoS settings are not a detail. Sensor streams should be best-effort and depth-1: a retransmitted camera frame from 200 ms ago is worse than no frame. Commands should be reliable. Getting this backwards produces a robot that appears to work and then, under load, silently acts on stale observations.

Data and policies: LeRobot

The most consequential open-source project in robot learning right now is not a model. It is Hugging Face's LeRobot, which did for robot data what datasets did for NLP: it defined a format.[97,98] LeRobotDataset stores synchronised camera video as MP4 and state/action streams as Parquet, with metadata describing the robot, the task, and the sampling rates — hosted on the Hub, streamable without downloading, and directly consumable by a PyTorch DataLoader with native windowing for action chunks.

Underneath it is a hardware abstraction that supports the SO-100 and SO-101 arms, Koch, ALOHA-2, LeKiwi, Stretch, and Reachy, and above it are trainable implementations of ACT, Diffusion Policy, SmolVLA, and GR00T. The practical consequence is that the entire loop — teleoperate, record, fine-tune a pretrained VLA, deploy — runs on a few hundred dollars of hardware and a consumer GPU.

The datasets worth knowing

  • Open X-Embodiment — 1M+ trajectories, 22 embodiments, 500+ skills, pooled from 21 institutions. The pretraining corpus for essentially every open VLA.[54]
  • DROID — 76k trajectories collected across 564 scenes and 86 tasks with a standardised Franka setup, deliberately built for in-the-wild diversity rather than lab-bench repetition.[99]
  • BridgeData V2 — 60k trajectories on a low-cost WidowX, with language annotations; the standard testbed for cheap-hardware generalisation.[100]
  • The NVIDIA Physical AI dataset — the synthetic side of the equation: hundreds of thousands of generated trajectories released alongside GR00T.[90]

Inference at the edge

The last piece is the one that decides whether any of this ships. A 7B VLA at bf16 is 14 GB of weights, and it must produce an action chunk in under 20 ms if you want 50 Hz. The options are: quantise (4-bit is now routine and costs a few points of success rate), distil into a smaller action expert, run the slow half in the cloud and the fast half on-robot, or put a datacentre-class module on the machine — which is what the current generation of humanoid platforms does.[64] None of these is free, and "what is your inference budget?" is the first question that separates a demo from a deployment.

09 · Measurement

How to tell whether any of it works

Robot learning has an evaluation problem, and it is more serious than the field's public output suggests.

The small-N catastrophe

Evaluating a manipulation policy on hardware costs a human an afternoon: reset the scene, run the rollout, judge success, repeat. Twenty trials is a typical budget, and twenty trials cannot resolve the effect sizes people routinely claim. At N = 20 and a true success rate near 50%, the 95% confidence interval on the estimate is roughly ±22 percentage points. A paper reporting "our method achieves 70% versus the baseline's 55%" over twenty trials each has, by Fisher's exact test, p = 0.26 — and the experiment as designed could only have detected a true improvement larger than about 39 points.

Kress-Gazit and colleagues at Toyota Research Institute wrote the paper the field needed on this, arguing that success rate reported without the number of runs, the initial-condition distribution, the success criterion, or any statistical analysis is not a result.[101] Their recommendations are unglamorous and correct: report N; specify and randomise initial conditions; use metrics that complement binary success; describe the failure modes narratively; and do the statistics.

10 · Assurance

A stochastic policy attached to an actuator

An industrial robot arm can exert enough force to kill a person, and the industry has spent fifty years building an assurance regime around that fact. That regime assumes the robot's behaviour is specified. It is not obvious what happens to it when the behaviour is sampled from a distribution learned from data.

The regime as it stands

The governing standards were substantially rewritten in 2025. ISO 10218-1 and 10218-2 — safety requirements for industrial robots and for robot applications and cells respectively — replaced their 2011 editions after roughly eight years of work by experts from more than twenty countries.[108,109] Three changes matter here.

  • ISO/TS 15066 was absorbed. The collaborative-robot technical specification no longer stands alone; its power-and-force-limiting and speed-and-separation-monitoring requirements are now inside the main standard.[109]
  • "Collaborative robot" is no longer a thing. The revision deliberately removes the term. Safety is a property of the application, not of the machine — a force-limited arm holding a scalpel is not safe, and a large industrial arm behind proper separation monitoring is.
  • Cybersecurity is now in scope, alongside explicit functional-safety requirements for safety functions.[108]

Personal-care and service robots fall under ISO 13482 instead.[110] In Europe, the Machinery Regulation[111] and the AI Act[112] now both apply, and a robot whose safety function depends on an AI component lands in the high-risk category, with conformity-assessment obligations attached.

What none of these standards yet tell you is how to verify a learned policy. The state of practice is honest about this: you do not certify the network. You certify a safety envelope around it — a separate, deterministic, safety-rated monitor with independent sensing that clamps velocity, limits force, enforces a protective separation distance, and can trip a category-0 stop regardless of what the policy is asking for. The network gets to propose. The envelope gets to dispose. This is exactly the architecture in Figure 2, and it is the reason that figure's bottom layer is not learned.

New attack surface

Connecting a language model to an actuator creates a class of vulnerability that has no precedent in either field.

RoboPAIR, from the University of Pennsylvania, is the demonstration everyone should read.[113] It adapts LLM jailbreaking to elicit harmful physical actions, and it was evaluated across three access levels: white-box against a self-driving LLM, grey-box against a wheeled UGV with a GPT-4o planner, and black-box against a commercially deployed quadruped with only query access. It found jailbreaks in all three, frequently at a 100% attack success rate. The quadruped result is the first successful jailbreak of a deployed commercial robot, and the harmful behaviours were not abstract — they included the robot searching for a person to deliver a (simulated) explosive payload to.

The paper's key observation is one that should reshape how the field thinks about alignment: a chatbot's guardrails are trained to refuse harmful text. But a robot planner is not asked to say anything harmful. It is asked to "walk forward one metre and then sit down", which is entirely benign as a sentence and lethal if the robot is on a bridge parapet. The harm is in the physical context, which the language model cannot see. BadRobot generalises the attack surface further, showing that embodied LLM agents can be induced to violate their own safety constraints through several distinct mechanisms.[114]

Below the planner, the perception stack has its own problem. Wang et al. showed that vision-language-action models are strikingly fragile to adversarial input: small, imperceptible perturbations to the camera image — or a small printed patch placed in the scene — can destabilise the emitted actions or steer the trajectory to an attacker-chosen target, in both digital and physical settings.[115] A robot that "sees" an object that is not there does not produce a wrong caption. It reaches into empty space with a closing gripper.

The asymmetry that should worry you

Every safety property the field has actually established is a property of the envelope: force limits, separation distances, stop categories. Every capability people are excited about is a property of the policy: generalisation, dexterity, open-world reasoning. These are not the same object, and the envelope does not scale with the policy. A force-limited arm that has been jailbroken into methodically dropping every glass in the kitchen is entirely compliant with ISO 10218 while destroying your house. Semantic safety — "should the robot do this at all?" — is a genuinely open problem, and the benchmarks for it are two years old.[63]

11 · Open problems

The seven things that are still broken

1. Data, still and always

Everything reduces to this. A frontier language model trains on something like 1015 tokens. Open X-Embodiment, the largest pooled robot corpus, is about 106 trajectories.[54] The gap is nine orders of magnitude, and unlike text, robot data does not exist waiting to be scraped — it has to be manufactured, one human-hour at a time, on hardware that wears out.

The three live strategies: synthesise it (the Omniverse flywheel — abundant, cheap, and systematically wrong in the ways that matter most for contact); harvest human video (egocentric footage has the right tasks and the wrong body, and the embodiment gap has not been convincingly bridged); or industrialise teleoperation (which is what the well-funded humanoid companies are quietly doing, and which is, so far, working). None of them is obviously sufficient. It is entirely possible that the correct answer is "all three, expensively, for a decade".

2. Dexterity

Current systems are good at pick-and-place and bad at everything a two-year-old finds trivial: unfolding a plastic bag, threading a shoelace, separating one sheet of paper from a stack, opening a zip-lock. These are contact-rich, deformable, high-frequency, and force-dominated. They fail for three compounding reasons: simulators model deformation and friction badly, so synthetic data does not help; visual observation is occluded by the hand doing the manipulating; and tactile sensing, which is the modality that would actually solve it, is not standard equipment on any commercial platform.[31]

3. Long horizons and memory

A VLA's memory is its context window. "Clean the kitchen" is a task that takes twenty minutes, involves hundreds of decisions, and requires remembering that you already put the cereal away. The current answer — a slow reasoning model decomposing the goal into subtasks that a fast VLA executes[62] — is a good architecture and an incomplete one, because it pushes the memory problem up a level rather than solving it. Persistent, structured, updatable memory across an hour of physical activity is not a solved problem in any modality, and it is harder when the state you must remember is the state of a physical world that other agents are also changing.

4. Cross-embodiment, properly

The Open X-Embodiment result — that pooling data across 22 robot types helps — is real and important.[54] But the current mechanism is closer to data augmentation than to genuine transfer. There is no principled representation of "the same skill, on a different body". Ask a policy trained on a parallel-jaw gripper to use a five-fingered hand and it has nothing to transfer, because the action space it learned is the wrong shape. Whether embodiment-agnostic skill representations are achievable, or whether every new morphology will always demand its own data, is arguably the central open scientific question in the field.

5. Failure awareness

A policy that knows it is about to fail is worth several policies that do not. Current VLAs emit an action with the same confidence whether the situation is squarely in distribution or wildly outside it. Calibrated uncertainty over actions — and, more usefully, over outcomes — would let a robot stop and ask for help, which is what makes deployment economically viable. Success detection is being added to the reasoning layer,[62] but detecting failure after it happens is a much weaker property than predicting it before.

6. World models

The most intellectually interesting frontier is the attempt to give policies an internal simulator. Cosmos is one instantiation: a generative video model trained on physical dynamics that can be conditioned on an action and asked to predict what happens next.[89] V-JEPA 2 is another, learning predictive representations from video and using them for planning without ever rendering a pixel.[116] If these work, the payoff is enormous: a policy that can imagine the consequence of an action before taking it recovers everything model-based control gave up, without needing an analytical model. If they do not, the field is committed to a purely reactive paradigm that will keep hitting the long tail.

7. The economics

Worth stating plainly, because the technical discussion tends to float free of it. There were 4.66 million industrial robots in operational use worldwide in 2024, with 542,000 installed that year, and essentially all of them are position-controlled machines executing hand-written programmes in structured cells.[117] None of the technology described in this article is deployed at that scale. The value proposition of a learned policy is that it works in an unstructured environment — and unstructured environments are exactly where the reliability requirements are strictest, the failure costs are highest, and the data is scarcest. The technology is real. Whether the economics close is a separate question, and it has not yet been answered anywhere except in a few high-value niches.

12 · Close

What to believe

Strip away the humanoid videos and the funding rounds and the claim underneath all of this is small, specific, and — as far as anyone can currently tell — true:

A network pretrained on the internet, then fine-tuned on robot trajectories, generalises to physical situations it has never seen, in a way that a network trained only on robot trajectories does not.

Everything else is engineering around that fact. Action tokenisation is engineering around the mismatch between a transformer's output and a servo's input. Action chunking is engineering around the mismatch between inference latency and control frequency. Domain randomisation, digital twins, and synthetic data pipelines are engineering around the fact that we have nine orders of magnitude less robot data than text. The dual-system architecture is engineering around the fact that thinking and reacting have irreconcilable timing requirements.

The right posture, then, is neither the dismissal ("it's just a chatbot with a gripper") nor the extrapolation ("household robots by 2027"). It is the one an engineer takes toward any capability that is real but immature: identify precisely what it can do, build the envelope that contains what it cannot, and measure it honestly enough that you would know if you were wrong.

The measuring is the part the field is worst at, and it is the part that will determine whether the next five years produce robots or demos. Report N. Report the failures. Report the conditions. The physics does not care how good the video looked.
