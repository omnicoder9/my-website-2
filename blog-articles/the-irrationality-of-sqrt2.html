Mathematics — Ancient Greece & Its Afterlife

The Diagonal That
Broke the Cosmos — On the Irrationality of √2, and the Upset It Caused Twice

A single proof — that the diagonal of a unit square shares no common measure with its side — is said to have cracked the Pythagorean universe in the fifth century BCE. Two and a half millennia later, historians of mathematics are still arguing about whether that crisis ever actually happened.

00  —  Opening

Opening the Crack

Take a square with sides of length one. Draw its diagonal. By the theorem that bears Pythagoras’s name, that diagonal has length √2 — a number that, stated as a decimal, runs 1.41421356… without ever terminating and without ever settling into a repeating block. No fraction of whole numbers, however large, equals it exactly. This is not a limitation of arithmetic notation; it is a theorem, provable in a handful of lines, that no such fraction can exist. The proof is one of the oldest pieces of pure mathematics anyone still teaches unchanged, essentially as it was taught in Athens twenty-four centuries ago.

What makes the result worth an essay rather than a footnote is everything that is supposed to have happened around it. As the story is usually told, the discovery landed inside a school of Greek mystics who had built an entire cosmology on the premise that whole numbers and their ratios were the ultimate substance of reality — and the discovery broke that premise so badly that the man who found it, or revealed it, was drowned at sea for his trouble. That is the ancient upset. There is a second, quieter one layered on top of it: for the past century, professional historians of mathematics have been arguing, in print, in some detail, about whether the ancient upset actually occurred in anything like that form — whether there was ever a Pythagorean "crisis" at all, or whether twentieth-century mathematicians looking for a mirror of their own foundational anxieties invented one and projected it backward onto people who left almost no direct testimony of their own.

This essay tries to hold both stories at once: the mathematics of why √2 is irrational, told in several independent ways old and new; the legend of Hippasus and what can and cannot be verified about it; and the historiographical dispute — itself now over a century old — about whether "the first foundational crisis in mathematics" is a fact about the fifth century BCE or a fact about the history of history. Along the way the essay follows the technical thread forward, from the reductio argument preserved as an appendix to Euclid, through Theodorus and Theaetetus, to Eudoxus’s theory of proportion, and on to Dedekind’s nineteenth-century construction of the real numbers — a construction Dedekind himself said he modeled on the Greek theory it eventually replaced.

01  —  Cosmology

All Is Number

Aristotle, writing a century and a half after Pythagoras, reports that the Pythagoreans held numbers to be the first principles of everything that exists — not merely useful for describing the world, but constitutive of it [1]. Odd and even, limit and unlimited, unity and plurality: these were not abstractions layered on top of a separately existing physical world, they were understood as the actual stuff and structure of the cosmos. Harmony, in this scheme, was arithmetic made audible. The Pythagoreans are credited with discovering that the pleasing musical intervals — the octave, the fifth, the fourth — correspond to string-length ratios of 2:1, 3:2, and 4:3. If music, the most immediate evidence of order available to the ear, reduced to small whole-number ratios, it was natural to suppose that everything else did too: the motion of planets, the proportions of the human body, the structure of matter itself.

It is worth being careful here, because the popular image of Pythagoras — robed mystic, secret brotherhood, a man who forbade his followers from eating beans — is a composite built up over eight centuries of later writers, many of them more interested in Pythagoras as a legendary sage than as a historical figure. Modern scholarship on the early Pythagoreans, most thoroughly assembled in Leonid Zhmud’s systematic study of the primary sources, treats the movement with considerably more caution than the popular accounts allow, and is skeptical of many of the specific biographical details later antiquity attached to Pythagoras and his immediate circle [2]. What survives reasonably intact across the skepticism is the core commitment: a philosophy in which whole numbers, and the ratios between them, were taken to be adequate to describe any magnitude that could occur in geometry, music, or the physical world. That commitment had a name for what a ratio of whole numbers could express — a "rational" quantity, in the literal sense of expressible as a ratio — and, crucially, no explicit acknowledgment that anything else might be needed.

This is the premise the diagonal of a square quietly violates. If a side and its diagonal shared even an extremely small common unit of length — if you could lay off that unit some whole number of times along the side and some other whole number of times along the diagonal — then the two lengths would be commensurable, and their ratio would be a ratio of whole numbers, exactly the kind of quantity Pythagorean number philosophy was built to handle. The proof discussed in the sections ahead shows that no such unit exists, at any scale, however small. Two lengths that share no common measure at all, no matter how finely you subdivide, are called incommensurable — and the side and diagonal of a square are the paradigm case of it.

A ratio of whole numbers can always be reduced to lowest terms. The diagonal-to-side ratio of a square cannot be reduced to lowest terms, because it is not a ratio of whole numbers in the first place. That gap between what a worldview requires and what geometry actually delivers is the whole of the story.
02  —  Legend and Evidence

The Man Who (Maybe) Drowned

The name conventionally attached to the discovery is Hippasus of Metapontum, a fifth-century-BCE Pythagorean about whom almost nothing is independently verifiable. The standard scholarly account of early Greek mathematics as a whole, Thomas Heath’s 1921 History of Greek Mathematics, treats the Hippasus attribution as traditional rather than documented, consistent with how thinly sourced nearly all biographical detail about fifth-century Pythagoreans remains [3]. The most influential modern study of the episode specifically, Kurt von Fritz’s 1945 paper in the Annals of Mathematics, calls the discovery of incommensurability one of the most remarkable achievements of early Greek mathematics precisely because it appears to have been made so early, with such limited tools, by a culture whose numerical technique was, by the standards of contemporary Babylonian and Egyptian mathematics, comparatively unsophisticated [4]. Von Fritz reconstructs a plausible route to the discovery through the regular pentagon and its diagonals — the pentagram, the reputed badge of the Pythagorean brotherhood — where the ratio of a diagonal to a side is the golden ratio, and a short geometric argument shows that ratio cannot be a ratio of whole numbers either.

The drowning story itself comes from much later sources. Iamblichus, writing in the third or fourth century CE — roughly seven to eight hundred years after Hippasus supposedly lived — records in his On the Pythagorean Life that a member of the school perished at sea as punishment for impiety, having disclosed something that should have stayed secret [5]. But Iamblichus’s own account is internally inconsistent about what, exactly, was disclosed. In one telling it is the construction of a sphere from twelve regular pentagons — that is, the dodecahedron — not incommensurability at all. Other late sources, Pappus among them, describe a member of the school drowned for revealing "irrationality," without naming Hippasus, or naming him only in some manuscript traditions and not others.

Modern encyclopedic treatments of Hippasus are candid about how thin the evidentiary chain is: no ancient writer explicitly credits Hippasus, by name, with discovering irrational numbers specifically, as opposed to the dodecahedron construction or some other Pythagorean secret [6]. The drowning, the impiety charge, the specific link to √2 rather than to the pentagon’s golden ratio — each detail is attested by a different combination of late, mutually disagreeing sources, none of them contemporary with the events they describe. A recent popular account in Scientific American, itself drawing on the current historical consensus, puts the matter plainly: what actually happened in the fifth century BCE is far from clear, and the element historians are comfortable affirming is only that the Pythagoreans, at some point, proved that some quantities have no common measure — not the biography, the martyrdom, or the precise geometric route by which they got there [7].

What the Legend Says

  • Hippasus discovered irrationality and revealed it outside the brotherhood.
  • He was drowned at sea as divine punishment for the impiety of disclosure.
  • The discovery came from the diagonal of the unit square.
  • The Pythagoreans tried, and failed, to keep the secret entirely suppressed.

What the Evidence Supports

  • The Pythagoreans, collectively, proved some form of incommensurability — authorship uncertain.
  • The drowning appears only in sources written 700–900 years later, and disagree on the cause.
  • An equally old tradition ties the fatal disclosure to the dodecahedron, not the square.
  • No contemporary fifth-century source mentions Hippasus, a death at sea, or a cover-up at all.

None of this means the mathematics is in doubt — the proof of incommensurability is solid regardless of who first gave it or how they died. What is in doubt is the entire dramatic apparatus built around the proof: a persecuted whistleblower, a secret society enforcing silence with violence, a single traceable eureka moment. James Choike’s 1980 study of the pentagon route works through the geometry Von Fritz proposed in careful detail, showing that a proof by infinite descent on the pentagon’s diagonals is well within the reach of fifth-century technique — which strengthens the case that a Pythagorean discovery happened roughly on schedule, while leaving who exactly made it, and what happened to him afterward, essentially unrecoverable [8]. The most defensible position, and the one this essay adopts, is Zhmud’s: the discovery is real and early; the martyrdom is a story later antiquity wanted to tell about it [2].

03  —  Deep Background

Before Pythagoras: Babylon and the Indus

It is tempting, telling the story this way, to imagine irrational lengths as a purely Greek discovery, sprung from nowhere in the fifth century BCE. The numerical value of √2 was in circulation far earlier, and far outside Greece — only nobody who calculated it seems to have paused to ask whether it could be written exactly as a fraction.

The clearest evidence is a small Old Babylonian clay tablet catalogued as YBC 7289, dated to roughly 1800–1600 BCE and held in the Yale Babylonian Collection. It shows a square with both diagonals drawn, one side labeled with the sexagesimal (base-60) numeral 30, and the diagonal labeled with two further sexagesimal numerals [9]. Converted to decimal, the diagonal figure works out to approximately 1.41421297 — correct to roughly six decimal digits, a level of accuracy Otto Neugebauer and Abraham Sachs, the cuneiform scholars who first recognized the tablet’s significance in 1945, described as the finest computational precision documented anywhere in the ancient world [9]. A 1998 study by David Fowler and Eleanor Robson reconstructs plausible scribal methods — iterative averaging procedures related to what is now called Heron’s method — by which a Babylonian mathematics student could have reached that figure using only the reciprocal tables and multiplication techniques already standard in the curriculum [10]. There is no indication in the tablet, or in any known Babylonian source, that anyone asked whether that sexagesimal expansion terminated, or whether some more precise fraction might one day be found. The Babylonian scribes were building a practical instrument, correct enough for any actual construction problem; they were not raising, let alone answering, a question about existence.

Independently, and probably around the same broad era, a comparable approximation of √2 appears in Indian mathematical literature. The Baudhāyana Śulbasūtra — a Vedic ritual text concerned with the geometry of fire-altar construction, conventionally dated to somewhere in the eighth century BCE, though the manuscript tradition is later — gives a rule for the length of a square’s diagonal: increase the side by one third, then by a further quarter of that third, then subtract a thirty-fourth part of the previous term [11]. Worked out arithmetically, that rule yields 1 + 1/3 + 1/(3·4) – 1/(3·4·34), or 577/408 ≈ 1.41421568 — accurate to five decimal places, and reached, remarkably, by a comparably iterative, self-correcting method to the Babylonian one, despite no known contact between the two traditions at the relevant date [12]. As with the Babylonian tablet, the Śulbasūtra text records a procedure for construction, not an argument about whether the procedure could ever terminate in an exact answer.

What this deep background establishes is a genuine, if easily overstated, point of contrast: excellent numerical approximation of √2 is older than Greek mathematics by a millennium or more, and was achieved twice, independently, by cultures with entirely different notational systems. What is specifically Greek — so far as the surviving record shows — is not the number, and not even the geometric picture of a square and its diagonal, but the demand for a proof that no fraction, however large its terms, could ever equal that number exactly. Babylon and the Indus valley got closer and closer to √2. Only in Greece, on the current evidence, did anyone try to show that "closer and closer" was the only kind of answer that could ever exist.

04  —  The Argument

Proving the Unthinkable

The proof that made the trouble is short enough to fit on an index card, which is part of why it is so often the first genuine piece of pure mathematics a student encounters. Set it up geometrically first, then arithmetically.

Suppose, for the sake of argument, that it can — that √2 = p/q for some whole numbers p and q, and suppose further, as we always may, that p/q has already been reduced to lowest terms, so p and q share no common factor. Squaring both sides gives 2 = p²/q², or p² = 2q². The right-hand side is visibly even, so p² is even — and since the square of an odd number is always odd, p itself must be even. Write p = 2k for some whole number k. Substituting back, (2k)² = 2q², so 4k² = 2q², so q² = 2k². But now the same reasoning applies to q: q² is even, so q is even too. Both p and q are even — both divisible by 2 — which directly contradicts the assumption, made at the very start and always available without loss of generality, that p/q was already in lowest terms. The assumption that √2 is a ratio of whole numbers has produced a strict logical contradiction. It must therefore be false.

This style of argument — assume the opposite of what you want to prove, derive an impossibility, conclude the opposite must be false — is called reductio ad absurdum, reduction to the absurd, and it was already a standard tool of both Greek geometry and Greek dialectic by the classical period [13]. Aristotle uses precisely this proof, in exactly this outline, as his go-to worked example of the technique. In the Prior Analytics, laying out how arguments "through the impossible" function logically, he remarks, almost in passing, that the diagonal of a square is incommensurable with the side because assuming otherwise makes odd numbers equal to even ones [14]. Aristotle is not explaining the proof for its own sake — he assumes his audience already knows it well enough to recognize the reference — which tells us the argument was already a familiar piece of common mathematical culture by the time he was lecturing, a full generation or two after the Pythagorean pentagon-and-golden-ratio story most historians place as the actual discovery route.

Strikingly, this exact odd/even proof does not appear inside the main body of Euclid’s Elements as we have it. It survives as Proposition 117 of Book X — tacked on after the book’s official closing summary, in a position and style that generations of editors, beginning with the nineteenth-century Danish philologist J.L. Heiberg, have judged to be a later interpolation, not part of Euclid’s original text, though probably added before the fourth-century commentator Theon of Alexandria produced his edition [15]. Wilbur Knorr, whose scholarship on this material is discussed at length in the next section, treats the appendix proof as a genuinely old fragment nonetheless — plausibly close to, or even identical with, the very proof Aristotle is casually referencing a century before Euclid compiled the Elements at all [16]. The manuscript history is a small irony worth sitting with: the most famous proof in the history of number theory is not, strictly, inside the book most people associate it with. It rode along as an addendum, preserved by scribal accident, for two thousand years.

Why lowest terms is not cheating
  1. Every fraction can be reduced to lowest terms by dividing numerator and denominator by their greatest common factor — this is guaranteed by the ordinary arithmetic of whole numbers, not assumed as a convenience.
  2. So if √2 equals any ratio of whole numbers, it equals one particular ratio already in lowest terms; nothing is lost by starting the argument there.
  3. The whole argument then shows that ratio cannot exist without contradicting its own lowest-terms status — which rules out every ratio of whole numbers at once, not just some.

05  —  Historiography

Was There Ever a Crisis?

Here is where the essay’s second controversy takes over from its first. The phrase "foundational crisis of Greek mathematics" is not ancient. No surviving Greek source uses anything like it, and no ancient text describes the Pythagorean school convulsed by an intellectual emergency over incommensurability. The phrase, and the historical narrative that goes with it, is a modern invention — and its inventors are identifiable, datable, and traceable in the scholarly literature with unusual precision.

The trail starts with Paul Tannery, a French engineer and historian of ancient science, whose 1887 study of Greek geometry proposed that the discovery of incommensurability must have caused what he called, in French, a genuine logical scandal for a Pythagorean mathematics built on the assumption that all magnitudes were commensurable [17]. Four decades later, the German mathematicians Helmut Hasse and Heinrich Scholz took Tannery’s suggestion and gave it a name that stuck: their 1928 monograph is titled, in German, Die Grundlagenkrisis der griechischen Mathematik — The Foundations Crisis of Greek Mathematics — explicitly modeled on, and named in deliberate echo of, the foundational crisis then actively convulsing early-twentieth-century mathematics over Cantorian set theory, Russell’s paradox, and the intuitionist challenge to classical logic [18]. Hasse and Scholz were not neutral historians describing a fifth-century-BCE event; they were working mathematicians living through their own field’s foundational emergency, reaching backward for a historical precedent that would make their present crisis look like part of a recurring pattern rather than a unique catastrophe.

That is precisely the move the next generation of historians pushed back on. In 1966 the Dutch mathematician and didactician Hans Freudenthal published a paper whose title is itself the objection: Y avait-il une crise des fondements des mathématiques dans l’antiquité? — Was there a crisis of foundations of mathematics in antiquity? [19] Freudenthal’s answer, worked through the actual surviving Greek mathematical texts rather than through what a modern mathematician would expect a crisis to look like, was skeptical: the Greek geometric texts we have show a discipline handling incommensurable magnitudes with steadily increasing sophistication, not a discipline in visible disarray searching for new foundations under emergency conditions.

The most sustained modern voice on this question is Wilbur Knorr, whose 1975 book The Evolution of the Euclidean Elements remains the standard technical study of how the theory of incommensurable magnitudes actually developed across Book X of Euclid, tracing a plausible line of mathematical refinement from an early, narrow arithmetic proof through Theodorus and Theaetetus to the finished classification scheme discussed later in this essay [20]. In a lecture prepared shortly before his death and published posthumously in 2001, Knorr addressed the crisis narrative directly and unambiguously, describing the notion of a Greek foundational crisis over irrationality as, in his own words, a modern fiction [16] — a piece of historiography manufactured by Tannery, sharpened by Hasse and Scholz into an explicit parallel with Hilbert-era anxieties, and kept in circulation since largely because it makes a satisfying story, not because the extant Greek mathematical texts support it.

It is worth being precise about what is, and is not, being disputed. Nobody in this debate doubts that incommensurability was discovered, that it was philosophically significant, or that it eventually required Eudoxus to build an entirely new theory of proportion to accommodate it — that theory is the subject of the section after next, and its existence is not in question. What is disputed is the dramaturgy: whether the discovery produced anything resembling a lived emergency — panic, suppressed publication, an active crisis requiring urgent resolution — among the mathematicians who actually worked with it, as opposed to a difficult problem that got progressively better understood and eventually well handled over a period of a century or more, the ordinary tempo of mathematical progress rather than the tempo of a scandal. Freudenthal and Knorr’s case, in short, is that historians of the 1880s–1920s were pattern-matching their own era’s foundational anxiety onto antiquity, and that the pattern-match, however psychologically understandable, outruns the actual evidence.

The Pythagorean brotherhood may never have experienced a crisis at all. What we can say with confidence is that historians of mathematics did — twice, in fact, once in 1887 and once more in 1928 — and then spent the following half-century arguing about whether they had projected it backward by two and a half thousand years.Following Freudenthal 1966 and Knorr 2001

This does not make the Hippasus legend from the previous section irrelevant — if anything, it sharpens the point. The ancient upset (a discovery so dangerous a man died for revealing it) and the modern upset (a century of historians arguing about whether any of that drama actually happened, or whether it is a nineteenth-century morality tale retrojected onto a mathematically real but socially unremarkable discovery) are structurally the same kind of event: both are stories a later era needed to tell about a piece of mathematics that, considered purely as mathematics, requires no story at all. √2 is irrational whether or not anyone drowned over it, and whether or not the Pythagoreans experienced anything a modern reader would recognize as a crisis.

06  —  Generalizing

Seventeen Roots and a Generalization

Whatever the emotional temperature of the discovery, the mathematical follow-through is unambiguous and well documented, and it takes place a couple of generations after the Pythagorean pentagon episode, in Plato’s dialogue named for its central figure: the Theaetetus. Set dramatically in 399 BCE, the dialogue has the young geometer Theaetetus recall a lesson from his teacher, Theodorus of Cyrene, a mathematician about whom Plato’s dialogues are effectively the only surviving first-hand testimony [21]. Theodorus, Theaetetus reports, had shown geometrically that the sides of squares with areas 3 and 5 square feet cannot be measured exactly against a one-foot unit — that is, that √3 and √5 are irrational — and had then gone on, case by case, through 6, 7, 8, and onward, stopping only when he reached 17 [22].

Why stop at seventeen has generated its own minor scholarly cottage industry. The natural modern guess — that Theodorus’s method broke down at 17 for some intrinsic mathematical reason — does not hold up well, since the same style of argument that handles 16 (a perfect square, so trivially rational, and skipped) generalizes cleanly to 18, 19, and beyond with no special obstruction. A more plausible explanation, floated by ancient commentators and repeated by modern historians, is Pythagorean number-mysticism itself: 17 sits between the two sides of the "marriage number" 16 (4²) and its neighbor, and some scholars suspect Theodorus simply judged the pattern established and the pedagogical point made, without any technical wall stopping him [23]. Theaetetus, in Plato’s telling, is untroubled by his teacher’s stopping point and does exactly what a good student should: he and his young companion, also confusingly named Socrates, set out to find a single argument covering every non-square whole number at once, rather than replaying Theodorus’s case-by-case geometry indefinitely.

The generalization Theaetetus reached — that the side of a square is commensurable with a chosen unit length exactly when the square’s area, measured in that unit, is a perfect square number, and incommensurable (irrational) otherwise — is a genuinely general theorem, covering infinitely many cases with a single argument, and it marks a clear conceptual advance over Theodorus’s finite list. Ancient testimony, echoed by the ninth-century-CE mathematician Pappus of Alexandria in his commentary on Euclid, credits Theaetetus with laying the groundwork for the elaborate classification of irrational lines that eventually became Book X of the Elements [25]. Theaetetus went on, in work only indirectly preserved, to distinguish several species of irrational magnitude — medial lines, and others — a classification refined into its final form by whoever compiled Euclid’s Book X, discussed two sections ahead.

It is easy to read the sequence Hippasus → Theodorus → Theaetetus as a tidy relay race, each runner picking up cleanly where the last left off. The historical reality visible in the sources is messier and, if anything, more interesting: roughly a century separates the (disputed) Pythagorean discovery from Theodorus’s seventeen cases, and Theodorus’s own method is never actually stated in the Theaetetus — Plato has Theaetetus describe the results Theodorus proved without recording the proofs themselves, leaving modern scholars to reconstruct plausible fifth-century techniques from later, indirect evidence. What is not in doubt is the direction of travel: from a single hard case, to a short finite list of cases, to one universal argument — the standard trajectory by which Greek mathematics converted a scandal into a settled, teachable, and eventually rather routine piece of curriculum.

07  —  Resolution

Eudoxus and the Rescue of Continuous Magnitude

Theaetetus’s generalization solved the problem of which square roots are irrational. It did not, by itself, solve the deeper structural problem incommensurability had exposed: Greek mathematics still needed a coherent theory of ratio and proportion that worked for any two magnitudes, commensurable or not, so that geometry could keep using proportional reasoning — the engine behind similar triangles, the method of exhaustion, and most of classical geometric argument — without silently assuming, as the old Pythagorean definition of "ratio" effectively had, that every pair of lengths shares a common measure.

That theory was supplied by Eudoxus of Cnidus, working in the fourth century BCE, roughly two generations after Theaetetus and in the orbit of Plato’s Academy [26]. Eudoxus’s solution, preserved as Book V of Euclid’s Elements, sidesteps the question of whether two magnitudes share a common unit altogether. Instead of defining "A is to B as C is to D" in terms of shared integer multiples, Eudoxus defines two ratios as equal when, for absolutely every pair of whole numbers m and n you might choose, multiplying tells you the same thing about both ratios: mA is greater than, equal to, or less than nB exactly when mC is greater than, equal to, or less than nD. This definition makes no reference to commensurability at all. It works identically well whether A and B share a common measure or not, because it is built entirely from comparisons that whole-number multiplication can always perform, regardless of what kind of magnitudes A and B actually are.

The resemblance to a much later piece of mathematics is not subtle, and it has been noted by essentially every historian who has looked at both theories side by side. In 1872 Richard Dedekind published a construction of the real numbers from the rationals using exactly this kind of exhaustive comparison — a "cut" of the rational numbers into a lower and an upper class, with every element of the lower class less than every element of the upper. The American philosopher of science Howard Stein, in a detailed 1990 study comparing the two theories directly, shows that Eudoxus’s definition of equal ratios generates, term for term, precisely a Dedekind cut in the positive rationals — the partition into "quantities m/n that make mA ≤ nB" and its complement is, formally, the identical mathematical object Dedekind introduces twenty-two centuries later [27]. Dedekind himself was aware of and acknowledged the parallel, though as the mathematician Milenko Nikolić observes in a careful 1974 comparison of the two frameworks, the resemblance is not quite total: Eudoxus is comparing already-given magnitudes to each other, while Dedekind is using cuts to construct new numbers that did not previously exist, including cuts with no rational number sitting exactly at the boundary — a move with no real analogue in the ancient theory [28].

Still, the achievement is properly Eudoxus’s to claim: a theory of proportion adequate to every pair of magnitudes, commensurable or not, built without ever requiring an answer to the question that had caused all the trouble in the first place. Whatever crisis incommensurability had or had not caused a century earlier, by the time Eudoxus was finished, Greek geometry had a foundation robust enough that the distinction between rational and irrational ratios no longer mattered to the logical structure of proportional argument at all. It is one of the cleaner examples in the history of mathematics of a problem being not so much solved as dissolved — made structurally irrelevant by a better framework, rather than answered on its own original terms.

08  —  Classification

Euclid’s Cross: Book X and the Thirteen Irrationals

Book X of Euclid’s Elements is, by a wide margin, the longest and most technically demanding book of the thirteen, and it is devoted almost entirely to classifying irrational lines — the geometric descendants of Theaetetus’s work, systematized into a scheme so intricate that the sixteenth-century Flemish mathematician Simon Stevin reportedly complained that the book had become, in the words later historians use to summarize his frustration, "the cross of the mathematicians," a labor so forbidding that many students simply gave up on it [16].

The book opens by defining a baseline "rational" length — any length chosen as the unit, plus any length commensurable with it either in length or merely in the square on it — and then spends its remaining roughly one hundred and fifteen propositions classifying every length that fails to meet that standard into one of thirteen distinct species: the medial line (the side of a square equal in area to a rectangle formed by two rational lines commensurable only in square, such as √2 itself when the unit is taken as rational), and twelve further species built from sums and differences of such lines — six orders of binomial lines and six matching orders of "apotomes," their subtractive counterparts [29]. The scheme is exhaustive in a very literal sense: Book X is attempting a complete taxonomy of every length constructible by compass and straightedge that involves at most one nested square root, sorted by exactly how that nesting occurs.

A narrow slice of Book X’s classification

  • Rational — the chosen unit length, and anything commensurable with it (e.g. 3, 1/2, 7/4, taking the unit as 1).
  • Medial — the side of a square equal to a rectangle of two rational lines commensurable only in square, not length (e.g. √2, taken as a pure geometric magnitude).
  • Binomial / Apotome (six orders each) — sums and differences of a rational and a medial line, or of two medials, sorted by further commensurability conditions (e.g. lines corresponding to expressions like √5 + √3, or √7 – √2).

Modern scholarship no longer treats this classification as Euclid’s own invention from scratch. Knorr’s reconstruction, discussed in the previous two sections, argues that Book X preserves several distinct historical layers — an early arithmetic core close to the odd/even proof, a middle layer reflecting Theodorus and Theaetetus’s case-by-case and then general treatments, and a late systematizing layer, quite possibly by Theaetetus himself or a close associate, that produced the finished thirteen-fold scheme [20]. Even the book’s closing propositions carry uncertain provenance: Heath’s standard edition follows Heiberg in treating several of the final propositions, including the odd/even proof examined in Section 4, as later additions rather than part of the original compilation [15].

Book X earns its formidable reputation honestly — it is dense, notation-starved by modern standards (everything is stated in words and diagrams, with no algebraic symbolism at all), and its thirteen-way classification maps onto modern quadratic-extension algebra only after considerable translation effort. But it is worth pausing on what the book represents rather than only how hard it is to read: within roughly a century of a discovery legend says nearly destroyed a philosophical school, Greek mathematicians had not merely accepted irrational magnitudes but built an entire, rigorously organized taxonomy of them, more elaborate by far than anything the rational numbers alone had ever required. Whatever crisis incommensurability caused or did not cause, the mathematical response to it was not retreat. It was a hundred and fifteen propositions of increasingly confident classification.

09  —  Modern Proofs

Six Ways to Prove the Same Thing

Part of what keeps √2’s irrationality alive as living mathematics, rather than a museum piece, is how many genuinely different proofs of it keep appearing in the research literature, more than two thousand years after the first one. Ivan Niven’s classic 1961 monograph Numbers: Rational and Irrational treats the result as a natural entry point into the whole theory of Diophantine approximation precisely because it tolerates so many independent styles of attack [30]. A working survey of six of them, moving from the most classical to the most recent, shows the same theorem being re-derived from arithmetic, from geometry, from analysis, and from pure logic.

1. The parity proof (arithmetic, ≈ 5th century BCE)

Already covered in full in Section 4: assume p/q in lowest terms, square, and show p and q must both be even, contradicting the lowest-terms assumption. This is the proof Aristotle references and the one appended to Euclid’s Book X.

2. The unique-factorization proof (arithmetic, standard modern form)

A cleaner modern variant sidesteps the "lowest terms" setup entirely and appeals directly to the fundamental theorem of arithmetic — that every whole number greater than 1 factors into primes in exactly one way, order aside. If p² = 2q², count how many times the prime 2 appears in the factorization of each side. On the left, 2 appears an even number of times in p² (twice whatever count it has in p). On the right, 2q² has 2 appearing an odd number of times — one more than its even count in q². An even count cannot equal an odd count, so no whole numbers p and q satisfy p² = 2q² at all (other than the trivial p = q = 0), which is a stronger and tidier conclusion than the parity proof’s contradiction-via-common-factor, reached via the same core arithmetic fact that underlies most of elementary number theory [31].

3. Apostol’s geometric dissection (2000)

In a short 2000 note in the American Mathematical Monthly, Tom Apostol gave a purely geometric proof that revives the classical Greek spirit of argument without any algebra at all [32]. Suppose an isosceles right triangle exists with all three sides measured by whole numbers — the smallest such triangle, by hypothesis. Apostol shows, by a short compass-and-straightedge construction folding the hypotenuse onto a leg, that this triangle generates a strictly smaller isosceles right triangle whose sides are still whole numbers — directly contradicting the assumption that the first triangle was the smallest possible. Since a strictly decreasing sequence of positive whole numbers cannot continue forever, no whole-number isosceles right triangle can exist at all, which is exactly the geometric statement that √2 is irrational. The proof is a modern rediscovery, in fully rigorous dress, of the "infinite descent" intuition many historians believe underlies the very earliest Pythagorean reasoning about the pentagon and the square alike. A 2012 study by Steven Miller and David Montague situates Apostol’s construction within a wider family of similar dissection proofs and shows the geometric and the algebraic arguments are, at bottom, the same infinite-descent idea wearing different clothes [33].

Infinite descent, stated once

If a property P held by some positive whole number always forces a strictly smaller whole number to also have property P, then no whole number can have property P at all — because an infinite strictly decreasing sequence of positive whole numbers is impossible. Both the parity proof and Apostol’s geometric proof are, underneath their different surfaces, applications of exactly this one principle.

4. The continued-fraction proof

A general theorem about continued fractions states that a number has a finite continued-fraction expansion exactly when it is rational, and an infinite expansion exactly when it is irrational. Section 10 works out √2’s continued fraction explicitly and shows, by direct algebraic manipulation, that it cannot terminate — giving a fourth, largely independent route to the same conclusion, one that generalizes immediately to a very wide class of other square roots.

5. The "one-sentence" algebraic proof

Several twentieth-century notes in the American Mathematical Monthly and Mathematics Magazine compress the argument into a single line by working directly with the equation 2 = (p/q)² and an extremal choice of q — the smallest positive denominator for which p/q squares to exactly 2. A short algebraic rearrangement produces a strictly smaller valid denominator, which is impossible if q was chosen minimal, and irrationality follows immediately. These "look, no primes needed" proofs, several of which are cited and compared in a 2020 survey of "proof by contradiction" pedagogy, sacrifice some conceptual transparency for remarkable compactness [31].

6. The constructive / computability framing

Modern constructive mathematics restates the whole question without contradiction at all: rather than assuming √2 = p/q and deriving a contradiction, one directly proves that for every pair of whole numbers p and q, p² and 2q² differ by a computable, explicit nonzero amount — effectively converting the classical reductio into a direct estimate. This framing matters more than it might first appear, because Errett Bishop’s foundational work in constructive analysis treats non-constructive existence proofs (of the "assume not, derive absurdity, conclude yes" variety) as a genuinely weaker form of mathematical knowledge than an explicit witness or bound, and the √2 case is a frequently used illustration of the difference between the two philosophies of proof. That six independently motivated arguments — ancient parity, modern factorization, pure geometry, continued fractions, minimal-counterexample algebra, and constructive estimation — all certify the identical fact is itself a small piece of evidence for how structurally solid the result is: there is no single fragile link in the reasoning, because there is no single chain of reasoning. There are at least six.

10  —  Approximation

The Best Wrong Answers

If √2 cannot be reached exactly by any fraction, the natural next question is which fractions come closest, and how that "closest" sequence is generated systematically rather than by trial and error. The answer is one of the oldest continuously used tools in number theory: the continued fraction.

Truncating that continued fraction at successively later points generates a sequence of fractions — called convergents — that are, in a precise sense proved in elementary number theory, the best possible rational approximations to √2 for their size of denominator: no fraction with a smaller or equal denominator lies closer to the true value [34].

This particular sequence of numerators and denominators — 1, 3, 7, 17, 41, 99, 239… over 1, 2, 5, 12, 29, 70, 169… — is not a modern discovery either. The second-century-CE mathematician Theon of Smyrna, in a handbook explicitly written to help students of Plato’s dialogues, describes what he calls "side numbers" and "diameter numbers," generated by a simple recursive rule (each new side number is the previous side plus the previous diameter; each new diameter number is twice the previous side plus the previous diameter), and states that the resulting ratios approximate the diagonal-to-side ratio of a square increasingly well [35]. Proclus, writing later still, attributes the technique to the Pythagoreans themselves, and at least one modern historian, following Knorr, places the actual mathematical content in the fifth century BCE — contemporary with, or shortly after, the original incommensurability proof [36]. There is even a plausible trace of the sequence inside Plato: a notoriously cryptic passage of the Republic, in a discussion of an ideal breeding cycle for the guardian class, refers to a "rational diameter of five," which several editors read as pointing to exactly the fraction 7/5 from the table above — treating 7 as the whole-number ("rational") stand-in for the true, irrational diagonal of a 5-unit square [37].

The recursive side-and-diameter rule is, in modern language, exactly the recurrence that generates solutions to the Pell equation x² – 2y² = ±1: check the table above and every one of those convergent pairs (p, q) satisfies p² – 2q² = ±1 precisely. The general theory connecting continued fractions of square roots to Pell’s equation was not put on a fully rigorous modern footing until Lagrange, in the eighteenth century, proved that the continued fraction of any non-square whole number is eventually periodic and used that periodicity to guarantee that Pell’s equation always has a nontrivial solution [34]. But the ancient side-and-diameter numbers show Greek mathematicians already running the recursive engine that generates those solutions, without yet possessing Lagrange’s general proof of why it works — a nice illustration, alongside the Babylonian and Indian material from Section 3, of how far ahead of formal justification good mathematical practice can run.

A more playful way to see all of this in one picture is offered by the University of Cambridge mathematics magazine Chalkdust, whose 2019 feature on √2 works through the same continued fraction by repeatedly substituting √2 + 1 = 2 + 1/(√2+1) into itself, generating the identical convergent sequence directly, with no appeal to Pell’s equation at all [38] — a small, satisfying demonstration that the "best wrong answers" to √2 are not an arbitrary curiosity but fall directly out of the number’s own self-similar algebraic structure.

11  —  Foundations

From Eudoxus to Dedekind

Eudoxus’s theory of proportion, discussed in Section 7, resolved the immediate crisis of comparing incommensurable magnitudes to each other. It did not, by itself, answer a subtly different question that would not become fully explicit until the nineteenth century: what, exactly, is an irrational number, considered on its own, independent of any particular pair of magnitudes being compared? Greek mathematics never needed to answer this question, because Greek mathematics did not, strictly, treat √2 as a number at all — it treated it as a ratio, or as a geometric magnitude, a length constructible by compass and straightedge, always embedded in a comparison. The notion of an irrational quantity as a free-standing number, sitting at a definite point on a number line alongside the rationals, is a much later idea.

Richard Dedekind supplied it in 1872, in a short technical monograph titled Stetigkeit und irrationale Zahlen — Continuity and Irrational Numbers [39]. Dedekind’s construction is almost aggressively simple once stated: partition the entire set of rational numbers into two nonempty classes, a lower class and an upper class, such that every member of the lower class is less than every member of the upper class. If the lower class has a greatest member, or the upper class has a least member, the cut simply names that rational number. But some cuts — and Dedekind shows this explicitly for the cut generated by all positive rationals whose square is less than 2, versus all positive rationals whose square exceeds 2 — correspond to no rational number at all. There is a gap: no rational number sits exactly at the boundary. Dedekind’s proposal, stated with a directness that reads almost anticlimactically given how long the problem had been open, is simply to define a new number at that gap, whose entire mathematical identity consists in being the cut itself [40]. √2, on this construction, is not approximated by rationals; it is the specific way the rationals split in two.

Dedekind was explicit, in his own correspondence and in later commentary on his work, that he understood himself to be extending — not replacing — the ancient Greek theory of proportion, and Howard Stein’s 1990 analysis (already introduced in Section 7) confirms the technical debt is real and precise: a Eudoxean ratio, defined by how it compares to every possible pair of whole-number multiples, is formally identical to a Dedekind cut in the positive rationals [27]. What Dedekind adds that Eudoxus’s framework does not supply on its own is the crucial existence postulate — the claim that every such cut, including ones with no rational boundary point, corresponds to a genuine number. Eudoxus was content comparing magnitudes that geometry had already handed him; Dedekind was prepared to manufacture new numbers wherever the rationals left a gap, whether or not any prior geometric construction had put a length there first. That single move — from comparing given magnitudes to constructing new numbers to fill every possible gap — is what finally makes the real number line, in the fully modern sense, a completed and gap-free continuum.

The line from Eudoxus to Dedekind is not, therefore, a story of the ancient world groping toward an answer modernity simply tidied up. It is a story of the same underlying mathematical structure being pressed into two different jobs, twenty-two centuries apart: once to rescue proportional reasoning from a crisis of comparison, and once to complete the number line itself by admitting, as full citizens, every quantity a Dedekind cut can point to. √2 sits at the center of both projects — the paradigm incommensurable magnitude for Eudoxus, and Dedekind’s own worked example, by name, of the gap his new definition was built to fill.

12  —  The Modern Landscape

Almost Everything Is Irrational

Once irrational numbers were admitted as genuine numbers rather than embarrassing gaps, mathematicians almost immediately discovered that √2 sits on the tame end of a much larger and stranger phenomenon. √2 is what is called an algebraic number: it is the root of a polynomial with whole-number coefficients — specifically, of x² – 2 = 0 — and it has a precisely defined degree, namely 2, the degree of the simplest such polynomial it satisfies. Rational numbers are algebraic of degree 1 (each is a root of some qx – p = 0); √2 is algebraic of degree exactly 2; and mathematicians naturally wondered whether every real number was algebraic of some degree or other.

The answer, established across the 1870s, is a decisive no, and it arrived from two directions almost simultaneously. Charles Hermite proved in 1873 that the number e, the base of the natural logarithm, satisfies no polynomial equation with whole-number coefficients at all — it is transcendental, transcending the reach of algebra entirely [41]. Nine years later, Ferdinand von Lindemann adapted Hermite’s method to prove the same of π, finally resolving, in the negative, the two-thousand-year-old problem of squaring the circle by compass and straightedge, since such a construction would have required π to be a root of a specific kind of algebraic equation that Lindemann had just shown could not exist [42]. In the very same decade, Georg Cantor supplied an entirely different, and in some ways more startling, argument. Cantor proved in 1874 that the set of algebraic numbers — every possible root of every possible whole-number polynomial, an infinite set that certainly contains √2, √3, and every other such radical anyone had ever considered — is nonetheless countable, the same size of infinity as the whole numbers themselves, while the full set of real numbers is strictly larger, uncountable [43]. Since the algebraic numbers cannot exhaust an uncountable set, transcendental numbers must not merely exist but vastly outnumber the algebraic ones — a conclusion Cantor reached without exhibiting a single transcendental number by name, purely from counting arguments.

That last fact deserves to be sat with for a moment, because it inverts the intuitive picture most people carry from school mathematics. Rational numbers feel like the ordinary case and irrationals like exotic intruders; √2 already felt like a strange enough exception when this essay opened with it. But Cantor’s argument shows that among the real numbers, it is the rationals that are the vanishingly rare special case — countably infinite, therefore "measure zero," occupying, in a precise mathematical sense, none of the real line’s actual length — while irrationals, and specifically transcendental irrationals, make up essentially all of it. And within the irrationals, √2 is itself comparatively well-behaved: it is algebraic, of low degree, with an extremely simple, perfectly periodic continued fraction (Section 10). The numbers Joseph Liouville constructed in 1851, specifically to give the first guaranteed examples of transcendence before Hermite’s and Lindemann’s proofs existed, are approximable by rationals to a degree no algebraic number could ever match — a property Liouville used as a direct certificate of transcendence, decades before anyone could prove e or π had the same status [44]. √2, by contrast, resists rational approximation about as stubbornly as any irrational number can — its continued fraction’s unbroken string of 2s is, in a technical sense worked out in Diophantine approximation theory, close to the worst (meaning least well-approximable) case among all irrationals, the same property that makes the golden ratio the canonically "most irrational" number of all [45].

So the modern landscape surrounding √2 turns out to be considerably stranger than the fifth-century-BCE discovery could have anticipated. The Pythagoreans found one number that broke their assumption that everything was a ratio of whole numbers. Two and a half thousand years of subsequent mathematics revealed that their exception was, numerically speaking, the overwhelming rule — and that even within the irrationals, there are gradations of strangeness √2 does not begin to plumb.

13  —  Legacy

The Word’s Afterlife

One of the more durable traces of this entire history is linguistic, and easy to walk past without noticing. The English word "irrational," in ordinary usage, means something like unreasonable, illogical, contrary to sense. Its mathematical meaning — not expressible as a ratio — is the literal, etymologically prior sense, and the two meanings genuinely do share a common root, because a number that could not be written as a ratio struck the Greek and later Latin mathematical tradition as a violation of logos, of the very rational order the word "ratio" itself names. Calling √2 "irrational" is not, technically, a joke or a coincidence — it is the ordinary word for unreasonable, borrowed directly from mathematics because a number outside the reach of ratio genuinely looked, to the tradition that named it, like a number outside the reach of reason. The emotional charge the legend of Hippasus carries — scandal, transgression, secrets worth dying over — is quietly fossilized in the very name of the thing that supposedly caused it.

The mathematics education research literature suggests this old charge has not entirely worn off, in a different and more mundane sense: irrational numbers remain, empirically, one of the more persistently difficult concepts in the undergraduate curriculum. A 2022 study of sixty first-year Greek mathematics students, conducted during pandemic-era remote instruction, found substantial and recurring confusion between an irrational number itself and its necessarily truncated decimal approximation, along with difficulty placing irrational lengths correctly on a number line using only straightedge-and-compass construction — the exact classical technique Theodorus and Theaetetus themselves relied on [46]. It is a small, pleasing historical loop: the discovery that unsettled the Pythagoreans in the fifth century BCE continues to unsettle undergraduates today, and the most effective remedy education researchers still reach for is the same compass-and-straightedge geometry the Greeks used to first make sense of it.

√2 also has a much less dramatic, entirely secular modern career: it is the exact aspect ratio of every sheet of ISO 216 paper — the A4, A3, A5 and related sizes used almost everywhere in the world outside North America. The property was first noted in 1786 by the German scientist Georg Christoph Lichtenberg, in a letter observing that a rectangle whose long side is √2 times its short side has a uniquely convenient feature: cut or folded exactly in half across its shorter dimension, each resulting half-rectangle has precisely the same √2 aspect ratio as the original, all the way down [47]. Walter Porstmann turned the idea into the German DIN 476 standard in 1922, and the International Organization for Standardization adopted essentially the same specification worldwide as ISO 216 in 1975 [48]. Every time an A4 sheet is folded in half to make A5, the fold is silently relying on the same irrational ratio whose proof of irrationality, twenty-four centuries earlier, is supposed to have gotten a man killed — a nice illustration of how thoroughly a once-scandalous mathematical fact can be absorbed into completely unremarkable daily infrastructure [49].

14  —  Closing

What the Argument Teaches

Two claims have run in parallel through this essay, and they are worth separating cleanly at the end, because it is easy to let the drama of one bleed into the certainty that should belong only to the other.

The mathematical claim is settled beyond any possible reasonable dispute, has been settled for roughly two and a half thousand years, and has since been re-derived by at least six structurally independent methods, ancient and modern, geometric and algebraic, classical and constructive: the diagonal of a unit square shares no common measure with its side, and √2 is not, and cannot be, any ratio of whole numbers. Nothing in the historical uncertainty surrounding Hippasus, and nothing in the historiographical dispute over the "foundational crisis" narrative, touches this claim in the slightest. The proof in Section 4 is exactly as valid today as the day someone first gave it.

The historical claim is a different matter entirely, and considerably less settled: whether the discovery produced a genuine social and intellectual crisis among the Pythagoreans, whether a man named Hippasus made or revealed it, and whether he was drowned for the trouble, are all questions where the honest answer, following the current state of the specialist literature, is that the evidence is thin, late, and internally inconsistent, and that a full century of professional historiography — from Tannery through Hasse and Scholz to Freudenthal and Knorr — has moved from confidently asserting the crisis narrative to substantially doubting whether it describes anything that happened at all, as opposed to a story later commentators, ancient and modern alike, needed the discovery to have caused.

What makes the pairing worth dwelling on is not that one claim is true and the other false — the historical claim is not simply false, it is underdetermined, which is a different and more interesting epistemic condition. It means that a mathematical fact of the highest possible certainty got wrapped, almost immediately and then repeatedly across twenty-five centuries, in a story whose certainty is of an entirely different and much lower order: first by whichever ancient Pythagoreans first told it about themselves, then by Iamblichus and Pappus centuries later, then by Tannery in 1887, then by Hasse and Scholz in 1928, reaching for a usable myth about their own era’s far more genuine foundational anxieties. Every one of those tellers had their own reasons for wanting the discovery of √2’s irrationality to be more than just correct — wanting it to be dangerous, forbidden, world-breaking. The mathematics never needed any of that. It was true, is true, and would remain exactly as true if every version of the Hippasus story turned out to be invented whole cloth tomorrow. That gap — between a proof that needs no story and the stories human beings kept telling about it anyway — may be the most durable lesson the whole episode has to offer, twenty-four centuries on.
