Investigations & Exposure · Vegan Activism Series

Eyes Behind
the Wall

How undercover investigators, whistleblowers, and data leaks are tearing down the secrecy of industrial animal agriculture — and why the industry is desperate to stop them.

by Nicholas Knapp

In 1904, a 26-year-old writer donned grimy clothes, carried a lunch pail, and slipped into Chicago's Packingtown — a dense labyrinth of slaughterhouses, feed lots, and meatpacking plants. For seven weeks he worked among what he later called "the wage slaves of the Beef Trust," recording everything he saw. When Upton Sinclair published his findings as The Jungle in 1906, the public response was immediate and explosive.[1] President Theodore Roosevelt initiated an independent investigation, which confirmed that conditions were as bad, if not worse, than Sinclair had described.[2] Within months, Congress passed the Pure Food and Drug Act and the Federal Meat Inspection Act — the legislative foundations still governing food safety in the United States today.[3]

Sinclair famously lamented that he had aimed for the public's heart but hit its stomach instead — he had wanted to expose the exploitation of workers, not just the contamination of their food. But the episode revealed a principle that remains the cornerstone of animal agriculture activism more than a century later: exposure works. When the public cannot see what happens behind closed doors, industry operates with impunity. When it can, everything changes.

This is the story of how that principle has been systematized, technologically upgraded, legally contested, and deployed at scale — and what it means for the future of the movement to end factory farming.

Part I

The Architecture of Concealment

Industrial animal agriculture is, by design, invisible. The vast majority of the nine billion land animals slaughtered in the United States each year never appear in public consciousness. They are born, raised, and killed inside windowless structures often located in remote rural areas, owned by corporations whose marketing departments construct elaborate pastoral fictions — rolling hills, red barns, smiling farmers — to paper over what actually happens inside.[4]

This invisibility is not accidental. It is structural, legal, and actively enforced. The industry has successfully lobbied for so-called "ag-gag" laws in multiple states, which criminalize the unauthorized recording of agricultural operations.[5] It has fought labeling requirements that would inform consumers of the conditions under which their food was produced. It has litigated against transparency at every level of government. The result is what advocates call the "veil of secrecy" over modern animal agriculture — a deliberate construction designed to separate the consumer from the consequences of their choices.

"Investigators document the shocking yet standard practices on factory farms by going undercover. Our brave investigators have a mission: exposing animal cruelty."

Animal Equality, Investigations Program

It is precisely this secrecy — and not any ancillary cruelty — that is the primary target of the investigations model. The core argument is simple: if people could see what they are paying for, most would demand change. The investigator's job is to make seeing unavoidable.

Part II

A History of Bearing Witness

The lineage of animal agriculture investigation runs from Sinclair's Packingtown to the smartphone-era livestream. But the modern era of systematic, camera-based undercover work began in earnest in the 1980s and 1990s, when groups like People for the Ethical Treatment of Animals (PETA) pioneered the practice of placing investigators inside fur farms, research laboratories, and slaughterhouses — often for weeks or months at a time.

These early investigations were costly, dangerous, and legally ambiguous. Investigators risked their physical and psychological wellbeing, often developing trauma from sustained exposure to mass suffering. The footage they gathered had to be smuggled out on physical media. Distribution was limited to VHS tape and activist networks. Media uptake was sporadic.

The Digital Turn

The internet changed everything. When Mercy For Animals was founded in 1999 by a fifteen-year-old named Milo Runkle — motivated by witnessing the killing of a piglet in a high school agricultural class — the organization almost immediately recognized that digital distribution could transform the reach of investigative footage.[6] Between 2001 and 2011, MFA conducted 17 undercover investigations, generating footage that reached millions online.[6] In 2012, an MFA investigation into a Butterball turkey facility resulted in the first felony conviction in United States history for cruelty to factory-farmed birds.[6]

Animal Equality, founded in 2006, brought a globalizing ambition to the model. Since its founding, Animal Equality investigators have filmed inside more than 900 factory farms and slaughterhouses across 13 countries — becoming, in 2008, the first organization to infiltrate slaughterhouses in Spain.[7] The organization pioneered the use of 360-degree virtual reality cameras, giving viewers an unprecedented first-person experience inside facilities that no paying customer would ever be permitted to visit.[8]

Part III

Methods: How Investigations Actually Work

The popular imagination of undercover investigation often involves a lone hero with a hidden camera. The reality is a sophisticated, multi-method discipline that has evolved dramatically with available technology.

Employment-Based Infiltration

The most consequential — and most legally contested — method involves an investigator obtaining employment at a target facility under a partially concealed identity. This is the approach that tends to generate the most comprehensive footage and the highest media coverage, because the investigator has sustained, insider access to routine practices rather than just a snapshot.[9] It is also the most psychologically grueling: investigators may work for weeks or months in conditions they find deeply distressing, unable to intervene on behalf of individual animals without blowing their cover.

When Mercy For Animals' investigator "Andy" secured a job at a Tyson Foods chicken farm, he documented systematic cruelty through cameras hidden on his body over an extended period — footage later featured in national media and used in corporate accountability campaigns.[10]

Drone and Aerial Documentation

Drone technology has opened a new dimension of investigation that bypasses facility access entirely. Aerial footage can document the scale of concentrated animal feeding operations (CAFOs), the environmental impact of waste lagoons, and the mass-death events — such as the killing of millions of birds in response to avian influenza outbreaks — that the industry would prefer to keep invisible. Mercy For Animals has deployed drones to document the catastrophic scale of bird flu culls, providing footage that offers what the organization describes as a "never-before-seen glimpse" into industry responses to disease.[11]

Underwater Investigation

Animal Equality extended the investigation model beneath the surface of the ocean, deploying underwater cameras to document the killing of bluefin tuna in Carloforte, Italy — a first-of-its-kind investigation that was later featured in the documentary Cowspiracy.[12]

Virtual Reality and Immersive Media

Perhaps the most psychologically sophisticated development in investigation methodology is the use of 360-degree cameras and virtual reality. By giving viewers a first-person perspective inside facilities, VR investigations make the experience of bearing witness far more immediate and difficult to intellectually distance oneself from. Animal Equality was the first organization to deploy VR cameras inside factory farms and slaughterhouses, producing films that force viewers to reckon with the spatial reality of industrial confinement rather than merely observe it at a remove.[8]

  • Employment-based infiltration with body-worn hidden cameras
  • Drone and aerial surveillance of facility exteriors and environmental impact
  • Underwater documentation of aquatic animal slaughter
  • 360-degree virtual reality footage for immersive public engagement
  • Livestreaming of hidden camera footage directly to social media
  • Whistleblower support and documentation of worker testimony
  • Data leaks and freedom-of-information requests targeting regulatory records
Part IV

Case Studies: When Exposure Creates Change

Case Study 01  ·  Legal Precedent

The Butterball Turkey Investigation (2012)

When Mercy For Animals investigators documented workers at a Butterball turkey facility in North Carolina kicking, stomping, and throwing live turkeys, the footage triggered what became a landmark criminal case. The resulting prosecution produced the first felony conviction for cruelty to factory-farmed birds in United States history — establishing that standard industry practices, if documented and prosecuted, could meet the legal threshold for animal cruelty.[6]

Case Study 02  ·  Corporate Accountability

Sparboe Egg Farms and McDonald's (2011)

After Mercy For Animals published undercover footage documenting conditions at Sparboe Egg Farm facilities in Iowa, Minnesota, and Colorado, McDonald's Corporation severed its supply relationship with Sparboe — one of the largest egg suppliers in the United States at the time. The case demonstrated that investigative footage, by threatening brand reputation, could produce supply chain consequences far exceeding what regulatory pressure alone could achieve.[15]

Case Study 03  ·  Legislative Change

Italy Bans Male Chick Culling (2022)

Following sustained investigation and advocacy by Animal Equality in Italy — including undercover footage from egg industry facilities — Italy passed a law banning the killing of day-old male chicks in the egg industry. The legislation was required to be implemented by 2026 and was projected to affect the lives of 25 to 40 million animals annually.[13]

Case Study 04  ·  International Policy

India: Foie Gras Import Ban and Welfare Rules

Animal Equality investigations conducted in India contributed to a suite of policy changes by the Government of India, including a ban on the import of foie gras and the introduction of new welfare rules for cattle, chickens, and hens.[12] The case illustrates how investigation-based advocacy can translate across different legal and cultural contexts to produce concrete regulatory outcomes.

Case Study 05  ·  Legal Action via Footage

Holden Farms and the False Claims Act Suit

The Animal Legal Defense Fund used footage from an undercover investigation showing pigs being fed a mixture of dead piglet intestines and feces to bring a federal lawsuit against Holden Farms, a pig-breeding facility in Minnesota, under the False Claims Act — alleging violations of the Swine Health Protection Act, the Minnesota Anti-Garbage Feeding Law, and the Minnesota Anti-Cruelty Law.[3] The case illustrates how investigative footage can function as the evidentiary foundation for complex, multi-statute litigation.

Case Study 06  ·  Facility Closure

Farm Closures in Spain and the UK

Following Animal Equality investigations, authorities ordered the closure of a foie gras farm in Spain and a pig farm in the United Kingdom — among the most direct facility-level outcomes available in the investigations model.[7] These closures demonstrate that footage, when combined with regulatory pressure, can produce immediate relief for animals in specific facilities rather than merely contributing to longer-term cultural change.

Part V

The Ag-Gag Counteroffensive

The most reliable indicator of an investigation's effectiveness is often the vigor with which it is legally suppressed. Since 1990, state legislatures have enacted laws specifically intended to punish whistleblowers for disclosing information about agricultural facilities to the news media — statutes that critics, journalists, and civil liberties organizations almost universally refer to as "ag-gag" laws.[16]

The first wave of modern ag-gag legislation followed a surge of high-profile investigations in the late 2000s and early 2010s. The pattern was transparent: an investigation would expose documented cruelty, generate national media coverage, and produce corporate or regulatory consequences; a legislature with close ties to the agricultural industry would then pass a law making the investigative methods that produced the footage illegal. Iowa's law, for instance, was introduced approximately three years after a widely publicized Humane Society investigation into the Hallmark-Westland meatpacking facility — an investigation that eventually produced a $497 million settlement.[20]

"The factory farm industry wants an ag-gag law on the books to intimidate and silence whistleblowers."

Adam Moser, Iowa Citizens for Community Improvement — via First Amendment Watch

Constitutional Defeats

Ag-gag laws have fared poorly in federal courts, where multiple circuits have found them unconstitutional as content-based restrictions on First Amendment-protected speech. In 2015, a federal district court struck down Idaho's ag-gag law, with Judge B. Lynn Winmill writing that while the state may disagree with the message activist groups seek to convey, it cannot deny them equal protection in exercising their right to free speech.[11] In 2023, the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Fourth Circuit ruled that undercover investigations and whistleblowing in agricultural facilities constitute newsgathering activities protected by the First Amendment — a decision the Supreme Court declined to review on October 16, 2023.[13]

Despite these victories, six states — Alabama, Arkansas, Iowa, Missouri, Montana, and North Dakota — still had ag-gag laws in effect as of late 2023, with South Dakota adding a new law in 2025.[19] Each defeat in court has tended to prompt more carefully drafted successor legislation designed to evade the specific constitutional objections that doomed its predecessor — a legislative arms race between industry suppression and investigative access.

The Broader Chill

Even where ag-gag laws have been struck down, they create a chilling effect. Investigators who fear prosecution, even under unconstitutional statutes, may self-censor. Organizations that cannot afford extended constitutional litigation may abandon planned investigations. The laws need not be enforced to accomplish their primary purpose — suppression — because the threat of prosecution is often sufficient.

It is worth noting that opposition to ag-gag laws extends far beyond the animal advocacy community. A coalition of 59 groups has publicly opposed such legislation, including the American Civil Liberties Union, the ASPCA, Amnesty International USA, United Farm Workers, the National Consumers League, and dozens of media organizations.[11] Food safety researchers, labor advocates, and environmental groups all recognize that the investigations model serves interests that transcend the animal rights frame in which it is often placed.

Part VI

What the Evidence Shows: Does Exposure Actually Work?

Advocates have long argued intuitively that if people could see inside factory farms, they would change their behavior. The empirical evidence is more nuanced — and more interesting — than either pure optimism or pure skepticism would suggest.

Attitudinal Change

A randomized controlled trial conducted jointly by Animal Equality and research organization Faunalytics — using a larger and more representative sample than previous studies — found that people who watched footage of pigs being confined and slaughtered in intensive farms expressed significantly stronger anti-pork attitudes both immediately after watching and one month later, compared to a control group. The group that viewed the footage also reported eating marginally less pork one month after the intervention.[4]

Legal and Policy Impact

The legal and policy impacts of investigations are more clearly documented than the behavioral impacts. Faunalytics researchers note that because of their positive legal and policy effects, support for undercover investigations remains crucial for animal advocates regardless of what role investigations may play in directly altering consumer behavior.[3] The record includes criminal convictions, facility closures, corporate supply-chain decisions affecting millions of animals, and legislative changes on multiple continents.

Risks and Limitations

Investigators whom researchers consulted for Animal Charity Evaluators highlighted that investigative footage is useful for validating activist claims about poor animal treatment, supporting legal actions and public outreach, and refuting industry denials — but acknowledged genuine risks of diminishing returns as audiences become habituated to distressing footage.[4] Mercy For Animals capped the number of U.S. investigations it released in 2015 for precisely this reason, redirecting resources to investigations in countries where public exposure to the subject is less saturated.[4]

A 2016 report from Animal Charity Evaluators, based on conversations with movement leaders, also noted the relatively high financial cost of employment-based investigations in the United States in particular — a constraint that shapes strategic decisions about which investigations are prioritized and which are foregone.[4]

Part VII

Strategic Framework: How Exposure Feeds the Broader Movement

Investigations are most powerful not as isolated events but as inputs into a broader strategic ecosystem. A single investigation can simultaneously generate media coverage, provide evidentiary material for litigation, create public pressure for corporate policy change, inform regulatory advocacy, and shift cultural narratives about animal agriculture. Understanding these interlocking functions helps explain why well-resourced organizations invest so heavily in what is, by any measure, an expensive and dangerous practice.

Media Amplification

High-quality investigative footage, particularly from facilities connected to recognizable brand names, reliably generates national and international media coverage. The Mercy For Animals investigation that exposed conditions at a pig farm in Nebraska — featuring footage of pigs exhibiting severe psychological distress — was featured in the New York Times.[6] Coverage at that scale reaches audiences far beyond the activist community and reframes public conversations about what "humane" practices actually look like in industrial settings.

Corporate Leverage

Investigations have been cited as an important factor in the success of historic corporate engagement campaigns, because they prime both companies and the general public to be receptive to demands for policy change.[4] A corporation that has already been embarrassed by investigative footage of a supplier facility is far more amenable to signing a cage-free commitment or a gestation-crate phase-out pledge than one that has never faced public scrutiny.

Legal Foundations

As the Holden Farms and Case Farms examples demonstrate, investigative footage can serve as the evidentiary core of complex litigation — enabling advocates to pursue legal remedies that would be impossible without documented evidence of specific, verifiable violations. The Animal Legal Defense Fund has made the strategic use of investigation footage a central pillar of its litigation program.

Cultural Narrative Shift

Perhaps most importantly for the long-term arc of the movement, investigations force a confrontation with the gap between what consumers believe about food production and what actually occurs. The 2024 Animal Equality investigation of a pig breeding facility in Kansas linked to Ahold Delhaize — which revealed pregnant pigs in crates barely wider than their bodies, dead and dying piglets, pigs with open wounds, and signs of severe psychological distress — challenges the mythology of responsible production that grocery chains deploy in their marketing.[7] Every investigation that reaches mainstream media makes that mythology marginally harder to sustain.

Part VIII

The Road Ahead

The investigations model faces genuine headwinds. Ag-gag laws continue to multiply and evolve, with each legislative defeat producing more sophisticated successor legislation. The psychological toll on investigators is real and largely unaddressed by the movement's infrastructure. And there are legitimate empirical questions about the long-term effectiveness of footage-based advocacy as audiences become increasingly habituated to disturbing content.

At the same time, the technological trajectory is clearly in favor of exposure. Drones, 360-degree cameras, improved miniaturized recording equipment, social media distribution, and AI-assisted analysis of large volumes of footage are all making investigations simultaneously cheaper, more far-reaching, and harder to suppress. The ag-gag strategy of criminalization is being fought by a coalition that now includes civil liberties organizations, food safety advocates, labor groups, and mainstream media companies — not just animal rights activists.

The deeper truth that Upton Sinclair intuited in 1904 — that the public, if it could actually see what it was participating in, would demand something different — remains as relevant as ever. What has changed is the technology available to make that seeing unavoidable, the legal infrastructure available to protect those who do the seeing, and the growing body of evidence that exposure, when strategically deployed, produces real and measurable change in law, corporate policy, and culture.

"Exposing the cruelty of factory farming is only possible with your help. Our brave investigators have a mission: exposing animal cruelty. They go to great lengths to ensure that defenseless animals are given a voice."

Animal Equality

The wall of secrecy that industrial animal agriculture has constructed around its operations is not impenetrable. It has been breached, again and again, by people willing to risk their safety and wellbeing to bear witness on behalf of those who cannot speak for themselves. The footage they bring back has shuttered farms, convicted abusers, changed laws, moved corporations, and shifted the terms of public debate. It is, by any reasonable measure, one of the most effective tactics available to the movement — and it is worth defending, funding, and continuing.
