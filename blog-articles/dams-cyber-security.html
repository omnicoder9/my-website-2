A dam is the most legible piece of critical infrastructure a society builds. You can see it from a highway; you can feel the cold air rolling off the spillway; you understand, without being told, that the wall is the only thing between the reservoir and the valley. What you cannot see from the highway is the part that decides, minute by minute, how much of that reservoir to let go and when. That decision now lives in a programmable logic controller, a supervisory application, a radio link, and — far too often — a device with a routable address and a default password.

This is the central tension of cyber and information security in the U.S. Dams Sector. The physical engineering of American dams is among the most conservative and best-understood in the world, governed by a century of failure forensics, federal guidelines, and licensed professional practice. The digital engineering that operates those same dams is, by comparison, a frontier: unevenly regulated, frequently legacy, and almost never built with an adversary in mind. The result is a sector where the consequence ceiling is catastrophic — measured in lives and in the simultaneous degradation of half a dozen other critical infrastructure sectors — while the cyber floor is, in many facilities, alarmingly low.

The Dams Sector is one of the sixteen critical infrastructure sectors designated under U.S. national policy, and the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA) is its designated Sector Risk Management Agency, a role reaffirmed in the 2024 National Security Memorandum on Critical Infrastructure Security and Resilience.12 Yet the sector is unlike any other in the catalogue. It is defined not by an industry or an output but by a physical function — the retention and controlled release of water — and that function is delivered by an extraordinarily heterogeneous fleet of structures owned by tens of thousands of different entities, governed by a patchwork of overlapping and incomplete authorities, and connected, increasingly, to the internet.

This piece is a practitioner-level tour of that problem. It maps the industry-specific technology that runs a dam and the failure modes specific to it; it traces the real intrusions that have already happened and the consequences a successful attack could produce; it follows the cascade outward into the other fifteen sectors that depend on dams for power, water, navigation, and protection; and it lays out the standards, architectures, tools, and unresolved research questions that define mitigation today. Throughout, the argument is the same one the schematic above makes: the water is physical, but the decisions that hold it back are digital, and securing those decisions is now a dam-safety problem.

01 Scope & Scale

The sector, defined

The Dams Sector "delivers critical water retention and control services in the United States," and the phrasing matters: the sector's product is control over water, not water itself. According to CISA, those services include hydroelectric power generation, municipal and industrial water supply, agricultural irrigation, sediment and flood control, river navigation for inland bulk shipping, industrial waste management, and recreation.1 Each of those services is, in effect, an interface to another critical infrastructure sector — a point we return to at length.

The scale is national and the inventory is enormous. CISA counts more than 92,000 dams in the United States, more than 24,000 miles of levees, and over 25,000 miles of waterways and channels made navigable by locks.1 The American Society of Civil Engineers, drawing on the National Inventory of Dams, puts the figure similarly above 92,000 structures and notes that nearly 17,000 of them — roughly 18 percent — are classified as high-hazard potential, meaning that their failure would likely cause loss of human life.3

Hazard potential ≠ condition

A high-hazard-potential rating is a statement about consequences, not probability. It means people would die if the dam failed; it says nothing about how likely failure is. This distinction is central to risk-ranking a fleet — and it is exactly the variable a cyber adversary manipulates, because the easiest way to turn a low-probability event into a real one is to seize the controls.

That fleet is old, and age is a security variable in its own right. ASCE's 2025 Report Card assigns the nation's dams a grade of D+, observing that the average dam is now more than 60 years old — about 64 on average — and that by 2025 roughly seven in ten will have passed the half-century mark.3 As of the latest assessment, some 2,522 high-hazard-potential dams — about 15 percent — were judged to be in poor or unsatisfactory condition.4 A structure built to mid-century standards was not designed for current hydrology; a control system retrofitted onto that structure decades later was not designed for the modern internet.

An ownership and regulatory patchwork

No single entity owns or governs American dams, and this fragmentation is the defining feature of the sector's risk posture. The Federal Emergency Management Agency reports that roughly 64 percent of U.S. dams are privately owned, with the remainder split among local governments, states, federal agencies, and utilities.5 The Association of State Dam Safety Officials offers a finer breakdown — about 65 percent private, 20 percent owned by local government, 7 percent by states, 4 percent federal, and 4 percent by utilities — and notes that every state but Alabama operates a dam-safety program.6

Regulatory authority is just as splintered. State agencies hold primary regulatory responsibility for roughly 70 percent of the nation's dams; federal agencies regulate only about 14 percent; and a residual fraction is effectively unregulated under state or federal exemptions.6 Among federally owned dams — the large ones, generally — the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers (USACE) and the Bureau of Reclamation together own 42 percent, with the balance spread across the Forest Service, the Bureau of Land Management, the Fish and Wildlife Service, the Department of Defense, the Bureau of Indian Affairs, the Tennessee Valley Authority, the Department of Energy, and the International Boundary and Water Commission.7 The Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC) licenses and oversees the nation's non-federal hydropower projects on top of that. FEMA's National Dam Safety Program, anchored in the National Dam Safety Program Act, knits these actors together through the Interagency Committee on Dam Safety and a grant program for rehabilitating high-hazard-potential dams — but it coordinates; it does not command.85

For cybersecurity, the implication is stark and worth stating plainly before we go further: the overwhelming majority of American dams operate under no mandatory cybersecurity regime at all. A FERC-licensed hydroelectric project connected to the bulk electric system inherits mandatory reliability standards for its generation assets; a Corps or Reclamation dam falls under its agency's own security program; but the tens of thousands of privately owned, state-regulated dams answer to state dam-safety offices whose statutes were written around structural integrity, spillway capacity, and emergency action planning — not around network segmentation or default credentials. The same fragmentation that makes the sector resilient to any single physical failure mode leaves it without a coherent digital defense.

02 The Machinery

How a dam is actually run

To reason about attacks, you have to know what is actually being controlled. Within the sector, the term control system is used more or less interchangeably with industrial control system (ICS): the facilities, systems, equipment, services, and diagnostics that provide the monitoring, control, and protection capabilities a dam needs to operate reliably.9 At the simplest dams, that "system" may be a set of non-electronic, relay-based components with no cyber assets attached, and therefore a low cyber risk.9 At a modern multipurpose project, it is a layered architecture of sensors, controllers, networks, and supervisory software that would be familiar to any process-automation engineer.

The control loop behind a spillway gate

Consider the single most safety-critical actuator on a typical dam: a spillway gate. Its job is to pass excess water before the reservoir overtops the structure, and the timing and magnitude of that release is, quite literally, a life-safety decision for everyone downstream. A documented control architecture for a spillway gate is a closed loop with five elements — water-level (and often temperature) sensors feeding a programmable logic controller (PLC); the PLC executing control logic; an operator interface (HMI/SCADA) for supervisory monitoring and manual commands; an actuator that physically drives the gate; and the communication links that tie them together.10 The same pattern repeats across the facility for the assets a dam must watch and move: gate position, reservoir level, hydroelectric generator output, and water flow rate are sensed, converted to electrical signals, processed, and acted upon either automatically or with a human in the loop.9

That closed loop is precisely the thing an attacker wants. Compromise the HMI and you can issue commands as though you were the operator; compromise the PLC and you can change what "open" and "closed" mean; spoof the sensors and you can blind the operator to the true reservoir level while the logic responds to a fiction. A widely cited cyber-physical risk taxonomy for dams enumerates exactly these classes — remote control-system attacks, sensor spoofing, and command interception or injection — and ties each one to the same consequence set: dam failure, potential loss of life, and environmental damage.11

The field devices that matter

PLC / RTU — the programmable logic controllers and remote terminal units that execute control logic at gates, pumps, and powerhouses. HMI / SCADA — the human-machine interface and supervisory software the operator watches and commands from. Sensors — reservoir-level transducers, gate-position encoders, flow meters, and the dam-safety instrumentation (piezometers, weirs) that report on the structure itself. Actuators — the motors and hydraulics that move gates and govern turbines. Conduits — radio, cellular, fiber, and serial links carrying it all.

From hand-cranked to remote and automated

The sector's cyber exposure is not a fixed property; it has grown as facilities have modernized. Industry analysis is blunt about the mechanism: as the number of connected technologies such as PLCs and SCADA increases in a facility's control systems, so does the cyber-attack exposure of those systems, and that exposure has risen specifically because previously manual components have become more complex and supplemented with remote capabilities.12 Automation delivers real benefits — efficiency, real-time data, the ability to operate gates in time during a flash flood from a remote location — but each remote capability is also a new path inward.13 The telemetry that links a remote gatehouse to a control center has historically ridden on whatever was available, from licensed radio and general packet radio service to commercial cellular, and each of those bearers carries its own interception and spoofing surface.14

Two structural features of dam control systems make this worse than in a greenfield IT environment. First, equipment lifecycles in industrial settings run fifteen to twenty-five years, which means an operational dam very likely contains controllers and software that cannot be patched, cannot be authenticated, and cannot be monitored with modern tooling.15 Second, the sector's own modernization guidance recognizes that legacy systems are constrained by the inherent limitations of existing equipment and configurations, and recommends that as they age they be replaced or upgraded with next-generation components that offer built-in, end-to-end security — and, in the interim, that defenses such as unidirectional data gateways (data diodes) be deployed to let information out for monitoring without letting commands in.9

The reference architecture: Purdue, zones, and conduits

The dominant mental model for organizing and defending this machinery is the Purdue Enterprise Reference Architecture, which arranges an OT environment into hierarchical levels: field devices such as sensors, actuators, PLCs, and RTUs at Levels 0–1; control systems such as HMIs, engineering workstations, and SCADA servers at Level 2; operations systems such as historians at Level 3; an industrial demilitarized zone at Level 3.5; and the enterprise IT and internet-facing systems at Levels 4–5.16 The model's value is that it defines trust boundaries — zones of assets with shared security requirements, joined by conduits that are the only sanctioned communication paths between them — so that a compromise at the enterprise level cannot cascade directly into the control network.16 The ISA-95 standard codified the hierarchy, and the ISA/IEC 62443 family built its zones-and-conduits security architecture directly on it.15 In practice — and this is the recurring failure — many organizations have eroded those boundaries, flattening IT and OT until an internet-exposed device sits one hop from a gate.16

Underneath the architecture run the industrial protocols themselves — Modbus, DNP3, EtherNet/IP, PROFINET, IEC 61850 — most of which were designed for reliability and determinism on a trusted wire, not for authentication or confidentiality on a hostile network.17 DNP3 and IEC 61850 in particular are the lingua franca of electric-utility and substation automation, which is exactly where a hydroelectric dam's generation controls live. The protocols will faithfully execute a well-formed command regardless of who sent it, which is precisely why the integrity of the path — not just the perimeter — is the thing that has to be defended.

03 Adversaries & Vectors

The threat landscape

Dams attract an unusually wide range of attackers, and the range itself is instructive. At one end sit nation-states with the patience and tradecraft to pre-position inside control networks for years and the strategic intent to cause physical harm at a moment of their choosing. At the other end sit ideologically motivated hacktivists who scan the internet for any exposed industrial device and seize whatever they find. What unites them — and what makes the small Bowman Avenue Dam as relevant as the largest hydroelectric project — is that the value of a dam to an attacker is not proportional to its size. It is proportional to its connectivity and to the consequences its controls can produce.

Nation-states with catastrophic intent

The defining strategic threat of the current decade is the Chinese state-sponsored actor tracked as Volt Typhoon. First publicly attributed by Microsoft in May 2023 and the subject of a Five Eyes advisory in March 2024, the group has been assessed to operate under numerous aliases and to have been active since at least mid-2021.18 A joint advisory from CISA, the NSA, and the FBI assessed that these actors are seeking to pre-position themselves on information-technology networks for disruptive or destructive cyberattacks against U.S. critical infrastructure in the event of a major crisis or conflict — and that they had, in some cases, maintained persistent access for as long as five years.19 The targeted sectors named repeatedly by U.S. officials are precisely the ones a dam touches: communications, energy, transportation, and water.20

Two features of Volt Typhoon's tradecraft matter for dam defenders specifically. The first is its reliance on living-off-the-land techniques — using built-in administrative tools and valid credentials rather than malware — which renders signature-based detection largely useless and forces a shift toward behavioral monitoring.19 The second is its use of compromised small-office and home-office routers as obfuscation infrastructure; the U.S. government's December 2023 court-authorized disruption of the associated KV Botnet underscored how the access path to a critical-infrastructure operator may run through unglamorous, end-of-life consumer hardware.20 The strategic message from the FBI's leadership has been consistent: the intent of this pre-positioning is to be able to inflict real-world harm on civilian infrastructure to induce panic at a time of geopolitical crisis.21

Nation-states with coercive or demonstrative intent

A second category uses access to dams not for wartime destruction but for signaling, coercion, and proof of reach. The canonical example is Iran. As the next section details, an Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps–affiliated actor obtained access to the supervisory control system of a New York flood-control dam in 2013, and a decade later an IRGC-linked hacktivist persona seized programmable controllers at U.S. water utilities. Neither operation produced physical damage, but both demonstrated capability and intent against the same fleet of control systems that runs the Dams Sector, and both were, in effect, public messages.

Hacktivists and opportunists

The hacktivist threat is lower in sophistication and higher in reach, and it punctures the comforting assumption that obscurity is protection. As one water-sector information-sharing advisory put it after a 2023 intrusion, operators are often targeted not for who or where they are, but for what they have and how accessible it is — regardless of the size of the organization.22 Internet-exposed programmable logic controllers are trivial to discover and their default passwords are widely known, which makes the "this won't happen to us" posture untenable for even the smallest dam.22

Criminals, ransomware, and insiders

Financially motivated ransomware crews rarely target OT directly, but they do not have to: an enterprise-side ransomware event can force a precautionary shutdown of connected operations, and the loss of engineering workstations, historians, or remote-access infrastructure can sever an operator's ability to monitor and control the physical plant. Insiders — disgruntled employees, careless contractors, or compromised system integrators — remain among the most dangerous vectors precisely because they begin on the trusted side of every boundary. And even an air-gapped control system is not immune: removable media and supply-chain implants have repeatedly bridged isolation, while targeted spear-phishing has been used to pivot from corporate IT into industrial environments, as in the well-documented German steel-mill intrusion that prevented a blast furnace from shutting down safely.23

The vectors, condensed

Across these actors, the technical paths into a dam's controls recur with depressing regularity: internet-exposed HMIs and PLCs protected only by default or weak credentials; flat networks where a single compromised enterprise host reaches the control LAN; remote-access services and VPNs without phishing-resistant multi-factor authentication; trusted but under-secured supply-chain and vendor connections; sensor spoofing and command injection that exploit unauthenticated industrial protocols; and removable media that defeat physical isolation. The cyber-physical risk taxonomy for dams collapses many of these into three integrity failures — control-system compromise, sensor spoofing, and the interception or injection of commands — each of which maps to the same downstream outcome: an uncontrolled change in how much water the structure is holding or releasing.11

04 What Has Already Happened

A short history of dam intrusions

Two intrusions anchor the empirical record for the Dams Sector and the water systems that share its technology. Neither caused a flood. Both should be read as demonstrations.

Incident File — Bowman Avenue Dam Rye Brook, NY · Aug–Sep 2013 · attributed: Iran / IRGC

The Bowman Avenue Dam is a small flood-control structure on Blind Brook, built in 1940, whose remotely controllable sluice gate regulates water flow as a function of creek levels and temperatures.27 Between August 28 and September 18, 2013, an attacker obtained unauthorized remote access to the dam's supervisory control and data acquisition (SCADA) system, gathering information on water levels, temperature, and the status of the sluice gate.28 Reporting indicates the access path ran through a cellular modem, and that the device was, at the time, effectively a standalone system logging water levels.26

The detail that turned a footnote into a landmark is what the attacker could not do: the sluice gate had been manually disconnected for maintenance, so direct control was impossible.28 Had it not been, an actor with command of that gate during a high-water event could have manipulated the release. The U.S. Department of Justice's 2016 indictment charged seven Iranian nationals employed by two IRGC-linked firms; one defendant, who had accessed the dam, was charged alongside a coordinated campaign of distributed-denial-of-service attacks against the U.S. financial sector spanning 176 days and 46 institutions.24 The FBI noted that although the actor never gained control of the dam, the access yielded critical information about the gates that control water levels and flow rates.25

Why it matters here: the same indictment binds a dam intrusion and a financial-sector campaign to a single set of actors — the first documented proof that a dam and a bank can be two fronts of one operation.

Incident File — Municipal Water Authority of Aliquippa Western PA · Nov 25, 2023 · attributed: Iran / "Cyber Av3ngers"

On November 25, 2023, an IRGC-affiliated hacktivist persona calling itself Cyber Av3ngers compromised a programmable logic controller at a booster station operated by the Municipal Water Authority of Aliquippa, near Pittsburgh, defacing the device's interface with an anti-Israel message that declared Israeli-made equipment a legitimate target.29 The compromised device was a Unitronics Vision-series PLC with an integrated human-machine interface, publicly exposed to the internet with a default password and reachable on its well-known programming port.30 The authority lost control of the booster station that regulates pressure for two townships, but recovered through manual operation with no impact on water quality or supply.29

The incident prompted a joint advisory from CISA, the FBI, the NSA, and the EPA, and a sobering census of the attack surface: independent scanning identified roughly 149 internet-exposed Unitronics devices in the United States, most of them remote field units connected over cellular links.3031

Why it matters here: Aliquippa is a water-utility story, but the technology is identical to the technology that operates dam gates, pumps, and powerhouses. PLCs with default credentials on the public internet are not a water-sector problem; they are an OT problem, and the Dams Sector is full of the same devices.

Behind these two hands-on-keyboard events sits the larger backdrop of Volt Typhoon's pre-positioning, which is best understood not as an incident but as a posture: persistent, quiet access maintained across the energy, water, communications, and transportation sectors, awaiting a trigger. Senior U.S. officials have publicly tied this campaign to the targeting of pipeline operators and water-treatment and electrical systems, which is to say the operational fabric in which dams are embedded.21 The lesson of the historical record is therefore not that a catastrophic dam cyberattack has happened — it has not — but that every prerequisite capability has been demonstrated, and the access required has, in the largest campaign, already been obtained.

05 The Consequence Ceiling

What failure looks like

The reason dam cybersecurity is categorically different from, say, retail or healthcare data security is the consequence ceiling. A successful manipulation of a dam's controls does not leak records; it moves water. To reason about that, it helps to separate the ways controls can be abused from the physical outcomes those abuses produce.

A taxonomy of control-system abuse

There are, broadly, six modes of abuse. Uncontrolled release — opening spillway gates or low-level outlets, or refusing to open them when hydrology demands, to drive an overtopping or a destructive downstream surge. Withholding — closing gates or stopping releases to deny water to downstream users, halt navigation, or starve a hydroelectric system of its resource. Reservoir-level manipulation with sensor blinding — spoofing level and inflow telemetry so operators act on a false picture while the structure approaches a real limit. Equipment destruction — driving turbine-generators or gate machinery into damaging states through malicious control, an OT analogue of the well-known demonstration that control commands alone can wreck rotating electrical plant. Loss of view and loss of control — denying operators the ability to see or command the plant during the exact window when intervention is needed. And data-integrity attacks on dam-safety instrumentation — falsifying the piezometer, seepage, and deflection data that engineers rely on to detect a structure beginning to fail. Each of these is a path to the same place: the controlled release of water becomes uncontrolled.

An attacker does not need to breach the dam. They need only to take command of the decision about how much water to let go, and choose the worst possible moment to make it.

— The asymmetry at the heart of dam cyber-risk

Calibrating the physical outcome

Because no cyberattack has yet produced a dam-failure flood, the honest way to calibrate the ceiling is to look at what comparable physical and operational failures have done — events driven by hydrology, erosion, or design error rather than by an adversary, but identical in their downstream physics to what malicious control could engineer.

The 2017 Oroville crisis is the most instructive, because it was fundamentally a crisis of release management at the tallest dam in the United States. When the main spillway chute disintegrated during high flows and managers were forced to send water over a never-before-used emergency spillway, rapid erosion threatened to undermine the weir and release an uncontrolled wall of water toward the Feather River.3234 Authorities ordered the evacuation of roughly 188,000 people and increased the damaged main-spillway discharge to about 100,000 cubic feet per second to draw the reservoir down; the dam itself never failed, yet the repair and recovery bill reached about $1.1 billion.33 The decisive point for security: the emergency was created and then contained by decisions about when and how much to release — exactly the decisions a cyber adversary seeks to control.

Cascading failure is not theoretical either. In May 2020 the Edenville Dam in Michigan breached during heavy rainfall, sending a surge downstream that overtopped and destroyed the Sanford Dam below it and forced thousands to evacuate — a single failure propagating into a second.35 Edenville is doubly instructive because federal regulators had revoked its hydropower license years earlier over unresolved safety deficiencies, a reminder that governance gaps and physical risk are connected.35

For the upper bound, history is unambiguous. The 1889 failure of the South Fork Dam above Johnstown, Pennsylvania, killed 2,208 people — more than one in five residents of the town — and remains the deadliest dam disaster in U.S. history; it is why May 31 is observed as National Dam Safety Awareness Day.3637 The 1928 collapse of the St. Francis Dam near Los Angeles sent a wall of water down San Francisquito Canyon, killed on the order of 425 people, destroyed some 900 homes, and swept away tens of thousands of acres — the second-deadliest U.S. dam failure and a foundational event in American dam-safety law.3839 These were failures of engineering and oversight, not of cybersecurity. But they define the magnitude of harm that sits at the end of a compromised spillway-gate command — and they explain why a control-system intrusion at a high-hazard dam is properly treated as a potential mass-casualty event rather than an IT incident.

06 The Cascade

The cross-sector cascade

The single most under-appreciated fact about the Dams Sector is that it is not really a standalone sector at all. It is a keystone — a provider of inputs and protections that the other fifteen critical infrastructure sectors silently depend on. CISA states the relationship plainly: the sector's services support multiple critical infrastructure sectors and industries, and the sector maintains dependencies and interdependencies with a wide range of others.1 That is why a control-system compromise at a dam is not a contained event. Depending on the structure, it can propagate into the power grid, the water supply, the food system, the inland freight network, and the financial markets that price all of them.

The propagation runs along two distinct paths. The first is functional dependency: another sector relies on a service the dam provides — hydroelectricity, cooling water, navigation, flood protection — and loses or degrades that service when the dam is manipulated. The second is shared technology and shared adversary: the same internet-exposed PLCs, the same unauthenticated protocols, and in documented cases the same threat actor span multiple sectors at once, so a campaign against dams is rarely only against dams. The table below traces the first path across the CISA sector taxonomy; the navigation discussion that follows develops the highest-leverage case in detail.

Energy
Hydropower is the most direct coupling: a dam's generation assets feed the bulk electric system, and manipulation of turbine governors or a forced generation trip removes supply and can stress grid stability. The reverse coupling is just as real — thermoelectric and nuclear plants draw cooling water from dam-regulated rivers and reservoirs, so a withholding or surge attack degrades generation the dam does not even house.
Water & Wastewater
Dams impound the raw-water supply for entire municipal and industrial systems. An attacker who drains a reservoir, contaminates intake conditions, or denies releases can threaten drinking-water availability downstream; the shared Unitronics-class PLCs mean the two sectors also share an attack surface, as Aliquippa demonstrated.
Transportation Systems
Inland navigation depends on locks and dams to maintain navigable pools. Disabling or mis-operating a lock halts barge traffic on a corridor that moves coal, grain, petroleum, and chemicals — a single point whose failure ripples into every sector that ships by water (developed below).
Food & Agriculture
Irrigation withdrawals and the barge export of grain both run through dam-controlled water. Withholding irrigation releases harms production; closing a navigation corridor strands the harvest, since the Mississippi system alone carries the majority of U.S. grain exports.
Chemical
Many chemical feedstocks and finished products move by barge, and process water is drawn from regulated rivers. Navigation disruption and water-supply manipulation both reach the sector; conversely, dams that impound industrial-waste or tailings water carry their own contamination-release hazard.
Financial Services
The coupling here is partly economic — a prolonged navigation or power outage is a macroeconomic shock — and partly adversarial: the 2016 Iranian indictment tied the same actors to both a dam intrusion and a 176-day denial-of-service campaign against dozens of U.S. financial institutions, the clearest proof that one operation can span both sectors.24
Communications
A bidirectional dependency. Dam telemetry and remote operation ride on commercial communications networks, so a comms outage blinds operators; and the same networks are a primary Volt Typhoon target, meaning the path used to reach a dam and the path used to operate it can be compromised together.
Nuclear Reactors, Materials & Waste
Nuclear plants depend on assured cooling-water supply and on protection from upstream flooding; both are functions of upstream dam operation. A surge engineered upstream, or a withholding that starves cooling intake, is a safety concern for a sector with the lowest tolerance for either.
Critical Manufacturing
Heavy manufacturing depends on reliable power (much of it hydro-influenced), barge-delivered raw materials, and process water — the same three dam-mediated inputs that reach the chemical and energy sectors.
Emergency Services
A dam-failure flood is itself the mass-casualty event emergency services must answer, and emergency action plans for high-hazard dams are predicated on warning time that a cyberattack — by spoofing the instrumentation that would raise the alarm — is specifically able to erase.
Healthcare & Public Health
Hospitals and care facilities in a downstream floodplain are directly exposed to an engineered release, and depend on the power and water that dams help provide to keep operating during any wider disruption.
Government Facilities
Federal dams are themselves government facilities, and many federal, state, and local sites sit within dam-protected floodplains, making the sector both an owner of the risk and a downstream consumer of it.
Information Technology
The IT sector supplies the software, remote-access tooling, and managed services that increasingly run dam operations; a supply-chain compromise in that layer is a path into the OT beneath it.
Commercial Facilities
Reservoirs anchor major recreation economies, and downstream commercial districts are exposed to release events — a smaller but real coupling of the sector to everyday economic life.
Defense Industrial Base
Certain dams supply power and water to defense installations and the industrial base that supports them; some are designated as defense-critical infrastructure, raising the national-security stakes of a successful intrusion.
Dams
The sector depends on itself in series: cascade systems of dams on a single river mean that the manipulation or failure of one structure loads the next, as the 2020 Edenville–Sanford sequence showed in purely physical terms.

The navigation case, in numbers

Inland navigation is where the cross-sector argument stops being abstract, because the dependency is concentrated in a small number of aging, remotely operable structures and the tonnage that flows through them is enormous. The U.S. inland waterway system comprises tens of thousands of miles of navigable rivers and canals, with 241 locks managed by the Army Corps of Engineers at 195 sites.40 Those locks and their associated dams are the mechanism that keeps commercially navigable pools open; without them, large stretches of river are simply not transitable by a loaded barge.

The freight that depends on this infrastructure is a cross-section of the entire critical-infrastructure economy. By one industry accounting, the inland waterways carry roughly 20 percent of the coal that the United States uses, coal that in turn generates about a tenth of the nation's electricity; they move about 40 percent of U.S. petroleum and petroleum products; and they carry around 60 percent of all U.S. grain exports.42 The Mississippi River corridor alone accounts for a majority of those grain exports.41 The modal leverage is the part that makes a lock outage bite: a single fifteen-barge tow carries the cargo of roughly 216 rail cars or about 1,050 large semi-trailer trucks, so when a corridor closes there is no surge capacity on road or rail that can absorb the displaced tonnage.42

Now layer the condition data onto the dependency. The Corps is, by its own reckoning, on the cusp of needing to replace some 260 hydraulic structures a year — many built between 1920 and 1975 — at an annual cost on the order of one to two billion dollars.41 An aging, under-recapitalized fleet of remotely operable locks, each a potential chokepoint for nationally significant tonnage, is exactly the profile an adversary seeking maximal economic disruption per unit of effort would select. The point is not that a lock attack is imminent; it is that the consequence is national and the surface is the same legacy OT that the rest of this article has described.

A dam is a node in five other sectors at once. To attack the water is to reach, through one set of controllers, the power, the harvest, the freight, and the markets that depend on them.

07 Defense

Mitigation: standards, architecture, tools

Defending the Dams Sector is a problem of imposing coherence on a system that has none by default. There is no single mandatory framework, no central operator, and no uniform technology baseline; there is instead a stack of voluntary standards, agency-specific programs, architectural patterns borrowed from the wider OT world, and a thin layer of sector-specific guidance. What follows organizes the credible mitigations into four layers — governance and standards, architecture, detection, and resilience — with the explicit caveat that for most privately owned dams every one of these is something the owner must choose to adopt.

Governance and standards

The foundational document for industrial-control-system security is NIST Special Publication 800-82, whose third revision recast the guidance as a broad treatment of operational-technology security rather than SCADA alone, and aligned its recommendations with the NIST control catalog so that OT inherits the same risk-management discipline as IT.43 Its central architectural prescription is layered defense-in-depth — segmenting networks, hardening hosts, controlling access, and monitoring continuously, so that no single failure exposes the control core.44 Sitting above it, the NIST Cybersecurity Framework — updated to version 2.0 in 2024 with the addition of a dedicated Govern function — provides the outcome-based vocabulary (Govern, Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover) that lets a dam operator describe and prioritize a security program in terms a board and a regulator can both read.45

For the control architecture itself, the ISA/IEC 62443 family is the international reference standard, defining the zones-and-conduits model and a ladder of security levels against which a facility's segmentation can be specified and assessed.17 The sector's own modernization guidance reinforces the same direction of travel and adds the interim control that matters most for un-upgradable equipment: unidirectional gateways that let monitoring data out without admitting a single inbound command.9

Mandatory authority, where it exists, is narrow and revealing. The NERC Critical Infrastructure Protection standards impose enforceable cybersecurity requirements — but only on assets that affect the reliability of the bulk electric system, which for a hydroelectric dam means its generation equipment, not its spillway gates or its water-retention function.46 This is the central regulatory gap of the sector: the one place dams are subject to mandatory cyber rules treats them as power plants, and is silent on the dam-safety consequences — the flood downstream — that make them uniquely dangerous.46 FERC's own security program for the hydropower projects it licenses works to address that downstream dimension more directly, extending expectations beyond generation reliability toward the protection of the project as a water-retention structure.47

Bridging the gap between generic OT standards and the specifics of a dam is the role of the sector's tailored guidance. The Dams Sector Cybersecurity Framework Implementation Guidance translates the NIST framework into the sector's own language and asset context, helping owners map the abstract functions onto gates, sensors, and powerhouses.48 The federal dam owners layer their own programs on top: research and response frameworks developed for the hydroelectric fleet document how agencies such as the Army Corps and the Bureau of Reclamation structure incident response and recovery for their own structures, and how those practices can inform the wider sector.50

Architecture: segment, isolate, and remove exposure

If governance sets the goals, architecture is where security is actually won or lost, and the dominant lesson of every incident in this article is the same: re-establish and enforce the boundaries that the Purdue model defines.16 In practice that means a disciplined separation of the enterprise IT network from the control network, with an industrial demilitarized zone mediating any traffic that must cross between them; segmentation within the OT environment so that a foothold on one controller does not grant the run of the plant; and, for the most safety-critical paths, unidirectional data diodes that make inbound command traffic physically impossible rather than merely forbidden.

The single highest-value action is also the simplest to state: remove control devices from direct internet exposure. Both the Aliquippa intrusion and the broader census of internet-reachable PLCs trace to the same root cause — programmable controllers with default credentials answering on their programming ports from the open internet — and the remediation is to take them off the public network, place them behind properly configured remote-access infrastructure, and require phishing-resistant multi-factor authentication for any human reaching in.30 Where remote operation is a genuine operational necessity — and for a gatehouse that must be operable during a flash flood, it often is — the access should be brokered, monitored, and credentialed, never standing open. Layered onto this, a zero-trust posture for OT replaces the obsolete assumption that anything inside the perimeter is trustworthy with continuous, per-session verification of every actor and device.

Detection: see the OT, watch the behavior

Segmentation buys time; detection is what turns that time into a response. The defining detection challenge for dams is that the most capable adversary, Volt Typhoon, deliberately avoids malware and operates with valid credentials and built-in tools, which defeats signature-based defenses and forces a shift to behavioral and anomaly detection that can flag a legitimate-looking action occurring at an illegitimate time or from an unexpected source.19 The practical foundations are an accurate, continuously maintained asset inventory — you cannot defend controllers you do not know you have — and passive monitoring of industrial-protocol traffic that can observe Modbus, DNP3, and the rest without injecting risk into fragile control loops.

Detection is also where the sector's fragmentation can be partly overcome through shared visibility. Information-sharing channels exist precisely so that an intrusion at one small utility becomes early warning for the rest: the Water Information Sharing and Analysis Center distributed the actionable detail of the Aliquippa compromise to its members, and equivalent bodies serve the electricity and multi-state-government communities.22 For the Dams Sector specifically, CISA maintains a dedicated set of resources and a partner portal through which owners, operators, and regulators coordinate, exchange threat information, and reach the sector's coordinating councils.49

Resilience: assume compromise, keep manual control

The most important security property a dam can have is the ability to fail safely, and here the sector's conservative engineering tradition is an asset the IT world lacks. Every incident that did not become a disaster — Bowman, Aliquippa — was contained because a human could fall back to manual operation when the digital layer was lost. Designing deliberately for that fallback — ensuring gates can be operated and reservoirs managed without the automation, that operators are trained and drilled on it, and that the loss of the control system is a planned-for contingency rather than a catastrophe — is the highest-leverage resilience investment in the sector.

That instinct generalizes into a resilience program. Emergency action plans, already a dam-safety staple for high-hazard structures, should explicitly incorporate cyber-induced scenarios — including the case in which the very instrumentation that would normally trigger a warning has been spoofed. Incident-response plans for the OT environment, exercised rather than shelved, are essential; the sector provides tabletop exercise material, including scenarios built around internal and external cyber threats, so that operators can rehearse a control-system compromise before they live one.49 Tested, offline-capable backups of controller logic and configurations turn a destructive attack into a recovery exercise rather than a rebuild from memory.

Finally, resilience extends up the supply chain and out to the workforce. The software, firmware, and managed services that increasingly run dam operations are themselves an attack surface, and regulators have begun to close the gap: FERC has acted to address supply-chain cyber-risk management for the bulk-power assets within its reach, and has continued to extend its control requirements toward modern virtualized and cloud-adjacent operating models.5152 A software bill of materials and procurement language that pushes security requirements onto vendors are the practical instruments. None of it works without people: the shortage of engineers fluent in both dam operations and OT security is itself a structural vulnerability, and one that no standard or appliance can patch.

08 Open Problems

Open problems & the research frontier

The uncomfortable truth running through the previous section is that the mitigations are better understood than they are deployed. The defining challenges of dam cybersecurity are not, for the most part, unsolved technical mysteries; they are structural, economic, and organizational gaps between what good practice requires and what a fragmented, under-resourced, aging sector can actually field. The research frontier is the work of closing those gaps — and, at its leading edge, of re-engineering the problem so that the worst outcomes become impossible by design rather than merely defended against.

The governance and funding gap

The first open problem is the one this article has returned to repeatedly: most American dams operate under no mandatory cybersecurity regime, and the one mandatory regime that does apply — NERC CIP — addresses grid reliability rather than dam safety. Closing that gap is a policy question as much as a technical one, and it collides directly with funding. The federal grant program meant to rehabilitate the nation's high-hazard-potential dams has been chronically under-funded, receiving no federal appropriation at all in fiscal years 2023 and 2024 despite a far larger statutory authorization — a striking signal of how thin the resources are even for physical dam safety, let alone the cyber upgrades layered on top.3 A sector that cannot fund spillway repairs will not easily fund network segmentation.

Legacy technology that cannot simply be patched

The second is the installed base. With industrial equipment lifecycles measured in decades, the controllers and software running many dams cannot be authenticated, patched, or monitored with modern tooling, and the recapitalization bill — exemplified by the Corps' need to replace hundreds of aging hydraulic structures a year — is so large that wholesale replacement is a generational project, not a remediation sprint. The research challenge is how to secure equipment that was never designed to be secured: compensating controls, bolt-on monitoring that does not destabilize fragile loops, and migration paths that retrofit integrity onto unauthenticated protocols without forced downtime.

Convergence, visibility, and the workforce

The third cluster is the convergence of IT and OT and the visibility deficit it creates. As remote operation and enterprise connectivity expand the attack surface, defenders are asked to see more while the most capable adversaries work specifically to be invisible — living off the land with valid credentials and native tools, which leaves behavioral anomaly detection as the principal hope and accurate asset inventory as its precondition.19 Compounding this, there is no comprehensive public picture of the sector's control-system exposure, and the information that does exist is often sensitive or restricted, which complicates the research community's ability to even measure the problem. Underneath all of it sits a workforce shortage: practitioners fluent in both dam operations and OT security are scarce, and the gap between the two professional cultures — dam-safety engineering and cybersecurity — is itself an unsolved integration problem.

Re-engineering the problem: CIE and CCE

The most consequential idea on the research frontier reframes the goal entirely. Rather than bolting security onto control systems after the fact, Cyber-Informed Engineering (CIE) proposes to build cybersecurity considerations into the engineering of a system from its earliest design, so that physics and engineering choices — not just digital controls — bound what an attacker can achieve.54 Its operational complement, Consequence-driven Cyber-informed Engineering (CCE), developed at Idaho National Laboratory, begins from the assumption that a sufficiently capable adversary will get in, and works backward from the handful of consequences a facility absolutely cannot tolerate to identify and engineer out the specific paths that could produce them.53 For a dam, this is a particularly natural fit: the catastrophic consequence is singular and well-defined — an uncontrolled release — and CCE's discipline of protecting the few "crown-jewel" functions that could cause it has been advanced as a national-security pillar for exactly this class of infrastructure.55 The engineering insight is that a gate which physically cannot open faster than a safe rate, or a reservoir whose critical limits are enforced by non-digital interlocks, is more secure than any amount of monitoring on a gate that can.

Tools on the horizon

Several technical research directions feed this re-engineering. Digital twins of dams and their control systems promise a safe environment in which to test responses, model an attacker's options, and validate that engineered safeguards behave as intended under manipulation. Anomaly detection driven by machine learning is the leading candidate for catching living-off-the-land behavior that signatures miss — even as the same artificial-intelligence capabilities lower the barrier for attackers, a duality that has prompted legislative proposals to require CISA to update sector cybersecurity plans specifically for AI-driven threats to critical infrastructure.56 Sensor and telemetry integrity — anti-spoofing for the level, flow, and dam-safety instrumentation that operators trust — is a research priority precisely because blinding the operator is a core attack mode. Formal verification of control logic and quantitative consequence modeling aim to prove properties of safety-critical code and to put rigorous numbers on the downstream harm of a given manipulation. And at the speculative edge, researchers have begun examining whether quantum key distribution could harden the communication links between remote dam sites and their control centers against interception, though this remains early and far from operational deployment.11

Where policy is heading

The governance frontier is moving, if unevenly. Federal regulators continue to extend mandatory control requirements within their jurisdiction, and national authorities have begun urging critical-infrastructure operators to prepare explicitly for cyber-induced operational outages — to be able to isolate and recover their systems on the assumption that prevention will sometimes fail.57 Whether that posture reaches the tens of thousands of privately owned, state-regulated dams that fall outside any federal cyber mandate is the open question on which the sector's aggregate risk most depends.

The compounding variable: climate

Overlaying every one of these challenges is a changing climate that stresses the physical system precisely where the cyber system is weakest. More frequent and more extreme hydrologic events mean more moments when a dam's release decisions are consequential, more reliance on the automated and remote control that expands the attack surface, and less margin for error in the exact scenarios — flash floods, rapid drawdowns — that an adversary would choose to exploit. The cyber-physical risk and the climate risk are not separate problems; they peak together, which is the strongest argument for treating dam cybersecurity as an urgent dimension of dam safety rather than a separate IT concern.

09 Closing

The resilience imperative

The argument of this article reduces to a single reframing. For a century, dam safety has been a discipline of concrete, hydrology, and oversight — of knowing how much water a structure can hold and ensuring it never holds more. That discipline remains necessary, but it is no longer sufficient, because the decision about how much water to hold and when to release it has migrated into a digital layer that the same century of practice never learned to defend. The water is physical; the control is cyber; and an adversary who seizes the control has, in effect, seized the water without ever touching the dam.

What makes this urgent rather than merely interesting is the collision of factors the preceding sections have laid out: a consequence ceiling measured in lives and in the simultaneous degradation of the power, water, food, freight, and financial sectors that depend on dams as a keystone; an aging, under-funded physical fleet retrofitted with legacy control systems that were never designed for an adversarial internet; a governance structure so fragmented that most dams answer to no mandatory cyber regime at all; and a threat landscape in which nation-states have already pre-positioned inside the critical-infrastructure networks dams inhabit and hacktivists have already seized the exact class of controllers that operate dam gates. None of the individual pieces is hypothetical. The intrusions have happened, the capability has been demonstrated, the access has in the largest case been obtained, and the physical consequences have been rehearsed — by hydrology rather than by an attacker, but rehearsed all the same.

The path forward is not a single technology or a single regulation. It is the steady imposition of coherence on a sector that lacks it by default: re-establishing the architectural boundaries that isolate control from exposure, removing the controllers from the open internet, building the detection and information-sharing that turn one utility's incident into the whole sector's early warning, and — most powerfully — re-engineering the most dangerous functions so that physics, not just software, bounds what an attacker can do. The deepest insight on the research frontier is also the most reassuring: the catastrophic outcome a dam can produce is singular and well-defined, which means it is the kind of consequence that consequence-driven engineering can be designed to foreclose. A gate that physically cannot open faster than is safe does not need to trust its network.

The dams will be here for another century. Whether the digital layer that now governs them is treated as an afterthought or as a first-class dimension of dam safety is a choice the sector is making right now, structure by structure, budget by budget. The reservoir does not care which choice is made. The valley below it does.
