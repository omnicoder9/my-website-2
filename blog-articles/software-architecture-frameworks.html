Sheet 00 · The drawing problem

A system you cannot see

Every non-trivial software system has an architecture — a set of structures comprising elements, their relationships, and the properties of both — whether or not anyone has ever written it down. The structure exists in the running code, in the network topology, in the deployment pipeline, and, most fragile of all, in the collective memory of the people who built it. The trouble is that this memory walks out the door at five o’clock, takes new jobs, and forgets. An architecture nobody can see is an architecture nobody can reason about, defend, or safely change.

The discipline of describing architecture — deliberately, in a form others can read — is younger than the systems it serves. The field acquired a shared intellectual footing only in 1992, when Dewayne Perry and Alexander Wolf published their foundational model of software architecture as three intertwined components: elements, form, and rationale.[1] That last word matters. An architecture description is not just a picture of boxes; it is also the record of why the boxes are arranged the way they are. Strip out the rationale and you are left with a diagram that will be quietly violated by the next person who does not understand it.

An architecture description framework is a reusable answer to two questions that every team faces and most answer badly: what should we document about our architecture, and how should we organise it so the right people can find what concerns them? Some frameworks answer chiefly the “what” (a fixed table of contents); some answer chiefly the “how” (a discipline of views and viewpoints); the strongest combine both. They range from a lightweight set of four diagram types a developer can sketch on a whiteboard, up to formal enterprise ontologies with thirty-six cells and a certification industry attached.[2]

One system, many projections

The central idea that unites almost every framework in this guide is borrowed wholesale from building architecture, and it is worth stating plainly because it explains the shape of everything that follows. A complex artefact cannot be captured in a single drawing. A building is documented through a floor plan, an elevation, a section, and a wiring diagram — each a projection of the same structure, each chosen to answer the concerns of a particular reader. The plumber and the planning officer never look at the same sheet. Software is the same. We call these projections views, and the rules that govern what a view contains and whom it serves are called viewpoints.

This is not a metaphor invented for convenience. It is the literal genealogy of the field. Philippe Kruchten titled his seminal 1995 paper Architectural Blueprints;[3] John Zachman built his enterprise framework by explicitly studying how architects and engineers document buildings and aircraft, right down to the “bubble charts” an architect sketches in the first meeting with an owner.[4] Once you see the projection idea, the proliferation of frameworks stops looking like chaos and starts looking like a family: different answers to the question of which projections are worth drawing, and how strictly to govern them.

The rest of this guide walks that family tree. We begin not with the most popular framework but with the most foundational one — the international standard that defines the very words view, viewpoint, stakeholder, and concern — because once those terms are pinned down, every framework after it reads as a particular configuration of the same handful of ideas.

Sheet 01 · Views & the standard

The grammar everyone shares

Before surveying the frameworks that practitioners actually reach for, it pays to install the vocabulary they all quietly assume. That vocabulary has a canonical source: the international standard now published as ISO/IEC/IEEE 42010. It is not a framework you would hand to a team and say “document the system like this.” It is something more useful and more abstract — a standard for what any architecture description must contain in order to be coherent, and a precise set of definitions for the words the whole field uses loosely. Learn it once and every other framework becomes legible as a special case.

The standard’s lineage mirrors the maturing of the discipline itself. It began life as IEEE Std 1471-2000, the first formally recognised standard for architecture description, which introduced the now-ubiquitous conceptual model of stakeholders, concerns, viewpoints, and views.[5] In 2007 it was adopted into the ISO/IEC family, and in 2011 a thoroughly revised joint edition — ISO/IEC/IEEE 42010:2011 — harmonised the IEEE and ISO traditions and tightened the conceptual model into something close to its modern form.[6] The current edition, ISO/IEC/IEEE 42010:2022, broadened the scope further still, retitling the document Software, systems and enterprise — Architecture description to signal that the same conceptual machinery governs the architecture of a single program, a system of systems, and an entire enterprise.[7]

Architecture versus its description

The standard’s first and most clarifying move is to separate two things that everyday speech runs together. The architecture of a system is a property of the system itself: its fundamental concepts or properties, embodied in its elements and relationships and in the principles of its design and evolution. The architecture description is a concrete work product — the documents, diagrams, and models — that expresses that architecture. You cannot email someone an architecture; you can only email them a description of one. This sounds pedantic until you notice how many arguments on real teams dissolve once people realise they are disputing the map, not the territory.

The conceptual model in one breath

A system has one or more stakeholders, each holding concerns about it. An architecture description frames those concerns by selecting a set of viewpoints; each viewpoint governs one view, which is the actual content addressing those concerns. A view is made of one or more architecture models, each built according to a model kind. Where views overlap, correspondences (and the rules that constrain them) keep them consistent.

Walk those terms slowly, because every framework later in this guide is a particular set of answers to them.[8] Stakeholders are anyone with a stake in the architecture: developers, operators, security auditors, the people paying for it, the regulator who will inspect it. A concern is any interest one of them holds — performance under load, the cost of a future migration, compliance with a data-residency law, or simply “will a new engineer understand this in a day?” Concerns are the reason the description exists; a view that addresses no one’s concern is decoration.

A viewpoint is the reusable specification — the rules, conventions, and notations — for constructing a certain kind of view. It is the genre; the view is the particular work in that genre. “A deployment viewpoint” says what a deployment diagram is for, who reads it, and what notation it uses; “the deployment view of the payments platform” is the diagram you actually drew. This distinction is the single most valuable idea the standard contributes, and it is precisely the distinction that lets us compare frameworks: a framework is, in large part, a curated library of viewpoints together with advice on when to use each.

What changed in 2022

The 2022 revision is worth understanding because it reveals where the field’s centre of gravity has moved. The most consequential change is one of scope: the standard generalised its subject from a system to an entity of interest, so that the same rules now apply whether you are describing a microservice, a fleet of vehicles, or a multinational’s entire IT estate. The notion of an architecture model was generalised to a view component, giving description authors more freedom in how views are assembled. The revision also added explicit support for architecture perspectives (sometimes called stakeholder perspectives) and architecture aspects — machinery for handling concerns such as security or performance that cut across many views at once, rather than living neatly inside one. Tellingly, the edition dropped the older normative UML metamodel, reflecting a field that no longer assumes any single notation.[7]

None of this requires memorising clause numbers. The payoff is conceptual: when the C4 model speaks of diagrams for different audiences, when 4+1 enumerates exactly five views, when arc42 lays out twelve sections, each is selecting and constraining viewpoints in the precise sense the standard defines. With that grammar installed, we can read the frameworks as sentences rather than as unrelated vocabularies.

Sheet 02 · The C4 model

Maps at four zoom levels

If ISO 42010 is the grammar, the C4 model is the most widely adopted modern sentence — a lightweight, developer-friendly way to draw software architecture that has spread through engineering teams precisely because it asks so little and gives so much. It was created by the software architect Simon Brown, distilled over 2006–2011 from his experience teaching architecture and from the older traditions of UML and Kruchten’s 4+1, and deliberately pared down to something a developer can draw on a whiteboard without consulting a manual.[9] The name is a mnemonic for its four levels of diagram: Context, Container, Component, and Code.[10]

The governing metaphor is cartographic. You would never navigate a country with a single map; you use a world map to find the country, a road map to find the city, a street map to find the building, and a floor plan to find the room. Each is the same territory at a different zoom level, and each deliberately omits what would clutter it at that scale. C4 applies exactly this idea to software: each level zooms one step further in, adding detail while discarding the breadth that no longer fits. The discipline is in the omission as much as the inclusion.[11]

Before the levels themselves, C4 fixes a small vocabulary of abstractions so that everyone draws the same things. A person is a human user of the system — a role, not an individual. A software system is the highest-level unit of something that delivers value, the thing your stakeholders would name. A container — the most frequently misunderstood term — is not a Docker container; it is a separately runnable or deployable unit such as a web server application, a single-page app, a mobile app, a database, or a file system. A component is a grouping of related functionality behind a well-defined interface inside a container. Pinning these words down is half the value, because architecture arguments are so often really arguments about words.

The four levels

The System Context diagram (Level 1) is the view you show anyone, technical or not. It places your system as a single box in the centre and surrounds it with the people who use it and the other systems it talks to. It deliberately shows no internal detail whatsoever — no technologies, no databases, nothing about how the system works. Its entire job is to set the scene and the scope: what is ours, what is not, and who cares. It is the one diagram you can put in front of a non-technical sponsor and expect them to follow.

The Container diagram (Level 2) zooms inside the system box to reveal the high-level technical building blocks — the separately deployable units and the major data stores — and how responsibility is divided among them. This is the level most teams find immediately useful, because it answers “what are the moving parts and what does each one do?” without drowning the reader in classes. Figure 1 is exactly such a diagram. Each container is typically annotated with its core technology choice, so a reader can see at a glance that the API is, say, a Java service and the store is PostgreSQL.

The Component diagram (Level 3) zooms into a single container and shows the components inside it and their interactions. Here you document how one deployable unit is internally organised — the controllers, services, and repositories, or whatever structural grouping the codebase uses. Brown’s own guidance is candid that not every container warrants a component diagram; you draw one where the internal structure is non-obvious and worth explaining, and you skip it where the code speaks for itself.

The Code diagram (Level 4) zooms one final step into a single component to show its implementation — classes, interfaces, and their relationships, typically as a UML class diagram. In practice this level is the least used and the most disposable: it is so close to the source that it ages the instant the code changes, and Brown explicitly recommends generating it on demand from the IDE rather than drawing and maintaining it by hand. For most teams the model that matters is the first three levels.

That widget captures the whole idea in three taps: the same system, drawn at three zoom levels, each sheet discarding what the next exists to reveal. The set of diagrams forms a navigable hierarchy — you descend from context to container to component the way you descend from a country map to a street map, never losing your place because each level names the box you just zoomed into.

Notation, supplementary diagrams, and tooling

A defining and slightly counter-intuitive feature of C4 is that it is notation-independent. It does not mandate a particular shape language the way UML does. The convention is simply that every box carries enough text to stand alone — the element’s name, its type (person, system, container, component), the chosen technology, and a short description — and that every line (a relationship) is labelled with its intent and, where it helps, its protocol. The guidance is blunt about this: a diagram should make sense to someone who has never seen the C4 model, which means colour and shape may aid comprehension but must never be the only carrier of meaning. A sensible key always accompanies the picture.

Beyond the four core levels, the model offers a few supplementary diagrams for when the hierarchy alone is not enough. A system landscape diagram zooms out beyond a single system to map the wider enterprise context — many systems and the people who span them. A dynamic diagram borrows from UML communication diagrams to show a specific runtime interaction or sequence of calls. A deployment diagram maps containers onto the infrastructure — the servers, clusters, and cloud services — they actually run on. These are optional tools reached for as needed, not mandatory sheets.

Because the diagrams are simple and rule-bound, they lend themselves to a diagrams-as-code workflow, and a healthy tool ecosystem has grown up around the model. Structurizr — Brown’s own tooling — lets you define a single model in a text-based DSL and render multiple C4 views from it, so the views never drift out of sync because they share one source of truth.[12] The popular C4-PlantUML and a range of other plugins bring C4 shapes to PlantUML, Mermaid, and similar text-to-diagram engines, while commercial tools such as IcePanel add interactive, navigable C4 models for larger organisations.[11] We return to this tooling landscape in detail in Sheet 08.

The C4 model asks one disciplined question at every zoom level: what can I leave out and still tell the truth?

— the omission, not the detail, is the craft

Why it spread — and where it stops

C4’s adoption is easy to explain. It is easy to understand, with a learning curve measured in minutes rather than days, which means the whole team can both read and produce the diagrams rather than depending on a single architect fluent in UML. It is excellent for microservices and distributed systems: the container level maps almost perfectly onto the reality of many independently deployable services and their data stores, giving teams a shared picture of a landscape that is otherwise hard to hold in one’s head. And it is strong for stakeholder communication, because the context diagram speaks to non-technical sponsors while the deeper levels serve engineers — one consistent model, several audiences, each entering at the zoom level that addresses their concerns.

It is equally important to be honest about C4’s boundaries, because no framework is universal. C4 is fundamentally a static-structure notation: it excels at showing what the pieces are and how they connect, but it is not designed to capture rich runtime behaviour, data models, business processes, or sequencing — the dynamic and deployment supplements exist precisely because the core does not cover that ground. Nor does C4 prescribe how to record decisions and rationale; it is a diagramming approach, not a documentation framework, and it pairs naturally with the architecture-decision records of Sheet 07 and the prose scaffolding of arc42 in Sheet 04. Used for what it is — a way to draw clear maps of static structure — it is hard to beat. Asked to be a complete documentation method on its own, it was never meant to be one.

Sheet 03 · The 4+1 view model

Four views and a reason to draw them

Long before C4 streamlined architecture diagrams for the whiteboard era, one paper did more than any other to establish the very idea that an architecture should be documented through multiple, distinct views. In November 1995, Philippe Kruchten — then at Rational Software — published “Architectural Blueprints — The ‘4+1’ View Model of Software Architecture” in IEEE Software.[3] Its influence is hard to overstate: it shaped the Rational Unified Process, it fed directly into the thinking behind ISO 42010, and its core insight — that no single diagram can serve every stakeholder, so you deliberately maintain several — is the seed from which the entire field of this guide grew.

Kruchten’s diagnosis was that architecture documents of the early 1990s tried to capture everything in one overloaded diagram and consequently served no one well. His prescription was to organise the description into four concurrent views, each addressing the concerns of a specific group of stakeholders, and then to tie them together with a fifth element — a set of scenarios — that both illustrates and validates the other four. Hence the arithmetic of the name: four views, plus one.

The four views

The logical view addresses the functionality the system provides to its end users. It is the object-oriented decomposition — the key abstractions, domain objects, and their relationships — expressed in Kruchten’s day as class and state diagrams. Its audience is the end users and the analysts and designers reasoning about what the system does. In a modern idiom this is the domain model: the entities, their behaviours, and how they collaborate to deliver features.

The process view addresses the system’s dynamic, run-time behaviour — concurrency, parallelism, synchronisation, and performance. It captures how the system decomposes into processes and threads, how those communicate, and how the design meets concerns such as throughput, response time, and fault tolerance. Its audience is the integrators and engineers worried about whether the system will actually perform and behave correctly under concurrent load. This is the view C4’s static notation deliberately does not cover, which is precisely why the two approaches complement rather than compete.

The development view — sometimes called the implementation view — addresses the software’s organisation in the development environment: the modules, layers, packages, and subsystems as they appear to programmers, and the dependencies among them. Its audience is the developers and managers who must plan the work, assign teams, and reason about build order and reuse. It is where architecture meets the file system and the org chart.

The physical view — today we would say the deployment view — addresses the mapping of software onto hardware: which processes run on which nodes, and how those nodes connect. Its audience is the system engineers concerned with topology, distribution, scalability, and reliability of the deployed system. It answers the question of where the software physically lives and how the boxes are wired together.

The “+1”: scenarios as the binding agent

The genius of the model is the fifth element. The scenarios — small instances of use cases — are not really a fifth view of equal weight; they are the redundant glue that holds the other four together. Each scenario is walked through all four views to show how the architecture’s elements collaborate to satisfy a concrete requirement. This serves two distinct purposes. First, it is a discovery and validation tool: working scenarios through the views during design surfaces missing elements and inconsistencies between views before they become defects. Second, it is a teaching tool, giving a newcomer a thread to follow through what would otherwise be four disconnected pictures. The scenarios are how you prove the four views describe the same system rather than four different ones.

A note on notation and longevity

Kruchten illustrated 4+1 with the notations of the early 1990s — Booch notation, soon to be subsumed by UML — but the model itself is notation-agnostic, which is why it has aged so gracefully. The same five-part structure maps cleanly onto modern tools: a domain model for the logical view, sequence and activity diagrams for the process view, a module or package diagram for the development view, and a C4 deployment diagram for the physical view. The vocabulary of “views serving stakeholder concerns” that 42010 later formalised is, in essence, 4+1 generalised.

For enterprise and large-scale systems, 4+1 remains a sound organising scaffold precisely because it forces explicit attention to the run-time and deployment concerns — concurrency, performance, distribution — that lighter diagramming approaches gloss over. Many teams today run a productive hybrid: they keep 4+1’s discipline of separating logical, process, development, and physical concerns, while drawing the individual views in C4’s more approachable notation. The paper that started the multi-view tradition still earns its place on the shelf three decades on.

It is worth being honest about where the model shows its age. 4+1 was conceived for the large, monolithic, often single-deployment systems typical of its era, and two of its assumptions strain under modern distributed architectures. The development view’s tidy picture of a layered subsystem hierarchy maps awkwardly onto a sprawl of independently deployable services owned by separate teams; and the once-crisp line between the process view and the physical view blurs when elastic orchestration platforms schedule containers across a shifting pool of nodes, so that “which process runs where” is a run-time property rather than a design-time diagram. The model also has comparatively little to say about the data architecture as a first-class concern — a gap that later catalogues such as Rozanski and Woods’ explicit Information viewpoint were created to fill. None of this retires 4+1; it simply clarifies its role. The model’s enduring contribution is not its specific list of four views but its founding argument — that a single diagram cannot serve every stakeholder, and that the honest response is a small, deliberate set of views bound together by scenarios. Every framework that followed, C4 and arc42 included, is a variation on that one durable insight.

Sheet 04 · arc42

A cabinet with twelve drawers

Where C4 answers “how do I draw this?” and 4+1 answers “which views do I keep?”, arc42 answers a third, equally practical question: “what goes in the document, and in what order?” It is a template — a fixed, battle-tested table of contents for an architecture document — created by Dr. Gernot Starke and Dr. Peter Hruschka, first released in 2005 and refined continuously since.[13] The name encodes its purpose: arc for architecture, 42 for the (tongue-in-cheek) answer to everything. It is free, available under a permissive licence, and provided in numerous formats and languages.[14]

The useful mental image is a cabinet with twelve labelled drawers. arc42 does not tell you how full each drawer must be or exactly what notation to put inside — it tells you that there are twelve drawers, what each is for, and that everything about your architecture has a natural home in one of them. The discipline it imposes is not on content but on structure: when every architecture document in an organisation has the same twelve sections in the same order, a reader knows exactly where to look for deployment, or for the rationale behind a contentious decision, without reading the whole thing.[15]

The twelve sections

The template’s sequence is deliberate, moving from why through what to how and finally to caveats.[16] (1) Introduction and Goals records the requirements driving the architecture and the top quality goals — the handful of qualities, such as performance or security, that the architecture must above all deliver. (2) Constraints captures the non-negotiable boundaries: mandated technologies, organisational rules, legal requirements. (3) Context and Scope delimits the system from its environment and names its external interfaces — the same job as a C4 context diagram. (4) Solution Strategy is a short, high-value summary of the fundamental decisions and approaches that shape everything else.

The middle drawers hold the structural heart. (5) Building Block View is the static decomposition — the system broken into parts and sub-parts, mapping directly onto C4’s container and component levels. (6) Runtime View shows how those building blocks collaborate in important scenarios, capturing the dynamic behaviour that C4’s static notation omits. (7) Deployment View maps the software onto its technical infrastructure — arc42’s equivalent of 4+1’s physical view.

The later drawers are where arc42’s thoroughness shows, because they cover ground that pure diagramming approaches leave undocumented. (8) Cross-cutting Concepts records the overarching solution ideas — domain models, security mechanisms, error-handling strategies, logging — that apply across many building blocks. (9) Architecture Decisions captures the important, hard, or expensive decisions along with their rationale — the natural home for the architecture-decision records of Sheet 07. (10) Quality Requirements makes the quality goals concrete, typically as a quality tree and specific scenarios. (11) Risks and Technical Debt names the known weaknesses honestly, and (12) Glossary fixes the meaning of domain and technical terms so that everyone reads the document the same way.

arc42 standardises the shape of the document, not the content of the architecture — twelve questions every system should be able to answer.

— structure as a shared index, not a straitjacket

Philosophy, tooling, and how it combines

arc42’s design philosophy is relentlessly pragmatic. Its authors stress that a section should be filled in only to the extent it adds value — an empty or one-line drawer is an acceptable and honest signal that the topic does not yet matter for this system, far better than padding. The template is explicitly meant to be adapted, not obeyed: teams routinely tailor it, and the goal is a living, useful document rather than a ceremonial one. A notable 2022 revision (version 8) added worked examples throughout, lowering the barrier for teams unsure what a good section actually looks like.

Because it is plain structure, arc42 is notation- and tool-agnostic. Teams maintain it in everything from wikis (Confluence is common) to Markdown or AsciiDoc kept under version control alongside the code — the docs-as-code approach — and there are dedicated authoring add-ons. Crucially, arc42 is the great integrator of the other approaches in this guide: it tells you what sections to write, and you fill the structural sections with C4 diagrams, the decision section with ADRs, and the quality section with concrete scenarios. Far from competing with C4 or 4+1, it provides the prose skeleton into which their visual artefacts slot. A great many well-documented systems today use precisely this stack, and we return to it as a recommended default in Sheet 09.

Sheet 05 · TOGAF & Zachman

When the system is the whole enterprise

The frameworks so far describe the architecture of a software system. A parallel tradition — enterprise architecture — scales the same instinct up to an entire organisation, asking how all of its business processes, data, applications, and technology fit together and align with strategy. Two names dominate this older, heavier world, and any serious survey must place them: the Zachman Framework, which is an ontology, and TOGAF, which is a method. They answer different questions, and understanding the distinction is the key to not confusing them.

TOGAF and the Architecture Development Method

TOGAF — The Open Group Architecture Framework — is the de facto standard process for enterprise architecture, maintained by The Open Group and first released as version 1.0 in 1995, with roots in the US Department of Defense’s TAFIM.[17] Unlike a documentation template, TOGAF is primarily a method: its beating heart is the Architecture Development Method (ADM), a cyclical, step-by-step process for planning, designing, governing, and implementing an enterprise architecture. The current edition, the TOGAF Standard, 10th Edition, was released in April 2022 and reorganised the material into a modular form intended to make the guidance easier to apply selectively.[18]

The ADM proceeds through a Preliminary phase — establishing the architecture capability and principles — followed by eight phases labelled A through H: Architecture Vision (A), then the three architecture domains in turn, Business Architecture (B), Information Systems Architectures covering data and applications (C), and Technology Architecture (D); then Opportunities and Solutions (E), Migration Planning (F), Implementation Governance (G), and Architecture Change Management (H). At the centre of the cycle sits Requirements Management, which is not a phase you pass through once but a continuous activity that informs and is informed by every other phase. TOGAF organises the enterprise into four architecture domains — Business, Data, Application, and Technology (the last three sometimes abbreviated together) — and supplies an Architecture Content Framework and a wealth of guidance, techniques, and deliverable definitions to populate them.

The Zachman Framework: an ontology, not a method

The Zachman Framework is the older and more foundational of the two. It originated in a 1987 paper by John A. Zachman in the IBM Systems Journal, titled “A Framework for Information Systems Architecture,” which proposed that an enterprise — like a complex engineering product — could be described systematically by crossing a set of fundamental questions with a set of stakeholder perspectives.[4] Crucially, Zachman is an ontology — a classification scheme — not a process. It tells you what kinds of descriptions can exist and how they relate, but says nothing about the order in which to produce them. This is exactly why Zachman and TOGAF are complementary rather than rival: Zachman is the periodic table, TOGAF is the recipe.

The matrix crosses six interrogatives as columns — What (data), How (function), Where (network), Who (people), When (time), and Why (motivation) — with six perspectives as rows, descending from the most abstract to the most concrete: the Executive (scope/contextual), Business Management (conceptual), Architect (logical/system), Engineer (physical/technology), Technician (detailed/component), and finally the functioning Enterprise itself. Each of the thirty-six cells holds a distinct, “primitive” model answering one question from one viewpoint — the data model in one cell, the process model in another, the network topology in a third. The framework’s rules hold that each cell is unique and that a complete, properly aligned set of cells fully describes the enterprise. In practice few organisations populate all thirty-six; the framework’s lasting value is as a thinking tool that reveals which descriptions exist and, more pointedly, which are missing.

The wider enterprise toolbox

Several other frameworks orbit this space and deserve at least a name. ArchiMate, also from The Open Group, is a formal modelling language for enterprise architecture — the notation that pairs naturally with TOGAF’s method, much as UML pairs with software design. DoDAF (the US Department of Defense Architecture Framework) and the UK’s MODAF are heavyweight defence-sector frameworks for vast systems-of-systems. And for modelling individual systems rather than enterprises, the general-purpose UML and its systems-engineering sibling SysML remain the lingua franca, supplying the class, sequence, and state diagrams that many of the views above are ultimately drawn in. These are the formal, often contract-mandated heavy machinery of architecture; most software teams will meet them only at the enterprise or government scale, but knowing they exist clarifies where the lightweight approaches stop.

Sheet 06 · Views & Beyond

The scholarly backbone

Between the whiteboard pragmatism of C4 and the enterprise heft of TOGAF sits a body of rigorous, practitioner-oriented work that gave the multi-view idea its intellectual depth. Two books in particular function as the field’s standard references, and any architect who wants to go beyond drawing pictures to genuinely engineering a description will end up reading them. Both build directly on the 42010 conceptual model; both predate and outlast any single tool.

Documenting Software Architectures: Views and Beyond

The first is Documenting Software Architectures: Views and Beyond, produced by a team at Carnegie Mellon’s Software Engineering Institute — Paul Clements, Felix Bachmann, Len Bass, David Garlan, James Ivers, Reed Little, Paulo Merson, Robert Nord, and Judith Stafford.[19] Its central organising idea is that architectural structures, and the views that document them, fall into three broad categories — a taxonomy that has quietly become standard vocabulary. Module views document the system’s static units of implementation: its decomposition into modules, layers, and classes, and the “uses” and “is-a” relations among them. Component-and-connector views document the run-time picture: the components that exist when the system executes and the connectors — the pathways, protocols, and data flows — through which they interact. Allocation views document the mapping of software onto its environment: onto hardware, onto the file system, and onto teams in the organisation.

The “and Beyond” in the title is the book’s second contribution: detailed, hard-won guidance on the documentation that surrounds the views themselves — how to record interfaces, how to write down the rationale behind decisions, how to document the mappings between views so a reader can trace an element from one to another, and how to assemble all of it into a coherent package with a roadmap that tells a reader where to start. It is, in effect, the engineering manual for everything 42010 specifies only in the abstract. The book also revived and popularised the view template idea — a consistent skeleton for presenting any view — which echoes through arc42 and every disciplined documentation effort since.

Software Systems Architecture: viewpoints and perspectives

The second standard reference is Software Systems Architecture by Nick Rozanski and Eoin Woods, whose distinctive contribution is captured in its subtitle: Working with Stakeholders Using Viewpoints and Perspectives.[20] Explicitly grounded in the 42010 model, it supplies a practical catalogue of reusable viewpoints — a richer set than 4+1’s four — commonly enumerated as the Functional, Information, Concurrency, Development, Deployment, and Operational viewpoints. The first three sharpen distinctions that 4+1 blurs: separating the functional structure from the information (data) structure from the concurrency structure, and adding an explicit operational viewpoint for how the system is run, monitored, and maintained in production — a concern earlier models neglected.

The idea worth stealing: perspectives

Rozanski and Woods’ most-borrowed innovation is the architectural perspective: a body of guidance for a cross-cutting quality — security, performance and scalability, availability and resilience, evolution, accessibility — that must be applied across several views rather than living in one. Where a viewpoint produces a view, a perspective is run through the existing views to assess and improve a quality property. This is precisely the “quality attributes cut across structure” insight that ISO/IEC/IEEE 42010:2022 later absorbed as its own notion of aspects and stakeholder perspectives.

The reason these works matter to a practitioner who may never read a 600-page book cover to cover is that they supply the concepts the lighter frameworks assume but do not teach. When you decide that a C4 container diagram is a component-and-connector view, or that your operational runbook is really an operational viewpoint, or that “is this secure?” is a perspective to run across all your views rather than a box to add to one — you are using this scholarship whether you cite it or not. C4 and arc42 are how most teams work; Views and Beyond and Software Systems Architecture are why those approaches are shaped the way they are.

The two books are best read as complements rather than rivals, because they answer different questions. Views and Beyond is the stronger reference when the pressing problem is structure — how to decompose a system, which kinds of view to draw, how to document interfaces and the mappings between views with engineering rigour; it is a manual for getting the description itself right. Software Systems Architecture is the stronger reference when the pressing problem is stakeholders and qualities — how to elicit the concerns that should drive the work in the first place, and how to reason about cross-cutting properties through its catalogue of perspectives. A team drowning in inconsistent diagrams reaches for the first; a team that draws clean diagrams nobody finds useful reaches for the second. Read together, they cover the full arc from “who needs this and what do they worry about” to “here is the precisely structured artefact that answers them” — which is, not coincidentally, exactly the span that ISO/IEC/IEEE 42010 maps out in the abstract.

Sheet 07 · Decision records

Capturing the why before it evaporates

Every framework so far documents what an architecture is. None of them, on their own, reliably captures the single most perishable and most valuable piece of architectural knowledge: why it is that way. Six months after a decision, the diagram survives but the reasoning is gone — the alternatives that were weighed, the constraint that ruled one out, the trade-off everyone agreed to accept. The next engineer, seeing only the result, “fixes” what was a deliberate choice and reintroduces the very problem it solved. The Architecture Decision Record (ADR) is the elegant, lightweight answer to this decay.

The practice was crystallised by Michael Nygard in an influential 2011 blog post, “Documenting Architecture Decisions,” which proposed capturing each significant architectural decision in a short, immutable text file kept alongside the code.[21] The intellectual debt runs back to Kruchten’s long advocacy for treating design rationale as a first-class part of architecture; ADRs made that idea cheap enough that ordinary teams would actually do it. An ADR is short by design — a page or less — and follows a simple, now-classic structure of five parts.

ADR-0007.mdACCEPTED
# 7. Use event sourcing for the ledger

## Status
Accepted — supersedes ADR-0003

## Context
The ledger must retain a complete, auditable history of every
balance change for regulatory reporting. A standard
update-in-place schema loses intermediate states and makes
point-in-time reconstruction expensive and error-prone.

## Decision
We will model the ledger as an append-only event store. Current
balances become a projection rebuilt from the event stream.

## Consequences
+ Full audit trail and time-travel queries come for free.
+ Writes are simple, contention-free appends.
- Read models add projection code and eventual-consistency lag.
- Engineers must learn an unfamiliar persistence style (see ADR-0008).

The five parts, and why each earns its place

The Title is a short noun phrase naming the decision, usually numbered so records can reference one another. The Status tracks the record through its life — proposed, then accepted or rejected, and later perhaps deprecated or superseded by a newer ADR. This status field is what makes a collection of ADRs into a navigable decision log: superseded records are never deleted, only marked, so the history of the thinking is preserved even as the decisions change.

The Context is the heart of the record — the forces at play: the technical, business, and political facts that make a decision necessary, stated neutrally. The Decision states the response in active voice (“we will…”). And the Consequences — the part teams are most tempted to skip and most regret skipping — record what results, the good and the bad, the new context the decision creates and the new trade-offs it imposes. An honest consequences section is what separates a decision record from marketing.

Formats, tooling, and where ADRs live

Nygard’s template is the most common, but variants abound: MADR (Markdown Any Decision Records) adds structure for explicitly listing considered options and decision drivers, while Y-statements compress a decision into a single structured sentence. The community hub at adr.github.io collects these conventions and tooling, and Nat Pryce’s adr-tools automates creating and superseding records from the command line.[22] Martin Fowler’s widely-read write-up helped carry the practice into the mainstream.[23] ADRs typically live as Markdown files in the repository — version-controlled, reviewed in pull requests like code — or populate section 9 of an arc42 document.

The reason ADRs have spread so fast is that they fit the grain of how software is actually built. They are diff-able, reviewable, and co-located with the code they explain, so they are written when the decision is fresh and the context is known — not reconstructed months later from memory. They impose almost no process and yet, accumulated over a project’s life, they become its architectural memory: the answer to the question every team eventually asks, “why on earth did we build it this way?”

It helps to place the ADR beside its near relative, the RFC (“request for comments”). The two are often confused because both are text documents that capture design thinking, but they sit at different points in a decision’s life. An RFC is a proposal circulated before a decision is made — a longer, discursive document inviting debate, exploring options at length, and gathering consensus. An ADR is the record of the decision after it is taken — terse, settled, and immutable. A healthy workflow frequently uses both in sequence: a substantial change begins as an RFC where the alternatives are argued out, and once the dust settles the conclusion is distilled into a one-page ADR that becomes the permanent, scannable entry in the log. The RFC preserves the conversation; the ADR preserves the verdict.

At organisational scale, decision records raise a governance question that single teams can ignore: at what level does a decision belong? A choice that binds one service — which test framework, which serialization format — is a team-local ADR. A choice that binds many teams — the standard authentication mechanism, the approved cloud regions, the house rule that all inter-service calls are asynchronous — is an organisational decision that deserves its own cross-cutting log, often owned by an architecture guild or a lightweight review body. Tooling has grown to match: projects such as Log4brains publish a repository’s ADRs as a searchable static website with a timeline, turning a folder of Markdown into a browsable decision history, while the same records can simultaneously feed section 9 of an arc42 document. The mechanism is deliberately humble, but its reach is wide: from one engineer’s choice of library to a multinational’s technology strategy, the same five headings do the work.

Sheet 08 · Tooling & as-code

Diagrams that survive the next sprint

A framework is only as useful as the documents it produces are current, and the perennial failure of architecture documentation is staleness: a beautiful diagram drawn once in a drawing tool, never updated, quietly diverging from reality until it actively misleads. The modern answer is diagrams-as-code — describing diagrams and models in plain text, kept in version control beside the source, rendered automatically. Text diffs cleanly, reviews in a pull request, and lives where developers already work, which is the only place documentation reliably gets maintained.

The most architecturally significant tool in this space is Structurizr, created by Simon Brown specifically to support the C4 model.[24] Its key idea is a departure from ordinary diagramming: instead of drawing diagrams, you define a single model of your system — the people, systems, containers, and components, declared once in a text DSL — and then create multiple views onto that one model. Because every diagram is a projection of the same underlying definition, the context, container, and component diagrams cannot contradict one another, and renaming an element updates everywhere it appears. This “model-once, view-many” approach is the natural technical expression of everything Sheet 02 argued.

For teams that want C4 without adopting a dedicated platform, C4-PlantUML brings the model’s notation to PlantUML, the long-established text-to-diagram engine, via a set of ready-made macros. Mermaid has become especially popular because it renders text-defined diagrams directly inside Markdown and is natively supported by GitHub, GitLab, and many wikis — so a diagram defined in a fenced code block appears, rendered, right in the README, with no separate tool at all.[25] Mermaid now includes dedicated C4 diagram support alongside its flowchart, sequence, and class diagrams.

Choosing a tool without overthinking it

A rough guide: reach for Mermaid when you want diagrams embedded painlessly in Markdown and your needs are modest; C4-PlantUML when you want more control and PlantUML’s maturity; Structurizr when you are serious about C4 and want one model driving many consistent, navigable views; and a commercial tool such as IcePanel when a large organisation needs interactive, explorable models that non-developers will browse. For enterprise modelling in ArchiMate, the free, open-source Archi is the standard starting point.

Two cautions keep tool choice in proportion. First, the tool is downstream of the thinking: no renderer improves a muddled architecture or supplies missing rationale — it only makes a clear one cheaper to maintain. Second, the best tool is the one your team will actually keep using, which usually means the one closest to the code and the existing workflow. A perfectly maintained Mermaid diagram in the README beats an elaborate model in a platform no one logs into. Whatever the choice, the governing principle holds: treat documentation like code — version it, review it, and where possible generate it — so that it evolves with the system instead of decaying away from it.

The logical endpoint of the as-code philosophy is to fold the documentation into the build itself. Once a model lives in text, a continuous-integration pipeline can render its diagrams on every commit and publish them automatically, so the picture a reader sees is never older than the last merge. The same pipeline can do something more ambitious: check the code against the documented architecture. This is the idea behind architecture fitness functions — automated tests that fail the build when the implementation drifts from its intended structure. Tools such as ArchUnit (for the JVM), NetArchTest (for .NET), and dependency-cruiser (for JavaScript) let a team encode rules like “the domain layer must not import the web layer” or “nothing outside the persistence package may touch the database driver” as executable assertions. Structurizr can perform analogous model-based checks against a declared C4 model. The effect is to close the gap that dooms most documentation: instead of a diagram that describes an architecture and slowly lies as the code moves on, the rules enforce the architecture, and a violation breaks the build the moment it is introduced — turning the architecture description from a passive artefact into an active guardrail.

Sheet 09 · Choosing & combining

Assembling your own kit

The single most important realisation about everything in this guide is that these frameworks are not rivals competing for a single slot. They operate at different layers and answer different questions, and the strongest documentation practices combine several deliberately. Asking “C4 or arc42 or ADRs?” is like asking “floor plan or building code or change order?” — the professional answer is “all three, each for its purpose.” The schedule below lays the options side by side so the complementarity is visible at a glance.

A sensible default stack

For the great majority of software teams — those documenting a system rather than running an enterprise-architecture programme — a particular combination has emerged as a pragmatic, low-ceremony default, and it is worth naming explicitly. Use arc42 as the overall document structure, so every system’s documentation has the same twelve familiar sections. Fill the structural sections (context, building blocks, deployment) with C4 diagrams, drawn as code so they stay current. Record the significant choices as ADRs in the repository, which populate arc42’s decision section. Reach for 4+1’s discipline or the richer Rozanski–Woods viewpoints and perspectives when a system’s concurrency, operational, or security concerns demand more than the defaults capture. That single stack — arc42 + C4 + ADRs — covers structure, visualisation, and rationale with almost no wasted effort.

Document for the reader who will maintain this system at 3 a.m. two years from now, knowing nothing you know today.

— the only audience that ultimately matters

Principles that outlast any framework

Whichever kit you assemble, a few principles decide whether the documentation earns its keep. Let the audience choose the view: the whole multi-view tradition exists because different stakeholders have different concerns, so always ask who will read a given artefact and what decision it must support — an unread diagram is pure cost. Keep it alive or let it go: documentation that lags reality is worse than none, because it misleads with an air of authority; favour as-code, version-controlled, generated-where-possible artefacts over polished pictures no one updates. Record rationale relentlessly: the structure is recoverable from the code in a pinch, but the why is gone the moment the people are, which is exactly the gap ADRs exist to fill.

Above all, right-size the effort. A two-person startup needs a one-page context diagram and a folder of ADRs, not a thirty-six-cell Zachman matrix; a multinational coordinating hundreds of systems genuinely needs TOGAF’s governance and a formal modelling language. The frameworks in this guide span that entire range deliberately, and maturity as an architect lies not in mastering the heaviest one but in choosing the lightest set that actually answers your stakeholders’ questions. Start small, document the decisions that hurt to lose, draw the maps people will actually read — and add formality only when the cost of not having it becomes real. An architecture you can see is an architecture you can defend, reason about, and safely change; that, in the end, is the entire point of writing any of it down.
