01 · Orientation

The calculus of belief

Suppose a disease affects one person in a hundred, and a test for it is genuinely good: it flags 99% of people who have the disease and wrongly flags only 5% of those who do not. You test positive. What is the probability you are ill? The intuitive answer — somewhere near 99% — is wrong by a wide margin. Of every 10,000 people, 100 are ill and about 99 of them test positive; but 9,900 are healthy and about 495 of them test positive too. Among the roughly 594 positive tests, only 99 mark real illness. The probability you are ill is 99 / 594 ≈ 17%.

Nothing in that calculation is controversial, yet the gap between 17% and the felt answer of 99% is the reason Bayesian reasoning is worth learning. The test's accuracy is only half the story; the other half is how rare the disease was to begin with — the prior. Bayesian statistics is the discipline of combining the two halves correctly, and of doing so not just for medical tests but for any quantity we wish to learn from data: a physical constant, a click-through rate, the mass of a planet, the bias of a coin.

The move that makes this possible is philosophical before it is mathematical. In the Bayesian view a probability is a degree of belief — a numerical statement of how strongly the available evidence supports a proposition — rather than a long-run frequency of repeatable events.1,2 Richard Cox showed that any measure of plausibility obeying a few consistency requirements must follow the ordinary rules of probability, so that probability theory is, in effect, the unique extension of logic to uncertain propositions.3,1 Two centuries earlier Laplace had already put it plainly: probability is, at bottom, good sense reduced to calculation.4 The rule that does the reducing is Bayes' theorem.

P(θ | D) = P(D | θ) · P(θ) P(D)
posterior — belief about θ after seeing the data likelihood — how well each θ explains the data prior — belief about θ before the data evidence — a normalising constant, P(D) = ∫ P(D | θ) P(θ) dθ

Read left to right, the equation says that your updated belief about an unknown θ is your prior belief reweighted by how well each value of θ predicted what you actually saw, then rescaled so the result is a proper probability distribution. The animation above shows the mechanism in miniature: a flat prior over a coin's bias, knowing nothing, is sharpened flip by flip into a confident posterior as evidence accrues. The remainder of this article unpacks the four objects in that plate, the closed-form shortcuts that make some of them trivial, the honest way to report the answer as an interval, and the computational engine — Markov chain Monte Carlo — that makes the whole framework work when the shortcuts run out.5

02 · History

From a country minister to the bomb

The theorem is named for the Reverend Thomas Bayes (c. 1701–1761), a Presbyterian minister and amateur mathematician elected a Fellow of the Royal Society in 1742. He never published his central idea. After his death his friend Richard Price found the manuscript among his papers, added an introduction and an appendix, and communicated it to the Royal Society; it appeared in 1763 as An Essay towards solving a Problem in the Doctrine of Chances.6,7 Bayes had asked an inverse question that was new: not “given a known cause, how probable is each effect?” but the reverse — given observed effects, how probable is each possible cause? This is the problem of learning parameters from data, and it is the problem the theorem solves.8

The idea might have languished had Pierre-Simon Laplace not rediscovered it, independently and in far more general form, in a 1774 memoir on the probability of causes. Laplace turned inverse probability into a working instrument, applying it to problems from the sex ratio of births to the masses of the planets, and codifying it in his monumental Théorie analytique des probabilités of 1812 and the popular Essai philosophique of 1814.9,10,4 So thoroughly did Laplace develop the subject that the historian Stephen Stigler has argued the method might more justly bear his name; for over a century it was known simply as “inverse probability” and it dominated statistical thinking.11,8

Then came a long eclipse. In the early twentieth century Ronald Fisher, and later Jerzy Neyman and Egon Pearson, built an alternative — the frequentist framework of significance tests, estimators, and confidence intervals — on the premise that probabilities are objective frequencies, not degrees of belief.12 Fisher in particular was hostile to priors, and inverse probability fell so far from fashion that a leading Bayesian text of the era was written against the current; the approach was, as one modern history puts it, in danger of becoming a relic.13,14 Strikingly, the very adjective “Bayesian” did not enter common statistical usage until around the middle of the twentieth century, nearly two hundred years after the Essay.14

A handful of thinkers kept the flame. Frank Ramsey (1926) and Bruno de Finetti (1937) gave subjective probability rigorous foundations, deriving the probability axioms from the requirement that one's bets be coherent, and — in de Finetti's celebrated representation theorem — showing how beliefs about exchangeable observations imply the existence of an underlying parameter with a prior.15,2 Leonard Savage (1954) welded probability and utility into a complete axiomatic theory of rational decision.16 On the objective wing, the geophysicist Harold Jeffreys (1939) built a Bayesian program for the physical sciences, introducing a rule for deriving non-informative priors that are invariant under reparameterisation — the Jeffreys prior — and a systematic treatment of Bayesian hypothesis testing via Bayes factors.17,13 Behind the scenes, Bayesian sequential reasoning even helped break wartime ciphers at Bletchley Park, a secret history recounted decades later.18

What finally ended the exile was not a new argument but new hardware. The obstacle had always been the denominator: the evidence P(D) is an integral over the whole parameter space, and for all but the simplest models it cannot be done in closed form. The escape route was invented for an entirely different purpose. Recovering from illness in 1946 and idly playing solitaire, Stanislaw Ulam wondered whether the odds of winning could be estimated by simply dealing many hands and counting — and realised the same trick could attack the neutron-diffusion calculations of the hydrogen-bomb project. With John von Neumann he developed it on the first electronic computers at Los Alamos, and Nicholas Metropolis gave it the name Monte Carlo, after the casino where Ulam's uncle gambled.19,18

From that seed grew the machinery this article ends with. Metropolis and colleagues (1953) published an algorithm for simulating physical systems that, decades on, would become the workhorse of Bayesian computation; W. K. Hastings (1970) generalised it into a tool for statistics; Stuart and Donald Geman (1984) introduced Gibbs sampling for image restoration, borrowing the name of the physicist Josiah Willard Gibbs; and in 1990 Alan Gelfand and Adrian Smith showed that these Markov-chain methods could crack the Bayesian integrals that had blocked the field for two centuries, igniting the modern era.20,21,22,23 Software soon put the power in every analyst's hands. The rest of this piece is the anatomy of the idea that waited so long for its engine.

03 · Anatomy

Prior, likelihood, posterior

Return to the plate. Three of its four pieces are objects you specify or compute; the fourth is bookkeeping. Understanding each in turn is most of the conceptual work.

The prior, P(θ), is a probability distribution over the unknown before you look at the current data. It encodes what earlier evidence, physical constraints, or domain knowledge already imply — that a coin is probably close to fair, that a rate must lie between 0 and 1, that a length cannot be negative. The likelihood, P(D | θ), is the probability of the data you observed, computed for each candidate value of θ. It is a function of θ with the data held fixed, and it is not a probability distribution over θ: it need not integrate to one, and it carries no notion of prior plausibility. The posterior, P(θ | D), is the object we want — the distribution over θ that results from combining the two.5,24

The evidence, P(D), is the probability of the data averaged over all values of θ weighted by the prior. It does not depend on θ, so within a single model it is merely the constant that makes the posterior integrate to one. That lets us write the theorem in the form actually used in practice, dropping the constant:

P(θ | D) ∝ P(D | θ) × P(θ)
posterior is proportional to likelihood times prior

Geometrically, the likelihood reshapes the prior. Wherever a value of θ both was plausible beforehand and explains the data well, the posterior is high; wherever either factor is small, the posterior is suppressed. Figure 1 shows the three curves for a coin observed to land heads 13 times in 18 tosses under a mild prior. The posterior sits between prior and likelihood and is narrower than either — two imperfect sources of information combine into a sharper one.25

Everything downstream depends on the prior, which is the framework's most debated feature. A prior can be informative, concentrating belief where strong prior knowledge exists; weakly informative, ruling out the absurd while letting the data dominate; or reference / non-informative, deliberately as neutral as the problem allows, of which the Jeffreys prior is the classic invariant example.17,5 The frequentist objection — that priors inject subjectivity into science — has a standard Bayesian answer: every analysis embeds assumptions, and the prior at least makes them explicit and auditable, while weakly informative priors demonstrably stabilise estimates that maximum likelihood would send to extremes on sparse data.26,25 A second foundational point, the likelihood principle, states that once the data are in hand, all the evidence they carry about θ is contained in the likelihood function; two experiments yielding proportional likelihoods should license the same inference.

One property of the theorem deserves emphasis because it drives the next section. Bayesian updating is sequential: feed in the first batch of data and the posterior you obtain becomes the prior for the second batch; the final result is identical to updating on all the data at once. Belief flows forward, each day's conclusion becoming the next day's starting point. When the prior and likelihood have compatible mathematical forms, that flow can be computed by hand — which is where we turn next.

04 · Closed forms

Conjugate priors: updating as arithmetic

For certain pairings of prior and likelihood, the posterior belongs to the same family as the prior, and Bayes' theorem collapses from an integral into a formula. Such a prior is called conjugate to the likelihood, and the payoff is that updating reduces to adjusting a few numbers. Before cheap computation, conjugacy was often the only route to a usable posterior, and much of decision theory was built on it.27,5

Beta–Binomial: the rate of a rare event

Consider learning a probability θ — a coin's bias, a conversion rate, a cure rate. The natural prior is the Beta distribution, Beta(α, β), whose density is proportional to θα−1(1−θ)β−1 on the interval [0, 1]. Observe n trials with s successes and f = n−s failures; the Binomial likelihood is proportional to θs(1−θ)f. Multiplying prior by likelihood simply adds the exponents:

Beta(α, β) × s successes, f failures ⟶ Beta(α + s,  β + f)
the Beta prior is conjugate to the Binomial likelihood

The update is pure arithmetic, and it has a vivid interpretation: α and β act as prior pseudo-counts — imagined successes and failures the prior contributes before any real data. A Beta(2, 2) prior behaves like two phantom heads and two phantom tails, gently favouring rates near one-half; the observed counts are then added on top. The posterior mean, (α+s) / (α+β+n), is exactly a weighted average of the prior mean and the observed proportion, with the data's weight growing as n grows. Figure 2 shows a Beta(2, 2) prior sharpening into ever-tighter posteriors as the sample size climbs and the estimate concentrates near the true rate.5,25

Because there is no integral to evaluate, the whole computation is a few lines of code. The listing below takes 40 trials with 26 successes under a Beta(2, 2) prior and reports the posterior, its mean, its mode (the maximum-a-posteriori estimate), and a 95% credible interval — the interval we will scrutinise in the next section.

beta_binomial.pypython
import numpy as np
from scipy.stats import beta

# Prior: Beta(a0, b0). A weakly-informative Beta(2, 2) favours no extreme rates.
a0, b0 = 2.0, 2.0

# Data: 40 trials, 26 "successes" (e.g. conversions, heads, cures).
n, s = 40, 26
f = n - s                       # failures

# Conjugacy: the posterior is Beta(a0 + s, b0 + f) -- a closed-form update.
a_post, b_post = a0 + s, b0 + f

post_mean = a_post / (a_post + b_post)
post_mode = (a_post - 1) / (a_post + b_post - 2)      # MAP estimate
lo, hi = beta.ppf([0.025, 0.975], a_post, b_post)     # 95% credible interval

print(f"posterior  Beta({a_post:.0f}, {b_post:.0f})")
print(f"mean = {post_mean:.3f}   mode = {post_mode:.3f}   CI = [{lo:.3f}, {hi:.3f}]")

# posterior  Beta(28, 16)
# mean = 0.636   mode = 0.643   CI = [0.491, 0.770]
Closed-form conjugate update: no sampling, no integral.

Normal–Normal: combining measurements

The second canonical case governs continuous quantities measured with Gaussian noise. If the data have a Normal likelihood with known variance and we place a Normal prior on the unknown mean μ, the posterior for μ is again Normal. The tidy way to state the update is in terms of precision — the reciprocal of variance, τ = 1/σ2, a measure of certainty. Precisions add, and the posterior mean is the precision-weighted average of the prior mean and the data mean:

μpost = τ0 μ0 + τ n x̄ τ0 + τ n , τpost = τ0+ τ n
certainty adds; the mean is pulled toward whichever source is more precise

The formula behaves exactly as intuition demands. A confident prior (large τ0) holds the estimate near μ0; abundant or precise data (large τn) pull it toward the sample mean x̄; and the posterior is always more precise than either input, because two independent pieces of evidence are worth more than one. Figure 3 shows a broad prior and a sharper likelihood merging into a posterior that is narrower than both and located between their centres.5

The general pattern — and its limits

These two examples are instances of a deep regularity. Every likelihood in the exponential family — which includes the Bernoulli, Binomial, Poisson, Exponential, and Normal distributions — admits a conjugate prior, and Persi Diaconis and Donald Ylvisaker proved that such conjugate priors are precisely the ones for which the posterior mean is a linear function of the sufficient statistic.28 The common cases are worth memorising.

Conjugacy is elegant, exact, and — for realistic models — usually unavailable. The moment a model has several interacting parameters, a hierarchy of groups, or a likelihood outside the exponential family, no conjugate prior exists and the denominator integral returns in full force, now over many dimensions. For most of the twentieth century that was where Bayesian analysis stopped. The methods that let it continue are the subject of the section after next.5,29

05 · Reporting uncertainty

Credible intervals versus confidence intervals

A posterior distribution is the complete answer, but people need a summary, and the usual one is an interval. Here Bayesian and frequentist statistics offer intervals that look identical on the page and mean profoundly different things — a difference that is among the most widely misunderstood in all of statistics.

A Bayesian credible interval is disarmingly direct. A 95% credible interval is simply a range that contains 95% of the posterior probability, so one may say, without qualification, that the probability the parameter lies in this interval, given the data, is 0.95.5,25 There is a choice of which 95% to report: the equal-tailed interval leaves 2.5% of the mass in each tail, while the highest-posterior-density interval takes the shortest range that captures the mass and thus always includes the most probable values. The left panel of Figure 4 shades the central 95% of a posterior; the statement beneath it is a genuine probability about the unknown.

A frequentist confidence interval makes a subtler promise. Following Neyman's 1937 construction, a 95% confidence procedure is one guaranteeing that, across hypothetical repetitions of the experiment, 95% of the intervals it produces will contain the fixed, unknown true value.12 The 95% is a property of the procedure over the long run, not of any particular interval it yields. Once you have collected your data and computed one interval, that interval either contains the true value or it does not; there is no probability left to assign to it. The right panel of Figure 4 makes the point concrete: twenty intervals from twenty hypothetical repetitions, nineteen catching the true value and one missing — but for the one interval you actually obtained, you cannot say which kind you hold.

The temptation to read a confidence interval as though it were a credible interval — to believe that a computed 95% interval has a 95% chance of containing the parameter — is nearly universal, and it is a genuine error. Richard Morey and colleagues catalogue how the “confidence” in an interval licenses none of the belief statements practitioners routinely attach to it, and how the same procedure can yield intervals that are absurd on their face while remaining perfectly valid by the frequentist criterion.30 The two intervals answer different questions: the credible interval asks what the data tell us to believe about the parameter, the confidence interval asks how often a rule succeeds across imagined datasets.1

None of this means the numbers always differ. With a flat or weak prior on a simple, symmetric problem, the Bayesian credible interval and the frequentist confidence interval frequently coincide to the printed digits — which is exactly why the conceptual gap is so easy to overlook. They part company when the prior carries real information, when the model is hierarchical or the likelihood irregular, or when the sample is small; and even when they agree numerically, only one of them supports the sentence most people want to say out loud.5,1

06 · The engine

When conjugacy runs out: Markov chain Monte Carlo

Strip away the special cases and the obstacle is stark. We can always evaluate the numerator of Bayes' theorem — likelihood times prior — at any value of θ. What we usually cannot do is the denominator, or the further integrals that a posterior mean, a credible interval, or a prediction all require. In a model with dozens or thousands of parameters, those integrals are over a space of dozens or thousands of dimensions, and no quadrature rule survives that.5,29

The Monte Carlo insight is to replace integration with sampling. If we could draw many independent values θ(1), …, θ(M) from the posterior, then any expectation — a mean, a variance, the probability of a region — is estimated by simply averaging over the draws, with error shrinking like 1/√M regardless of the dimension.19 The catch is circular: to sample the posterior we would seem to need the very normalising constant we cannot compute.

Markov chain Monte Carlo breaks the circle with a beautiful sleight of hand. Instead of drawing independent samples, construct a Markov chain — a random walk whose next step depends only on the current one — engineered so that its long-run stationary distribution is exactly the posterior. Run the walk long enough to forget its starting point, and its successive states, though correlated, are draws from the posterior. Crucially, the construction depends only on ratios of the posterior density at pairs of points, and in any such ratio the unknown constant P(D) cancels. The obstacle simply disappears.21,29

The Metropolis–Hastings algorithm

The oldest and most general recipe is Metropolis–Hastings. From the current point θ, propose a candidate θ′ from some proposal distribution — often a small Gaussian step. Then accept the candidate with probability

a = min(1,  p(θ′) q(θ | θ′) p(θ) q(θ′ | θ) )
accept upward moves; accept downward moves only sometimes

where p is the unnormalised posterior and q the proposal density. If the candidate is more probable than the current point the move is always taken; if less probable it is taken with a probability equal to the ratio, and otherwise the chain stays put and records the current point again. For a symmetric proposal the q terms cancel and the rule is just min(1, p(θ′)/p(θ)). This acceptance rule is what guarantees, through a condition called detailed balance, that the posterior is the chain's stationary distribution.20,21 Figure 5 shows such a walk finding and then exploring a correlated posterior, alongside a trace of one parameter settling from its arbitrary start into a stable band.

The algorithm is remarkably short to implement. The listing below infers a coin's bias with no conjugacy at all — only the log-likelihood, a Uniform prior, and a random-walk proposal — and, reassuringly, its answer (posterior mean 0.643) matches the exact conjugate result for the same data, a self-check that the sampler is exploring the right distribution.

metropolis.pypython
import numpy as np

rng = np.random.default_rng(20260717)
n, s = 40, 26                   # 26 successes in 40 trials
f = n - s

def log_posterior(theta):
    """Log posterior for a coin's bias under a Uniform(0, 1) prior."""
    if theta <= 0.0 or theta >= 1.0:
        return -np.inf                    # zero prior density outside (0, 1)
    return s * np.log(theta) + f * np.log(1.0 - theta)      # (+ constant)

def metropolis(log_p, init=0.5, steps=40_000, proposal_sd=0.05):
    x, lp = init, log_p(init)
    chain = np.empty(steps)
    accepts = 0
    for t in range(steps):
        x_prop = x + rng.normal(0.0, proposal_sd)          # symmetric proposal
        lp_prop = log_p(x_prop)
        # accept with probability min(1, p_prop / p_current), in log-space
        if np.log(rng.random()) < lp_prop - lp:
            x, lp = x_prop, lp_prop
            accepts += 1
        chain[t] = x
    return chain, accepts / steps

chain, acc = metropolis(log_posterior)
draws = chain[5_000:]                     # discard the warm-up ("burn-in")

print(f"acceptance rate : {acc:.2f}")
print(f"posterior mean  : {draws.mean():.3f}")
print(f"95% credible    : [{np.quantile(draws, .025):.3f}, {np.quantile(draws, .975):.3f}]")

# acceptance rate : 0.79
# posterior mean  : 0.643
# 95% credible    : [0.492, 0.779]
A general-purpose sampler in ~20 lines; it recovers the conjugate answer.

Gibbs sampling

When a model's full conditional distributions — the distribution of each parameter given the current values of all the others and the data — can themselves be sampled, a special case becomes available that needs no accept/reject step at all. Gibbs sampling cycles through the parameters one at a time, replacing each with a fresh draw from its full conditional; every proposal is accepted by construction. Introduced by Geman and Geman for restoring noisy images and named for the physicist J. W. Gibbs, it was the method Gelfand and Smith used to show that Bayesian computation was tractable, and it powered the first generation of general-purpose Bayesian software.22,23

gibbs.txtsketch
initialise  θ = (θ₁, …, θₖ)
repeat each sweep:
    for i in 1 .. k:
        draw  θₜ  from  p(θₜ | all other θ, data)
collect  θ  after a burn-in period

Hamiltonian Monte Carlo and NUTS

A random walk explores slowly: in high dimensions it diffuses through the posterior like a drunkard, and successive samples are highly correlated. Hamiltonian Monte Carlo replaces blind steps with informed ones by borrowing the mathematics of physical motion. It treats the negative log-posterior as an energy landscape, gives the parameter a random “momentum,” and simulates the resulting frictionless trajectory, so that proposals travel far across the distribution while rarely being rejected. The method was invented for lattice quantum chromodynamics by Duane and colleagues in 1987 and imported into statistics by Radford Neal.31,32 Its one awkward tuning knob — how long to simulate each trajectory — was automated by Matthew Hoffman and Andrew Gelman's No-U-Turn Sampler, which runs each trajectory until it starts to double back, and which now drives the most widely used Bayesian software.33,34

Trust, but verify

Because MCMC yields a dependent, approximate sample rather than the exact posterior, diagnostics are not optional. The early portion of each chain, still influenced by its starting point, is discarded as burn-in (or warm-up). A trace plot of a parameter against iteration should look like a featureless, well-mixed band — a “fuzzy caterpillar” — not a slow drift. The standard convergence statistic is the potential scale reduction factor R̂, introduced by Gelman and Rubin: it runs several chains from dispersed starting points and compares the variance within chains to the variance between them, a ratio that should fall to essentially one if the chains have converged on the same distribution; a recent rank-normalised version sharpens the test.35,36 Finally, because the draws are correlated, the effective sample size — the number of independent draws the chain is worth — is smaller than the raw count and is the honest measure of how much information the run actually contains.29 The intuition to carry away is simple: a well-run chain spends time in each region of parameter space in proportion to that region's posterior probability, and the collected samples are the posterior, ready to be summarised however the question demands.

07 · Practice

Tools of the trade

Few practitioners write samplers by hand any more. The dominant paradigm is probabilistic programming: you declare the model — priors, likelihood, and the observed data — in a high-level language, and the system compiles it and runs state-of-the-art inference for you. Two tools lead the field. Stan is a declarative modelling language, compiled to C++ and sampled with the No-U-Turn variant of Hamiltonian Monte Carlo, callable from R, Python, and Julia.34 PyMC lets you build models directly in Python objects and samples them with the same class of gradient-based algorithm.37 They inherit a lineage that runs through the Gibbs-based BUGS and JAGS projects of the 1990s and now includes JAX- and PyTorch-based systems such as NumPyro and Pyro, and TensorFlow Probability. The two listings below express the coin model of this article in each tool; the contrast with the hand-written sampler is the point — the model is stated, the inference is delegated.

model_pymc.pypython
import pymc as pm

n, s = 40, 26                          # 26 successes in 40 trials

with pm.Model() as model:
    # Prior belief about the unknown rate.
    theta = pm.Beta("theta", alpha=2.0, beta=2.0)
    # Likelihood: the success count is Binomial(n, theta).
    pm.Binomial("obs", n=n, p=theta, observed=s)
    # Draw from the posterior with NUTS (gradient-based Hamiltonian Monte Carlo).
    idata = pm.sample(2000, tune=1000, chains=4, target_accept=0.9)

print(pm.summary(idata, var_names=["theta"]))

#         mean     sd  hdi_3%  hdi_97%  ess_bulk  r_hat
# theta  0.636  0.071   0.505    0.769      6021    1.0
Declare the model; PyMC compiles it and samples with NUTS.
model.stanstan
data {
  int<lower=0> n;                 // number of trials
  int<lower=0, upper=n> s;        // number of successes
}
parameters {
  real<lower=0, upper=1> theta;   // the rate we want to learn
}
model {
  theta ~ beta(2, 2);             // prior
  s ~ binomial(n, theta);         // likelihood
}
The same model in Stan's declarative modelling language.

Getting a number out is only the start; trusting it requires a discipline now codified as the Bayesian workflow.26 Before fitting, prior predictive checks simulate fake datasets from the prior alone and ask whether they look remotely plausible — a prior that predicts impossible data is a prior to fix. After fitting, one inspects the convergence diagnostics of the previous section, then runs posterior predictive checks: simulate new data from the fitted model and compare them with what was actually observed, since a model that cannot reproduce the features of its own training data has no business making predictions. The loop — propose, fit, criticise, revise — is the real practice of applied Bayesian statistics.25

Choosing between competing models is its own topic. The classical Bayesian instrument is the Bayes factor, the ratio of the models' evidence terms, which quantifies how much the data shift the odds from one model to another but is notoriously sensitive to the priors on each side.38 The modern default for predictive comparison is cross-validation estimated efficiently from the posterior samples — the widely applicable information criterion and Pareto-smoothed importance-sampling leave-one-out — which target a model's expected performance on new data rather than its fit to old.39

08 · End to end

A worked example

Put the pieces together on a small but real question. An online store runs a new checkout design, variant B, and records 26 purchases from 40 visitors. What should we believe about its true conversion rate, and how likely is it to beat the historical baseline of 50%?

The quantity of interest is a probability, so the Beta–Binomial machinery applies directly. Choosing a weakly informative Beta(2, 2) prior — two phantom purchases, two phantom abandonments, mildly sceptical of extreme rates — and updating on 26 successes and 14 failures gives, by the arithmetic of conjugacy, a Beta(28, 16) posterior. That single distribution answers every question we might ask, and none of the answers requires an integral we cannot do by hand.

posteriorBeta(28, 16)
posterior mean0.636
95% credible interval[0.491, 0.770]
P(rate > 0.5 ∣ data)0.967

Now read the numbers as Bayesian statements, because that is their power. Our best single estimate of the conversion rate is about 64%, and — unlike a confidence interval — we may say directly that there is a 95% probability the true rate lies between 49% and 77%, given the data and prior. Most useful of all, the decision-relevant quantity is a probability we can compute exactly: integrating the posterior above one-half gives a 96.7% probability that variant B genuinely converts better than the baseline. That is precisely the sentence a stakeholder wants — “how likely is B to be better?” — and it falls straight out of the posterior, where a frequentist analysis would return a p-value that answers a different and more slippery question.5,25

For this problem conjugacy delivered the answer in closed form, but the identical posterior is what the hand-written Metropolis sampler and the two probabilistic-programming models earlier in this article all target, and their agreement (a posterior mean of 0.643 from the sampler against 0.636 exactly) is a small demonstration that the general engine reproduces the special case. The reason to reach for that engine is that the moment we add a second variant, an informative prior from last quarter's data, or a hierarchy across product categories, the closed form vanishes and MCMC becomes the only way through — while every interpretive sentence above continues to hold unchanged. And it is the same machinery, end to end, that resolved the medical-test puzzle we opened with: a prior, a likelihood, and one rule for combining them.

09 · Coda

Belief, calibrated

Bayesian statistics is, in the end, a machine for changing your mind in the right proportion. It insists that you state what you believed before, in the open where it can be argued with; it combines that belief with evidence by a single rule that admits no exceptions; and it hands back not a verdict but a distribution — a full account of what remains uncertain, from which any probability you need to act on can be read off. Its two-century exile was never a failure of the mathematics, which was settled by 1812, but a failure of arithmetic: the integrals were simply too hard until the computer, by way of the hydrogen bomb and a game of solitaire, made them easy.18,23

The framework is powerful but not automatic, and honesty about its failure modes is part of using it well. Priors matter, and a confidently wrong prior yields a confidently wrong posterior; the marginal likelihood remains genuinely hard to compute; MCMC returns an approximation that must be checked, not trusted on sight; and a well-sampled model can still be the wrong model. But against those costs stands a rare virtue: an analysis whose assumptions are explicit, whose output is a probability you can quantify, communicate, and revise as the next datum arrives.26,1 Laplace's verdict has worn well. Probability really is nothing but good sense reduced to calculation — and Bayesian inference is the calculation.4
