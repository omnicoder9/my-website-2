The Art of the Conversation

What does the evidence say about converting individuals through dialogue — from the Cube of Truth to the kitchen table?

By Nicholas Knapp May 4, 2026

When Tolstoy wrote that everyone thinks of changing the world but no one thinks of changing himself, he could not have anticipated the modern vegan activist — standing on a rain-slicked pavement in all black, holding a laptop screen showing footage of a slaughterhouse, asking strangers: How does this make you feel? Interpersonal advocacy is the oldest tool in the activist's kit. It is also, in many ways, the most difficult to measure, and the most widely misunderstood.

This article surveys the evidence on one-on-one and small-group vegan advocacy: what works, what backfires, how psychology can sharpen our conversations, and which resources can guide your practice. It is written for the advocate who wants depth over scale — who is willing to sit with a friend for an hour knowing they may never reach a million people, but believing that one person, genuinely moved, is worth a great deal.

Why Interpersonal Advocacy Matters

There is a persistent assumption in activist circles that reach equals impact — that the podcast with ten thousand listeners must matter more than the conversation with one colleague. The evidence doesn't quite support this.

A 2019 survey by Vomadlife — one of the largest surveys of vegans' reported transition catalysts — found that documentary films and conversations with family and friends were among the most commonly cited first triggers for seriously considering veganism, far outpacing street activism as an initial spark.1 Separately, research published in npj Climate Action (2024) used a pre-registered randomised controlled trial with 1,213 participants and found that encouraging people to involve family and friends in a 30-day plant-based challenge produced lasting reductions in animal product consumption at the 3-month follow-up — whereas the individual-only condition did not maintain the same staying power.2

Research Snapshot

A 2024 randomised controlled trial (n=1,213) found that both individual and socially-framed app-based plant-based challenges reduced animal product consumption by 16–17% versus control. Crucially, only the social condition — where participants were encouraged to involve friends and family — showed a statistically significant lasting effect at 3 months.2

Associated greenhouse gas emissions fell by 21–24% across both groups, with the social treatment again demonstrating greater durability.2

Qualitative research from Springer's Discover Sustainability journal (2025) found that peer networks — friends, roommates, classmates — were frequently described as the "catalyst for action" among participants who shifted toward plant-based diets. Singular conversations, described as "turning points," were especially significant during major life transitions such as starting university or moving cities.3 Emotionally charged or judgmental exchanges, by contrast, were found to stall progress or lead to avoidance of the topic altogether.3

The Vegan Society's own commissioned research found that social relationships and health were among the most effective pathways into veganism, and that family relations could serve as both barriers and pathways — not simply opposition.4

Food practices constitute everyday routines which can be resistant to change. Family relations were seen to be both barriers and pathways to veganism. — The Vegan Society Research Report, 2019 4

Street Outreach: The Cube of Truth

Perhaps no form of interpersonal vegan advocacy is as visually distinctive as the Cube of Truth, pioneered by Anonymous for the Voiceless (AV). Founded in Melbourne in April 2016, AV now coordinates over 100,000 volunteers in 375 chapters across 61 countries.5 The format is deliberate: a square of activists in Guy Fawkes masks silently hold screens showing standard animal agriculture footage while unmasked outreachers engage curious onlookers in structured dialogue.

The conversations follow a Socratic arc — beginning with open questions ("Have you seen this before? How does this make you feel?") and moving toward deeper ethical inquiry, guiding participants to articulate their own contradictions rather than having arguments handed to them.6 AV volunteers describe the process explicitly: "We ask questions that basically lead to them making the conclusion for themselves — that this is wrong, and veganism is right."7

What the Evidence Says About Street Outreach

The honest assessment of street outreach as an initial conversion mechanism is sobering. The 2019 Vomadlife survey found that public activism, including Cubes of Truth, was reported by only 1.4% of respondents as the first thing that made them seriously consider veganism — ranking below documentaries, online content, personal conversations, and even leafleting.1

This finding invites nuance rather than dismissal. Street outreach may function less as a first-touch conversion mechanism and more as an acceleration tool — deepening the resolve of people already on the cusp of change. AV's own data noted hundreds of thousands of people who reported "taking veganism seriously" after a Cube encounter,5 and anecdotal accounts from participants frequently describe emotional breakthroughs that preceded longer-term transitions.8

There is also the question of emotional framing. AV's co-founder Paul Bashir has argued that guilt is the primary engine of behaviour change — but this position is not well-supported by the broader psychological literature. A body of research on environmental messaging shows that guilt can inspire constructive action or trigger negative attitudes and even anger, depending on how it is deployed.1 Research on environmental behaviour change published in PLOS ONE found that Motivational Interviewing — which reduces confrontation and builds intrinsic motivation — significantly lowered defensive responses compared to standard persuasion approaches.9 Pride-based appeals have also been shown to outperform guilt in some environmental messaging contexts.1

01

Open with Curiosity

Lead with questions, not arguments. "What do you see here? How does it make you feel?" Letting the person arrive at their own discomfort is more durable than shock-delivered guilt.

02

Socratic Questioning

Guide the person toward their own logical contradictions — between loving animals and eating them — without forcing a conclusion. The realisation that lands hardest is the one they reach themselves.

03

Match Tone to Readiness

Not every passer-by is at the same stage of openness. Someone mid-curiosity requires a different conversation than someone who is defensive. Read the cues and adjust in real time.

04

Close with a Bridge

Always offer a next step: a vegan challenge, a recipe app, a documentary. AV recommends Challenge 22 — a 22-day guided vegan trial with mentor support — as a low-barrier handoff.

Conversations with Friends and Family

If street outreach is the high-profile, low-frequency form of interpersonal advocacy, personal conversations are its quieter, more pervasive counterpart. They are also, by most measures, more effective as first catalysts for dietary change.

The challenge is well-documented: vegans routinely report that family settings are among the most fraught arenas for advocacy. Research drawing on the communication theory of identity found that social support profoundly shaped the experience of vegans navigating dietary difference — and that choosing not to disclose one's veganism to avoid conflict had adverse consequences for both relationship health and communication satisfaction.10

One recurring research finding is that vegan role modelling is among the most quietly powerful forms of influence. A 2025 qualitative study in Discover Sustainability found that friends and roommates who practised plant-based diets helped normalise such choices for others — without a single argument being made.3 An earlier survey found that 80.1% of vegans reported having family or friends who were also vegan, indicating the depth of social network effects on dietary identity.11

People rarely become vegan overnight. Learning about the ethical, emotional and practical implications can be a long adjustment. — The Conversation, 2022 12

Practical Principles for Personal Conversations

Nick Cooney's landmark book Change of Heart: What Psychology Can Teach Us About Spreading Social Change (2010) remains one of the most comprehensive syntheses of persuasion research applied to advocacy.13 Cooney draws on over 80 years of empirical research in social psychology, diffusion studies, and social marketing to identify approaches that produce genuine, lasting attitude change — including the importance of meeting people where they are, avoiding blame, and making requests that feel achievable rather than overwhelming.13

Key evidence-backed principles for personal advocacy conversations include:

Avoid unsolicited moralising. Uninvited ethical lectures reliably trigger psychological reactance — the urge to reassert autonomy by doing the opposite of what you're told. Research on self-determination theory confirms that externally imposed pressure undermines intrinsic motivation for lasting change, while autonomy-supportive communication supports it.14

Use Motivational Interviewing (MI) techniques. MI is a clinical behaviour-change method built on empathy, open-ended questions, and reflective listening. A systematic review of 104 reviews covering MI (including 39 meta-analyses) found evidence for its effectiveness across a range of behaviour change domains.15 Applied to vegan advocacy, MI means prioritising the person's own language, values, and stated reasons for change — and working within those — rather than importing your own urgency.

Share your story, not your verdict. First-person accounts of transition are far less confrontational than ethical judgments. Research on persuasion consistently shows that narrative is more effective than statistics at shifting deeply held values — particularly when the listener identifies with the narrator.13

Think long-term. A single conversation rarely converts. But a friendship in which someone sees you thriving on a plant-based diet, cooking delicious food, not being preachy, and remaining happy — that is a slow, steady, and durable influence. Be the living counterexample to every vegan stereotype.

Psychological Insight

Motivational Interviewing research distinguishes between "change talk" (personal statements in favour of changing) and "sustain talk" (reasons against change). A higher proportion of change talk in a conversation is associated with reduced risk-seeking behaviour and greater follow-through.16

The goal of a good advocacy conversation is not to speak more persuasively yourself — it is to elicit more change talk from the other person. Ask questions that invite them to voice their own reasons for change.

Coaching Individuals Through the Transition

For those already curious or committed to trying veganism, the advocacy role shifts from persuasion to support. Research consistently shows that this practical, logistical scaffolding is crucial to whether dietary change sticks.

Vegan Outreach's evidence-based transition program, 10 Weeks to Vegan, was developed explicitly on the finding that guided veg challenges are highly effective at facilitating lasting change — and has been tested through a randomised controlled trial measuring dietary outcomes.17 Challenge 22, recommended by Anonymous for the Voiceless, offers a similar 22-day mentored trial that provides social accountability alongside practical guidance.5

Transition coaching at the personal level involves:

Nutritional orientation. Help the person understand where protein, iron, B12, omega-3s, and calcium come from on a plant-based diet. Anxiety about nutrition is one of the most commonly reported barriers to transition, and much of it is grounded in misinformation or habit rather than genuine deficiency risk.

Social situation rehearsal. Research identifies social challenges — navigating restaurants, family dinners, colleagues — as among the biggest obstacles to maintaining a vegan lifestyle.18 Walking someone through real scenarios they're likely to encounter ("What do I say when my mum makes chicken and expects me to eat it?") can meaningfully reduce dropout.

Connecting them to community. Peer support is a robust predictor of dietary adherence. Connecting a transitioning person to vegan communities — online or local — provides the social norming and ongoing support that individual advocacy conversations cannot sustain alone.

A Note on Debate and Socratic Dialogue

Debate has a mixed track record in advocacy. When two people enter a conversation as adversaries, cognitive and emotional resources go toward defending existing positions rather than genuinely processing new information. This is why Socratic questioning — in which you guide a person through their own reasoning via careful, non-aggressive questions — tends to outperform head-on argument.

The Socratic method does not assume the other person is wrong and needs to be corrected. It assumes they are capable of reaching ethical clarity themselves, and that your role is to remove the obstacles — contradiction, unexamined assumption, motivated reasoning — that stand in the way. This distinction matters enormously for the emotional tone of a conversation and for whether the person is left feeling respected or attacked.

If we have a discussion with someone, we often use the Socratic method, which means we ask questions that basically lead to them making the conclusion for themselves. — Anonymous for the Voiceless volunteer, 2018 7

Debate does have a place — particularly in structured public forums, podcasts, or online spaces where the audience, rather than the debating partner, is the real target. In those contexts, the clarity and composure with which you hold your position may matter more than whether your interlocutor changes their mind.

Recommended Guides & Resources

  • Change of Heart — Nick Cooney (Lantern Books, 2010) The most comprehensive application of persuasion research to social change advocacy. Essential reading for any serious advocate. Covers social psychology, diffusion theory, and practical messaging.13
  • Motivational Methods for Vegan Advocacy — Dr Casey T. Taft (Vegan Publishers, 2016) Applies clinical psychology — including motivational interviewing — directly to vegan advocacy contexts. Written by a Boston University Professor of Psychiatry.19
  • Effective Vegan Advocacy (EVA) Online Course — CEVA Described by reviewers as "the best resource for one-to-one advocates," this course is rooted in psychological science and covers how to avoid unproductive conversations and increase advocacy impact.20 Available at veganadvocacy.org.
  • 10 Weeks to Vegan — Vegan Outreach An evidence-tested email/app-based transition program to share with people who are ready to try. Covers nutrition, recipes, and social navigation. Tested via randomised controlled trial.17
  • Challenge 22 — Anonymous for the Voiceless A 22-day guided vegan trial with mentor support. The recommended post-conversation referral for AV street outreach participants.5
  • Cube Etiquette & Outreach Training — anonymousforthevoiceless.org AV's official guide for volunteers, including their conversation structure and values framework. Also links to Paul Bashir's outreach workshop video.21
  • Striking at the Roots — Mark Hawthorne (O Books, 2008) A broad practical guide to animal activism, covering a range of tactics including interpersonal outreach. Recommended as preliminary reading before Cooney's Change of Heart.

Leverage Profile

Depth: Very high. One-on-one conversations can produce genuine, lasting ethical transformation.

Scalability: Low. Each meaningful conversation requires significant time and emotional investment.

Best for: Warm relationships, people already curious, and as a complement to mass-reach approaches.

Common Pitfalls

Moralising unprompted triggers reactance — the urge to do the opposite.

Leading with guilt without empathy can produce anger rather than reflection.

Winning the argument but losing the person. Being right matters less than being heard.

Failing to offer a practical next step once someone is moved.

Key Concepts

Motivational Interviewing (MI) — a clinical technique that builds intrinsic motivation through open-ended questions and reflective listening.

Socratic Method — guiding someone to their own conclusions by questioning assumptions rather than asserting truths.

Change Talk — the person's own verbal statements in favour of change; more change talk predicts greater follow-through.

Psychological Reactance — the defensive urge to resist perceived pressure on one's autonomy.
