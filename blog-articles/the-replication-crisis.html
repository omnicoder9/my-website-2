The Decline Effect

When science turned its instruments on itself, a startling share of its landmark findings failed to survive re-examination. The reckoning that followed is still unfolding.

An expert technical briefing 42-min read · 9,271 words 74 references · Vancouver 2010 → present

Around 2011, a quiet methodological anxiety that had simmered in the back rooms of psychology and biomedicine for half a century broke into the open. Within a few years it had a name — the replication crisis — a catalogue of failed re-tests, a wave of retractions, a civil war over statistics, and a generation of researchers asking whether large tracts of their own literature described anything real at all.

§ 01The shape of the problem

Replication is the closest thing experimental science has to a constitution. A claim earns the status of knowledge not because a clever person asserted it, nor because a single dataset cleared a significance threshold, but because the result recurs when independent hands repeat the procedure. If an effect cannot be reproduced, it is — at best — a property of one laboratory on one afternoon, and at worst an artifact of chance, bias, or wishful analysis. The discipline's confidence in itself rested on an untested assumption: that the published record was, in the main, reproducible.

That assumption had never actually been checked at scale. When it finally was, the results were sobering. In 2015 the Open Science Collaboration — a consortium of some 270 researchers coordinated by Brian Nosek's Center for Open Science — published the outcome of a multi-year effort to replicate one hundred studies drawn from three flagship psychology journals. Ninety-seven percent of the original studies had reported a statistically significant result. In the high-powered replications, only thirty-six percent reached significance in the same direction, and the average replication effect was roughly half the size of the original.1 A poll of more than 1,500 researchers conducted by Nature the following year found that about ninety percent acknowledged a reproducibility crisis of some degree, and over half called it "significant."2

None of this came entirely from nowhere. A decade earlier the epidemiologist John Ioannidis had published an essay with a deliberately incendiary title — "Why Most Published Research Findings Are False" — arguing from first principles that, given the base rates, statistical power, and biases prevailing in much of the literature, the majority of published positive claims in many fields should be expected to be wrong.3 At the time the paper was treated as a provocation. The replication projects of the 2010s turned it into something closer to a prediction borne out.

It is worth being precise about vocabulary, because the controversy partly turns on it. Reproducibility in the narrow, computational sense means obtaining the same numbers from the same data and code. Replicability means obtaining consistent results from a new sample collected under the same protocol — a fresh test of the same hypothesis. Robustness means that a finding survives reasonable changes in analytic choices. The crisis is overwhelmingly about the second and third: not arithmetic errors, but findings that evaporate when genuinely re-tested or re-analyzed.4 And a crucial framing point, easy to lose in the alarm: a single failed replication does not, by itself, prove the original false. Replications have their own sampling error, their own infidelities, their own statistical power. The signal is not any individual failure but the pattern — effects shrinking systematically toward zero, original "discoveries" replicating at rates barely better than a coin flip, across hundreds of attempts and multiple disciplines.

That pattern is what this briefing is about: where it came from, the statistical machinery that produced it, the empirical projects that documented it, the fraud cases that ran alongside it, the bitter dispute over what it all meant, and the reform movement — now itself under scrutiny — that grew up in response. Some participants prefer to call the episode not a crisis but a "credibility revolution," emphasizing the methods and transparency it forced into being.5 Both descriptions are accurate. It has been, by turns, an embarrassment and a renaissance.

§ 02The warnings that went unheeded

A crisis with a long fuse

The striking thing about the replication crisis is not that it was unforeseen but that it was foreseen, repeatedly, for decades, by statisticians and methodologists whose warnings were absorbed as ritual caveats and then ignored in practice. The intellectual scaffolding of the critique was almost entirely in place by 1980. What was missing was the empirical demonstration and the collective will to act.

The first beam went up in 1959, when Theodore Sterling examined which results journals actually printed. He found that the overwhelming majority of published articles in several psychology journals reported a statistically significant rejection of the null hypothesis — a proportion so high it could not reflect the true mix of studies being run. The literature, he argued, was a biased sample of the work performed: significant results were published, null results were quietly shelved.6 Two decades later Robert Rosenthal gave the phenomenon its enduring image, the file drawer problem — the unknown mass of unpublished, non-significant studies sitting in researchers' drawers, which could in principle be large enough to render a body of "significant" published findings meaningless.7 Publication bias was not a footnote to the crisis; it was its precondition.

A second, equally damaging beam was the chronic neglect of statistical power. In 1962 Jacob Cohen surveyed a year's worth of articles in the Journal of Abnormal and Social Psychology and computed the studies' ability to detect the effects they were hunting. The median power to detect a medium effect was about one in two; for small effects it was far worse.8 The implication was devastating and almost universally missed: a literature of underpowered studies that nonetheless overflows with significant results is a literature in which something other than honest sampling is generating the positives. Cohen spent thirty years pleading for larger samples and effect-size reporting. In 1994, near the end of his career, he wrote a famously exasperated paper — "The Earth Is Round (p < .05)" — dismantling the cult of the significance threshold and the systematic misinterpretation of the p-value as the probability that the null hypothesis is true.9 It changed little.

The deepest of the early critiques was Paul Meehl's. In a series of papers culminating in a 1978 essay on "theoretical risks and tabular asterisks," Meehl argued that the entire ritual of null-hypothesis significance testing was epistemically backward for the "soft" social sciences. Because in psychology everything correlates with everything to some nonzero degree — the crud factor — a null hypothesis of exactly zero effect is almost always false a priori, so rejecting it with enough data is a test of sample size, not of theory. Real theories, Meehl insisted, should make risky point predictions that could be decisively wrong; psychology had instead settled for the easy ritual of refuting straw-man nulls and decorating the result with asterisks.10 His critique anticipated, by forty years, the "theory crisis" that metascientists would rediscover in the 2010s.

By the time Ioannidis formalized the argument in 2005, the pieces were ready to be assembled into a quantitative prediction. His model expressed the positive predictive value of a research finding — the probability that a claimed effect is real given that it was reported as significant — as a function of three quantities: the pre-study odds that the hypothesis is true, the study's statistical power, and the Type I error rate, all degraded further by analytic bias and by the number of teams chasing the same question. The arithmetic is unforgiving. In a field exploring mostly long-shot hypotheses with underpowered studies and flexible analysis, the proportion of published positives that are false can easily exceed one half.3 The crisis, in this light, was not a failure of any individual study but a structural property of how the enterprise had been incentivized to operate. The literature was behaving exactly as the mathematics said it should.

§ 032011 — the dam breaks

Precognition, p-hacking, and a fabricator

Diffuse warnings became an acute crisis in a single remarkable year. Three events in 2011, arriving almost on top of one another, made the abstract concrete and turned methodological grumbling into a discipline-wide emergency.

The first was an embarrassment of the most instructive kind. Daryl Bem, an eminent social psychologist, published nine experiments in the Journal of Personality and Social Psychology — psychology's most prestigious outlet — purporting to demonstrate precognition: that future events could reach backward in time to influence present behavior. Across more than a thousand participants, Bem reported that people could, in effect, feel the future.11 The methods were entirely conventional. That was the point. Bem had used the standard tools of experimental social psychology — the same designs, sample sizes, and analytic latitude as everyone else — and arrived at a significant, peer-reviewed, publishable result for an impossible phenomenon. If the field's ordinary methods could "prove" precognition, what did they prove about anything? A team led by Eric-Jan Wagenmakers made the moral explicit in a rebuttal: the result was not evidence for psi but a reductio ad absurdum of the discipline's reliance on flexible, exploratory significance testing, and a case for Bayesian re-analysis and pre-registration.12 When other groups attempted straightforward replications of Bem's central effect, it vanished.13

The second event named the mechanism. In a paper whose title became a slogan — "False-Positive Psychology" — Joseph Simmons, Leif Nelson, and Uri Simonsohn showed, with simulations and a tongue-in-cheek real experiment, how ordinary, undisclosed analytic flexibility could manufacture significance at will. They coined the term researcher degrees of freedom: the many small, defensible-sounding choices a researcher makes after seeing the data — which observations to exclude, which of several outcome measures to report, whether to add a covariate, when to stop collecting data. Each choice is individually innocent; exploited collectively, even unconsciously, they inflate the false-positive rate catastrophically. Their simulations showed that a single such liberty could push the nominal five-percent error rate above twelve percent, and a modest combination of four could drive it past sixty.14 The practice acquired a name of its own — p-hacking — and a companion sin, HARKing, or hypothesizing after the results are known: dressing up a pattern discovered in the data as a hypothesis the study had been designed to test all along.15

How common were these practices? A survey by Leslie John, George Loewenstein, and Drazen Prelec, which used an incentive-compatible design to encourage honest disclosure, suggested they were not deviant but normative. Large fractions of academic psychologists admitted to selectively reporting studies that "worked," collecting more data after peeking to see whether results crossed significance, and excluding conditions or measures after the fact. The authors estimated that, with appropriate correction for under-reporting, such questionable research practices were close to ubiquitous.16 These were not the acts of a corrupt few; they were the water the field swam in.

The third event was darker, and it ensured the others could not be dismissed. Diederik Stapel, a celebrated and prolific Dutch social psychologist, was exposed in 2011 as a serial fabricator who had for years simply invented his data — handing co-authors and students immaculate spreadsheets for studies that had never been run. The investigating committees ultimately determined that fraud underlay dozens of his papers; the retraction count reached fifty-eight, one of the largest in the history of the social sciences.17 The Stapel affair was, in one sense, a story about a single dishonest man rather than about flawed methods. But its timing was lethal to complacency, and it raised a more corrosive question that two of his contemporaries, Wolfgang Stroebe and colleagues, posed directly: if a fabricator this productive could operate undetected for so long, what did that say about science's cherished belief in its own self-correction? Fraud, they argued, is almost never caught by the formal mechanisms of peer review and replication that the field assumes will protect it; Stapel was undone by whistleblowing students, not by the system.18 The same machinery that failed to catch outright invention was hardly likely to catch the subtler, sincere distortions of p-hacking.

§ 04Anatomy of an unreliable literature

How real fields produce false records

To understand why so many findings failed to replicate, it helps to stop thinking about individual studies and start thinking about the literature as a filter — a process that takes the full set of experiments run and selectively admits some into the published record. Three forces operate on that filter simultaneously, and they compound. Each is comprehensible on its own; together they are sufficient to populate a journal with effects that are exaggerated, fragile, or simply not there.

Publication bias and the winner's curse

The first force is selection on significance. Journals, reviewers, and authors all prefer positive results; null findings are harder to publish and easier to abandon. This is Sterling's and Rosenthal's file drawer, and its effect is not merely that the literature is incomplete. Selection actively distorts the magnitude of the effects that do appear. Because a study only crosses the significance threshold when its observed effect is large enough — and because, under low power, the observed effect must be substantially larger than the true effect to clear that bar — the published estimates are systematically inflated. Ioannidis named this the winner's curse of research: conditional on being statistically significant and therefore publishable, an estimate is biased upward, often severely.19 This is the precise statistical reason replication effects come in at roughly half the original size again and again. The replications are not failing to find a real effect that is simply smaller; they are measuring closer to the truth, while the originals were the lucky overshoots that selection let through.

A related dynamic, the Proteus phenomenon, governs how a literature evolves over time: early dramatic claims invite rapid contradiction, and the published effect sizes oscillate and "decline" as more — and more honestly null — studies accumulate. The popular press would later christen this the "decline effect," as though nature itself were fading. The mundane explanation is regression to the mean operating on a selected sample.

Low power, and the corrosion it causes

The second force is chronic underpowering, the problem Cohen diagnosed in 1962 and which a half-century of exhortation barely improved. In 2013 Katherine Button, Ioannidis, and colleagues estimated the median statistical power of studies across the neurosciences at around twenty-one percent.20 A field operating at one-fifth power is in a perverse position. Most of its true effects are missed, so its negative results are uninformative; and — counterintuitively — when an underpowered study does report a positive, that positive is more likely to be a false alarm and, if real, badly inflated, because only the overestimates survived. Low power thus poisons inference from both directions: it wastes true effects and it degrades the reliability of the discoveries it does yield. Worse, underpowered designs make a literature cheap to populate: many small studies, each with a decent chance of a publishable fluke, are a more efficient engine of false positives than a few large ones.

The garden of forking paths

The third force is the most subtle, because it implicates honest researchers who never consciously cheat. Andrew Gelman and Eric Loken called it the garden of forking paths. Even a scientist who runs a single analysis and reports a single p-value may be making an error, if the specific analysis chosen was contingent on the data — if, had the numbers come out differently, a different but equally reasonable analysis would have been run and reported. The space of analyses the researcher would have conducted across possible datasets is what determines the true false-positive rate, and that space can be vast even when no explicit "fishing" occurs. Multiple comparisons need not be performed to do their damage; they need only be available.21 This is why the problem could not be solved by simply asking researchers to stop p-hacking. Much of the inflation came from sincere, one-shot analyses whose flexibility was invisible to the analyst.

Layered on top of these structural forces was a pervasive conceptual error: the misinterpretation of the p-value itself. A p-value is the probability of data at least as extreme as observed, assuming the null hypothesis is true. It is not the probability that the null is true, not the probability the result is a fluke, and not a measure of effect size or importance — yet it was routinely read as all three. A widely circulated Nature feature catalogued the confusions and the false sense of evidential certainty that a result at p just under .05 conveys, when in fact such a result is statistically quite weak evidence against the null.22 The bright line at .05 — an arbitrary convention Ronald Fisher never intended as a universal standard — had become a gatekeeper for publication, a target for optimization, and a generator of dichotomous "discovered / not discovered" thinking that the underlying continuous evidence did not support.

Two further empirical observations rounded out the diagnosis. Daniele Fanelli, mining the literature, showed that the proportion of papers reporting positive (hypothesis-confirming) results increased as one moved "down" the hierarchy of the sciences from the physical toward the social and behavioral — exactly the gradient one would expect if softer fields, with more analytic flexibility and weaker theoretical constraints, were more susceptible to bias.23 And in a meta-analysis of confidential surveys, Fanelli estimated that around two percent of scientists admitted to having fabricated or falsified data at least once, while a third confessed to other questionable practices — figures that, given the obvious incentives to deny, are best read as lower bounds.24 Outright fraud was rare; the gray zone of distortion was not.

§ 05The reckoning — psychology

Counting the survivors

Diagnosis is cheap; measurement is expensive. The decisive move of the 2010s was to stop arguing about whether the literature was reliable and to test it, systematically, at scale. The instrument was the coordinated, pre-registered, high-powered replication project — itself a methodological innovation, since direct replications had been so rare as to be almost absent from the published record. A bibliometric audit found that explicit replications accounted for roughly one percent of published psychology articles, and that even those overwhelmingly "succeeded," because failed replications were as unpublishable as any other null.25 The field had effectively never checked its own work.

The flagship was the Reproducibility Project: Psychology, whose headline numbers — 97% of originals significant, 36% of replications significant, effect sizes halved — became the most-cited statistics of the entire episode.1 But the project's value lay as much in its texture as its top line. Replication success was strongly predicted by the strength of the original evidence: studies with smaller original p-values and larger original effects were far more likely to hold up, while results that had originally squeaked past .05 mostly collapsed. Cognitive psychology fared considerably better than social psychology. And the effect sizes did not merely shrink on average; in many cases the replication estimate was statistically indistinguishable from zero. Whatever else it was, the literature was not uniformly solid — and its weakest, most surprising, most heavily promoted findings were precisely the ones least likely to survive.

The project also triggered the crisis's defining intellectual brawl. In 2016 Daniel Gilbert, Gary King, Stephen Pettigrew, and Timothy Wilson published a technical comment in Science arguing that the Reproducibility Project had badly underestimated reproducibility. They contended that the replications were often statistically underpowered to detect the original effects; that some had deviated from the original protocols in ways the originals' authors had flagged in advance as consequential; and that, once sampling error and these infidelities were accounted for, the data were consistent with most of the original effects being real.26 The Open Science Collaboration replied that Gilbert and colleagues had made optimistic assumptions of their own, that their preferred benchmark assumed away publication bias, and that the central finding — pervasive effect-size decline — was robust to the criticisms.27 The exchange was never fully resolved, and its irresolution is itself instructive: reasonable, technically sophisticated people could look at the same replication data and disagree about whether the glass was one-third full or two-thirds empty, because "successful replication" has no single agreed definition. Significance in the same direction, effect size within a confidence interval, a subjective judgment, a meta-analytic combination — each yields a different number, and the Reproducibility Project's own figures ranged from 36% to 68% depending on the criterion.

Many labs, one finding at a time

A complementary design attacked a different question. Where the Reproducibility Project was broad and shallow — one replication each of a hundred findings — the "Many Labs" projects were narrow and deep, having dozens of laboratories around the world each replicate the same small set of effects. The first, led by Richard Klein, took thirteen classic findings to thirty-six samples.28 The payoff was not just a success rate but a map of where variability came from. The headline result of the much larger Many Labs 2 — twenty-eight findings, each run in scores of samples across dozens of countries — was quietly profound. Of the effects examined, only about half replicated; but for those that did, the effect was strikingly consistent from sample to sample and culture to culture. Variation across settings was generally small, and differences between "WEIRD" (Western, educated, industrialized, rich, democratic) and less-WEIRD samples were minor.29,30

This finding cut directly against the most popular defense of failed replications — the appeal to hidden moderators. When a result failed to reproduce, its defenders frequently argued that the effect was real but context-sensitive: subtly dependent on the population, the locale, the season, the unmeasured rapport between experimenter and subject. Many Labs 2 showed that, for the effects it tested, this was largely not so. Effects that were real tended to be robustly real everywhere; effects that failed tended to fail everywhere. Context-sensitivity could not, in general, rescue the missing findings — though, importantly, it could and did matter for some, and the debate over which is which continues.

The casualties

Abstract replication rates were made vivid by the fall of specific, famous effects. Ego depletion — the influential theory that self-control draws on a limited, glucose-like resource that fatigues with use — had hundreds of supporting studies and a celebrated originator in Roy Baumeister. A pre-registered, multi-laboratory replication coordinated by Martin Hagger, involving roughly two thousand participants across more than twenty sites, failed to find the effect at all.31 Power posing — the claim, launched in a 2010 paper by Dana Carney, Amy Cuddy, and Andy Yap and amplified by one of the most-viewed TED talks ever given, that holding an expansive posture for two minutes raises testosterone, lowers cortisol, and increases risk tolerance — met a similar fate. A large, better-powered replication by Eva Ranehill and colleagues recovered only a self-reported feeling of power, with none of the hormonal or behavioral effects.32 A p-curve analysis of the broader literature found it contained no evidential value for the headline claims, and Carney, the lead author, publicly disavowed the effect and stated she no longer believed it was real.33 The original power-posing study had enrolled forty-two participants — a sample size that, in hindsight, exemplified the entire problem.

It is important not to caricature this as a parade of stupidity or bad faith. The researchers involved were, in the main, talented people working competently within the prevailing norms of their field. That is exactly what made the crisis a crisis rather than a scandal about a few bad actors. The norms themselves — small samples, flexible analysis, publication contingent on novelty and significance — were the problem, and they had produced a literature in which a substantial fraction of textbook findings could not be reproduced.

§ 06The reckoning — economics & medicine

Not a psychology problem

For a few years it was tempting for other disciplines to treat the crisis as psychology's private humiliation — a pathology of a soft science with squishy constructs and impressionable subjects. That comfort did not survive contact with the data. Wherever the replication audit was extended, it returned variations on the same number.

Experimental economics, a field that prides itself on incentivized designs and methodological rigor, was tested by Colin Camerer and colleagues, who replicated eighteen laboratory studies from the discipline's two leading journals. Eleven of the eighteen — sixty-one percent — produced a significant effect in the original direction, with replication effect sizes averaging about two-thirds of the originals.34 The same team then turned to the social sciences' most visible venues, replicating twenty-one experiments published in Nature and Science — the journals whose imprimatur is supposed to certify the most important and best-vetted findings. Thirteen of twenty-one replicated, again at roughly sixty-two percent, again with effects about half the original magnitude.35 That the failure rate was so similar in the field's flagship outlets was, if anything, more alarming than the psychology results: prestige and selectivity had not purchased reliability.

A fascinating sidelight of these projects was the use of prediction markets. Before each replication ran, researchers were invited to bet on whether it would succeed. The markets — and even simple surveys of peer opinion — predicted the outcomes with considerable accuracy, substantially better than chance.36 The community, in other words, collectively knew which findings were shaky. The knowledge was tacit, distributed in the hallway skepticism of experts, but it was real. The crisis was not that no one suspected; it was that the formal incentives of publication rewarded producing and citing the dubious findings anyway.

The biomedical reckoning

The highest stakes were in biomedicine, where irreproducible preclinical research does not merely clutter a literature but misdirects drug development and, downstream, exposes patients in clinical trials to molecules whose rationale was never real. The alarms here actually predated the psychology projects and came, tellingly, from industry rather than the academy. In 2011 scientists at Bayer reported that their teams could reproduce the published findings underpinning potential drug targets only about a quarter of the time across dozens of oncology, women's-health, and cardiovascular projects.37 The following year, C. Glenn Begley and Lee Ellis described an even starker result from Amgen: over a decade, the company's haematology and oncology group had attempted to confirm fifty-three "landmark" preclinical cancer papers and succeeded with just six — an eleven percent reproducibility rate.38 Because these were the studies chosen as foundations for real therapeutic programs, the failure had immediate financial and clinical consequences. One economic analysis put the cost of irreproducible preclinical research in the United States alone at roughly twenty-eight billion dollars a year.39

These industry reports were suggestive but opaque: the companies would not disclose which studies they had tried or exactly how. The Reproducibility Project: Cancer Biology, a collaboration between the Center for Open Science and Science Exchange, set out to do it transparently. The plan was to replicate 193 experiments from 53 high-impact cancer papers published between 2010 and 2012, with every protocol peer-reviewed and registered in advance. The project's most important finding emerged before any result was tallied: it proved nearly impossible to do. Not one of the 193 experiments was described with enough methodological detail to be repeated without going back to the original authors; the data needed to compute effect sizes was publicly available for just four of them; and for the majority, the original authors were unable or unwilling to supply the missing materials, reagents, or analysis details. After eight years and enormous expense, the team managed to complete only 50 experiments from 23 papers.40 The bottleneck was not that the findings were false — it was that the published record was too incomplete to even constitute a testable claim. Opacity, in cancer biology, came before irreproducibility.

Of the experiments that could be completed, the meta-analysis told a now-familiar story. Replication effect sizes were, for positive findings, a median of eighty-five percent smaller than the originals; ninety-two percent of replication effects were smaller than their original counterparts. By the project's summary criteria, fewer than half of the effects clearly replicated, and original positive results were only about half as likely to replicate successfully as original null results.41 The winner's curse, the decline effect, the systematic inflation — all of it reappeared in petri dishes and mouse models, untouched by the supposed objectivity of the bench.

The clinical-trial natural experiment

Perhaps the single most persuasive piece of evidence that flexible analysis had been inflating positive results came not from a replication project but from a policy change. In 2000, registration on ClinicalTrials.gov — specifying outcomes and analyses in advance — became required for large trials funded by the U.S. National Heart, Lung, and Blood Institute. Robert Kaplan and Veronica Irvin examined the major NHLBI trials of cardiovascular drugs and dietary supplements before and after this requirement. Of the trials run before mandatory pre-registration, the majority — about 57% — had reported a significant benefit on the primary outcome. After pre-registration became compulsory, that share collapsed to roughly 8%.42 The interventions had not changed; the analytic freedom had. When researchers were forced to commit to their hypotheses and endpoints before seeing the data, the positive results very largely disappeared. It is hard to imagine a cleaner demonstration that much of what the prior literature called "effective" was an artifact of post hoc flexibility.

One field, meanwhile, offered a hopeful counter-model: human genetics. The candidate-gene era of the late 1990s and early 2000s had been a replication catastrophe, with a flood of reported gene–trait associations almost none of which held up.43 But genetics reformed itself, and did so before the word "crisis" was fashionable. It adopted genome-wide significance thresholds orders of magnitude stricter than .05 (around 5 × 10⁻⁸ to correct for the millions of variants tested), demanded independent replication cohorts as a condition of publication, and built large consortia to pool data. The result is that modern genome-wide association findings replicate at very high rates. Genetics demonstrated that the crisis was not a law of nature but a consequence of correctable incentives — a proof of concept the reform movement would lean on heavily.

§ 07The hard edge

When honesty research was dishonest

Fraud and irreproducibility are distinct problems, and conflating them is a common error. The replication crisis is overwhelmingly a story about sincere researchers and broken incentives, not liars; the great majority of findings that fail to replicate were produced in good faith. But the two problems are entangled at the edges, because a system that cannot detect honest distortion certainly cannot detect deliberate fabrication, and because the same culture of post-publication scrutiny that drove the replication movement also became the apparatus that exposed fraud. The most consequential recent fraud cases were uncovered not by journals or institutions but by the independent data sleuths the era produced.

No episode captured the recursion more perfectly than the unraveling of a body of celebrated research on honesty itself. In 2012, a paper in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences by Lisa Shu, Nina Mazar, Francesca Gino, Dan Ariely, and Max Bazerman reported a beguiling result: people were more truthful when they signed an honesty declaration at the top of a form rather than the bottom, because signing first made ethics salient before the opportunity to cheat. The finding included a field experiment with an insurance company in which customers reported their odometer mileage.44 It was elegant, actionable, widely cited, and embedded in popular books and corporate ethics programs. There was only one difficulty. In 2020, the original authors themselves, attempting to build on the work, ran a large replication and found nothing — the sign-at-the-top effect simply was not there.45 That failure was, at the time, read as one more honest casualty of the crisis.

It was worse than that. The independent blog Data Colada — run by Uri Simonsohn, Joseph Simmons, and Leif Nelson, the same trio behind "False-Positive Psychology" — examined the underlying data and found that the field-experiment dataset was not merely weak but fabricated. The give-away was forensic: rows of data that were impossibly, mechanically duplicated, and a spreadsheet whose metadata indicated the fraudulent values had been introduced after the fact. In 2021 the PNAS paper was retracted, with the authors acknowledging the data were fabricated; the fabricated portion traced to data Ariely said he had obtained from the insurance company.46 Then, in 2023, Data Colada published a four-part series alleging that a second author on that paper, the Harvard Business School star Francesca Gino, had independently fabricated or manipulated data in several other papers on honesty, creativity, and ethics.47

The aftermath became a landmark in the law and politics of scientific misconduct. A Harvard Business School investigation, ultimately producing a roughly 1,300-page report, concluded that Gino had committed research misconduct; the university placed her on administrative leave and moved to revoke her tenure, and several of her papers were retracted.48 Gino sued Harvard and the three Data Colada bloggers for defamation, seeking twenty-five million dollars. In 2024 a federal judge dismissed the defamation claims against the bloggers, holding that their analyses were protected expressions of opinion based on disclosed evidence.49 The case crystallized a tension the crisis had created: post-publication critics, operating outside the formal institutions of science, had become indispensable to its integrity — and were now exposed to the legal risk that came with that role.

The honesty saga was the most ironic but not the only sign that scrutiny had industrialized. Image forensics emerged as a discipline of its own, after analysts such as Elisabeth Bik showed that inappropriate duplication and manipulation of images — gels, blots, micrographs — was detectable at scale and disturbingly common in the biomedical literature, appearing in a few percent of papers in some samples.50 The same techniques contributed, in 2023, to the resignation of Stanford's president, the neuroscientist Marc Tessier-Lavigne, after a review found manipulated images and substandard rigor in papers from his laboratories, several of which were corrected or retracted; the review did not find that he had personally falsified data, but it documented a failure of oversight in labs he led.51 Underpinning all of it was the rise of Retraction Watch, founded in 2010, which turned the once-obscure event of a retraction into a tracked, public, searchable phenomenon and made the scale of the problem visible in a way it had never been.52

The relationship between these fraud cases and the broader crisis is best stated carefully. Fraud is not why most findings fail to replicate; the structural forces described earlier are more than sufficient without invoking dishonesty. But the fraud cases mattered enormously to the crisis's politics. They demonstrated, viscerally, that the self-correcting mechanisms scientists trusted were inert against even crude fabrication, and that the new, informal, sometimes adversarial culture of data forensics and public criticism was doing work the official institutions had failed to do. That shift — from trust to verification, from gatekeeping to auditing — was the crisis's deepest cultural consequence, and the source of its bitterest disputes.

§ 08The backlash

"Methodological terrorism" and its discontents

It would be a sanitized history that presented the replication movement as a serene self-correction welcomed by all. It was a fight, and at times an ugly one, because the stakes were not merely epistemic. Careers, reputations, textbooks, grant portfolios, and entire research programs were built on findings the replicators were now publicly declaring unreliable. To be told that your most-cited result is a false positive is not a neutral scientific update; it is, inevitably, felt as an accusation, however carefully the critic frames it as systemic rather than personal.

The tension surfaced most memorably in 2016, when the eminent social psychologist Susan Fiske circulated a column denouncing what she characterized as a new breed of online critics — "destructo-critics" engaged in what she termed methodological terrorism: unmoderated, reputation-destroying attacks conducted on blogs and social media without the buffering of peer review, disproportionately aimed at junior researchers and, she argued, at women.53 The phrase detonated. To the reform movement it confirmed exactly the instinct they were fighting — the impulse to treat criticism as incivility and to protect senior scientists from scrutiny by appealing to tone. To Fiske's defenders it named something real: a genuine asymmetry of power and consequence in which a viral takedown could end a career, and in which the rhetoric of "rigor" sometimes masked ordinary academic cruelty.

Beneath the rhetoric were substantive disagreements that remain unresolved, and the most serious deserve a fair hearing rather than caricature. Wolfgang Stroebe and Fritz Strack mounted the strongest principled defense, arguing that the entire enterprise rested on an illusion: the idea of an "exact" replication is incoherent, because no replication ever recreates the original conditions — the subjects, the era, the cultural surround, the experimenter all differ. A failed replication, on this view, is profoundly ambiguous: it might mean the original was false, or it might mean the effect is real but bounded by conditions the replicators failed to reproduce. They argued further that conceptual replication — testing the same theory through a deliberately different operationalization — is more scientifically valuable than rote direct replication, and that the field's existing reliance on conceptual replication had been undervalued by the crisis narrative.54

This is not a frivolous position, and the reform movement's best answer was empirical rather than rhetorical. The hidden-moderator defense is testable, and Many Labs 2 tested it: for the effects examined, replications were highly consistent across exactly the contextual variations — population, country, setting — that the moderator argument invokes.29 Where effects were real, context did not erase them; where they failed, no plausible moderator rescued them. The defense survives only for the specific subset of phenomena that are genuinely fragile, and the burden of showing that a given effect belongs to that subset falls on its defenders, not its critics. As for conceptual replication, the reformers turned the argument around: a research culture in which only conceptual replications are run, and only successful ones are published, is precisely a machine for never disconfirming anything — because any failure can be attributed to the changed operationalization rather than to the theory. Conceptual replication without direct replication is unfalsifiable by construction.

There was, finally, a sociological dimension that no one disputes in private. The crisis had a generational and a status valence. Many of its most energetic drivers were early-career researchers and methodologists with relatively little invested in the threatened findings and a great deal of facility with the new tools of data forensics and open science; many of its most pained targets were established figures whose canonical results were suddenly contestable. This is not a reason to discount the substance — the data are the data regardless of who is uncomfortable — but it shaped the affective temperature of the dispute, and it explains why a debate ostensibly about statistical power so often read as a struggle over authority and the right to certify knowledge.

§ 09The repair

Metascience and the machinery of reform

Out of the wreckage grew something genuinely new: a self-conscious science of science, or metascience, devoted to studying and improving research practices empirically. Its program had two halves — fix the incentives that produced false positives, and build infrastructure that makes rigor the path of least resistance — and a manifesto, drafted by Marcus Munafò and a coalition of reformers, laid out the agenda across the whole research lifecycle: better methods, independent replication, transparency, and aligned incentives.55

The keystone reform was pre-registration — the public, time-stamped specification of hypotheses, methods, and analysis plans before data are collected — which directly disarms the garden of forking paths by removing the analyst's data-contingent flexibility. Its more demanding form, the Registered Report, championed by Chris Chambers, restructures publishing itself: peer review happens before the study is run, and a journal commits to publishing the result regardless of how it turns out, on the basis of the question's importance and the design's soundness rather than the outcome's novelty.56 This single change attacks publication bias at its root, because acceptance no longer depends on getting a significant result. Pre-registration migrated from psychology into a discipline-wide practice, and the broader case for it was made formally as a "pre-registration revolution."57

Infrastructure followed. The Center for Open Science built the Open Science Framework, a free repository for pre-registrations, data, and materials; and the community codified a set of Transparency and Openness Promotion (TOP) guidelines that journals could adopt to require data sharing, materials sharing, and pre-registration, with "badges" to reward compliance.58 The empirical question of whether any of this was changing behavior became, fittingly, a metascientific research topic in its own right.

The war over the p-value

The statistical reform front was the most contentious, because experts genuinely disagreed about the cure even when they agreed on the disease. In 2016 the American Statistical Association took the unprecedented step of issuing a formal statement on p-values, enumerating their proper interpretation and warning explicitly against the bright-line use of significance thresholds and the conflation of statistical with practical significance.59 But agreement on the "don'ts" fractured immediately into rival "do's." One camp, in a paper signed by seventy-two authors, proposed simply tightening the threshold for claiming a new discovery from .05 to .005, reserving the old level for "suggestive" evidence.60 A second camp countered that no fixed threshold should be privileged, and that researchers should instead justify their alpha for the specific inferential context.61 A third, more radical camp argued for abolishing the very category of "statistical significance" — ceasing to dichotomize results into significant and non-significant at all — a position advanced in a Nature comment that gathered more than 800 signatories and in a companion call to "abandon statistical significance."62,63 The ASA's own follow-up special issue went further than its 2016 statement, with its editors urging that the term "statistically significant" be retired entirely.64 No consensus emerged, and none has since; the dispute is a live one. But the cumulative effect was to dethrone the .05 ritual from its unquestioned status and to push the field toward effect sizes, interval estimates, and explicit evidential reasoning.

Many analysts, and the multiverse

A parallel innovation made the garden of forking paths visible by walking many of its paths at once. In "many-analyst" studies, dozens of independent teams are given the identical dataset and research question and asked to analyze it however they see fit. The results are humbling: in one such study, twenty-nine teams analyzing the same data on whether soccer referees give more red cards to darker-skinned players reached substantially different conclusions, with effect estimates ranging from negligible to large.65 A neuroimaging version, in which seventy teams analyzed one brain-imaging dataset, found that no two teams used the same analytic pipeline and that their conclusions about which brain regions were active diverged widely.66 The lesson was not that analysis is arbitrary but that any single analytic path is one draw from a distribution the reader never sees. The constructive response was the multiverse or specification-curve analysis: rather than reporting one preferred specification, report the results across the entire defensible space of analytic choices, so that the robustness — or fragility — of a finding is laid bare.

Underlying all these technical fixes was a structural diagnosis that the subtlest reformers insisted on: the problem was evolutionary, not moral. Paul Smaldino and Richard McElreath modeled science as a population of laboratories competing for the currency of publication, and showed that if the rewards flow to whoever produces the most positive results, then low-rigor methods — small samples, flexible analysis — will be selected for and spread, regardless of the intentions of individual scientists, in a process they called "the natural selection of bad science."67 On this view, exhorting researchers to be more careful is futile so long as the incentive gradient rewards carelessness. Only changing what gets rewarded — what journals publish, what funders fund, what hiring committees count — can change the equilibrium. The reform movement's deepest bet is that pre-registration, Registered Reports, open data, and replication are not just better practices but a different selective environment.

§ 10The recursive turn

Open problems, and a reform that failed to replicate

More than a decade in, the honest assessment is mixed and still being written. There is real evidence that the reforms work in principle. The clinical-trial natural experiment showed pre-registration deflating spurious positives; genetics showed that strict thresholds plus mandatory replication produce a reliable literature; and a synthesis across more than three hundred systematic and multi-site replications in psychology found an aggregate replication rate of around sixty-four percent — better than the gloomiest early figures, with much of the variance explained by the rigor of the original work.4 Pre-registrations now number in the millions, Registered Reports are offered by hundreds of journals, and a generation of researchers has been trained to treat power analysis and open data as defaults rather than virtues. By the standards of how slowly scientific culture usually changes, this is fast.

And yet the crisis has a way of recurring inside its own solutions, and the most cautionary recent episode is precisely about that. In 2023 a high-profile collaboration including several leaders of the reform movement — Brian Nosek, Leif Nelson, Jonathan Schooler, and others — published a paper in Nature Human Behaviour with an encouraging title: "High replicability of newly discovered social-behavioural findings is achievable." Four coordinated laboratories had generated sixteen new effects using the full suite of rigor-enhancing practices — confirmatory testing, large samples, pre-registration, transparency — and reported that, when one lab's effect was re-tested by another, eighty-six percent replicated and the replication effect sizes were ninety-seven percent of the originals. Here, at last, seemed proof that doing it right yields a reliable science.68

Then the paper was retracted. Joseph Bak-Coleman and Berna Devezer scrutinized it and argued, in a critique pointedly titled "Claims about scientific rigour require rigour," that the study did not live up to its own standard: the hypotheses and predictions had been misstated, key measures and analyses supporting the headline claim had not actually been pre-registered despite the paper's assertions to the contrary, and outcomes had been selected with knowledge of the data.69 After editorial review upheld the central concerns, Nature Human Behaviour retracted the article in 2024. The authors agreed to the retraction on the specific ground that the meta-study's pre-registration had been incorrectly described, while disputing the other criticisms.70 The flagship demonstration that rigor produces replicability had itself failed a rigor audit — over, of all things, a misstated pre-registration.

It would be cynical, and wrong, to read this as proof that the reforms are a sham. The more honest reading is almost the opposite: the episode shows the auditing culture working, turned without favoritism on the reformers themselves, with no canonical figure too senior and no result too congenial to escape scrutiny. That is what a self-correcting science is supposed to look like, and it is more than the pre-crisis field could manage. But it is also a humbling reminder that pre-registration is a discipline, not a talisman; that practices can be performed in name while their substance is evaded; and that the gap between methodological aspiration and methodological reality does not close just because everyone has agreed on the right words.

86% retracted

The 2023 paper reporting that rigor-enhancing practices yield 86% replicability — co-authored by leaders of the reform movement — was itself retracted in 2024, in part for misstating its own pre-registration.68,70

The problems the reforms do not touch

Pre-registration and replication address how findings are generated and vetted. They do not, by themselves, repair two deeper problems that the crisis exposed and that remain substantially open.

The first is the generalizability crisis, articulated most sharply by Tal Yarkoni. Even a perfectly pre-registered, well-powered, replicable study typically licenses a vastly narrower conclusion than the one its authors draw. A finding obtained with a particular task, a particular stimulus set, particular experimenters, and particular subjects is routinely written up as a claim about "people" or "cognition" in general — but the statistical model used to test it treats only the subjects as a random, generalizable factor, while implicitly treating the stimuli, tasks, and operationalizations as fixed. Properly modeling all those sources of variation as random, Yarkoni argues, would inflate the uncertainty around most psychological claims so dramatically that the field's standard inferences are unsupported by its standard methods — a problem orthogonal to replication, and arguably more fundamental.71 A study can replicate perfectly and still not generalize an inch beyond its own narrow design.

The second is the theory crisis, which is Meehl's old complaint returned with force. Klaus Oberauer and Stephan Lewandowsky argued that psychology's replication problems are downstream of a more basic deficit: its theories are too vague to make the risky, quantitative predictions that would let data genuinely test them. When a theory predicts only that two conditions will "differ," almost any significant result can be claimed as support and almost any null explained away, so the literature accumulates confirmations without ever forcing a theory to stake something it could lose.72 Better methods produce more trustworthy facts; they do not, on their own, produce better theories to organize those facts. A field can have impeccably replicable findings and still lack any cumulative theoretical structure to make sense of them.

Everywhere, now

What began in social psychology and preclinical biology has propagated through the empirical sciences, and few fields have proven immune once they looked. Machine learning and artificial intelligence developed an acute reproducibility problem of their own, driven by undocumented hyperparameters, unshared code and data, sensitivity to random seeds, and competitive pressure to report state-of-the-art numbers — prompting Science to report on an "AI reproducibility crisis" and the field's leading venues to introduce reproducibility checklists and structured reporting requirements.73,74 Water, energy, government services, the chemical sector — wherever empirical claims feed real decisions, the same fault lines between what is published and what is reproducible reappear. The replication crisis turned out not to be a property of any one subject matter but of a way of doing science: under-powered, flexibly analyzed, selectively published, and trusted rather than verified.

Crisis, or maturation?

So which is it — a crisis or a revolution? The framing genuinely matters, and the answer is that it has been both, sequentially. The discovery phase was a crisis: a profession that prided itself on being the self-correcting enterprise par excellence learned that large parts of its literature were unreliable, that its statistical rituals were poorly understood even by their practitioners, that its incentives rewarded the production of false positives, and that its vaunted self-correction had failed to catch even brazen fraud. That was, and should have been, humbling. But the response phase has looked more like maturation: the construction of a metascience, the normalization of transparency, the dethroning of the significance ritual, the spread of pre-registration, and a culture — imperfect, sometimes vicious, occasionally tripping over its own feet — willing to turn its scrutiny on its most celebrated findings and its own reformers alike.5

The deepest lesson is not about any particular statistic or practice. It is that the reliability of knowledge is not a natural byproduct of intelligence, sincerity, or peer review, but an institutional achievement that has to be deliberately engineered and continuously maintained — and that can quietly erode whenever the incentives reward the appearance of discovery over the substance of it. Science did not stop being trustworthy when it admitted how much of its record could not be reproduced. If anything, the admission was the most trustworthy thing it had done in a long time. The work now is to ensure that the engineering holds, that the words do not outrun the practice, and that the next generation's literature is built to survive the only test that has ever certified knowledge: someone else, somewhere else, doing it again, and finding the same thing.
