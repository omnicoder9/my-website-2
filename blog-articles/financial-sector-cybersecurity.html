Securing the Ledger

Orientation

A sector apart

Money is information. A bank balance, a wire instruction, a securities position — none of these are physical things; they are authoritative records held in databases and moved across messaging networks. That makes the financial sector unusual among critical infrastructures: its product is data, and the integrity of that data is inseparable from the stability of the economy itself.

The United States treats financial services as one of the 16 critical infrastructure sectors designated under Presidential Policy Directive 21 and reaffirmed under National Security Memorandum 22. The U.S. Department of the Treasury is the designated Sector Risk Management Agency, responsible for sector-specific knowledge, coordination, and incident support.1 The sector was formerly called "Banking and Finance" and was renamed the Financial Services Sector in 2013.2 CISA enumerates a designated Sector Risk Management Agency for each of the 16 sectors, from Chemical and Communications to Energy and Water.3

What sits inside the sector is enormous and heterogeneous. It spans thousands of depository institutions, investment firms, insurers, other credit and financing organizations, and the critical financial utilities that knit them together — ranging from globe-spanning banks with trillions in assets to single-branch community banks and credit unions.2 Treasury coordinates with the industry through a public–private structure: the Financial Services Sector Coordinating Council on the private side and the Financial and Banking Information Infrastructure Committee on the government side, with the sector's cybersecurity profile now maintained by the Cyber Risk Institute.4

Why it matters

Because finance is the medium other sectors transact in, a loss of confidence — not merely a loss of data — is the systemic risk. The defining objective of financial-sector security is therefore resilience of critical functions: keeping payments clearing and accounts accessible even when individual firms are compromised.

Attack surface

Technology & sector-specific risk

The sector's technology stack is layered: customer-facing apps and web portals sit atop core banking systems, which connect outward to shared payment rails and market infrastructure. Each layer carries a different risk character, and the most consequential risks live in the plumbing that few customers ever see.

Payment rails and messaging

Large-value and retail payments move over a handful of shared systems: the Federal Reserve's Fedwire and FedNow services, the privately operated CHIPS network for large-value clearing, the Automated Clearing House for batch retail transfers, and the SWIFT messaging cooperative for cross-border instructions. These systems are designed for availability and finality — once a payment settles, it is generally irrevocable. That finality is precisely what attackers monetize: a fraudulent instruction that is accepted and settled can be effectively impossible to claw back, as the 2016 Bangladesh Bank theft demonstrated when attackers issued instructions over SWIFT against an account at the Federal Reserve Bank of New York.5

Market and post-trade infrastructure

Securities trading depends on a small set of highly concentrated utilities — exchanges, clearing houses, and central securities depositories — where settlement, financing, and collateral movements are processed. Concentration is efficient but fragile: when the U.S. subsidiary of a single large bank was hit by ransomware in November 2023, it was temporarily unable to clear U.S. Treasury trades, and because the Bank of New York Mellon acts as the sole settlement agent for Treasury securities, BNY Mellon was forced to clear affected trades through manual processes.6 A failure at one node propagated immediately into the broader market's plumbing.

Cards, ATMs, and consumer access

Card networks, ATM fleets, and online/mobile banking form the visible edge of the sector. CISA's pandemic-era guidance treated the workers who keep ATMs stocked, move currency and payments, and staff data and security operations centers as essential critical-infrastructure workers — a recognition that consumer access to cash and accounts is itself a critical function, not a convenience.7

Third parties, cloud, and concentration

Modern institutions run on outsourced and cloud infrastructure, which moves a large share of the attack surface outside the bank's own walls. The risk is twofold: misconfiguration of cloud services by the institution, and concentration on a small number of shared providers whose failure becomes everyone's failure. The 2019 Capital One breach is the canonical cloud-misconfiguration case — a misconfigured web application firewall with overly permissive identity-and-access-management rights was exploited via server-side request forgery to obtain temporary credentials and exfiltrate roughly 106 million credit-application records from cloud storage.8 Concentration risk is just as real: in 2024 a ransomware attack on a single cloud IT service provider caused outages across roughly 60 U.S. credit unions at once.9

Case ledger

Threats & consequences: a ledger of incidents

The sector's threat history is the best teacher of its consequences. The entries below are recorded in the form the sector itself uses — as transactions, each with a date, a value, and a settlement note. They trace a clear escalation: from disruption, to theft, to data compromise, to systemic market risk.

Entry 01 · 2012–2013 · DDoSAvailability

Operation Ababil

Beginning in September 2012, a group calling itself the Izz ad-Din al-Qassam Cyber Fighters launched waves of distributed denial-of-service attacks against major U.S. banks, framed as retaliation over an anti-Islamic video and widely linked to Iran.10 The campaign knocked out the public websites of institutions including Bank of America, Wells Fargo, and PNC, using readily available attack tooling and continuing in phases into mid-2013.11

Entry 02 · Feb 2016 · Wire fraudUS$81,000,000 lost

The Bangladesh Bank / SWIFT heist

Attackers who had implanted malware — reportedly including a keystroke logger that captured credentials and tampered with the bank's local SWIFT software — issued 35 fraudulent transfer instructions seeking roughly US$951 million from Bangladesh Bank's account at the Federal Reserve Bank of New York.5 Five orders totaling about US$101 million settled; US$81 million was routed through a Manila bank and largely laundered through casinos, while US$20 million sent to Sri Lanka was stopped.5 The attack was attributed to the North Korea-linked Lazarus Group, and investigators noted reconnaissance dating back to 2014.12

Entry 03 · 2017 · Data breach147.9M records

Equifax

Attackers exploited an unpatched critical vulnerability in the Apache Struts web framework (CVE-2017-5638) on a consumer dispute portal. A patch had been released in March 2017, but the flaw remained unaddressed; attackers maintained access for roughly two and a half months, and an expired internal certificate had disabled the network monitoring that would have detected exfiltration.13 The breach exposed the personal data of roughly 147.9 million Americans and led to a settlement of up to US$700 million with federal and state authorities; the U.S. Department of Justice later indicted members of China's People's Liberation Army in connection with the intrusion.1415

Entry 04 · 2019 · Cloud breach~106M records

Capital One

A misconfigured web application firewall running in the bank's cloud environment was abused via server-side request forgery to read temporary credentials from the instance metadata service; those credentials, attached to an over-privileged role, granted access to cloud storage holding consumer data.8 Roughly 106 million accounts were affected, including some 140,000 Social Security numbers; the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency levied an US$80 million penalty and the bank later agreed to a US$190 million class-action settlement.16 The incident prompted the cloud provider to harden its metadata service.8

Entry 05 · Nov 2023 · RansomwareSystemic risk

ICBC Financial Services

The LockBit ransomware group struck the U.S. broker-dealer subsidiary of the Industrial and Commercial Bank of China; the affiliates are believed to have exploited Citrix NetScaler vulnerabilities (CVE-2023-4966 / CVE-2023-4967).17 The subsidiary isolated systems and was temporarily unable to clear U.S. Treasury trades in the roughly US$26 trillion Treasury market; the parent injected emergency capital and BNY Mellon manually cleared trades, and one analysis put failed Treasury-market trades near US$62 billion at the peak.618 Treasury activated its sector-wide cyber incident response procedures; officials said the disruption did not have a material effect on the Treasury market overall.17

Across these entries the through-line is that the sector's worst outcomes are rarely about a single firm's losses. They are about integrity (can balances be trusted?), availability (can payments clear and customers reach their money?), and confidence (will counterparties keep transacting?). LockBit, the strain behind the ICBC attack, illustrates how industrialized the threat has become — it operated as ransomware-as-a-service and accounted for a substantial share of known ransomware attacks in 2022–2023.19

Interdependency

Cross-sector cascade

The 16 critical infrastructure sectors are not islands; a blow to one can produce cascading second-order effects in others.20 Finance sits at a peculiar position in this web — it is deeply dependent on a few "lifeline" sectors, and at the same time it is itself an upstream dependency for almost everything else.

What finance depends on

Banks cannot run without electricity, and they cannot move money without communications. Energy is often described as "super-critical" precisely because most other sectors — finance included — cannot operate if power is unavailable, and telecommunications and the power grid are themselves mutually dependent.20 A sufficiently large or prolonged disruption to electricity or communications does not merely inconvenience a bank; it can sever its access to payment rails, market data, and its own customers. Analyses of infrastructure interdependency map exactly these chains of first- and second-order dependence on lifeline services such as electric power, communications, and transportation.20

What depends on finance

The reverse dependency is just as strong and often overlooked. Utilities (water, wastewater, electricity), chemical plants, transportation operators, healthcare providers, and the food and agriculture supply chain all rely on functioning payment, payroll, settlement, and lending systems to operate day to day. The other 15 sectors transact in money; if the financial sector's critical functions are impaired, the disruption radiates outward into operations that have nothing to do with banking. This is why the financial sector's defining security goal is continuity of critical functions rather than the protection of any single institution.

A cyberattack on one sector can trigger cascading second-order effects on others — and finance is both downstream of the lifeline sectors and upstream of nearly everything else.

The public–private machinery reflects this reality. Treasury, as Sector Risk Management Agency, coordinates incident response not only within finance but across the federal government; CISA conducts the cross-sector Cyber Storm exercise series, in which the financial sector participates, to rehearse response to incidents that span multiple critical infrastructures at once.4

Governance

Frameworks, standards & regulation

No single rulebook governs the sector. Instead, a layered mix of voluntary frameworks, prudential examination guidance, contractual standards, and binding regulation applies simultaneously — and the more stringent control tends to win where they overlap.

Voluntary frameworks and examination guidance

The NIST Cybersecurity Framework provides the common risk-management vocabulary; its 2.0 revision is increasingly the baseline self-assessment tool. As of 2026, with the retirement of the older FFIEC Cybersecurity Assessment Tool, institutions are directed toward NIST CSF 2.0 and CISA's Cybersecurity Performance Goals as primary risk-management references.21 The Federal Financial Institutions Examination Council's handbooks remain the practical yardstick examiners apply across log management, third-party risk, authentication, and incident response.22

Binding rules on data and disclosure

The Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act's Safeguards Rule requires financial institutions to maintain a written information security program; the Federal Trade Commission's 2023 amendments converted what had been a principles-based standard into prescriptive technical mandates — including encryption of nonpublic personal information, multi-factor authentication, a designated qualified individual to run the program, and breach notification to the FTC within 30 days for events affecting 500 or more consumers.2324 The Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard governs any entity handling cardholder data; its version 4.0 added 64 new requirements over the prior version and became fully mandatory on 31 March 2025.24

State and securities regulation

New York's "first-in-the-nation" cybersecurity regulation, 23 NYCRR Part 500, took effect in 2017 — partly in response to breaches such as the 2014 JPMorgan Chase intrusion that affected about 76 million households — and was substantially amended on 1 November 2023.25 The amended rule requires a documented program and a qualified CISO who reports at least annually to the board, mandates notice to the Department within 72 hours of a determined cybersecurity incident and within 24 hours of a ransom payment, and adds heightened obligations for large "Class A" companies along with dual senior-executive/CISO compliance certifications.2627

At the federal securities level, the SEC's 2023 rules require public companies to disclose a material cybersecurity incident on Item 1.05 of Form 8-K, generally within four business days of determining the incident is material, with a limited delay available where the U.S. Attorney General certifies a substantial risk to national security or public safety.28 Compliance for most registrants began in December 2023, with smaller reporting companies phased in during 2024.29

Cross-sector incident reporting (CIRCIA)

The Cyber Incident Reporting for Critical Infrastructure Act of 2022 will, once its implementing rule is final, require covered critical-infrastructure entities to report substantial cyber incidents to CISA within 72 hours and ransom payments within 24 hours.30 CISA estimates the rule could cover more than 300,000 entities; the final rule has slipped, with the agency targeting around May 2026 amid heavy public comment and funding disruptions.31

The compliance reality

A single large institution may be answerable to the FFIEC agencies, the FTC, the SEC, the PCI Council, state regulators such as NYDFS, and — soon — CIRCIA, each with its own definitions and clocks. Harmonizing these overlapping reporting timelines (4 business days, 72 hours, 24 hours, 30 days) is itself an open governance problem.

Defense

Controls, tooling & collective defense

Underneath the frameworks sit the actual controls. The case ledger above maps neatly onto them: most of those incidents would have been blunted by a small set of well-understood practices applied consistently.

Technical controls

The recurring lessons are unglamorous. Patch and vulnerability management would have closed the Apache Struts flaw before Equifax was breached.13 Least-privilege identity management and hardened metadata services would have prevented a firewall compromise from becoming a 106-million-record breach at Capital One.8 Phishing-resistant multi-factor authentication is now treated as a baseline by both the GLBA Safeguards Rule and NYDFS, which has warned that SMS- and push-based methods are weak against modern attacks.27 Add to these network segmentation, endpoint detection and response, comprehensive logging and monitoring, and encryption of sensitive data in transit and at rest, several of which are explicit GLBA and PCI requirements.23

Resilience and recovery: data vaulting

Because finality and ransomware make "recover the records" the decisive question, the sector built a shared resilience standard. Sheltered Harbor, conceived in 2015 out of the public–private "Hamilton" exercise series and established as an industry-led not-for-profit, specified an encrypted, immutable, air-gapped data vault: participating firms convert customer-account data into a common format each night, encrypt it, and store it in isolated storage — often with a "restoration partner" able to make customers whole if an institution cannot recover in time.3233 The model emphasizes resilience by design and data integrity for the continuity of critical customer services.34 After roughly a decade, the Sheltered Harbor organization began winding down, with its specification and assets transferred to FS-ISAC and managed through a Resilience Congress of senior member executives.35

Collective defense and information sharing

The sector's distinctive advantage is that its firms defend together. The Financial Services Information Sharing and Analysis Center, formed in 1999, now serves over 5,000 member firms across some 75 countries, operating as a "neighborhood watch" for cyber and physical threat intelligence.36 FS-ISAC runs sector-wide exercises — including its Steel Resolve scenario for a large attack on a global institution, dedicated post-quantum-computing exercises, and Sheltered Harbor recovery drills — and participates in CISA's cross-sector Cyber Storm.37 The FSSCC, in partnership with Treasury, develops sector strategy and maintains tooling such as the sector cybersecurity profile and an automated assessment tool aligned to FFIEC guidance.4

Frontiers

Open problems & research frontiers

The sector's defenses are mature by critical-infrastructure standards, yet several problems remain unsolved or are actively worsening.

Concentration and third-party risk

The same outsourcing and cloud adoption that improve baseline security also concentrate systemic risk into a handful of providers. The 2024 outage that hit roughly 60 credit unions through a single cloud IT vendor, and the structural dependence of the Treasury market on a single settlement agent revealed by the ICBC incident, are warnings that the sector's resilience is only as strong as its least-redundant shared node.96 Mapping and stress-testing these concentrations across firms remains an open analytical challenge.

Ransomware as systemic risk

Ransomware has shifted from a nuisance to a financial-stability concern. The industrialized ransomware-as-a-service model behind LockBit lowers the barrier to entry, and a credit-rating agency warned after the ICBC attack that such incidents highlight financial institutions' growing exposure to payment-interruption risk as attacks become more frequent and severe.17 Recovery economics are also deteriorating, with average ransomware recovery costs rising year over year.

The quantum transition

The most consequential long-horizon problem is cryptographic. A sufficiently powerful quantum computer running Shor's algorithm would break the public-key cryptography (RSA and elliptic-curve) that underpins TLS, VPNs, and key exchange across the financial system. The threat is already present through "harvest now, decrypt later": adversaries can collect encrypted traffic today and decrypt it once the capability arrives — a live risk for any data whose confidentiality must outlast the arrival of cryptographically relevant quantum computers.38 A Federal Reserve working note has examined harvest-now-decrypt-later specifically as a present-day risk to financial networks and ledgers.39 NIST finalized its first post-quantum cryptography standards in 2024 — FIPS 203 (ML-KEM), FIPS 204 (ML-DSA), and FIPS 205 (SLH-DSA) — and major providers have begun deploying hybrid post-quantum key exchange in production, with over half of human-initiated traffic on one large network already using post-quantum key agreement by late 2025.3840 The open problem for finance is crypto-agility at scale: migrating decades of embedded cryptography across legacy core systems before the decryption horizon closes.

AI, regulatory harmonization, and talent

Generative AI cuts both ways — accelerating defenders' detection and triage while lowering the cost of convincing phishing, deepfake-enabled fraud, and attacks on AI-driven models themselves. Meanwhile the proliferation of overlapping reporting regimes (SEC, NYDFS, GLBA, and the forthcoming CIRCIA rule) creates genuine compliance friction that policymakers are explicitly trying to harmonize.31 And persistent shortages of cyber talent, especially acute for the thousands of community banks and credit unions operating with lean teams, leave the long tail of the sector under-defended even as the largest institutions invest heavily.

Coda

Closing the books

The financial sector defends an unusual asset: not a power plant or a pipeline, but the authoritative records and messaging that let the rest of the economy function. Its incident history shows the stakes climbing from website outages to irreversible theft to systemic settlement risk, and its response — layered regulation, shared resilience standards like data vaulting, and unusually mature collective defense through FS-ISAC and Treasury coordination — is among the most developed of any critical-infrastructure sector. The unfinished work is concentration risk, industrialized ransomware, the looming quantum transition, and the harmonization of a tangle of overlapping rules. The objective, as ever, is not to prevent every breach but to ensure that when one happens, the money keeps moving and the ledger can still be trusted.
