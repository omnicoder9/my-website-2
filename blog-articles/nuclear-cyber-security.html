Defending the Atom: Cyber Security in the U.S. Nuclear Sector

The technology that keeps a reactor safe is increasingly the technology an adversary wants to reach. A field guide to the industrial systems at stake, the cross-sector blast radius of a compromise, and the layered engineering and regulation built to prevent one.

01 — Orientation

The sector at a glance

A nuclear power plant is one of the few machines humanity has built where a software fault and a public-safety event live in the same building. That proximity is what makes the sector's cyber security both unusually disciplined and unusually consequential. Get it wrong and the failure modes are not measured in lost revenue but in radiological release, grid collapse, and decades of cleanup.

The United States operates the largest commercial nuclear fleet on Earth: 94 power reactors spread across 54 plants in 28 states, supplying roughly 19 percent of the nation's electricity and running at an average capacity factor near 92 percent — meaning these units are generating almost all the time.1 In the federal taxonomy of critical infrastructure, this fleet — together with research reactors, fuel-cycle facilities, the radioactive sources used in medicine and industry, and the transport and storage of nuclear materials — forms the Nuclear Reactors, Materials, and Waste Sector, one of sixteen sectors whose incapacitation would have a debilitating effect on national security, the economy, or public health.23

Those sixteen sectors were codified in Presidential Policy Directive 21 and reaffirmed in 2024 by the National Security Memorandum on Critical Infrastructure Security and Resilience, which kept the count at sixteen and assigned each a Sector Risk Management Agency.34 The Department of Homeland Security holds that coordinating role for the nuclear sector, but the binding cyber security regulation belongs to a different body entirely: the Nuclear Regulatory Commission (NRC), whose 2009 rule made cyber protection a license condition and whose inspectors enforce it on site.5 The dual structure — DHS for sector coordination, the NRC for enforceable safety regulation — is a recurring theme in everything that follows.

This briefing walks the full chain: the industrial technology that actually sits inside a plant and where it can be attacked; the threat actors and consequences that make it a target; the historical incidents that turned theory into evidence; the way a nuclear compromise can ripple across the other fifteen sectors; the layered regulatory, architectural, and procedural controls that defend it; and the unsolved problems that should worry anyone planning the next generation of reactors.

02 — The machine

Industrial technology & attack surface

To reason about nuclear cyber risk you have to know what is being defended. Unlike an enterprise network, where the crown jewels are data, the assets that matter most in a reactor are the systems that turn sensor readings into physical action — and the ones whose failure can defeat the plant's safety case.

From sensors to control rods: the I&C stack

A plant's nervous system is its instrumentation and control (I&C). At the bottom are field devices — neutron-flux detectors, temperature and pressure sensors, valve and pump actuators. Above them sit the control systems that interpret those signals, and above those the reactor protection system, the function that automatically trips the reactor and inserts control rods when parameters cross safe limits. Safety-critical I&C is held to a special engineering bar, internationally categorised through frameworks such as IEC 61226 and the Class 1E reliability requirements, which impose strict fault-tolerance and verification obligations on both hardware and software.6 A separate human-facing layer — the control-room displays, alarm panels, and the safety parameter display system (SPDS) that summarises core conditions for operators — is how humans stay in the loop.

The NRC's regulatory model does not protect every computer equally. It protects functions: anything supporting safety, security, or emergency preparedness — the SSEP functions — and the digital devices that perform or support them are designated critical digital assets (CDAs). The implementing guidance, Regulatory Guide 5.71, provides the method for identifying CDAs and then applies a catalogue of more than one hundred security controls drawn from NIST's standards, organised into eighteen families across technical, operational, and management classes.7 Identifying the right CDAs is itself a security problem: miss one, and a control you assumed was protected is not.

Analog heritage, digital future

One feature of the U.S. fleet has quietly shaped its threat profile for decades: most of these reactors were designed in an analog era, and much of their core protection logic is implemented in hardwired, non-programmable circuitry that simply has no network port to attack. Officials have repeatedly pointed to this analog heritage as a reason the operational risk from a typical intrusion has so far been limited.8 But the heritage is eroding. Plants are modernising to digital I&C for obsolescence, accuracy, and cost reasons; new builds are digital from the start; and each conversion replaces a circuit that could not be hacked with software that can be. The central design question of the modern era, as one analysis put it, is no longer whether digital systems exist but exactly where the digital boundary is drawn and whether the architecture around it can hold.9

The IT / OT divide — and the bridges across it

Every nuclear site runs two broad worlds of technology. The operational technology (OT) world — the I&C, the safety systems, the physical-security systems — values determinism, availability, and integrity above all and changes slowly. The information technology (IT) world — corporate email, business planning, document management, engineering workstations, the procurement chain — values confidentiality and agility and changes constantly. Almost every real nuclear incident in the historical record began in the IT world or in a vendor's, and the recurring question was whether it could reach the OT world.

The bridges that make that crossing possible are predictable: a contractor's laptop carried inside the perimeter, a connection that quietly bypasses a firewall, a removable USB drive, a poisoned software update from a trusted supplier, or a remote-access pathway opened for legitimate maintenance and never properly closed. Hold that list in mind — the case studies in Section 04 are, almost without exception, variations on exactly these bridges.

03 — The adversary

Risks, threat actors & consequences

The nuclear sector is targeted not in spite of how hard it is to hit but because of what a hit would mean. A reactor sits at the intersection of three things adversaries prize: a large block of grid generation, a trove of sensitive design information, and a symbol whose disruption produces outsized public fear. Each draws a different kind of attacker.

Who is at the keyboard

  • Nation-state intelligence and military services. The most capable and persistent threat. Russian, Chinese, North Korean, and Iranian groups have all been tied to operations against energy and nuclear targets, ranging from espionage to the pre-positioning of access for future disruption.
  • State-sponsored sabotage developers. The actors behind purpose-built industrial malware — the lineage that runs from Stuxnet onward — who invest in understanding specific control systems well enough to make them misbehave physically.
  • Criminal and hacktivist actors. Ransomware crews and ideologically motivated groups, generally aiming at business networks, data, and reputation rather than reactor controls, but capable of forcing costly shutdowns and eroding public confidence.

Security researchers have warned for years that the risk of a serious cyberattack on the nuclear industry is rising, with worst-case scenarios that run from grid blackouts to reactor damage; the 2011 Fukushima disaster, though not a cyber event, is routinely invoked to make the consequences of disrupting a plant concrete rather than abstract.10

What can actually go wrong

It helps to separate consequence classes, because they call for different defenses:

The most strategically alarming risk is the quietest. In 2024, U.S. and allied agencies warned that the Chinese state-sponsored group tracked as Volt Typhoon had been pre-positioning inside U.S. critical-infrastructure networks — using living-off-the-land techniques that abuse legitimate built-in tools to avoid detection — and in some victims had maintained that access for at least five years. The observed compromises clustered in the communications, energy, transportation, and water and wastewater sectors.11 Officials assessed that the goal was not espionage but positioning to launch disruptive or destructive attacks during a future crisis, including attacks that could jeopardize physical safety.12 A nuclear operator's IT network is exactly the kind of foothold such a campaign seeks.

A reactor is attacked for its grid, its blueprints, and its symbolism — three different prizes that draw three different adversaries to the same fence.
04 — The record

A history written in incidents

Nuclear cyber security is unusual among security disciplines in how much of its doctrine was written in response to specific, documented events. Five of them, taken together, map the whole threat surface — and each one reduces, at its core, to one of the IT/OT bridges named in Section 02.

2003Davis-Besse · Ohio · USAWorm / collateral

The Slammer worm and the firewall that wasn't there

In January 2003 the fast-spreading SQL Slammer worm reached the Davis-Besse plant — not through the front door, but through a contractor's connection that bypassed the corporate firewall entirely.13 The worm crossed from the consultant's network to the licensee's corporate network and onto the plant's process-control network.14 Its traffic congested the network and knocked out the Safety Parameter Display System — the operators' summary of core conditions — for nearly five hours, and crashed the plant process computer as well; recovery of the two systems took roughly five and six hours respectively.15 The plant happened to be offline at the time and an independent analog display remained available, so there was no safety event — but the lesson was permanent: a worm with no interest in nuclear plants had still reached a safety-monitoring system because a single trusted connection silently bridged two worlds.13

2010Natanz · IranTargeted cyber-physical sabotage

Stuxnet: when software broke hardware

Stuxnet is the event that ended the debate about whether code could cause physical destruction. Reaching an air-gapped enrichment facility via infected USB drives, it used four previously unknown Windows vulnerabilities to spread, then sought out Siemens Step7 engineering software and the S7 programmable logic controllers governing the centrifuges.16 Its payload was specific to a configuration of SIMATIC WinCC and PCS7 software driving particular variable-frequency drives over the PROFIBUS protocol; it altered rotor speeds to damage the machines while replaying normal readings so operators saw nothing wrong.17 The campaign reportedly spread to more than 200,000 computers and forced Iran to replace on the order of a thousand centrifuges.16 It also pioneered the supply-chain tradecraft that defines the modern threat: stolen, legitimate code-signing certificates that made the malware look trustworthy to the systems it infected.18

2014Korea Hydro & Nuclear Power · South KoreaEspionage / coercion

Stolen blueprints and a Christmas ultimatum

In December 2014 the operator of South Korea's reactor fleet was breached through spear-phishing emails sent to thousands of current and former employees.19 The attackers exfiltrated reactor blueprints, equipment manuals, personal data on around ten thousand staff, and a confidential safety-analysis code, then published material on social media with a demand that specific reactors be shut down — coupling data theft to public coercion.20 Investigators attributed the operation to the North Korea–linked Kimsuky group. Control systems were not reached, but the incident showed how the information half of information security — design documents that provide reconnaissance for a future physical or cyber attack — is a target in its own right.19

2014–2017Wolf Creek · Kansas · USAState-sponsored intrusion

Dragonfly reaches a U.S. nuclear operator's business network

Between 2012 and 2017, a unit of Russia's FSB — tracked by researchers as Dragonfly, Energetic Bear, and Berserk Bear — ran a two-phase campaign against the global energy sector, first hiding the Havex malware inside legitimate software updates from industrial vendors, then turning to targeted spear-phishing of engineers who work directly with control systems.21 The second phase reached more than 3,300 users at over 500 companies, including the U.S. Nuclear Regulatory Commission and the Wolf Creek Nuclear Operating Corporation in Kansas, whose business network was compromised.21 The attackers' technique of choice was a fake résumé for a control-engineering job, mailed to senior engineers — and DHS and the FBI issued an amber-level alert to the industry.22 Crucially, the intrusion stayed on the business side; officials stressed there was no operational impact, and noted that the predominantly analog U.S. fleet is less exposed to remote manipulation than a fully digital one would be.8

2019Kudankulam · IndiaEspionage / staging

DTrack and the value of a foothold

In 2019, the administrative network of India's largest nuclear plant was found infected with DTrack, a remote-access tool associated with the North Korea–linked Lazarus group; the breach was traced to an infected personal computer connected to the internet-facing administrative network, isolated from the critical control network.23 Analysts read the malware sample, which carried credentials specific to the plant's internal network, as evidence of domain-level access on the IT side and a likely attempt to stage for something larger.24 The episode is the clearest modern reminder that an administrative-network compromise is rarely the goal — it is the beachhead.

Pattern recognition

Across every case the entry vector is mundane — a contractor link, a USB stick, a phishing email, a tampered update. The sophistication lives in what happens after entry. This is why nuclear cyber defense invests so heavily in the boundaries between zones rather than relying on keeping every adversary out of every network.

05 — Blast radius

The cross-sector cascade

Critical infrastructure is a system of systems. The sixteen sectors are not independent silos; each has a designated risk-management agency precisely because their interdependencies are a national-level concern, and disruption in one can propagate into others.25 A nuclear compromise is dangerous partly for what it does to the reactor and partly for what the reactor does for everyone else.

Nuclear as a dependency for other sectors

Because nuclear units supply roughly a fifth of U.S. electricity as steady baseload, an event that removes gigawatts of that generation at once stresses the broader Energy sector and, through it, almost everything. Energy is the sector other sectors run on. The downstream dependents are not hypothetical:

  • Water & Wastewater — pumping, treatment, and chemical dosing all depend on power.
  • Healthcare & Public Health — hospitals depend on grid power, and the sector also relies on the medical isotopes produced within nuclear materials operations.2
  • Financial Services — data centers, exchanges, and payment systems require uninterrupted power and cooling.
  • Communications — the networks that carry the monitoring and control signals of every other sector.
  • Chemical and Critical Manufacturing — continuous processes that fail unsafely or expensively when power is lost; CISA explicitly maps Critical Manufacturing's heavy dependence on energy and on the components it in turn supplies back to the grid.26
  • Transportation and Water control systems that lose their power source cascade into public-safety effects.

Other sectors as a path into nuclear

The dependency runs both ways, and that is the security problem. A reactor relies on the Communications sector for connectivity, on Critical Manufacturing and the broader supply chain for its digital components, on Transportation for fuel and materials, and on Information Technology for the software it runs. Each of those relationships is a potential ingress. The Dragonfly campaign's first phase — hiding malware in legitimate industrial software updates — was an attack on nuclear through its supply chain, not on it directly.21 And Volt Typhoon's strategy of lodging in smaller, less-defended providers that serve larger operators is a deliberate exploitation of these inter-sector seams.11

This bidirectional coupling is why federal oversight bodies stress system-wide risk assessment rather than per-facility hardening alone: the Government Accountability Office has repeatedly framed critical-infrastructure protection as a cross-sector coordination problem, with each risk-management agency obligated to assess sector risk and feed it into a national picture.27

06 — The defense

Mitigation: rules, architecture, controls

The good news is that nuclear is among the most heavily and specifically regulated cyber environments in existence. Its defense rests on three layers that reinforce one another: a regulatory mandate, a defensive architecture, and a control catalogue — wrapped in an engineering philosophy that treats cyber risk as a safety problem.

The regulatory stack

The foundation is 10 CFR 73.54, the 2009 NRC rule that requires power-reactor licensees to protect the digital systems supporting safety, security, and emergency-preparedness functions, and to fold cyber security into their physical-protection programs.5 The rule is performance-based: it demands a documented program providing defense-in-depth and the capability to detect, prevent, respond to, mitigate, and recover from attacks.28 Two documents tell licensees how to satisfy it. Regulatory Guide 5.71 is the NRC's own acceptable method — the CDA-identification framework and the NIST-derived control set — and its 2023 Revision 1 refreshed the guidance against current NIST and IAEA standards and clarified the defense-in-depth expectations.728 The Nuclear Energy Institute's NEI 08-09 is an industry template that nearly every plant used to write the cyber security plan the NRC reviews, approves, and then inspects against.29 A separate rule, 10 CFR 73.77, sets cyber-event notification timelines so that incidents are reported, not buried.29

The defensive architecture: levels and diodes

The structural heart of the model is a graded defensive architecture — typically drawn as five concentric security levels, numbered 0 through 4, separated by boundary devices such as firewalls and data diodes where digital traffic is monitored and restricted. The most safety-critical assets live at the innermost, highest-protection level; an attacker must defeat boundary after boundary to reach them.30 The signature device of this architecture is the data diode: a hardware-enforced, physically one-way channel. RG 5.71's reference architecture allows information to flow outward from the protected levels — so the business side can read plant data — while making inbound flow physically impossible.31 In practice this is how operators replicate process-historian data to corporate users without ever opening a return path into the control network.32

The NRC is emphatic on one point that distinguishes serious defense from box-checking: a data diode is necessary but not sufficient. Defense-in-depth means multiple complementary, redundant boundaries and controls, such that the failure of any single protection does not compromise a safety, security, or emergency-preparedness function — a diode alone does not satisfy the rule.28 Research on advanced-reactor architectures frames the same idea as three reinforcing strategies — fortification around critical assets, chokepoints that constrain access, and anti-access measures that keep adversaries out of sensitive zones — with diodes enforcing the one-way boundary between the highest levels.33

The control catalogue and the standards around it

Inside the architecture, each CDA is hardened with a catalogue of more than a hundred security controls adapted from NIST's SP 800-53 and the industrial-systems guidance SP 800-82, grouped into eighteen families spanning technical, operational, and management measures.7 The sector also threads together the broader standards landscape: the voluntary NIST Cybersecurity Framework, which CISA tailored into dedicated implementation guidance for nuclear owners and operators;34 the international industrial-automation security standard IEC 62443 and its nuclear-specific sibling IEC 62645; and the safety-classification standards such as IEC 61226 that govern how trustworthy I&C software must be in the first place.6

Cyber-informed engineering

The most important recent shift is conceptual. Rather than bolting security onto a finished design, cyber-informed engineering treats the possibility of digital compromise as a hazard to be engineered against from the first design decision — choosing architectures that isolate critical functions, minimise the attack surface, and, where the consequence is severe enough, retain a non-digital means of achieving a safety function so that no amount of code can defeat it.31 It is the same instinct that left Davis-Besse with an analog backup display in 2003, formalised into a design discipline.

Why nuclear is different

In most sectors, security and safety are separate programs. In nuclear they converge: the regulator that licenses the reactor also mandates the cyber program, the controls protect functions rather than devices, and the highest aspiration is not merely to detect attackers but to engineer systems whose worst outcomes remain bounded even if an attacker wins a given layer.

07 — The frontier

Open problems & research frontiers

The defensive model described above was built for a fleet of large, analog-heavy, slowly changing reactors. Almost every force now reshaping the sector pushes against the assumptions that made it work.

Digital modernisation erodes the air gap

Every analog control replaced with digital I&C, and every new connection added for analytics or efficiency, moves the digital boundary outward and gives an attacker more to reach. The hard engineering and regulatory question is no longer whether digital systems exist but where to draw the boundary and whether the defensive architecture is strong enough to resist the integration pressures — vendor platforms, data analytics, adjacent data-center models — that quietly want to punch holes through segmentation.9

Small modular and advanced reactors change the shape of the problem

The next generation is being designed digital-first, and with operating concepts that would have been unthinkable in the legacy fleet: heavy automation, remote monitoring, even aspirations toward remote operation, autonomous functions, and digital-twin modelling.35 Studies of small modular reactor (SMR) cyber risk warn that manipulating turbine control, voltage regulation, or the sensors feeding them could produce unstable power or, in the worst case, contribute to a cascading grid failure — and that the appeal of siting SMRs in remote or distributed locations amplifies the consequences of losing remote management.36 Fleets of nominally identical factory-built modules also raise a homogeneity risk: a single exploitable flaw could apply across many units at once.36

The AI and data-center collision

Two trends are arriving together. Technology firms have committed billions of dollars to pair nuclear generation — especially SMRs — with the enormous, around-the-clock power demand of AI data centers, with the first such deployments targeted around the end of the decade.37 At the same time, AI and cloud tooling are entering reactor engineering itself. That introduces failure modes the existing regime was never written for: data poisoning and adversarial manipulation of models, continuous data flows to cloud environments, and globally distributed development teams — none of which fit cleanly into an air-gapped, static-by-design safety model.38 The regulations assumed digital safety systems were fixed and that plant data did not stream off-site to learning systems; that world is ending.38

Supply chain and counterfeit components

Stuxnet's deepest lesson was about trust in the supply chain, and that problem has only grown. As reactor components are sourced globally — sometimes from suppliers operating under different cyber standards — the risk of counterfeit, fraudulent, or tampered digital parts rises, and the IAEA continues to flag the difficulty of managing it as original vendors end support and adversaries deliberately substitute compromised parts.39 Fifteen years on, analysts note that the core conditions Stuxnet exploited — trusted code, trusted updates, trusted media crossing an air gap — are still present in industrial environments.18

IT/OT convergence and the workforce gap

The new technologies blur a line the sector spent decades enforcing. SMR oversight in particular demands people fluent in both IT and OT security, and regulators have warned that safety and security teams operating in separate silos undermine effective oversight of the converged systems now appearing in nuclear facilities.35 Building that cross-disciplinary workforce — and the research base for securing AI-enabled, remotely operable, cloud-connected reactors — is the open problem that underlies all the others.

The defenses that worked were written for reactors that could not be reached. The reactors now being built are designed, from the first line of code, to be reachable.The central tension of the next decade
08 — Assessment

Closing assessment

The U.S. nuclear sector's cyber security record is, by the standards of critical infrastructure, genuinely strong. No documented attack has reached the controls of an American reactor; the regulatory regime is specific and enforced; the architecture is principled; and the analog heritage of the existing fleet has provided a margin of safety that no policy could have bought. The incidents that did occur — Davis-Besse, Wolf Creek, Kudankulam abroad — all stopped at monitoring or business networks, which is exactly where the model is designed to stop them.

But the conditions that produced that record are dissolving. Digital modernisation, small modular and advanced reactors, AI integration, the data-center power rush, and a globalised supply chain are converging to make tomorrow's reactors more connected, more autonomous, and more software-defined than anything the current rules contemplated — at the same moment that the most capable adversaries have shifted from stealing secrets to pre-positioning for disruption. The task ahead is to carry the sector's hard-won discipline — defense-in-depth, protect the function not the device, keep a non-digital fallback where the consequence is severe — into a generation of machines built to be reachable. The atom can still be defended. The defense simply has to be re-engineered as fast as the machine is.
