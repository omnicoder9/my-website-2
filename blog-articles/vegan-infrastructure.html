The Infrastructure of Choice: How Better Tools Are Dismantling the Friction That Keeps People Eating Animals

The most underrated engine of vegan growth isn't moral persuasion — it's the quiet, unglamorous work of making plant-based choices easy. Apps, databases, certification systems, and supply chain transparency tools are building a new decision architecture, and it's working.

By Nicholas Knapp May 1, 2026 14 min read

Every time someone abandons a half-formed intention to eat more plant-based food, it rarely happens in a dramatic moment of philosophical reversal. It happens in a supermarket aisle, scanning an ingredient list full of incomprehensible additives, or on a foreign street unable to read the menu, or in a moment of exhaustion when the vegan option requires twelve more steps than the default. Friction wins. And friction, it turns out, is one of the most powerful forces in dietary behaviour.

The field of behavioural economics has a name for this: the friction cost of decision-making. When a choice requires more cognitive effort, time, or uncertainty, people reliably default to what is easiest — not what they believe is best.[1] For decades, the default was animal products. Built into every grocery label, every restaurant menu, every supply chain, the infrastructure of food systematically made veganism harder. The good news is that a new wave of information tools is systematically dismantling those barriers, one scan at a time.

"Making vegan choices easier is not a compromise with purity — it is the most strategically sound investment we can make in long-term change."

The Friction Problem: Why Good Intentions Aren't Enough

Research published in the journal Nutrients (2024) identified convenience as one of the most significant structural barriers to plant-based diet adoption. A quantitative analysis from Finland found that a lack of time significantly impacted decisions to eat plant-based food, with a correlation coefficient of r = 0.64 — a strong positive relationship indicating that perceived time burden was a major deterrent.[2] Studies from New Zealand and Sweden corroborated this, noting that the perceived effort of preparing plant-based meals and an inability to easily identify suitable products in stores were key friction points.[2]

A separate review in Sustainability synthesised international literature using the Theory of Planned Behavior and found that for behaviour change to occur, perceived benefits must outweigh perceived barriers — and crucially, those barriers must be addressed structurally, not just rhetorically.[3] Telling people that veganism is good for the planet does not, by itself, reduce the cognitive load of decoding a twelve-syllable ingredient in a yoghurt.

The Decision Lab, a behavioural science consultancy, frames this sharply: the problem of promoting plant-based diets is no longer primarily about spreading awareness. It is about the architecture of choices — the environment in which decisions are made.[4] Change that environment, and you change behaviour at scale without requiring a moral epiphany from everyone involved. This is where information infrastructure enters as a decisive tool.

1. Ingredient Scanners: The Pocket Vegan Expert

The most immediately impactful category of vegan infrastructure is the smartphone barcode scanner. These apps address one of the most concrete friction points in the vegan experience: standing in a shop aisle unable to determine whether a product contains hidden animal derivatives like casein, gelatin, shellac, carmine, or any of the dozens of other ingredient names that most consumers cannot identify on sight.[8]

How They Work

Modern vegan scanner apps use smartphone cameras to read barcodes and cross-reference product data against ingredient databases. Upon scanning, the app analyses each listed ingredient — including additives with E-numbers — and flags any that are of animal origin, ambiguous, or uncertain. The better apps also account for labelling laws that differ between countries, which is particularly important given that ingredient transparency regulations vary significantly between the EU and the US, for example.[8]

Key Apps to Know

Leading ingredient scanners include Open Food Facts (open-source, available on iOS and Android, covers vegan and vegetarian status, allergens, environmental data, and nutritional scores), WhatsVegan (AI-powered ingredient analysis), VegVisor (barcode scanner with machine learning ingredient classification), and Is It Vegan? (instant barcode scanning with a streamlined UI). The open-source Veganify webapp offers similar functionality with a database of over 3 million products, without a subscription requirement.[8,9,10]

Critically, AI has moved the field forward. Vision-Language Models (VLMs) and Optical Character Recognition (OCR) now allow some apps to analyse ingredient lists directly from a photo of a product's packaging — useful when products have no barcode or when scanning fails on damaged packaging.[11] These same models can handle low-contrast text, distorted packaging, and non-standardised label formats, substantially broadening accessibility across different markets.

One important limitation to acknowledge: scanner apps are only as good as the databases they draw from. Crowdsourced databases are vulnerable to errors and gaps, particularly for smaller brands and regional products. Apps that rely on company self-reporting for vegan status also carry inherent uncertainty. The best tools are explicit about the confidence level of their determination and flag ambiguous ingredients rather than issuing false certainty.

2. Restaurant Finders: Solving the Social Friction Problem

One of the most persistent social barriers to veganism — particularly in the early stages of transition — is the anxiety of eating out. Research has found that vegans frequently face practical difficulties locating suitable options in restaurants, and that the need to scrutinise menus or make special requests can create social discomfort that discourages persistence.[2] Restaurant-finding apps directly attack this barrier.

HappyCow is the category leader. Active for over 25 years, it now lists over 256,000 restaurants and food businesses across 185 countries, with over 1.8 million user reviews and 3 million photos. Its filter system allows users to search by fully vegan, vegetarian, vegan-friendly, gluten-free, raw, or organic criteria, and it offers offline map access — critical for travellers without data access.[6] The community-curation model means that reviews come primarily from vegans and vegetarians, providing relevant, context-specific feedback rather than generic dining assessments.

Abillion is a strong alternative, with an emphasis on user-generated reviews and a social networking dimension that connects plant-based eaters globally. Both apps illustrate an important principle: the social infrastructure of veganism matters as much as the product infrastructure. When eating plant-based is associated with community, discovery, and pleasure rather than sacrifice and deprivation, the cultural valence of the choice shifts.[4]

3. Product Databases: The Commons of Food Knowledge

Open Food Facts is arguably the most strategically important infrastructure project in food transparency globally. A non-profit association of volunteers, it has assembled over 4 million products from 150 countries through the contributions of more than 25,000 people. Unlike commercial food databases, it is released under an open database licence — meaning anyone can access, analyse, or build upon it for any purpose, without cost.[5]

The breadth of data is extraordinary. For each product, the database captures ingredients, allergens, nutritional facts, additives, certifications (including organic, vegan, and vegetarian labels), production origins, packaging information, traceability codes, and environmental scores. This makes it useful not just for ingredient scanning but for research, public health analysis, product development, and regulatory advocacy.[12]

"Open Food Facts treats food data as a public good — precisely the framing that vegan infrastructure needs to adopt at scale."

The system also generates meaningful secondary effects. The popularity of open food apps encourages companies to provide clearer and more comprehensive labelling, and in some cases to contribute directly to open databases, effectively creating market pressure for greater transparency. This is a powerful leverage mechanism: the infrastructure doesn't just help existing vegans navigate the market — it gradually reshapes the market itself.[11]

Beyond Open Food Facts, the OpenFDA database provides regulatory-level food data for US products, and several commercial databases (Nutritionix, Edamam) serve as the backbone for recipe apps and meal planners. The challenge for the vegan movement is ensuring these systems include robust vegan-status fields — a gap that still exists in many commercial implementations.

4. Certification Labels: Trust Infrastructure at a Glance

Certification labels are perhaps the most visible piece of vegan information infrastructure. They perform a specific and crucial function: collapsing the cognitive work of verifying a product's ethical status into a single visual signal that requires no database lookup, no ingredient analysis, and no expert knowledge. For the majority of consumers who will never scan a barcode in a supermarket, a trusted label is the decision architecture.

The Certification Landscape

  • Vegan Action "Certified Vegan" (V-Label)

    Verifies that products contain no animal ingredients and have not been tested on animals. One of the most widely recognised vegan-specific marks in North America, administered by Vegan Action. Unlike cruelty-free certifications, this mark directly verifies ingredient composition.[13]

  • Leaping Bunny (CCIC / Cruelty-Free International)

    The Leaping Bunny Program, formed by the Coalition for Consumer Information on Cosmetics (CCIC), provides globally recognised assurance that products have not undergone animal testing during development. It is regarded as the gold standard for cruelty-free certification, requiring annual recommitment and covering the entire supply chain — not just the finished product. Important caveat: Leaping Bunny certification addresses animal testing, not ingredient sourcing; some certified brands may still use animal-derived ingredients such as beeswax or lanolin.[14,15]

  • PETA's "Beauty Without Bunnies" & Vegan Logo

    PETA operates two separate marks: one for cruelty-free products (based on company self-declaration via questionnaire) and a vegan logo for products that additionally exclude all animal-derived ingredients. The cruelty-free mark is considered less rigorous than Leaping Bunny due to its reliance on self-reporting; the vegan mark provides more relevant assurance for ingredient-conscious consumers.[15,16]

  • BeVeg International Vegan Certification

    A global vegan trademark that certifies products across food, beverages, cosmetics, and supplements. Its international scope makes it particularly useful for cross-border supply chains and export markets where regional labels may not be recognised.[16]

  • The EU V-Label (European Vegetarian Union)

    Used across Europe as a standardised mark for vegan and vegetarian products, regulated by the European Vegetarian Union. It operates across 30+ countries, providing a consistent trust signal in a region where labelling laws vary significantly by member state.[13]

Critical Distinction

Consumers and advocates must understand that "cruelty-free" does not mean "vegan." A cruelty-free certification typically verifies that a product was not tested on animals — it says nothing about whether its ingredients are derived from animals. Always look for certifications that explicitly verify ingredient composition if that is your concern. The Certified Vegan (Vegan Action) and BeVeg marks are designed specifically for this purpose.[13,15]

A persistent challenge in the certification landscape is proliferation and greenwashing. Because terms like "vegan," "cruelty-free," and "natural" are unregulated in many jurisdictions, any company can print them on packaging without third-party verification. The EU's FIC Regulation establishes baseline requirements for ingredient and allergen labelling, but eco-labelling and ethical claims remain largely voluntary — meaning that harmonisation and standardisation are ongoing regulatory debates. This underscores why third-party certification, however imperfect, remains essential trust infrastructure.[11]

5. Supply Chain Transparency Tools: Tracing the Chain

The frontier of vegan information infrastructure is supply chain transparency — the ability to trace a product not just by its label but through every upstream step of its production. This matters because vegan activism increasingly recognises that ingredient lists alone are insufficient. A product may contain no animal-derived ingredients while its production process relies on animal agriculture inputs (some fertilisers), or its supply chain implicates labour conditions that intersect with animal harm. Transparency tools are beginning to address this complexity.

Blockchain as a Transparency Mechanism

Research published in 2025 found that the adoption of blockchain in food supply chains correlates with increased consumer trust and satisfaction, and that integrating this technology in restaurants increases the perceived safety and traceability of food. The most cited real-world demonstration of blockchain's impact remains the IBM–Walmart collaboration, which reduced the time to identify the origin of food products from six days to 2.2 seconds.[7]

For vegan purposes, immutable supply chain records could, in principle, allow a consumer or advocate to verify that a product certified as vegan actually sourced ingredients from facilities without animal processing, or that "organic" claims correspond to actual farming practices. The technology is mature enough; what lags is adoption incentive for producers with something to hide. Regulatory pressure — particularly from the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) and Germany's Supply Chain Due Diligence Act — is beginning to provide that incentive.[11]

Open Data and Ingredient Origin Tracing

Beyond blockchain, open data enables origin tracing, allowing users to view and compare the provenance of ingredients — whether local or global, fair trade or conventional, organic or not — as well as emerging environmental and sustainability metrics. Open Food Facts' Eco-Score, based on data from ADEME (the French environmental agency), already provides product-level environmental impact data including carbon footprint estimates and packaging recyclability.[12] This is not yet vegan-specific supply chain verification, but it builds the evidentiary foundation for it.

The Strategic Case: Infrastructure as Leverage

The tools described above share a common strategic logic: they reduce friction at decision points. Behavioural economics is clear that the path of least resistance wins most dietary decisions, most of the time. Choice architecture — making the right decision irresistible through the design of the decision environment — is a proven mechanism for shifting behaviour without requiring people to override strong habits and preferences.[1]

Information infrastructure is precisely this kind of choice architecture. When a scanner app renders instant verdicts on a product's vegan status, it eliminates the cognitive cost that causes people to default to familiar, presumably safe options. When HappyCow makes plant-based dining a five-second decision rather than a twenty-minute research project, it removes the friction that causes people to abandon vegan intention in real-world situations. When a certification label provides immediate visual assurance, it eliminates the need for expert knowledge as a prerequisite for ethical purchasing.

The cumulative effect is a ratchet effect on culture. As more people successfully navigate vegan choices because the tools make it easy, plant-based products build market share. Growing market share incentivises more producers to seek certification and improve labelling. Better labelling generates more data for databases. Richer databases make scanner apps more accurate. More accurate apps make vegan shopping less intimidating. The cycle reinforces itself, gradually normalising plant-based choices across the population — including among people who would never describe themselves as vegan activists.

What You Can Do

Contribute product data to Open Food Facts. Add listings to HappyCow. Choose certified products when possible and explain the distinctions to others. Advocate for mandatory standardised vegan labelling at the regulatory level. Support open-source infrastructure projects financially. And whenever you scan a product and find it's vegan, remember: someone before you added that data, and your scan might help train the next improvement.

Conclusion: The Unglamorous Work That Changes Everything

There is nothing dramatic about scanning a barcode. There is no moral clarity in an open-source database. A certification label won't go viral. But this unglamorous infrastructure is, in many ways, the most consequential work being done for veganism right now — because it operates at the level of everyday reality, where most human decisions actually live.

The animal agriculture industry benefits enormously from friction: from the difficulty of identifying hidden ingredients, from the scarcity of vegan-friendly restaurants in unfamiliar towns, from the opacity of supply chains, and from the cognitive effort required to shop ethically without specialist knowledge. Every tool that reduces that friction is a direct challenge to the structural advantage that animal products currently enjoy.

The goal is not a world where everyone has read every animal rights argument and been persuaded. The goal is a world where the plant-based option is, more often than not, the default — not because it was mandated, but because someone built the infrastructure that made it easy enough that most people chose it. That work is already underway. It deserves our attention, our contributions, and our appreciation for the leverage it provides.
