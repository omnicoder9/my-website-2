Essay  ·  Technology & Society

The Oldest Game:
Fraud in the Age of AI

by Nicholas Knapp

From a sunken ship in 300 BC to a $25 million deepfake video call — a history of deception, and why generative AI may be its most dangerous enabler yet.

April 2026  ·  Long Read  ·  ~20 min

Before there was money, there was fraud. Before there was writing, there was lying. The impulse to deceive for personal gain is not a modern pathology — it is, arguably, one of the oldest human technologies. What changes across history is not the motive but the medium. Every communications revolution — the printing press, the telegraph, the telephone, email, the internet — has been almost immediately co-opted by those who wish to exploit it. We are now at the edge of a new such revolution, one that may prove to be the most consequential in the history of deception.

Generative artificial intelligence does not merely give fraudsters better tools. It collapses the barriers that once constrained them: the need for technical skill, the cost of scale, the requirement of physical presence, and the irreducible human tells that once allowed victims to detect a lie. This essay traces fraud from its ancient origins to the digital present, examines how each major technological shift changed the landscape of deception, and confronts what the generative AI moment means for trust itself.

Chapter I

The Original Sin: Ancient Fraud

The earliest documented fraud in recorded history did not involve money — it involved a ship. In approximately 300 BC, a Greek sea merchant named Hegestratos took out what was known as a "bottomry" policy: a loan secured against his vessel and its cargo of corn, to be repaid with interest upon delivery. His plan was simpler and more audacious than most: he would sink the ship, keep the cargo, and collect on the policy without ever delivering it. His crew caught him in the act, and Hegestratos drowned attempting to escape — likely the earliest recorded case of a fraudster being undone by the very scheme he devised.[1]

The case is instructive not merely because it is old, but because it establishes the template: wherever a financial instrument or system of trust exists, someone will attempt to exploit it. Insurance fraud, at its heart, has not changed in 2,300 years.

In ancient Egypt, tax collectors were known to use inaccurate weights and measures to force citizens to overpay their grain taxes — skimming the difference into their own pockets. Severe penalties existed, but enforcement was patchy, and the asymmetry of information between state official and ordinary farmer created fertile ground for exploitation.[2] The pattern recurs: power, information asymmetry, and weak oversight are the perennial ingredients of fraud.

In 193 AD came one of antiquity's most brazen schemes. The Praetorian Guard — Rome's elite imperial bodyguard — assassinated Emperor Pertinax and proceeded to auction the Roman Empire itself to the highest bidder. The winning bidder, Didius Julianus, reportedly paid 25,000 sesterces per soldier. He lasted sixty-six days before being executed.[3] It was, in its way, a form of advanced-fee fraud: payment for an asset the sellers did not legitimately own.

Even the Renaissance was not immune. A twenty-year-old Michelangelo — struggling for income — artificially aged a marble sculpture of a sleeping cupid, sold it as a genuine antiquity to Cardinal Raffaele Riario, and collected handsomely before the forgery was discovered.[4] The cardinal demanded his money back. Art forgery, another ancient form of deception, continues to cost the art market hundreds of millions annually.

A Note on Definition

Throughout this essay, "fraud" is used broadly to encompass any act that uses deliberate deception for personal gain — including lying, forgery, impersonation, misrepresentation, and the manufacture of false evidence. The legal definitions vary by jurisdiction; the human behavior they describe does not.

Chapter II

Technology as Multiplier: Five Revolutions in Fraud

The deepest pattern in the history of fraud is straightforward: every major communications technology expands the scale and reach of deception before defenses catch up. The interval between innovation and exploitation has generally been short.

c. 1440
The Printing Press: Scaling the Lie

Gutenberg's press democratized both knowledge and misinformation. By the 17th century, "canards" — sensational and frequently fabricated news sheets — were being sold on Parisian street corners.[5] Benjamin Franklin himself authored fabricated atrocity stories about "scalping Indians" to build support for the American Revolution.[5] The press enabled fraud at scale for the first time: false prospectuses, counterfeit share certificates, and the infamous "snake oil" pamphlets of the 19th century all relied on cheap, printed legitimacy.[6]

1820s–1900s
The Confidence Era: Mail, Telegraph & the Modern Con

The 1800s saw an explosion of confidence schemes made possible by postal infrastructure and the telegraph. Gregor MacGregor sold land in a fictional Central American country called "Poyais" to thousands of Scottish settlers in 1821 — complete with forged maps, a fake currency, and a fabricated constitution.[7] The "Spanish Prisoner" scam — a letter claiming a jailed nobleman needed money to retrieve his fortune — became a template for what would later be known as the Nigerian Prince email.[8] The telegraph allowed false financial information to travel faster than the truth, enabling stock manipulation across continents.

1920s–1980s
The Telephone: Voice as Identity

The telephone introduced something revolutionary to fraud: the human voice. It conferred trust in ways a letter could not. Charles Ponzi's original scheme of 1920 — costing investors an estimated $20 million in eight months — relied heavily on personal calls and charm as much as any financial mechanism.[9] Frank Abagnale Jr., whose exploits were dramatized in Catch Me If You Can, impersonated an airline pilot, a doctor, and a lawyer in the 1960s, relying on telephone calls to pre-verify his false credentials before any physical encounter.[10] The phone made the lie warmer, more personal, harder to question.

1990s–2000s
Email & the Internet: Industrial-Scale Deception

The first email spam was sent in 1978, just seven years after the invention of electronic mail.[11] By the mid-1990s, America Online users were targeted by mass phishing campaigns — hackers posing as AOL employees to harvest login credentials at extraordinary scale.[11] The internet did not invent any new category of fraud; it industrialized every existing one. Advanced-fee scams reached millions of inboxes simultaneously. Credit card fraud became a global business. The cost of attempting a fraud dropped toward zero, meaning fraudsters could profit even from minuscule conversion rates on mass campaigns.

2010s
Social Media & Cryptocurrency: Trust Laundering

Social platforms created new attack surfaces: synthetic identities, fake reviews, manipulated markets, romance scams. Cryptocurrency introduced a payment system designed to be irreversible and pseudonymous — ideal properties for a fraudster. OneCoin, a cryptocurrency Ponzi scheme, defrauded investors of over $4 billion.[12] By 2024, scammers had stolen a record $16.6 billion from Americans — a 33% increase from the prior year, according to the FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center.[12]

"Innovation often enables exploitation. Fraudsters capitalized on rapid societal changes, manipulating new systems and products as well as unregulated markets to deceive both investors and consumers."

— MoneyThumb, "The Totally Fascinating History of Fraud," 2025
Chapter III

The Defenses: What Made Certain Frauds Obsolete

It would be misleading to tell only the story of fraud's expansion. Technology has also made many historical frauds dramatically harder to execute. Understanding this dynamic is essential to grasping what is so unusual — and alarming — about the current moment.

Photography and fingerprinting made long-term identity fraud far more risky in the late 19th century. Before photographic ID, an impostor like Frank Abagnale operated in an environment where identity was verified largely through social cues, paper documents, and telephone calls. The introduction of photo ID, and later biometric verification, raised the cost of physical impersonation significantly.

Cryptography transformed financial communications. The shift to encrypted card transactions, digital signatures, and two-factor authentication made many forms of financial fraud that were trivial in the 1980s — copying a magnetic stripe, intercepting a wire transfer, forging a paper check — substantially more difficult and costly.

Machine learning-based fraud detection changed the economics of transaction fraud. Systems that can analyze thousands of behavioral signals — typing patterns, device fingerprints, transaction geography, timing — made mass credit card fraud substantially harder. Mastercard now scans one trillion data points per transaction to assess authenticity.[13]

Caller ID and spam filters reduced the yield from mass telephone and email fraud campaigns. The Nigerian Prince scam — once sophisticated enough to net $703,000 from Americans in a single year — became a cultural punchline precisely because its signals became recognizable.[8]

The pattern across history is an arms race: new fraud capabilities emerge, defenses adapt, and fraudsters pivot. What matters is the interval between the new capability and the effective defense. When that interval is long, victims are many. The question now is: how long is the interval with generative AI?

Chapter IV

The Generative Moment: What Has Changed

In February 2024, a finance employee at Arup, a global engineering firm, joined a video conference call with what appeared to be the company's CFO and several senior executives. They instructed him to wire $25 million to a series of accounts. He complied. Every person on that call — every face, every voice — was a deepfake, entirely AI-generated.[14]

This is not science fiction. It happened. And it is, by the standards of what the technology is now capable of, a comparatively simple attack. What makes generative AI categorically different from previous fraud-enabling technologies is not merely that it improves existing capabilities — it eliminates the human bottlenecks that previously constrained fraud at scale.

Consider what the technology now enables, capability by capability:

Voice cloning. With as little as three seconds of audio — easily harvested from a public podcast, social media video, or corporate webinar — tools can generate a voice clone that achieves an 85% match to the original speaker.[15] The "tells" that once allowed listeners to identify synthetic audio — unnatural intonation, missing breath sounds, wrong rhythm — have largely disappeared in the latest generation of models. A researcher writing in Fortune at the end of 2025 stated that voice cloning has crossed what she called "the indistinguishable threshold," and that some major retailers were receiving over 1,000 AI-generated scam calls per day.[16] A 2024 McAfee study found that one in four adults had experienced an AI voice scam, and one in ten had been personally targeted.[15]

Face and video deepfakes. The Arup attack used multi-person video conference deepfakes in real time. In 2019, this would have required significant technical expertise and computing resources. By 2025, consumer tools from companies including OpenAI, Google, and a wave of startups allowed anyone to generate polished audiovisual content — complete with realistic faces, synchronized lip movements, and contextually appropriate responses — from a simple text prompt.[16] Human detection rates for high-quality video deepfakes have fallen to approximately 24.5%.[17]

Hyper-personalized phishing. Large language models can generate grammatically perfect, culturally contextually appropriate phishing emails in any language, tailored to a specific individual's professional role, recent public activity, and known contacts. Over 82% of phishing emails are now created with AI assistance, allowing fraudsters to produce convincing scams up to 40% faster than before.[18] The "Nigerian Prince" scam was detectable partly because its grammar and syntax were inconsistent with claimed identities. That tell is gone.

Synthetic identity fraud. AI systems can generate complete synthetic identities — photographs, biometric signatures, browsing histories, even synthetic credit histories — that fool automated verification systems. Forty-six percent of fraud experts have already encountered synthetic identity fraud in their work.[17] Deepfake face-swap attacks used to bypass biometric identity verification increased by 704% in 2023 alone.[17]

"The emotional realism of a cloned voice removes the mental barrier to skepticism. If it sounds like your loved one, your rational defenses tend to shut down."

— IT.wisc.edu, cited in American Bar Association, Voice of Experience, 2025

Scale and automation. Previous fraud required human operators: a caller on the phone, a writer composing an email, a graphic designer forging a document. Generative AI allows the entire attack pipeline — target selection, content generation, delivery, and follow-up — to be automated. AI agents can conduct thousands of simultaneous social engineering attacks without human intervention. This shifts fraud from an artisanal activity to an industrial one.

Chapter V

The Liar's Dividend: When Everything Can Be Faked

There is a second-order effect that may prove even more consequential than fraud itself: the epistemic collapse of trust in authentic evidence. Scholars have begun calling this the liar's dividend — the ability of a bad actor to credibly dismiss authentic recordings, photographs, or documents as probable AI-fabrications.[19]

The mechanism is simple and pernicious. In a world where deep-fake video of any person saying anything can be generated in minutes, the existence of genuinely incriminating video, audio, or documentary evidence loses its probative force. A politician caught on tape can plausibly claim it was fabricated. An executive captured in a damaging conversation can assert deepfake. A fraudster producing a false document gains a mirror defense: the genuine contract can be questioned on the same grounds as the forgery.

This dynamic extends beyond fraud into the foundations of institutional trust. Falsified medical records, fabricated legal evidence, manufactured financial disclosures, and synthetic witness testimony all become more plausible threats when the tools to create them are widely available and the public knows it.[19] A UNESCO report on synthetic media noted that the liar's dividend "creates a double bind where neither belief nor disbelief in evidence can be justified," fundamentally destabilizing the epistemological basis of institutions that depend on verifiable information.[19]

The World Economic Forum has ranked the proliferation of AI-generated misinformation among the world's top systemic risks.[19] This is not hyperbole. The rule of law, financial markets, democratic elections, and the practice of medicine all depend on the assumption that evidence can be authenticated. When that assumption erodes, the institutions themselves erode with it.

Case Study: The "Grandparent Scam," Upgraded

In July 2025, Sharon Brightwell of Dover, Florida, received a call from what sounded exactly like her daughter — crying, claiming to have been in a car accident, lost her unborn child, and facing criminal charges. She needed $15,000 for a lawyer immediately.

Brightwell wired the money. Her real daughter was fine. The voice had been cloned from audio harvested from social media.[20]

This is the "Grandparent Scam" — a telephone con that has operated for decades — augmented by AI voice cloning. The scam's core mechanism (urgency, emotional pressure, impersonated family member) is unchanged. The tool that makes it nearly undetectable is entirely new. The FBI reported over 845,000 imposter scams in 2024, many leveraging AI-generated voices.[21]

Chapter VI

What Comes Next: The Near-Future of AI Fraud

Current deepfake attacks are, in important respects, still relatively static — pre-rendered videos, cloned audio for single calls, scripted phishing messages. The frontier is moving toward something far more dynamic: real-time, interactive, fully synthetic personas.

The next generation of fraud will feature AI agents capable of sustaining extended relationships — as a romantic partner, a financial advisor, a corporate colleague — adapting in real time to the target's responses, adjusting their emotional register, surfacing contextually appropriate memories, and maintaining consistent behavioral patterns over days, weeks, or months. Romance scam losses already topped $1.3 billion in 2024, and 40% of online daters report being targeted by dating scams.[22] Victims now report video calls with people who are real-time deepfakes — synthetic faces that smile, nod, and react convincingly enough to sustain months of emotional investment.[22]

In the corporate realm, AI-powered business email compromise (BEC) is evolving from text-based impersonation to multimodal attacks combining cloned voices, synthetic video, and contextually appropriate document forgeries. The CEO of WPP, one of the world's largest advertising conglomerates, was targeted in 2025 by scammers who cloned his voice for a fake Teams-style call, instructing staff to transfer funds and share credentials.[23]

Several specific threat vectors deserve particular attention in the coming years:

Neural interface fraud. As brain-computer interface technology matures, novel attack surfaces will emerge around neural data — the possibility of exploiting biometric neural signals for authentication bypass, or manipulating behavioral data to compromise identity systems. This remains speculative but is already attracting research attention from security communities.[24]

Agentic fraud at scale. AI agents — software systems that can browse the web, send emails, place calls, and execute multi-step plans autonomously — will be deployable for fraud at a cost approaching zero, with no human bottleneck in the attack chain. The economics of fraud will shift from a labor-intensive activity constrained by the number of skilled operators to a capital-intensive one constrained only by compute.

AI-generated false evidence in legal proceedings. Fabricated video, audio, and document evidence submitted to courts, insurers, and regulatory bodies represents a systemic threat to legal and financial institutions that current evidence-authentication protocols are not designed to address.[19]

Chapter VII

Defenses: The Arms Race Continues

The arms race has not stopped; it has accelerated. The market for AI deepfake detection is growing at a compound annual rate of 28–42%, projected to reach $15.7 billion by 2026.[17] Promising technical defenses include:

Content provenance and watermarking. Google's SynthID system has watermarked over 10 billion pieces of AI-generated content with pixel-level signals designed to survive compression and editing.[13] The EU AI Act requires that AI-generated content be labeled and machine-readable as such.[25] The Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity (C2PA) is working to build cryptographic signing into cameras and media platforms, creating an auditable chain of authenticity.

AI-powered detection. The same machine learning techniques that enable deepfake generation can be applied to detection — analyzing micro-expressions, subtle temporal inconsistencies, compression artifacts, and phonetic patterns that distinguish synthetic from genuine audio and video. OpenAI has launched a deepfake detection initiative for images.[25]

Out-of-band verification protocols. For high-stakes business communications, the simplest and most robust defense remains non-digital verification: pre-agreed code words, callbacks to known numbers, mandatory in-person confirmation for large transactions. Many security professionals recommend establishing a "family safe word" for emergency calls — a phrase only genuine family members would know, that AI cannot conjure from scraped audio.[23]

Behavioral and institutional reform. UNESCO and security researchers increasingly argue that purely technical approaches are insufficient. The speed of synthetic media production now exceeds the capacity of detection systems to keep pace. The deeper challenge, they contend, requires redesigning institutional knowledge-validation systems — how courts authenticate evidence, how financial institutions verify counterparties, how media organizations certify sources — to function in an environment where visual and audio evidence cannot be assumed genuine.[19]

Conclusion

The Nature of the Threat

Hegestratos tried to sink his ship for profit in 300 BC. He failed, partly because his scheme required him to be physically present at the scene of the fraud. The 2024 Arup deepfake attack required its perpetrators to be present nowhere — not in body, not in genuine voice, not in authentic face. The crime was committed entirely through synthetic proxies, controlled remotely, at a profit of $25 million.

The history of fraud is the history of the exploitation of trust. Trust in documents. Trust in signatures. Trust in institutional authority. Trust in the printed word. Trust in a voice on the telephone. In each era, the question is the same: what evidence do we rely on to verify that someone is who they claim to be, and that what they are showing us is real?

Generative AI does not merely make that question harder to answer. It makes the asking of it uncomfortable, laborious, and socially costly in ways that fraudsters count on. The mother who pauses to verify her daughter's voice is the mother who — in a genuine emergency — might delay too long. The employee who demands secondary verification of every executive instruction creates friction that organizations resist. The friction of vigilance is a feature, from the fraudster's perspective.

What is unusual about this moment is not that it represents a new chapter in the old story of fraud. Every technology has been a new chapter. What is unusual is that, for the first time, the technology attacks not a specific authentication mechanism but the category of evidence itself. Not "here is a forged signature" but "signatures cannot be trusted." Not "this photograph is fake" but "photographs cannot be trusted." Not "that voice was synthesized" but "voices cannot be trusted."

That is a different kind of threat. It is not a fraud to be prosecuted. It is an epistemological condition to be navigated — by individuals, institutions, legal systems, and democracies — at the same time that the technology creating it is accelerating faster than the defenses being built against it.

We have been here before, in some sense. Every new communications technology widened the playing field for deception before the defenses caught up. The printing press, the telegraph, the telephone, email — all arrived to a chorus of concern that proved, in retrospect, partially warranted but ultimately surmountable. Human institutions adapted. Defenses emerged. Norms changed.

The question is whether the interval this time — between the new capability and the effective response — will be short enough to prevent the kind of structural damage to institutional trust that, once done, is very difficult to undo. That question does not yet have an answer. But the history of fraud suggests that waiting for the answer before acting is itself a form of risk.
