01 ·· Definition & Conceptual FoundationThe Purple Teamer

In most mature security organisations, offensive capability and defensive capability have historically been divided between two distinct functions: the red team, which simulates adversary attacks, and the blue team, which monitors, detects, and responds to those attacks. The purple teamer exists precisely at the seam between them.

The Colour Metaphor Red (attacker) mixed with blue (defender) produces purple—a deliberately blended function that inherits the knowledge base of both disciplines and translates one into the other.

A purple team is the combination of both offensive and defensive cybersecurity professionals working as a unified function, rather than as separate, competing entities.[6] The SANS Institute defines a purple team as the collaborative fusion of red team and blue team working together to strengthen an organisation's defence capabilities.[1] Crucially, purple teaming is sometimes a dedicated organisational team and sometimes a function—a mindset and methodology applied by existing red and blue personnel, rather than a permanent headcount of its own.[34]

The purple teamer's core intellectual contribution is the ability to hold two opposing mental models simultaneously: thinking like a threat actor while evaluating a defensive system's ability to observe and interrupt that actor. This dual fluency—what practitioners sometimes call "adversary empathy"—is rare and difficult to develop, making experienced purple teamers among the most versatile professionals in the field.

Offense informs defense and defense informs offense. Understanding how attacks work is critical for defense.

— SANS Institute[30]

The purple team's key responsibilities are to facilitate structured interactions between red and blue teams and guide improvements for maximum results. Each sub-team retains distinct responsibilities, while the purple function aligns efforts toward shared security objectives for improved detection and response capabilities.[1]

02 ·· History & InnovationFrom Siloed Testing to Collaborative Security

The concept of organised offensive security testing predates purple teaming by decades, but the modern purple team role emerged from the recognition that siloed red and blue functions were producing diminishing returns. The problem was structural: after a red team engagement, the blue team received a report, remediated findings, and the cycle repeated—but without real-time knowledge transfer, the same gaps often reappeared.

1990s
Red Teaming Formalised

Military-derived red team concepts were adapted for information security, producing the first formal penetration testing services. Red and blue functions operated with near-complete information separation.

2009
Cyber Kill Chain Published

Lockheed Martin introduced the Cyber Kill Chain model, providing a shared vocabulary for describing attack progression. This common language was a prerequisite for meaningful red–blue dialogue.

2013
MITRE ATT&CK Project Begins

MITRE initiated its Adversarial Tactics, Techniques, and Common Knowledge project internally, building a structured taxonomy of adversary post-compromise behaviours observed in real networks—the foundational tool of modern purple teaming.[29]

2015
MITRE ATT&CK Public Release

The first public release of the ATT&CK for Enterprise matrix introduced 9 tactics and 96 techniques, marking a significant milestone in how security teams could analyse and describe threat intelligence.[31] The emphasis on Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures (TTPs) shifted the paradigm from indicator-based to behaviour-based defence.[31]

2017–18
Purple Teaming Gains Industry Traction

The term "purple team" entered widespread use in conference talks, vendor offerings, and job postings. MITRE ATT&CKcon (2018) featured sessions on using ATT&CK to create better detections through adversary simulation—a seminal articulation of the purple team methodology in practice.[23]

2019
Formalisation of Frameworks and Training

SCYTHE released the Purple Team Exercise Framework (PTEF), codifying a methodology for joint exercises. Training organisations began offering dedicated purple team curriculum. Defensive Origins launched its Atomic Purple Team course and subsequently pivoted to "Applied Purple Teaming."[37]

2021–23
Breach & Attack Simulation (BAS) Matures

BAS platforms such as AttackIQ, Picus, SafeBreach, and Cymulate reached enterprise adoption, enabling continuous automated purple team-style testing without requiring manual coordination. The concept of the "virtual purple team" entered the practitioner lexicon.[39]

2024–25
Purple Skills Drive Career Resilience

Research by INE Security (2025) confirmed that professionals with purple team capabilities command salaries significantly higher than traditional specialists and show faster career progression to senior roles.[46] The 2024 ISC² Cybersecurity Workforce Study found that 64% of organisations cite skills gaps—rather than headcount—as their primary security challenge, with purple team hybrid skills identified as the critical gap.[50]

03 ·· Equivalent Job TitlesWhat the Role Is Called

Purple teaming is a function that can be embodied by a range of job titles, depending on organisational maturity, structure, and whether the role is primarily internal or consultant-facing. Common titles include:

Purple Team Engineer / Purple Team Analyst
The most explicit designation. Used by large enterprises and financial institutions. Signals a dedicated role rather than a function distributed across existing staff.[48]
Purple Team Lead / Purple Team Manager
Senior variant responsible for planning and coordinating exercises across red and blue functions. The Purple Team Lead oversees operations and coordinates between teams to ensure exercises are effective and learnings are applied.[2]
Detection Engineer
A closely aligned title where the primary output is SIEM rules, EDR signatures, and detection logic—informed by adversary emulation. Many detection engineers operate in a de facto purple team capacity.[51]
Security Validation Engineer
Emphasises the control-validation dimension: measuring whether security tools actually perform as configured. Used at technology companies and MSSPs.[51]
Threat-Informed Defence Analyst
A title popularised by the Center for Threat-Informed Defense (a MITRE-affiliated research initiative). Signals a strategic focus on operationalising CTI to guide both offensive testing and defensive tuning.
Adversary Emulation Specialist
Leans toward the offensive side of the spectrum. Common in red team consultancies that have expanded their services to include collaborative feedback loops with client blue teams.
Cloud Purple Team Engineer
A cloud-specific variant explicitly named in some enterprise job postings—e.g., Cloud Red Team Blue Team (CRTBT) engagements at hyperscale organisations.[47]
Cybersecurity Strategist / Cybersecurity Architect (Purple Focus)
More senior titles held by purple team professionals who have transitioned from hands-on exercise execution to programme design and executive risk communication.

04 ·· QualificationsCertifications, Education & Experience

Educational Background

Employers commonly seek a bachelor's degree in Computer Science, Information Security, Cybersecurity, Information Systems Engineering, or a related technical field.[53] However, because purple teaming demands hands-on craft skill that formal education rarely develops, many practitioners arrive through non-traditional paths: military signals intelligence, self-directed CTF competition, or progression through SOC analyst and penetration tester roles.

Prior Experience Profile

Typical career pathways into a purple team role include:

  • SOC Analyst → Threat Hunter → Purple Teamer: Builds strong detection intuition, log familiarity, and incident response context before adding offensive simulation skills.
  • Penetration Tester → Purple Teamer: Arrives with deep offensive tradecraft; needs to develop detection engineering and blue-side communication skills.
  • Incident Responder → Purple Teamer: Strong adversary profiling capability from post-breach forensics; brings realistic threat modelling to exercise design.

Most purple team job postings cite 5+ years of information security experience, with demonstrated hands-on exposure to both offensive tooling (Metasploit, Cobalt Strike, Burp Suite) and defensive platforms (SIEM, EDR/XDR, SOAR).[47] Cloud environment experience across at least one major provider (AWS, Azure, GCP) is increasingly a baseline requirement at technology and financial services firms.[47]

Soft Skills & Cognitive Attributes

  • Ability to communicate technical and risk-based information to audiences ranging from SOC analysts to executive boards[48]
  • Structured thinking for exercise design and after-action documentation
  • Intellectual empathy—the ability to reason from an adversary's perspective without operational context bias
  • Comfort with ambiguity and incomplete information, characteristic of realistic threat scenarios
  • Cross-team diplomacy: navigating the cultural tension between red and blue functions

05 ·· Responsibilities, Problems & ChallengesWhat the Role Actually Does

Core Responsibilities

  • Facilitating knowledge sharing and collaboration between red and blue teams, taking charge of security initiatives that require input from both offensive and defensive sides[3]
  • Designing and executing joint attack simulation exercises aligned to real-world threat actor TTPs
  • Conducting realistic attack simulations that mirror real-world threat actor behaviour, and documenting every step of the attack methodology including successful techniques and failed attempts[1]
  • Identifying gaps in detection and response capabilities, then translating those gaps into concrete defensive improvements: new logging requirements, SIEM rule changes, EDR configuration updates, and network segmentation recommendations
  • Validating the effectiveness of existing security controls against a defined threat model
  • Generating actionable, robust defensive recommendations such as new data collection requirements, mitigations, system reconfigurations, and detection analytics[15]
  • Maintaining an ATT&CK-mapped inventory of tested techniques and coverage gaps over time, enabling measurable progress tracking
  • Producing after-action reports that communicate findings in formats appropriate for both technical and executive audiences
  • Staying current with threat intelligence—tracking new TTPs employed by APT groups relevant to the organisation's industry and geography[47]

Central Problems of Interest

The Detection Gap Problem

A blue team cannot defend against what it cannot see. Purple teamers are primarily concerned with identifying and closing visibility gaps: attack techniques that produce no alert, no log entry, or no analyst action. After running simulations, the purple teamer diagnoses whether a failure to detect was caused by missing telemetry, a poorly tuned rule, an analyst workload problem, or a process failure.[51]

The Assumptions-of-Security Problem

Organisations frequently operate on untested assumptions: "our EDR would catch that," or "our network segmentation prevents lateral movement." The purple teamer's exercise framework surfaces the difference between assumed security and demonstrated security—a distinction that becomes critical during actual incidents.

The Adversarial Relevance Problem

Generic penetration test findings often fail to reflect the actual threat actors targeting a given organisation. Purple teamers contextualise simulations using cyber threat intelligence (CTI) to focus exercises on the TTPs of real threat groups—for example, APT33 for an energy company, or FIN7 for a retailer—producing results with direct defensive relevance.

Organisational Culture Challenges

The success of a purple team heavily relies on effective communication and coordination between red and blue teams. The lack of communication and coordination can hinder the effectiveness of the purple team and undermine the collaboration between the two teams.[7] Red teamers may resist sharing tradecraft they consider proprietary; blue teamers may perceive exercises as criticism of their work. Purple teamers frequently serve as cultural brokers, framing exercises as learning systems rather than evaluations.

Structural Challenge

In a red team exercise, resentment can develop between opposing sides who lose sight of the ultimate goal. Purple teams exist in part to prevent this: by making both teams collaborators in a shared objective, they prevent the adversarial exercise from becoming adversarial interpersonally.[8]

Resource and Scope Challenges

Allocating the necessary resources—skilled personnel, tooling, and lab infrastructure—is a persistent challenge. The red and blue teams require adequate resources to perform their respective roles effectively, and organisations must ensure they have the necessary budget and support to build and maintain a successful programme.[7]

06 ·· Processes & MethodologiesHow Purple Teaming Works in Practice

Purple team exercises are structured collaborative events, not ad hoc testing. Several formal process frameworks govern how they are planned, executed, and documented.

The Purple Team Exercise Framework (PTEF)

Created by SCYTHE and updated through version 4 (PTEFv4), the Purple Team Exercise Framework codifies a complete methodology including ATT&CK v18 alignment, guidance on continuous purple teaming with automated regression testing, BAS integration, validation metrics, and remote exercise logistics.[22] It is the closest thing the discipline has to a universal process standard.

The ATT&CK-Based Three-Step Engine

The most widely adopted operational pattern—described by Picus Security and others—proceeds in three phases:[34]

  1. Simulate: Red team members execute adversary TTPs from the ATT&CK matrix against production or lab environments. Each technique is performed with the blue team aware of the general threat profile but not the specific timing or vector.
  2. Detect: Blue team members attempt to observe, alert on, and contextualise the simulated attack activity using their existing tooling. Coverage is measured technique-by-technique.
  3. Improve: The purple team facilitates a structured debrief. For each gap, the team jointly designs remediation: a new detection rule, a logging configuration change, a playbook update, or a control addition. These improvements are implemented and the technique is re-simulated to validate the fix.

Atomic Testing vs. Full Campaign Exercises

There are two main types of purple team assessments. The first is Atomic Testing—the use of scripted techniques that are easy to run and automate, each targeting a single ATT&CK technique. The second is full-campaign Purple Teaming, which is more comprehensive and sequences techniques into realistic kill-chain scenarios to ensure detection capabilities are thoroughly tested and not easily bypassed by trivial adjustments.[35]

Continuous Purple Teaming

In mature organisations, individual exercises evolve into a continuous feedback loop. Automated BAS platforms run subsets of the TTP library on a scheduled or change-triggered basis, flagging regressions when a deployment update inadvertently breaks a detection rule. This operationalises purple teaming as a standing capability rather than a periodic event.

Tabletop Exercises

Not all purple team work involves live technical simulation. Tabletop discussions—structured conversations in which red and blue team members walk through hypothetical attack scenarios against documented response procedures—are used to test incident response plans, validate communication protocols, and identify process gaps without system impact.

Threat Intelligence Integration

Effective purple teaming is threat-informed by design. Exercise planners use CTI reports, ISAC feeds, and ATT&CK group profiles to select the subset of techniques most relevant to current adversaries targeting the organisation's sector. This ensures that the exercises address actual risk, not merely theoretical coverage of the ATT&CK matrix.

07 ·· Tools & TechnologyThe Purple Team Technology Stack

Adversary Emulation & Simulation Platforms

MITRE CALDERA
Open Source · Adversary Emulation
Developed by MITRE, CALDERA uses the ATT&CK framework to automate complex, multi-stage attack patterns. It operates on an agent/server model: a central server orchestrates agents deployed across a lab network, executing adversary techniques autonomously.[21] Particularly valuable for simulating Lateral Movement, Command & Control, and Persistence chains.
Atomic Red Team
Open Source · Technique Library
A community-driven library of small, modular tests mapped to individual ATT&CK techniques. Each test runs independently to validate a specific control—ideal for quickly verifying whether EDR or SIEM alerts trigger correctly after rule changes.[26] Developed by Red Canary; widely used in detection engineering pipelines.
VECTR
Freemium · Campaign Management
A purple team exercise management platform from Security Risk Advisors. VECTR enables planning of test campaigns, recording of per-technique detection outcomes (detected / not detected / alerted), remediation tracking, and ATT&CK coverage reporting.[27] Serves as the audit trail and measurement layer of purple team programmes.
SCYTHE
Commercial · Full-Chain Emulation
An enterprise adversary emulation platform supporting true kill-chain campaign simulation, campaign customisation, auto-pausing for real-time analysis, and integrated reporting.[51] Supports OT/ICS environments and integrates with BAS workflows for continuous validation.
ATT&CK Navigator
Open Source · Visualisation
MITRE's own matrix visualisation tool. Purple teams use it to build coverage heatmaps—showing which techniques have been tested, which are detected, and which remain blind spots. Layers can represent threat actor profiles, tested techniques, and detection coverage simultaneously.[27]
DeTT&CT
Open Source · Detection Measurement
A framework for mapping telemetry quality and detection capability to the ATT&CK matrix. Often deployed alongside VECTR to quantify whether log sources are sufficiently rich to detect each technique, before asking whether detection rules exist.[27]
PurpleSharp
Open Source · Windows Simulation
A C# adversary simulation tool for Windows environments. Generates realistic attack telemetry using .NET executables, helping blue teams validate EDR alerts and log sources in domain-joined environments.[26] Simple to integrate into automated testing pipelines.
Breach & Attack Simulation Platforms
Commercial · Continuous Validation
Platforms including AttackIQ, Picus, SafeBreach, and Cymulate automate continuous purple team-style testing. BAS acts as a "virtual purple team," regularly running simulated attacks and instantly reporting defensive efficacy without requiring human coordination for each test.[39]

Supporting Red Team Toolchain

Purple team exercises also draw on the standard red team toolset for realistic simulation: Cobalt Strike or Havoc C2 for advanced command-and-control simulation, Mimikatz for Credential Access testing, BloodHound for analysing and testing Active Directory attack paths, and Metasploit for Initial Access and exploitation simulation.[27]

Defensive Telemetry & Analytics

On the blue side of the exercise, purple teamers work with SIEM platforms (Splunk, Microsoft Sentinel, Elastic SIEM) to assess log coverage and tune detection rules; EDR/XDR platforms (CrowdStrike Falcon, SentinelOne, Microsoft Defender for Endpoint) to validate endpoint telemetry; and SOAR platforms to test automated response playbooks. The ELK stack is commonly used for post-exercise dashboard analysis, confirming detection coverage and identifying missing telemetry.[26]

08 ·· Industry & Domain VariationsSector-Specific Requirements

Financial Services

Financial institutions are subject to some of the most prescriptive regulatory requirements for adversarial testing. The EU's TIBER-EU (Threat Intelligence-Based Ethical Red Teaming) framework mandates threat-intelligence-led red team testing for critical financial infrastructure, requiring testing against institution-specific threat scenarios rather than generic attack libraries.[38] US financial institutions face equivalent expectations under FFIEC cybersecurity guidance. Purple teamers in financial services must understand these regulatory obligations and structure exercises to produce audit-ready documentation. Bank of America, for example, lists explicit purple team engineering roles requiring hands-on experience with ATM and banking system security alongside standard offensive tooling.[48]

Healthcare

Healthcare organisations must validate controls protecting electronic health records under HIPAA Security Rule requirements. Attack simulations targeting patient data access, medical device networks, and clinical system availability address the specific threats healthcare providers face.[38] Purple teaming in healthcare is complicated by the sensitivity of live system disruption: exercises must be carefully scoped to avoid impacting clinical operations, and medical device networks often require specialised approaches distinct from standard IT environments.

Critical Infrastructure / Operational Technology (OT/ICS)

Industrial control system environments introduce unique purple team challenges. Standard IT-based attack simulations cannot be executed directly in OT environments without risk of physical disruption. Emerging approaches use digital twin technology—creating a virtual image of the OT network—to enable safe, non-disruptive OT-BAS that simulates adversary behaviour against the digital replica rather than live systems.[44] Purple teamers in this domain need familiarity with ICS protocols (Modbus, DNP3, OPC-UA), the Purdue model of industrial network architecture, and threat groups such as Sandworm and OilRig that specifically target industrial infrastructure.[41]

Government & Defence

Government purple team roles frequently require security clearances and operate within classified network environments. The threat model is different—nation-state APTs with advanced persistent access capabilities—and exercises must be structured accordingly. Many roles in this domain operate under frameworks like NIST SP 800-115 (Technical Guide to Information Security Testing).[32]

Cloud-Native & Technology Companies

At hyperscale technology organisations, purple team exercises focus heavily on cloud-native attack surfaces: IAM privilege escalation, serverless function abuse, container escape, and supply chain compromise. Cloud purple team engineers must understand how traditional ATT&CK techniques manifest differently in IaaS and PaaS environments—for example, how credential theft operates in an environment using short-lived IAM role assumptions rather than static passwords.[47]

Regulated Industries Generally

Organisations handling sensitive data leverage purple team programmes to proactively test their defences and demonstrate compliance with regulations including GDPR, HIPAA, and PCI-DSS. BAS reporting capabilities are specifically used to illustrate adherence to regulatory requirements in audit contexts.[45]

09 ·· Sub-SpecialtiesDimensions Within the Role

Detection Engineering (Purple-Flavoured)

Detection engineers who work within a purple team framework focus primarily on writing, tuning, and validating SIEM detection rules and EDR signatures informed by live adversary simulation. The feedback loop is tight: a red team member executes a technique, the detection engineer observes whether their rule fires, adjusts the logic, and re-runs the technique until coverage is confirmed. This sub-specialty requires deep knowledge of query languages (SPL for Splunk, KQL for Sentinel) and log source taxonomy.

Threat Intelligence-Driven Purple Teaming

Some purple teamers specialise in integrating Cyber Threat Intelligence (CTI) reports into exercise planning. This means tracking specific threat actor groups, parsing IOC and TTP feeds, and translating raw intelligence into executable test cases. Practitioners in this lane often have backgrounds in CTI or threat hunting and may hold credentials such as GCTI.

Adversary Emulation Engineering

A more offensive-leaning sub-specialty focused on building high-fidelity adversary simulations: custom C2 infrastructure, beacon profiles that match observed malware communication patterns, and living-off-the-land techniques that evade signature-based detection. These practitioners often develop custom capabilities in C, C#, or Python and contribute to open tools like CALDERA plugins or Atomic Red Team tests.

BAS Operations & Continuous Validation

As BAS platforms mature, a technical sub-specialty has emerged around their operation: configuring and maintaining BAS environments, building custom scenario libraries, integrating BAS output with GRC platforms for automated risk reporting, and interpreting coverage dashboards for executive consumption. This sub-specialty is less "hacker" and more "security automation engineer with threat context."

OT/ICS Purple Teaming

As noted in §8, this variant demands a distinct technical skill set around industrial protocols and architectures. OT purple teamers often have backgrounds in engineering or operational technology before transitioning into cybersecurity, giving them the context to understand safety implications of simulated attacks.

10 ·· Closely Related RolesThe Surrounding Ecosystem

Red Teamer / Penetration Tester
The offensive counterpart. Red teamers simulate adversaries in adversarial mode—without coordinating with defenders in real time. Purple teamers draw on red team skills but shift the mode from competition to collaboration. Many purple teamers are former penetration testers who found greater organisational impact in the collaborative model.
Blue Teamer / SOC Analyst
The defensive counterpart. SOC analysts operate detection and response tooling. Purple teaming informs their work by validating whether their tools actually detect what they are assumed to detect, and improving the quality of the detection rules they rely on. Senior blue teamers who develop an offensive secondary skill set naturally gravitate toward purple team work.
Threat Hunter
Threat hunters proactively search for adversary activity not caught by automated detection. Their hypothesis-driven investigation methodology is conceptually close to the purple team's detection gap analysis, and many threat hunters participate in purple team exercises as the blue-side subject matter experts on telemetry and EDR behaviour.
Cyber Threat Intelligence (CTI) Analyst
CTI analysts track adversary groups, analyse malware, and produce intelligence products. Purple teamers consume CTI to make exercises threat-relevant. In mature organisations, the CTI and purple team functions are tightly coupled, with CTI reports directly feeding exercise TTP selection.
Detection Engineer
As discussed in §3, detection engineers who receive exercise outputs from purple teams—and who ideally participate in exercises directly—represent the clearest adjacent role. The boundary between detection engineer and purple teamer is porous in practice.
Incident Responder
Incident responders analyse and contain real attacks. Purple team exercises simulate the conditions under which responders must act, allowing playbooks and response times to be validated before an actual incident. Responders often participate in tabletop components of purple team programmes.
Security Architect
Security architects design control environments. Purple team findings—particularly recurring detection gaps—surface architectural weaknesses that require redesign rather than rule tuning. Senior purple teamers and security architects work together to translate exercise results into infrastructure-level changes.
CISO / Security Programme Director
The purple team function reports into (or informs) senior leadership. Common career progression moves purple team leads toward Director of Offensive Security, Head of Detection Engineering, or ultimately CISO roles—having developed the rare combination of technical depth and programme-level thinking.[51]
