Together We Root: Making Veganism Socially Sustainable

The evidence is clear: isolated vegans quit. Connected ones thrive. Here's how to build — and find — the social infrastructure that turns a dietary choice into a lasting way of life.

by Nicholas Knapp · April 30, 2026 · ~14 min read

Veganism is often framed as a personal choice — something you decide in the quiet of your conscience and carry out alone in a world that mostly doesn't share your values. But that framing, however sincere, may be what's causing the movement to leak people faster than it gains them. The data on vegan retention is sobering: in Faunalytics' landmark study of current and former vegetarians and vegans, former vegans outnumbered current vegans by nearly five to one.1 Community, it turns out, isn't a nice-to-have. It's the infrastructure that makes the whole project work.

This article is a practical guide to the social ecosystem of veganism — the meetups, online spaces, sanctuaries, festivals, and mentorship programs that transform an ethical commitment into a lived identity. If you're an activist, an organiser, or simply a vegan wondering why staying vegan feels lonely, read on. The solution almost always involves other people.

Why Community Is the Missing Variable

There's a compelling body of research linking social embeddedness to vegan retention. Faunalytics found that a striking 84% of former vegetarians and vegans reported not being actively involved in any vegetarian or vegan group or organisation — whether a potluck, an online community, or otherwise.1 Even more revealing: 58% of former vegans said they did not see veganism as part of their identity.1 These two findings are deeply connected. Community is how identity gets reinforced, practised, and made real.

A qualitative study published in Appetite (2022) found that socialising with other vegans provided participants with a sense of belonging, affiliation, and support, directly reducing feelings of segregation and isolation.2 Interestingly, community also created accountability: some participants noted they felt less inclined to deviate from their diet because they didn't want to explain themselves to fellow vegans they respected.2

Research on vegan and vegetarian identity by Vestergren and Uysal (2022) confirms that social group membership is central to how veganism is sustained over time — consistent with the social identity theory developed by Tajfel and Turner, in which the groups we belong to shape our values and behaviour across all the contexts in which they're relevant.3 The Vegan Society has similarly noted that "pull" factors within social networks — recipe-sharing, leading by example, peer guidance — foster a greater chance of sustained vegan identity than "push" strategies associated with confrontational activism.4

"The sense of belonging and access to support would certainly help people overcome the stress of stepping outside the mainstream."

— Respondent, Faunalytics / Humane Research Council Survey1

In short: the mechanisms that help people stay vegan are the same mechanisms that religion, weight-loss programmes, and recovery communities have relied on for decades. Shared identity, regular contact, and mutual accountability are not soft extras — they are the architecture of durable behaviour change.

01
Local Infrastructure Meetups & In-Person Groups

The most straightforward antidote to vegan isolation is the local meetup. Whether it's a monthly dinner, a fortnightly walk, or an activist reading group, in-person gatherings provide the social glue that digital connection cannot fully replicate. They also make veganism visible in a community — which matters both for retention among existing vegans and for normalising plant-based living for curious onlookers.

How to Find or Start a Local Group

Meetup.com hosts hundreds of vegan-specific groups in cities around the world, ranging from potluck dinners to hiking clubs to cooking workshops. If none exists in your area, starting one costs nothing beyond the platform's modest hosting fee. Veganuary's community map (veganuary.com) also connects participants with local support networks, especially in January when new vegans are actively looking for community. The Anonymous for the Voiceless Cube of Truth chapters (anonymousforthevoiceless.org) operate in over 900 cities globally and combine public outreach with chapter community events.

If you're building a group from scratch, consider anchoring it in a shared activity rather than shared ideology — a monthly restaurant discovery dinner, a plant-based baking club, or a film-and-discussion night tends to attract both committed vegans and curious omnivores, widening the tent and reducing the in-group insularity that can make vegan spaces feel unwelcoming to newcomers.

02
Digital Connection Online Communities

For vegans in rural areas, those with disabilities, or anyone whose schedule makes in-person attendance difficult, online community is not a consolation prize — it can be equally sustaining. The Faunalytics study included online communities in its list of vegan group types whose absence predicted lapsing, and research on vegan involvement on social platforms confirms their role in normalising and sustaining the lifestyle.1,4

Platforms and Spaces Worth Knowing

The largest general communities live on Reddit — particularly r/vegan (over 700,000 members), r/PlantBasedDiet, and r/veganrecipes. Facebook still hosts some of the most active regional vegan groups; searching "[your city] vegans" typically surfaces several options. For more activist-focused conversation, Animal Rights Zone (arzone.ning.com) and the broader ecosystem of Discord servers tied to organisations like Vegan Outreach and Direct Action Everywhere offer dedicated spaces.

The research paper examining vegan social practices by Twine (2014, 2018), cited by the Vegan Society, found that food experimentation and recipe-sharing on internet forums and among friends was a meaningful contributor to both the rise in veganism and the consolidation of vegan identity.4 This suggests that the most effective online community work isn't necessarily about debating ethics — it's about making vegan eating feel abundant, joyful, and achievable.

03
Grounded Connection Sanctuaries & Volunteer Networks

Of all the community structures available to vegans, animal sanctuaries occupy a unique and powerful position. They are simultaneously spaces of advocacy, healing, and belonging — for the animals and for the humans who show up to care for them.

The evidence for sanctuary volunteering's psychological benefits is consistent. Research cited by the Vegan Society shows that spending time with animals reduces stress, lowers cortisol levels, and encourages mindfulness.5 Surge Sanctuary (UK) has documented how volunteers frequently describe the experience of sitting quietly with a cow or watching pigs in the mud as profoundly grounding — a counterpoint to the grief and moral distress that can accompany sustained engagement with animal advocacy.6 Sanctuary volunteering also adds a sense of purpose and routine, which is particularly valuable for preventing the burnout and isolation that causes many committed vegans to withdraw from advocacy altogether.

"Animal sanctuaries don't just exist for the animals — they're community spaces too. They offer opportunities to learn, connect, and contribute to something meaningful."

— Surge Sanctuary6

Farm Sanctuary, one of the oldest and largest in North America, has operated volunteer programmes since 1986. Its sites in Watkins Glen (NY) and Los Angeles offer farm chores, visitor programming, and community outreach roles, with monthly group support events open to all.7 Catskill Animal Sanctuary (NY) similarly relies on volunteers for the majority of its animal care, buildings and grounds work, and tours.8

04
Celebration & Visibility Festivals, Events & Potlucks

If community is the architecture of vegan retention, festivals and shared meals are its most joyful expression. They serve multiple functions simultaneously: they reinforce identity for existing vegans, they provide low-barrier entry points for curious omnivores, and they make plant-based food visible and abundant in ways that pure advocacy rarely achieves.

Vegan food festivals have proliferated significantly in the past decade, with major events now held annually in most large cities in North America, the UK, and Australia. The Vegan Street Fair (Los Angeles) draws tens of thousands of attendees. VegFest events run in cities from London to Seattle to Toronto, typically combining food vendors with speakers, cooking demos, and activist tabling. These events matter not just as consumer showcases but as gathering points where vegans who may feel socially marginalised can experience, even briefly, a world that reflects their values.

The Potluck as Activism

For everyday community-building, the humble potluck deserves more credit than it typically gets. A shared meal is arguably the most effective possible environment for normalising vegan food: it is intimate, low-pressure, centred on pleasure rather than argument, and it puts the food itself — rather than the ideology — at the centre. The Vegan Living Program, which has run structured community events since 2011 in multiple US cities, has found that social and culinary events are among the most effective tools for helping new vegans build community and sustain their commitment.9

05
Structured Support Mentorship Programs

Mentorship is one of the most underutilised tools in the vegan movement's social infrastructure. The transition to veganism raises practical challenges — navigating social situations, reading ingredient labels, finding restaurants, handling family pushback — that an experienced vegan can help resolve quickly and with warmth. But mentorship also does something deeper: it creates a one-to-one bond that makes the community feel personal rather than abstract.

Vegan Outreach's Vegan Mentor Program currently connects mentees with over 2,300 mentors in 1,150 cities across 60 countries, and has facilitated more than 4,300 mentor-mentee matches.10 Participation is free, and mentors communicate with their mentees via email, helping with everything from grocery shopping to handling social pressure. The program was built explicitly in response to evidence that vegans who lack social support are significantly more likely to lapse.10

Challenge 22 (challenge22.com) pairs a structured 22-day vegan commitment with group support and individual mentoring, delivered online. Unlike programs based on starter kits and emails alone, Challenge 22 provides a unique combination of group accountability and personal guidance.11 The Vegan Living Program operates free month-long educational and social event series in several US cities, explicitly designed to build community while providing mentorship — and has helped over 1,300 people transition to veganism since 2011.9

For communities where cost and cultural context are significant barriers, the LIVE (Low-Income Vegan Education) Mentorship Program is notably progressive in design: it matches participants with mentors of similar socioeconomic and cultural backgrounds and includes a full cost-effective meal plan with its 21-day supported programme.11

Retention & Identity: The Research Case for Community

The evidence linking community participation to vegan identity — and identity to retention — is well-established in the literature. A systematic review of 307 quantitative studies on vegetarianism and veganism published in Heliyon (2023) found that moral and social factors, including group identification, were consistently associated with sustained dietary adherence.12 The review spanned 45 years of research and consistently identified ideological and social embeddedness as key predictors of whether people maintained their commitment.

Research published in Appetite (2021) found that vegans engaged in activism more frequently when they had stronger collective efficacy — that is, a belief that vegans as a group can make a meaningful difference — and when their identification with the vegan community was stronger.13 This suggests a virtuous cycle: community participation builds collective efficacy, which in turn drives activism, which in turn deepens identity and reinforces retention.

A 2026 study applying the Theory of Planned Behaviour to veganism found that moral identity and perceived social expectations were among the strongest predictors of sustained adherence — while motivation based on health or environment alone was, paradoxically, negatively associated with long-term adherence.14 This aligns with Faunalytics' earlier finding that health motivation was the primary driver for most people who eventually lapsed.1 The implication is important: a vegan community rooted in shared ethics and social bonds is more likely to produce long-term vegans than one rooted in personal health goals alone.

"Vegan identity goes beyond the choice of diet to incorporate veganism as an identity characteristic."

— Vestergren & Uysal, Frontiers in Psychology, 20223

Taken together, this body of evidence points to one practical conclusion: if you want to stay vegan, find your people. If you want others to stay vegan, help them find theirs.

Building the World You Want to Live In

The most powerful argument for community-building as activism is not merely strategic — it's visionary. A rich, welcoming, joyful vegan community isn't just a means to reducing animal suffering; it's a glimpse of the world we're building. When someone attends their first vegan potluck and realises that the food is extraordinary, the people are warm, and the conversation is alive with purpose, they're not just adding a social engagement to their calendar. They're internalising a new image of what human life could look like.

That's the leverage. Not just retention. Not just identity reinforcement. But the slow, undeniable work of making a better world feel real, attainable, and worth staying for.
