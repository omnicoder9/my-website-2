The Enabling Layer: Securing the Communications Sector

The Communications Sector is one of the few critical infrastructure sectors that has almost no dependencies on the others — and the only one on which all sixteen depend. That asymmetry makes its compromise a uniquely systemic event. This is a technical briefing on how the substrate of American connectivity can fail, who is already inside it, and what it takes to hold the line.

Section 01

The enabling layer

Every other piece of American critical infrastructure assumes the network underneath it works. The power grid assumes its operators can talk to substations; the banking system assumes settlement messages reach their counterparties; a hospital assumes its 911 trunk is up. The Communications Sector is the assumption that everything else makes — and that is precisely why its security is not a sector problem but a national one.

U.S. critical infrastructure policy has long recognized this. The federal designation that established the modern sector model identified the Communications Sector as critical specifically because it provides an “enabling function” across all critical infrastructure sectors.1 The Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA) puts the dependency relationship more bluntly in its own materials: while the Communications Sector itself has few significant dependencies on other sectors, the other sectors depend heavily on it — making communications one of the only sectors that can affect all others.2 Government accountability reviewers reached the same structural conclusion, noting that damage or disruption to the sector could severely degrade the operations of local, regional, and national networks that the rest of the economy runs on.3

CISA models four sectors — Energy, Communications, Water, and Transportation — as foundational to the operations of almost all the others, observing that virtually every modern infrastructure system relies at least in part on communications networks to monitor, control, and manage its operations.4 Communications is the one of those four that is also the connective tissue between the rest: it is how the grid coordinates, how water utilities run their supervisory control, and how transportation systems signal. A failure that begins in the network does not stay in the network.

Concept — The enabling-function asymmetry

Most interdependency analysis is bidirectional: Energy needs Communications, and Communications needs Energy (to power towers and central offices). But the dependency is lopsided. A regional power outage degrades communications gradually, as batteries and generators run down. A loss of communications degrades everything at once, because monitoring, control, dispatch, payment, and coordination all traverse the same packet-switched substrate. The sector's low inbound dependency and near-universal outbound dependency is the single most important fact in this entire briefing.

What “the sector” actually is

The Communications Sector is not a single industry but a convergent stack of them. Over the last quarter century it evolved from a predominantly voice-services business into a diverse, competitive, and densely interconnected industry spanning terrestrial, satellite, and wireless transmission. CISA describes the collective infrastructure through at least five major access pathways into the same core network: broadcasting, cable, satellite, wireless, and wireline.2 Satellite, wireless, and wireline providers depend on one another to carry and terminate each other's traffic, and they routinely share physical facilities and technology — which means a vulnerability in one segment is rarely contained to that segment.

Ownership and defense are overwhelmingly private. As with most U.S. critical infrastructure, the private sector is primarily responsible for protecting the sector's assets; the federal role is to help operators anticipate, predict, and respond to outages and threats rather than to run the networks.1 That public-private division of labor — and the friction inside it — recurs throughout the mitigation and policy discussion below.

Who governs it

Three federal centers of gravity shape sector security. CISA serves as the Sector Risk Management Agency (SRMA) for Communications and, since 2024, as the National Coordinator for the Security and Resilience of Critical Infrastructure — a role formalized when the White House issued National Security Memorandum 22 (NSM-22) on April 30, 2024, superseding the decade-old Presidential Policy Directive 21 (PPD-21).5 NSM-22 keeps the familiar sixteen-sector structure but shifts the posture: it directs an accelerated, biennial risk-management cycle and, significantly, presses federal agencies toward mandatory minimum security and resilience requirements rather than the purely voluntary partnerships that defined the model from the Clinton administration onward.56 The framework retained all sixteen sectors after the first consensus review of sector boundaries since 2013.7

The second center is the Federal Communications Commission (FCC), whose statutory authority over carriers, spectrum, equipment authorization, and submarine cable landings gives it regulatory levers that CISA lacks — levers it has used aggressively and, as we will see, inconsistently. The third is the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST), whose Cybersecurity Framework and Special Publications supply the technical vocabulary and control catalogs that the rest of the system references. Together these three — coordinator, regulator, and standards body — form the scaffolding inside which the sector's defense is organized.

The rest of this briefing works from the inside out: first the architecture and the specific technologies that define the attack surface; then the threat landscape against that surface; then the mechanism by which a communications compromise propagates into the other fifteen sectors; then the frameworks, tools, and processes assembled to defend it; and finally the unsolved problems where research and policy are still visibly behind the adversary.

Section 02

Anatomy of the sector and its attack surface

To reason about how the Communications Sector fails, you have to see it as the layered, protocol-defined system it actually is — a stack assembled over fifty years, in which decades-old signaling logic still underpins state-of-the-art radio, and in which the control planes that route, name, and connect traffic are largely separate from (and weaker than) the data they carry.

It is useful to decompose the sector along the planes where attacks land, rather than along corporate or service boundaries. Five planes matter: the physical plane (fiber, submarine cables, towers, landing stations, and the timing and power they depend on); the access plane (radio access networks, cable, fixed wireless, and customer-premises and IoT devices); the transport and routing plane (the IP backbone and the Border Gateway Protocol that stitches independent networks together); the naming and addressing plane (the Domain Name System); and the signaling and control plane (SS7, Diameter, and their 5G successors, plus the lawful-intercept machinery bolted onto carrier cores). Each has its own threat model, and the most consequential incidents tend to exploit the planes that operators monitor least.

A compressed history: how the stack accreted

The sector's security posture is a product of its history, and four inflection points explain most of today's structural weaknesses.

The first is the signaling system itself. Signaling System No. 7 (SS7), designed in the 1970s and deployed through the 1980s, became the out-of-band control protocol for the global telephone network — setting up calls, routing texts, and enabling roaming and interconnection. It was engineered for a closed club of trusted national carriers and therefore contains essentially no authentication or encryption.10 Its 4G/5G successor, Diameter, modernized the transport but inherited the trust model; Diameter does not encrypt originating IP addresses in transit, which leaves it exposed to spoofing by an attacker posing as a legitimate roaming partner.10 These protocols remain the foundation for mobile interconnection and roaming to this day — a 1970s trust assumption carried, almost unmodified, into the 5G era.

The second is the operational coordination apparatus. In 1982, government and industry officials recognized the need for a joint mechanism to coordinate national-security and emergency-preparedness communications; Executive Order 12472 created the National Coordinating Center for Communications (NCC) in 1984, and from 2000 the Communications Information Sharing and Analysis Center (Comms-ISAC) grew around it.9 That public-private operational fabric — predating CISA by decades — is still the sector's nervous system for threat sharing and outage response.

The third is lawful interception. The Communications Assistance for Law Enforcement Act (CALEA) of 1994 required carriers to build their networks so that communications could be intercepted when legally compelled. In 2005 the FCC interpreted CALEA's reach to extend beyond traditional telephony to facilities-based broadband access and interconnected Voice over IP, under a “substantial replacement” reading of the statute.49 The consequence is that virtually every U.S. carrier core contains a standing interface designed to copy and divert targeted traffic. That interface is a compliance feature; it is also, as the Salt Typhoon campaign would demonstrate three decades later, an extraordinary attack surface.

The fourth is convergence and concentration. The Telecommunications Act of 1996 accelerated the collapse of separate voice, video, and data businesses onto a common IP substrate, and the subsequent two decades concentrated critical functions into a small number of backbone carriers, DNS and content-delivery providers, cloud operators, and submarine-cable consortia. The benefits in cost and capability are enormous; the cost is systemic, because a handful of chokepoints now sit beneath a disproportionate share of the digital economy.

The routing and naming planes

Two protocols quietly determine whether the internet works on any given day, and both predate the security requirements now placed on them. The Border Gateway Protocol (BGP) is the inter-domain routing protocol that lets the roughly one hundred thousand independently administered networks (autonomous systems) on the internet exchange reachability information and choose paths. CISA has called it “the most important part of the Internet you've probably never heard of.”14 BGP's original design, still widely deployed, lacks built-in origin and path validation: a router can announce a prefix it does not own, and neighbors that accept the announcement will route traffic to the announcer.16 The Domain Name System (DNS) resolves human-readable names to addresses and is similarly load-bearing and similarly targetable — both as a direct denial-of-service target and as a manipulation vector. Section 03 treats the concrete failure modes of each.

Concept — Control plane vs. data plane

In communications networks the data plane carries user traffic, while the control plane decides where that traffic goes and how sessions are established — routing (BGP), naming (DNS), and signaling (SS7/Diameter). Encryption protects the data plane increasingly well; the control plane is where trust was historically assumed and where authentication is still being retrofitted. Most of the sector's distinctive risks live in the control plane, because compromising it lets an adversary redirect, intercept, or deny traffic without ever breaking the cryptography protecting its contents.

The access plane: 5G, Open RAN, and the device frontier

The radio edge is undergoing its own architectural shift. Standalone 5G introduces a cloud-native, service-based core and network slicing — logically isolated virtual networks running over shared physical infrastructure, each tuned for a use case (ultra-reliable low-latency control traffic on one slice, mass IoT telemetry on another). Slicing's promise of isolation is also its risk: the boundaries between slices are software-defined and therefore software-breakable. In parallel, Open RAN (O-RAN) disaggregates the once-monolithic, single-vendor radio access network into interoperable components — multi-vendor management, an open fronthaul linking radios to baseband, and a new application framework of rApps and xApps running on RAN Intelligent Controllers, increasingly driven by AI/ML for optimization.12 The architecture buys supply-chain diversity and innovation at the cost of a larger, more heterogeneous attack surface: more interfaces, more software suppliers, more open-source dependencies, and a novel AI-driven control surface whose failure modes are not yet well understood.

Below the radio sit the endpoints, and the sector's most consequential availability incidents have come from the least-managed of them: consumer routers, cameras, and other Internet-of-Things devices shipped with default credentials. The same devices that extend connectivity also constitute a globally distributed, weaponizable population — a point the 2016 Dyn attack made unforgettable, and one we return to in Section 03.

The physical plane: cables, landing stations, timing, and power

Beneath every abstraction is glass and electricity. Roughly 600 submarine cables in service or under construction carry on the order of 95–99% of intercontinental data traffic — a share that is rising as cloud computing, IoT, integrated 5G, and AI training workloads push demand that satellites cannot meet.11 Cable landing stations, terrestrial fiber routes, and the central offices that aggregate them are physical chokepoints. Two less visible dependencies compound the physical risk: precise timing, on which synchronization of cellular and transport networks depends and which is largely sourced from satellite positioning systems, and power, which the sector consumes to run towers and offices and which it also helps the Energy Sector manage — a mutual dependency that can become a shared failure mode. The FCC's own advisory committees have specifically studied submarine-cable resiliency and the single-source risk of network timing as discrete reliability problems.46

With the anatomy in view, the threat landscape becomes legible: the most damaging attacks are not generic malware but plane-specific exploitation of routing, naming, signaling, supply chain, and physical infrastructure — often by adversaries patient enough to wait years for the moment the access matters.

Section 03

The threat landscape

The Communications Sector faces the full spectrum of physical, cyber, and human threats, but its distinctive risks cluster in the control plane and the physical plane — the places where trust was assumed and where the consequences propagate fastest. What follows is organized by plane, anchored to the incidents that defined each one.

3.1 — The routing plane: BGP hijacks and leaks

Because BGP lacks native origin and path validation, traffic can be intercepted, redirected, or black-holed by either accidental misconfiguration or deliberate manipulation — and the two are often indistinguishable from the outside.16 A “hijack” occurs when an autonomous system originates a prefix it is not authorized to announce; a “route leak” occurs when announcements propagate beyond their intended scope. Either can pull a target's traffic onto an attacker-controlled or simply wrong path, exposing it to interception or dropping it entirely.

The historical record is unambiguous. In 2018, China Telecom misdirected U.S. internet traffic for an extended period; in 2020 a hijack associated with Rostelecom disrupted service to networks across the globe, an incident analysts concluded could have been blocked had a directly connected peer enforced strict route-origin filtering.13 The FCC opened its formal inquiry into BGP security in 2022 after Ukrainian authorities observed an alleged Russian-linked hijack less than a day before the invasion — routing manipulation as a precursor to kinetic conflict.16 The financial-sector exposure is concrete: in August 2022 an attacker stole roughly US$235,000 in cryptocurrency from the Celer Bridge service by hijacking the routes to it for about three hours, redirecting users to attacker-controlled servers.14

The lesson generalizes: in the 2022 Rostelecom/Apple hijack, roughly 80% of the affected routes had no Route Origin Authorizations and propagated unchecked, while the ~7,000 prefixes that did have valid authorizations were filtered out by many operators — concrete evidence that broader adoption materially contains these incidents.15

The structural problem is a collective-action problem: route-origin validation only protects you to the extent your peers also enforce it. That is why the routing plane is simultaneously the best-understood and slowest-fixed of the sector's weaknesses, and why it has drawn direct regulatory attention (Section 05).

3.2 — The naming and availability plane: DNS and DDoS

If routing decides where traffic goes, DNS decides whether names resolve at all — and a sufficiently large flood can deny either. The weaponization of consumer IoT devices transformed the scale of what is possible, and one event marks the before-and-after.

Dyn faced severe business interruption and never fully assessed the attack's total weight; the incident became the canonical demonstration that insecure endpoints in the Communications/IT supply chain are a population-level weapon, not an individual nuisance.1819 CISA issued its own alert on the heightened DDoS threat from Mirai and similar botnets in the same window.17

Two properties make this class of attack uniquely a Communications Sector problem. First, the targets are shared infrastructure — a single managed-DNS or content-delivery provider sits beneath thousands of brands, so one outage is many outages. Second, the ammunition is the sector's own product: every insecure router or camera sold expands the attackable population. The defensive response — anycast, scrubbing, and a long-running push toward secure-by-design endpoints — is treated in Section 05, but the underlying incentive problem (the buyer of a cheap camera does not bear the cost of its insecurity) remains substantially unsolved.

3.3 — The signaling plane: SS7, Diameter, and the 2FA problem

The signaling plane is where a 1970s trust model meets modern surveillance economics. Significant weaknesses in SS7 have been documented for more than a decade: the ability to determine a device's physical location, to intercept or block text messages, to disrupt service, and to redirect or eavesdrop on voice calls.20 Crucially, these vulnerabilities persist even on upgraded networks, because devices can fall back to older protocols when roaming or during SMS transmission, and 4G/5G users can be downgraded to SS7 when roaming.22 By one 2018 estimate, nine of ten SMS messages were interceptable — which is why SMS-based two-factor authentication, still pervasive across banking and account-recovery flows, is a standing liability.20

The contemporary picture is worse than a single criminal case. A senior CISA official told the FCC in a 2024 public filing that SS7 and Diameter weaknesses had been exploited in “numerous” attempts to acquire location data, intercept calls and texts, and deliver spyware to targets in the United States — warning that the known incidents were likely “just the tip of the proverbial iceberg.”21 Senator Ron Wyden's correspondence with the Department of Defense documented that Russia, China, Israel, and Iran have used the telecom assets of third countries to route SS7 attacks against U.S. subscribers.22 Notably, when the FCC sought comment, the major carriers and their trade association maintained that their signaling firewalls were working and that they had not detected such incidents on their networks since 2018 — a confidence that independent researchers and privacy advocates publicly disputed.23 That gap between carrier assurance and outside skepticism is itself a feature of the threat landscape.

3.4 — The supply chain: equipment, edge devices, and trust

The sector's supply chain is a security problem on two levels: the integrity of the vendors whose equipment sits in the network core, and the patch state of the internet-facing edge devices that adversaries actually break in through. On the first, U.S. policy concluded that equipment from specific foreign manufacturers posed unacceptable risk: the Secure and Trusted Communications Networks Act of 2019 directed the FCC to maintain a “Covered List” of prohibited equipment and services, with Huawei and ZTE as the initial designations, and the Secure Equipment Act of 2021 barred the FCC from authorizing covered equipment thereafter — turning a warning list into a market ban.24

On the second level, the manufacturer's nameplate matters less than the device's exposure. The defining state-sponsored campaigns of the 2020s gained entry not through exotic implants but through known, unpatched vulnerabilities in internet-facing routers, firewalls, and VPN appliances — the edge of the network, where logging is thin and patch cycles are slow.34 The supply-chain risk and the edge-hygiene risk converge: a network full of trusted-vendor equipment is still trivially compromised if that equipment is unpatched and exposed.

3.5 — The physical plane: cutting the cables

The most analog threat is resurgent. Submarine cables suffer 100–200 faults per year from anchors, fishing, and age, which is why operators build redundancy as standard practice — but the same shallow-water vulnerability that makes accidental damage common makes deliberate damage easy to disguise.27 Since 2023 there have been at least eleven incidents of cable damage around Taiwan and at least eleven in the Baltic Sea, a pattern security analysts characterize as “gray-zone” activity: low-grade coercion that is opaque, deniable, and hard to attribute.27

Attribution remains the central difficulty — in March 2026 Finland's security service publicly stated it had found no evidence of deliberate state activity behind certain Baltic incidents, underscoring how plausible deniability is the point of the tactic, not an accident of it.2930 A November 2025 congressional hearing put the dependency in stark terms: roughly 97% of global data traffic and roughly US$9 trillion in daily trade ride these cables, many of which are poorly protected.28

3.6 — Espionage and pre-positioning: the two Typhoons

The most serious threats to the sector are not financially motivated criminals but patient, state-sponsored actors pursuing two distinct objectives: intelligence and pre-positioning for disruption. Two PRC-attributed campaigns — Salt Typhoon and Volt Typhoon — embody each, and together they have reframed how the U.S. government thinks about communications security.

The campaign's most alarming feature is its reach into the CALEA lawful-intercept infrastructure — the very system built so that U.S. law enforcement could wiretap with a court order was used by a foreign intelligence service against the people on the wiretap list. As CISA's Jeff Greene put it during the response, officials could not say with certainty the adversary had been evicted because they did not yet know the full scope of the activity.35 The joint advisory built on investigations running through July 2025 and reflected unusually broad international coordination.343637

Taken together, the two Typhoons establish the defining strategic fact of the current era: a capable adversary is simultaneously harvesting the sector's intelligence value and pre-staging the access needed to deny its function on demand. Every mitigation in Section 05 should be read against that reality. But before defenses, the mechanism of contagion: why a compromise of this one sector does not stay contained.

Section 04

The cross-sector cascade

The reason a Communications Sector compromise is a national-security event rather than an industry inconvenience is mechanical, not rhetorical: the other fifteen sectors do not merely use communications, they are operationally dependent on it for the monitoring, control, coordination, and settlement functions that constitute their core missions. Disrupt the substrate and you do not damage one sector; you degrade the functional layer beneath all of them.

The mechanism: from enabling function to functional collapse

CISA's risk methodology has moved beyond asset-by-asset thinking toward National Critical Functions (NCFs) — the functions of government and the private sector so vital that their disruption would debilitate national security, economic security, or public health. Those functions are organized into four areas, and three of the four names are themselves communications verbs: connect, distribute, manage, and supply.38 Viewing risk through this functional lens is precisely what reveals the cascade: a single communications failure does not knock out one asset, it degrades a function — “connect” or “manage” — on which many sectors simultaneously rely.

The incidents already discussed are, in this light, cross-sector events that merely originated in communications. The 2016 Dyn outage was nominally a DNS incident; in practice it denied service across retail, media, software, and payments at once.18 The 2017 O2-Telefonica case was nominally an SS7 incident; in practice it was a bank theft.21 The Celer Bridge hijack was nominally a routing incident; in practice it was a financial-sector loss.14 The pattern is the rule, not the exception.

Sector-by-sector contagion

Energy

The Energy and Communications sectors are mutually dependent: power runs the towers, central offices, and cable landing stations, while communications carries the monitoring and control traffic the grid uses to deliver electricity.1 That coupling is a shared failure mode — and it is no accident that Volt Typhoon's confirmed footprint spans both sectors. An adversary pre-positioned in communications gains a path toward the grid's operational technology; an adversary in the grid can starve communications of power. The February 2024 advisory's inclusion of the Department of Energy as a co-author reflects exactly this entanglement.31

Financial Services

No sector is more exposed to communications integrity. Trading, clearing, settlement, and authentication are all messages in motion, and three distinct communications-plane weaknesses have already produced financial loss: SS7 interception of SMS second factors (O2-Telefonica), BGP hijacking of routes to a financial service (Celer Bridge), and the physical fragility of the subsea cables that carry the bulk of cross-border value — on the order of US$9–10 trillion daily.30 The sector's continued reliance on SMS-based authentication, despite a decade of documented SS7 weakness, is one of the clearest unmitigated cross-sector exposures in the system.

Water and Wastewater

Water utilities run on supervisory control and data acquisition systems whose remote monitoring and control traverse communications networks. Volt Typhoon's confirmed presence in Water and Wastewater IT environments — with the EPA co-authoring the advisory — demonstrates the path: compromise the IT network that the control systems depend on, then move laterally toward the operational technology that opens valves and doses chemicals.33 For smaller utilities with limited cybersecurity capacity, the communications dependency is also the principal avenue of attack.

Emergency Services and 911

The most immediate human consequence of a communications failure is the loss of the ability to call for help. Public-safety answering points, Enhanced 911, and the ongoing transition to Next Generation 911 are communications services end to end — which is why the FCC's Communications Security, Reliability, and Interoperability Council (CSRIC) treats 911, E911, and NG911 reliability as a core part of its remit, alongside emergency alerting.8 A regional outage that would be a nuisance for streaming is a life-safety event for dispatch, and the NG911 transition to IP-based call handling imports the same routing, DNS, and DDoS risks that afflict the rest of the network into the emergency-response system itself.

Information Technology, Transportation, Healthcare, Chemical, and beyond

The Communications and Information Technology sectors are so tightly coupled that CISA treats IT as both a provider to and a dependent of communications — IT supplies the control systems, internet infrastructure, and physical architecture, while relying on communications to deliver and distribute applications and services.1 Transportation systems depend on communications for signaling, dispatch, and logistics; healthcare depends on it for records, telemedicine, and increasingly for connected devices; the Chemical Sector depends on it for both process control and the emergency communications that contain an incident when process control fails. CISA's own determination is that disruption to Energy, IT, or Transportation could severely impair communications — and the corollary, that disruption to communications impairs all of them, is the asymmetry this section began with.3

Why this matters for risk modeling

Treating communications as “just another sector” in a portfolio of sixteen understates its risk by a structural factor. A correct model treats it as a common-mode dependency: a single compromise can produce correlated failure across sectors that an asset-level analysis would score as independent. This is the analytical reason NSM-22 directs CISA, as National Coordinator, to identify systemic, cross-sector, and nationally significant risk rather than leaving each SRMA to assess its own sector in isolation.6

The cascade is the case for treating communications security as a public good rather than a private cost — and it sets up the central tension of the next section, where the tools to defend the substrate are mature but the obligation to deploy them is contested.

05 — Defending the substrate

Defending the substrate

The defensive position of the Communications Sector is defined by a peculiar asymmetry: nearly every technical control needed to close the major attack surfaces already exists and is standardized, yet the obligation to deploy those controls is partial, voluntary, and — as of late 2025 — actively contested. The history of sector defense is therefore less a story of inventing protections than of negotiating who must turn them on, who pays, and whether security is a private cost or a public good.

This section walks the defensive stack from the top down: the governance and policy layer that sets obligations; the framework and baseline layer that translates obligations into engineering; and then plane by plane — routing, naming, signaling, supply chain, operations, physical, and architecture — the concrete tools, standards, and coordinating bodies that operators actually use.

Governance and policy: the contested obligation

At the apex sits National Security Memorandum 22 (NSM-22), issued April 30, 2024, which supersedes the decade-old Presidential Policy Directive 21 and reframes critical-infrastructure protection around managing systemic, cross-sector, and nationally significant risk. NSM-22 designates CISA as National Coordinator for the security and resilience of the nation's critical infrastructure and signals a deliberate move away from the purely voluntary model that governed the prior decade, previewing minimum security requirements and a focus on the most systemically important entities.5 For the Communications Sector specifically, this elevation matters because the sector's defining property — its role as common-mode dependency for the other fifteen — is exactly the kind of cross-sector risk the National Coordinator is directed to identify and address rather than leave to a single agency.6

Underneath that sits a patchwork of authorities. The Cyber Incident Reporting for Critical Infrastructure Act of 2022 (CIRCIA) moves the sector toward mandatory incident and ransomware-payment reporting to CISA, displacing the assumption that disclosure is discretionary. The Sector Risk Management Agency (SRMA) model assigns CISA as the SRMA for Communications, while the Federal Communications Commission retains the regulatory levers — equipment authorization, the Covered List, outage reporting, and licensing — that give sector policy actual teeth. The result is a shared-custody arrangement in which CISA convenes and coordinates while the FCC regulates, and the seams between them are where the most consequential policy fights occur.

Case study: the CALEA reversal and the limits of voluntary security

No episode illustrates the contested-obligation problem more sharply than the 2025 fight over the Communications Assistance for Law Enforcement Act (CALEA). In the immediate aftermath of the Salt Typhoon disclosures, the FCC adopted a Declaratory Ruling (FCC 25-9) on January 15, 2025, holding that Section 105 of CALEA affirmatively requires telecommunications carriers to secure their networks against unlawful access and interception — an interpretation that, for the first time, read a binding cybersecurity duty into a statute that had been understood mainly as a lawful-intercept mandate. The accompanying rulemaking proposed annual cybersecurity certifications and supply-chain risk-management plans.48

Less than a year later, on November 20, 2025, a reconstituted FCC under Chairman Brendan Carr adopted an Order on Reconsideration rescinding that ruling, characterizing the January interpretation as “unlawful and unnecessary” and favoring what it framed as a more agile, collaborative approach over prescriptive mandates.47 The Federal Register notice recorded the Commission's view that the original ruling had misconstrued CALEA and had been adopted just five days before a change in administration without adequate notice and comment.49 The reversal drew sharp opposition: Senator Maria Cantwell, ranking member of the Senate Commerce Committee, accused the Commission of rolling back the very network-protection rules put in place after Salt Typhoon, and pointed to carrier lobbying as the driver.50 Critics including Commissioner Anna Gomez and outside analysts argued the move stripped a rare enforceable federal cybersecurity safeguard from an industry that the same government had just finished warning was deeply penetrated.51

The lesson is structural, not partisan. When the strongest available cybersecurity obligation for the sector can be created and dissolved by administrative interpretation within ten months, operators face profound uncertainty about what they are actually required to do — and the default reverts to voluntary action, which is precisely the regime that left the surfaces in Section 03 unmitigated for a decade.

Frameworks and baselines

Where obligation is ambiguous, frameworks supply the engineering consensus operators converge on regardless. The anchor is the NIST Cybersecurity Framework, whose version 2.0 was published February 26, 2024 — the first major revision since 2014. CSF 2.0 organizes cybersecurity outcomes into six functions: Govern, Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, and Recover.39 The headline change is the addition of Govern as a function in its own right, elevating organizational cybersecurity governance and supply-chain risk to first-class status, and the framework's explicit expansion of scope beyond critical infrastructure to organizations of every size and sector. In its current form CSF 2.0 spans 22 categories and 106 subcategories of outcomes.40 For a sector whose worst incidents combine technical penetration with governance gaps — unpatched edge routers, unmonitored signaling links, unfunded equipment replacement — the Govern function is not a bureaucratic addendum but a direct response to the observed failure mode.

Beneath the framework sit the prescriptive NIST Special Publications that operators map to: SP 800-53 for the security and privacy control catalog; SP 800-61 for computer-security incident handling; and SP 800-207 for Zero Trust Architecture, whose “never trust, always verify” model directly addresses the lateral-movement tradecraft that both Volt Typhoon and Salt Typhoon relied on. For the routing plane specifically, NIST SP 800-189 provides the authoritative guidance on resilient interdomain routing and the deployment of route-origin validation using the Resource Public Key Infrastructure.41 Overlaying all of these is CISA's Secure by Design initiative, which pushes the cost of security upstream to manufacturers — a structural complement to operator-side controls, since much of the sector's exposure originates in the default-insecure devices (the Mirai story) and unhardened network equipment that vendors ship.

For the newest architecture, the multi-agency Enduring Security Framework (ESF) — a public-private working group led by NSA and CISA — has produced the sector's 5G guidance. Its work on Open RAN analyzes the expanded attack surface of disaggregated, multi-vendor radio networks with their open fronthaul interfaces and AI/ML-driven control applications.12 The ESF's guidance on network slicing — a 49-page document addressing how to maintain isolation between logically separated slices that share physical infrastructure, including proper cloud tenant separation — tackles a threat model that simply did not exist in prior generations.45 CISA's broader 5G Security and Resilience program, organized around the national 5G strategy, coordinates these strands and issued the network-slicing security guidance jointly with NSA and ODNI.44

Finally, in direct response to Salt Typhoon, CISA and its partners published the Enhanced Visibility and Hardening Guidance for Communications Infrastructure on December 3, 2024 — a consolidated set of best practices for telecommunications engineers covering configuration management, traffic and access monitoring, and device hardening.42 The guidance, which drew contributions from industry including Cisco and Google, emphasizes strengthening visibility into network activity precisely because the campaign demonstrated how long a sophisticated actor can dwell undetected inside a carrier core; as the FBI's Cynthia Kaiser framed the broader problem, defenders must assume that a network secure six months ago may be compromised now.43

Securing the routing plane

The defense against BGP hijacking and route leaks is cryptographic route validation. The Resource Public Key Infrastructure (RPKI) binds IP-address blocks to the autonomous systems authorized to originate them through signed Route Origin Authorizations (ROAs); neighbors then perform Route Origin Validation (ROV), dropping or de-preferring announcements that conflict with a published ROA. The technology is mature and standardized, and NIST SP 800-189 codifies its deployment for U.S. operators.41 Its weakness is coverage: route-origin validation only protects against hijacks of prefixes whose owners have published ROAs and whose transit providers actually enforce them, and adoption has lagged for more than a decade — a substantial share of address space still has no ROAs at all, leaving large swaths of the routing table unprotected.15 Complementary to RPKI is the industry self-governance initiative MANRS (Mutually Agreed Norms for Routing Security), which codifies four concrete actions — filtering, anti-spoofing, coordination, and global validation — that networks commit to voluntarily.

To convert voluntary best practice into measurable accountability, the FCC issued a Notice of Proposed Rulemaking in June 2024 that would require the largest broadband providers to develop confidential BGP security risk-management plans and the nine largest to file quarterly progress reports on their RPKI deployment.16 The proposal frames route-origin validation and the publication of ROAs as the baseline expectation, with techniques such as blackholing of malicious traffic as operational complements.13 It is a characteristic example of the sector's regulatory style: rather than mandating a specific technology outright, the Commission mandates reporting on progress toward it, using transparency as the lever.

Securing naming and availability

The naming plane is defended on two axes: integrity and availability. For integrity, DNS Security Extensions (DNSSEC) add cryptographic signatures to DNS records so that resolvers can detect tampering and cache poisoning — closing the manipulation vector that makes DNS attractive as a redirection tool. For availability, the dominant pattern is anycast, in which the same IP address is announced from many geographically distributed sites so that a volumetric flood is absorbed across the constellation rather than concentrated on a single resolver; this is the architectural lesson operators drew from the Dyn outage, where a centralized managed-DNS dependency became a single point of failure for dozens of major properties.18 Layered on top are dedicated DDoS scrubbing services that divert and filter attack traffic upstream, and the now-standard practice of provisioning secondary DNS across independent providers so that no one managed-DNS compromise can sever a zone from the internet.

Securing the signaling plane

For SS7 and Diameter, the front-line control is the signaling firewall — a security gateway at the network edge that inspects inter-carrier signaling messages and filters those that are illegitimate, such as location-tracking queries or interception commands arriving from networks that have no business sending them. The FCC's signaling-security proceeding points operators to the best practices developed by CSRIC Working Group 10, which addressed SS7 and Diameter security directly.10 Carriers and their trade association have asserted that firewalls and monitoring have substantially closed the gap, while independent researchers and advocacy groups dispute that the protections are adequate and argue the underlying vulnerabilities persist — a live disagreement the FCC's record reflects.23

The more decisive mitigation is to stop trusting the signaling plane for security-critical functions at all. Because roughly nine in ten SMS messages were assessed as interceptable, the durable fix for authentication is to abandon SMS-based two-factor authentication in favor of app-based authenticators and, better still, phishing-resistant FIDO2/WebAuthn hardware-backed credentials that never traverse the carrier signaling network.20 For confidentiality of content, the lesson operators and officials drew from Salt Typhoon's interception of calls and texts was blunt: use end-to-end encrypted messaging. In its post-incident guidance, CISA explicitly recommended that highly targeted individuals adopt end-to-end encrypted communications so that even a compromised carrier core yields only ciphertext.35

Securing the supply chain

The sector's supply-chain defense is built on exclusion plus replacement. The Secure and Trusted Communications Networks Act of 2019 directed the FCC to publish a Covered List of communications equipment and services deemed an unacceptable national-security risk — most prominently gear from Huawei and ZTE — and the Secure Equipment Act of 2021 closed the loophole that had still allowed such equipment to obtain FCC authorization.24 Removing already-installed equipment is the “rip-and-replace” program, and its execution exposes the funding problem that recurs throughout sector policy. Congress initially appropriated US$1.9 billion, but verified program costs rose to roughly US$4.98 billion, leaving a shortfall on the order of US$3.08 billion that stranded many small and rural carriers mid-replacement.25 That gap was eventually closed when Congress authorized the missing funds — supporting removal of more than 24,000 pieces of insecure equipment — through borrowing against future spectrum-auction proceeds under the Spectrum and Secure Technology and Innovation Act, with carriers such as Windstream replacing Huawei gear with domestic and allied alternatives.26 Going forward, the Software Bill of Materials (SBOM) — an itemized inventory of the components inside software and firmware — is the emerging instrument for managing the supply-chain risk that a Covered List cannot, since it addresses the transitive dependencies and embedded components that no exclusion list enumerates.

Operational coordination

Defense at the sector scale is a coordination problem as much as a technical one, and the institutional architecture predates the modern threat. Executive Order 12472, signed in 1984, established the National Coordinating Center for Communications (NCC) to coordinate the restoration of national-security and emergency-preparedness communications; the NCC has operated the Communications Information Sharing and Analysis Center (Comms-ISAC) since 2000, today encompassing more than fifty member companies that exchange threat and vulnerability information across the public-private boundary.9 Standards and best practices flow from the FCC's Communications Security, Reliability, and Interoperability Council (CSRIC), the federal advisory committee — rechartered roughly every two years since 2009, with its tenth iteration running 2026–2028 — whose recommendations span network reliability, 911 continuity, and emergency alerting.8 Operational telemetry is captured through the FCC's outage-reporting systems (NORS, the Network Outage Reporting System, and DIRS during disasters), and the sector's strategic coordination runs through the paired Sector Coordinating Council and Government Coordinating Council that the SRMA model establishes.

Physical and submarine cable resilience

For the physical plane, resilience is principally a matter of redundancy and diversity — multiple cables on diverse routes, diverse landing stations, and diverse terrestrial backhaul so that no single cut isolates a region — backed by the specialized but globally scarce fleet of cable-repair ships that fix faults at a baseline rate of one to two hundred per year. The policy dimension sharpened in 2025: confronting a wave of suspected grey-zone cable damage, the FCC adopted new rules on August 7, 2025 addressing the security and resilience of the submarine cables that carry well over ninety percent of intercontinental data traffic, tightening review of ownership, equipment, and protection against foreign-adversary risk.11 The standards community has worked the problem in parallel: CSRIC working groups have produced recommendations on submarine cable resiliency and on reducing single-source dependencies in network timing, the latter addressing the often-overlooked reality that precise time synchronization is itself a single point of failure for modern networks.46

Architectural hardening

Cutting across every plane is a shift in security architecture from perimeter defense to Zero Trust — the assumption, formalized in NIST SP 800-207, that no user, device, or network segment is inherently trusted and that every access request must be authenticated, authorized, and continuously validated. For carriers, the concrete expression is aggressive network segmentation that prevents an intruder who compromises one element from moving laterally to the call-routing core or the lawful-intercept system; rigorous edge-device hardening and patch discipline for the routers and appliances that Salt Typhoon exploited; and comprehensive logging and visibility so that anomalous administrative activity is detected rather than dwelling for years, the central thrust of CISA's December 2024 hardening guidance.42 Looking forward, the migration to post-quantum cryptography (PQC) — NIST standardized its first PQC algorithms in 2024 — is becoming an architectural priority for the sector precisely because of the “harvest now, decrypt later” threat: traffic intercepted from cables or signaling links today can be stored and decrypted once a cryptographically relevant quantum computer exists, making the long-lived confidentiality of communications a present-tense engineering concern.

06 — Open problems & research

Open problems & the research frontier

The uncomfortable truth of the preceding section is that the sector's defensive gaps are largely not gaps in knowledge. The cryptography to validate routes, the firewalls to filter signaling, the architecture to isolate slices, and the discipline to harden edge devices all exist. The persistent problems are problems of incentives, coverage, attribution, and a handful of genuinely hard technical frontiers where research is still required. This section catalogues the open problems an expert should track.

The incentive problem: voluntary security at national scale

The central unresolved question is whether a sector this systemically important can be secured through voluntary action and reversible administrative guidance. The CALEA episode is the proof of concept for pessimism: a binding cybersecurity obligation was read into the statute and then rescinded within ten months, leaving carriers with regulatory whiplash rather than a stable baseline.47 The economic structure is the root cause — because a carrier's security failures impose costs primarily on other sectors downstream (the cascade of Section 04), each operator rationally under-invests relative to the social optimum. NSM-22's pivot toward minimum requirements and systemically important entities is a policy hypothesis about how to correct that externality;5 whether it survives contact with the same lobbying and legal-authority constraints that unwound the CALEA ruling is, as of late 2025, an open question.50

The coverage problem: partial deployment of mature controls

Several of the most important defenses fail open when adoption is incomplete. Route-origin validation only protects prefixes whose owners publish ROAs and whose providers enforce them, and with a large share of address space still lacking ROAs after more than a decade, the routing table remains broadly hijackable despite a mature, standardized fix.15 DNSSEC adoption remains similarly uneven. The research and operational challenge is not designing the protocol but driving deployment past the threshold where network effects make validation the default — a question of economics, tooling, and possibly regulation as much as engineering. The FCC's choice to mandate progress reporting rather than the technology itself is one experiment in moving the needle;16 scalable, low-friction enforcement of ROV across the long tail of smaller networks remains an open problem.

The legacy problem: protocols that cannot be retired

SS7 and Diameter embody a trust model from the 1970s, yet they remain the foundation for global mobile roaming and interconnection and cannot simply be switched off, because 4G and 5G users are routinely downgraded to legacy signaling when roaming or when sending SMS.22 Firewalls mitigate but do not eliminate the exposure, and the operators most able to deploy them are not always the ones whose interconnect partners present the risk. The deeper research question is how to evolve global signaling toward an authenticated, encrypted model without a flag-day cutover across thousands of independently operated networks — an internet-scale protocol-transition problem with few good precedents.

The lawful-intercept paradox

Salt Typhoon surfaced a contradiction at the heart of communications policy. The same CALEA architecture that requires carriers to build lawful-intercept access for authorized government use is itself a high-value attack surface: reporting on the campaign indicated the intruders reached the systems used to service lawful-intercept requests, turning a surveillance capability into an espionage vector.35 This sharpens the long-running encryption debate. Proposals for guaranteed lawful access to encrypted communications presuppose that an access mechanism can be built that only authorized parties can use — precisely the assumption Salt Typhoon falsified at the network layer. The unresolved tension between the state's interest in lawful access and the demonstrated reality that any built-in access point can be subverted is now an empirical finding, not merely a theoretical objection, and it has no settled policy answer.

The attribution and deterrence problem in the grey zone

The physical plane has become a theatre of grey-zone conflict for which the sector has no deterrence doctrine. The wave of submarine-cable damage incidents around Taiwan and in the Baltic since 2023 has been extraordinarily difficult to attribute: dragged anchors are plausibly deniable as accidents, and even when a suspect vessel is identified, proving intent and establishing jurisdiction over an incident in international waters is hard enough that authorities have repeatedly declined to allege sabotage.27 The result is a class of attacks that are cheap to mount, hard to prove, and therefore poorly deterred — carried out with modified commercial vessels and, increasingly, the prospect of uncrewed underwater systems.30 Research needs span the technical (real-time cable-monitoring and fault-localization sensing, autonomous inspection and repair) and the legal-strategic (an attribution and response framework for sub-threshold attacks on shared infrastructure in international waters).

Scale at the edge: IoT and the disaggregated network

The Mirai-Dyn episode demonstrated that the security of the communications core can be overwhelmed from the consumer edge, where hundreds of thousands of default-credentialed devices were trivially conscripted into a record-setting botnet.19 The number and heterogeneity of connected devices has only grown, and the architectural shift to disaggregated, software-defined, multi-vendor 5G networks — with AI/ML-driven control logic in Open RAN's near-real-time RIC and rApps/xApps from many suppliers — expands the surface further, introducing the novel problem of securing machine-learning components that themselves become an attack target.12 Maintaining rigorous isolation between network slices that share physical infrastructure, so that a breach of a low-assurance consumer slice cannot reach a high-assurance public-safety or industrial slice, is an active and unfinished area of standardization and research.45

The concentration problem: efficiency versus systemic risk

Market consolidation has concentrated the substrate into a small number of backbone carriers, managed-DNS providers, cloud platforms, content-delivery networks, and submarine-cable consortia. Each concentration is individually efficient and individually a systemic single point of failure: the Dyn outage showed how one managed-DNS provider's compromise cascades across dozens of dependent properties,18 and Salt Typhoon's simultaneous presence across multiple major U.S. carriers showed how a handful of compromises can constitute near-comprehensive access.36 Modeling and governing this systemic, correlated risk — rather than scoring each provider's security in isolation — is exactly the cross-sector analytical mandate NSM-22 assigns to CISA as National Coordinator, and it remains methodologically immature.6

The cryptographic horizon: harvest now, decrypt later

Finally, the migration to post-quantum cryptography is a research-and-engineering program the sector has only begun. With NIST's first post-quantum standards published in 2024, the task is to inventory and replace the public-key cryptography embedded throughout signaling, routing security, DNSSEC, and transport — a transition complicated by long-lived embedded devices and the sheer scale of the installed base. The urgency is driven by the “harvest now, decrypt later” threat model: an adversary intercepting traffic from a tapped cable or signaling link today can store it against the day a cryptographically relevant quantum computer can decrypt it, which makes the confidentiality of today's communications a function of a migration that will take years to complete.

The research agenda in brief

An expert tracking the field should watch for progress on: scalable, low-friction enforcement of RPKI route-origin validation across the long tail of networks; formal verification and authenticated evolution of inter-domain routing and global signaling; real-time submarine-cable monitoring with autonomous inspection and repair, paired with a legal framework for grey-zone attribution; provable isolation guarantees for 5G network slices and security for AI/ML components in Open RAN; supply-chain provenance beyond exclusion lists, including machine-verifiable SBOMs for firmware; AI-assisted anomaly detection tuned to the long-dwell, living-off-the-land tradecraft of state actors; and a practical, scaled migration path to post-quantum cryptography across legacy infrastructure. None of these is blocked on a single breakthrough; all are blocked on the harder problem of deployment at national and global scale.

07 — Conclusion

The substrate must hold

The Communications Sector is the one critical-infrastructure sector whose failure is never confined to itself. It is the connective tissue through which the other fifteen sense, decide, coordinate, and act — and that role, formalized in CISA's designation of communications as an enabling function for operations across all critical infrastructure, is the single fact from which everything in this analysis follows.1

Three themes recur. The first is asymmetry of dependence: communications depends upward on almost nothing while nearly everything depends downward on it, which converts any sufficiently broad communications compromise into a correlated, cross-sector event rather than an isolated outage. The second is the persistence of foundational fragility: the routing, naming, and signaling planes that determine whether the network works on any given day were designed for a smaller, more trusting world, and a 1970s trust model still underlies state-of-the-art 5G radio. The third is the gap between capability and obligation: the technical means to close the major surfaces largely exist and are standardized, but their deployment is throttled by incomplete coverage, unfunded mandates, and a governance regime that — as the CALEA reversal demonstrated — can create and dissolve the sector's strongest cybersecurity duty within a single year.51

The threat actors have already internalized the sector's strategic value. Volt Typhoon's pre-positioning inside communications and the infrastructure sectors it touches is a contingency plan for disruption at a moment of geopolitical choosing;31 Salt Typhoon's deep, persistent penetration of major carriers — reaching even the lawful-intercept machinery and resisting eviction long after discovery — is espionage at the scale of the substrate itself.34 Neither is a smash-and-grab; both are patient campaigns that treat the communications layer as the terrain on which a future conflict will be fought.

The work ahead is therefore less about discovery than about resolve: driving mature controls past the coverage thresholds where they protect by default, funding the replacement of equipment the government itself has deemed unsafe, building a deterrence doctrine for grey-zone attacks on shared physical infrastructure, and settling on a governance model stable enough that operators know what they must do from one year to the next. The substrate is holding, but it is holding on voluntary effort and contested authority — and the sector that everything else depends on is precisely the one that can least afford to depend on goodwill.

Just because it was secure six months ago does not mean it is now.

— characterization of the Salt Typhoon lesson, attributed to FBI cyber official Cynthia Kaiser

Resources for practitioners

The following authoritative bodies, frameworks, and standing guidance are the primary references an operator, regulator, or researcher works from. They are grouped by function; full bibliographic details for every factual claim appear in the References that follow.

Government & coordinating bodies

CISA — Communications Sector Risk Management Agency

The Sector Risk Management Agency for Communications and the National Coordinator for critical-infrastructure security under NSM-22. Source for sector profiles, the Infrastructure Dependency Primer, and National Critical Functions analysis.

FCC — Communications Security, Reliability & Interoperability Council (CSRIC)

The federal advisory committee producing consensus best practices on network reliability, 911/NG911 continuity, signaling (SS7/Diameter) security, submarine-cable resiliency, and timing resilience. Rechartered roughly every two years; CSRIC X runs 2026–2028.

National Coordinating Center for Communications (NCC) & Comms-ISAC

Established under Executive Order 12472 (1984) to coordinate restoration of national-security and emergency-preparedness communications; operates the Communications Information Sharing and Analysis Center for public-private threat exchange.

Frameworks & technical standards

NIST Cybersecurity Framework 2.0 (CSWP 29)

The cross-sector framework organizing outcomes into six functions — Govern, Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover. The 2024 revision elevates governance and supply-chain risk to first-class status.

NIST SP 800-189 — Resilient Interdomain Routing

Authoritative U.S. guidance on securing BGP through RPKI, Route Origin Authorizations, and Route Origin Validation. Paired in practice with the MANRS initiative and NIST SP 800-207 (Zero Trust Architecture).

Enduring Security Framework (ESF) — 5G & Open RAN guidance

NSA/CISA-led public-private guidance on the security of 5G, Open RAN's disaggregated multi-vendor architecture, and network-slicing isolation, including cloud tenant separation.

Key advisories & incident guidance

CISA Enhanced Visibility & Hardening Guidance for Communications Infrastructure (Dec 2024)

Post–Salt Typhoon consolidated best practices for telecommunications engineers covering configuration management, monitoring, and device hardening, with industry contributions.

Multi-agency advisory documenting living-off-the-land pre-positioning across Communications, Energy, Transportation, and Water and Wastewater for potential disruptive effect.

Joint Advisory on Salt Typhoon / countering PRC compromise of comms (Aug 2025)

International advisory detailing router-exploitation tradecraft against telecommunications and other sectors, with mitigation and detection guidance.

Supply chain & infrastructure security

FCC Covered List & the Secure and Trusted Communications Networks program

The exclusion list of unacceptable-risk equipment and services, the equipment-authorization regime under the Secure Equipment Act, and the rip-and-replace reimbursement program.

FCC submarine cable security rules & reporting systems

The 2025 submarine-cable security and resilience rules, plus the Network Outage Reporting System (NORS) and Disaster Information Reporting System (DIRS) for operational telemetry.
