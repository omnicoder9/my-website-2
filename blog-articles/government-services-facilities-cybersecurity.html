Sector Risk Profile — Cyber & Information Security

Defending
the State

Cyber and information security across the United States’ government services and facilities — the one sector whose compromise reaches into every other.

Domain United States
Citations Vancouver
Format Technical briefing

Every critical infrastructure sector in the United States has a defender. The Government Services and Facilities Sector is, in a sense, the defender — the buildings, networks, and people through which the American state issues benefits, prosecutes crimes, defends borders, conducts elections, and stores the secrets that protect everything else. Its scope runs from general-use office buildings to military installations, embassies, courthouses, and national laboratories, and it explicitly takes in the cyber elements that protect those assets, such as access-control systems and closed-circuit television, along with the individuals who hold tactical, operational, or strategic knowledge.1 When this sector is compromised, the damage rarely stays inside it.

CISA, together with the General Services Administration, is the designated Sector Risk Management Agency for government facilities.2 Under the 2024 National Security Memorandum on Critical Infrastructure Security and Resilience, which replaced the decade-old Presidential Policy Directive 21, CISA additionally serves as the National Coordinator for the security and resilience of all sixteen sectors — a whole-of-government role meant to give the nation a single, comprehensive view of physical and cyber threats.3 This briefing examines that sector’s technology, the threats and consequences specific to it, the way a single intrusion can propagate across the other fifteen sectors, the frameworks built to defend it, and the work that remains undone.

§ 1 — The sector and its perimeter

What “government facilities” actually means

The sector is unusually heterogeneous. Beyond office buildings, it is formally divided into three subsectors: Education Facilities, covering pre-kindergarten through twelfth-grade schools, higher education, and trade schools; Election Infrastructure; and National Monuments and Icons.1 Some of these assets are open to the public for business or recreation; others hold highly sensitive information, materials, and equipment that is never meant to be seen.1

The Election Infrastructure Subsector is the newest member. On 6 January 2017, following Russian interference in the 2016 election, the Department of Homeland Security designated election infrastructure as critical infrastructure, placing voter-registration databases, voting machines, and the facilities that house them under the Government Facilities Sector.4 The designation reflects the legal threshold for the term itself: systems and assets, physical or virtual, so vital that their incapacity or destruction would have a debilitating effect on national security, the economy, or public health and safety.4

What gives the sector its outsized importance, though, is not a building but a network. The civilian agencies of the federal executive branch — the Federal Civilian Executive Branch, or FCEB — run the .gov systems that constitute the digital body of the state. The Federal Information Security Modernization Act makes these systems part of the Government Services and Facilities Sector and authorizes CISA to issue Binding Operational Directives compelling agencies to remediate specific vulnerabilities.5 The sixteen-sector model these directives serve was established under Presidential Policy Directive 21 in 2013 and reaffirmed, with a restructured set of designations, in the 2024 memorandum.5

§ 2 — The technology estate

Two attack surfaces, increasingly one

A government facility presents two distinct technological layers, and the defining security trend of the past decade is their convergence. The first layer is informational: the high-value data systems that issue tax refunds and benefits, manage court and personnel records, and, at the upper reaches, process classified material. The second is physical: the operational technology that runs the building itself.

Identity at the door

Access to a federal building turns on identity. Under Homeland Security Presidential Directive 12 and the Federal Information Processing Standard 201, federal employees and contractors carry Personal Identity Verification credentials, and the Physical Access Control Systems that read them — identification cards, card readers, and validation infrastructure — electronically confirm who a person is and what they may enter.6 The Office of Management and Budget and GSA have spent years pushing agencies toward secure, interoperable, government-approved access-control systems, though oversight of that effort has been uneven.6

The building as a computer

Modern federal buildings are saturated with networked control systems: building automation systems for heating, ventilation, and air conditioning; advanced metering; lighting control; digital signage; and the same access-control and camera systems that secure the perimeter. GSA’s own technical guidance is blunt about the implication — these are information-technology systems that collect building data, and as such they fall under the Federal Information Security Modernization Act and the same security standards as any other federal system.7 As these systems are automated and connected to other networks and the internet, the line between a facilities problem and a cyber problem dissolves.7

This is operational technology, and it does not behave like an office network. Facility-related control systems and industrial control systems directly monitor or control physical devices and processes; if they are compromised, the consequences are both cyber and physical, and the security tools built for traditional IT frequently do not work on them.8 The risk is not theoretical or new. As far back as 2014, the Government Accountability Office warned that the federal government was not addressing cyber risk to the building and access-control systems at the roughly 9,000 facilities then protected by the Federal Protective Service, and that it lacked a strategy even defining the problem or assigning responsibility for it.8

The remedy has been slow and structural. In 2024, GSA announced it would standardize a single building-automation-system solution across its portfolio, explicitly to mitigate the cybersecurity risk created by a sprawl of disparate hardware and software.9 The convergence cuts the other way for elections, too: the subsector’s voter-registration databases, voting machines, and electronic poll books are ordinary information systems running in extraordinary circumstances, exposed to the same intrusion techniques as any other government network but operating under an unforgiving public deadline.4

§ 3 — The threat landscape

Theft, disruption, and footholds held in reserve

The sector draws the full spectrum of adversaries — foreign intelligence services, criminal ransomware crews, and hacktivists — but the harms cluster into three recurring forms: the theft of uniquely sensitive data, the disruption of public services, and the quiet pre-positioning of access for future sabotage. Each has a defining case.

Case File — Espionage2014–2015

The OPM background-investigation breach

Between mid-2014 and early 2015, intruders moved through the networks of the Office of Personnel Management and exfiltrated the Standard Form 86 security-clearance records it held. By the time the damage assessment closed, roughly 22.1 million individuals were affected; the background-investigation database alone yielded the Social Security numbers of 21.5 million people, covering 19.7 million clearance applicants and 1.8 million spouses, relatives, and associates, along with fingerprints and the credentials applicants had used to file their forms.10 One of the largest breaches of government data in U.S. history, it was attributed to a China-based advanced persistent threat, widely assessed to be an arm of the Ministry of State Security; the activity was detected only in April 2015, and the OPM director resigned.11

An SF-86 is not an ordinary record. It is a counterintelligence dossier — foreign contacts, finances, health, family, every vulnerability a background investigator could surface. Its theft is not a privacy incident that ages out of relevance; it is a permanent intelligence windfall against the people the United States trusts with its secrets.

Case File — Supply Chain2019–2020

SolarWinds and the SUNBURST backdoor

Russia’s Foreign Intelligence Service breached the network of SolarWinds, a Texas software company, in September 2019, conducted a dry run injecting test code into its Orion network-monitoring suite, and then, beginning in early 2020, inserted a trojanized backdoor known as SUNBURST into Orion software updates that SolarWinds distributed, unaware, to its customers.12 As many as 18,000 organizations downloaded the compromised build; a much smaller, deliberately selected subset received follow-on intrusion, and the campaign was publicly attributed to the Russian service in April 2021.13 The confirmed federal victims read like a roster of the state itself — the Departments of Treasury, Commerce, State, Justice, Energy including the National Nuclear Security Administration, and Homeland Security, alongside the agency charged with defending federal networks.14

The discovery came from none of the government’s monitoring programs but from the security firm FireEye investigating its own compromise in December 2020.12 The lesson was structural: the trust an organization places in a digitally signed update from a vetted vendor had itself become the attack vector, and defenses that recognize only previously seen malicious traffic were blind to a threat that arrived through the front door.

When the services stop

Where intelligence services steal, criminals disrupt. State, local, tribal, and territorial governments — which deliver the services the public actually touches — have proven a soft and lucrative target for ransomware. On 22 March 2018, the SamSam ransomware, which spreads not through phishing but by brute-forcing weak credentials on internet-facing remote access, encrypted roughly a third of the City of Atlanta’s applications. It took down court systems, online bill payment, and even the public Wi-Fi at Hartsfield-Jackson, the nation’s busiest airport; residents reverted to paying bills on paper.15 A city audit two months earlier had already counted 1,500 to 2,000 vulnerabilities. Atlanta refused the ransom of roughly $51,000; its recovery was first budgeted at $2.7 million and projected toward $17 million.15 SamSam ultimately claimed more than 200 victims, and the Justice Department charged two Iranian nationals who had collected some $6 million in ransom and inflicted an estimated $30 million in damage.16

The pattern repeated in Baltimore. On 7 May 2019, the RobbinHood ransomware took most of the city’s systems offline, with restoration stretching across weeks and an estimated cost near $18 million.17 Property transactions, water billing, and city email all stalled, and the damage was magnified by aging, unsupported software that had gone unpatched.18 The civic lesson is plain: to the public, government is its services. When the records system is encrypted, a city governs on paper, and the cost of recovery dwarfs the ransom many times over.

Threat Brief — Pre-Positioning2023–present

Volt Typhoon and Salt Typhoon

The most consequential current threat is neither theft nor extortion but emplacement. In a February 2024 joint advisory, CISA, the National Security Agency, and the FBI assessed that the People’s Republic of China state-sponsored actor known as Volt Typhoon had compromised IT and operational-technology networks across U.S. critical infrastructure — communications, energy, transportation, and water and wastewater — using “living off the land” techniques that rely on a system’s own built-in administrative tools rather than detectable malware.19 The agencies judged the purpose to be pre-positioning for disruptive or destructive attacks in the event of a major crisis or conflict, and CISA’s then-director described what had been found as “the tip of the iceberg.”20 The Justice Department disrupted a botnet of compromised home and small-office routers the group used to obscure its operations.19

A parallel campaign, Salt Typhoon, disclosed by the FBI and CISA in November 2024, penetrated U.S. telecommunications carriers and accessed customer communications and metadata — who contacted whom, when, and from where.21 The intelligence value of that metadata is independent of any message content, and it strikes a lifeline on which the federal government itself depends.22 These are not data thefts to be totted up and closed out. They are footholds, held in reserve.

Corroding the count

The election subsector faces a threat aimed less at machinery than at belief. During the 2024 cycle, foreign adversaries targeted state and local election offices with network probes, distributed-denial-of-service attacks, and ransomware operations, while CISA and the Election Infrastructure Information Sharing and Analysis Center also coordinated warnings about envelopes of white powder mailed to election offices in at least fifteen states.2324 The strategic goal is frequently not to alter a tally — a difficult and detectable act against a paper-backed, audited system — but to corrode public confidence that the tally is true. The integrity of the count and the public’s belief in it are distinct targets, and the second is the softer one.24

The sector’s adversaries are increasingly after leverage rather than loot: a clearance file that never loses its value, a router that waits, a service that can be switched off on a day of someone else’s choosing.

§ 4 — The blast radius

Why a breach here reaches everywhere

The defining feature of critical infrastructure is not the assets but the wiring between them. The National Infrastructure Protection Plan organizes national preparedness around a set of lifeline functions — communications, energy, transportation, and water — precisely because the other sectors cannot function without them.25 Analysts treat these dependencies as a risk multiplier: understanding and addressing cross-sector dependencies is one of the core tenets of the plan, and a disruption in one network can propagate as cascading and escalating failures into others.26

The 2017 hurricane season in Puerto Rico is the textbook demonstration. The loss of the power grid cascaded into a near-total loss of cellular communication, a scarcity of drinking water, and impassable roads; the compounded failure of those lifeline systems contributed to nearly 3,000 deaths.27 The coupling runs even among the lifelines themselves — energy-sector control systems depend on reliable networks, and communications networks depend on electricity — so that a fault in one can ripple through the rest.28

Government’s double exposure

The Government Services and Facilities Sector occupies a uniquely dangerous position in this web, because it is both a consumer of the lifelines and an enabler of everything else. A federal courthouse or data center needs power, water, and telecommunications like any other building; that is the downstream dependency. But government services — emergency response, regulatory oversight, public safety, the courts, benefits administration, the issuance of identity itself — are upstream of all sixteen sectors. A compromised government is a compromised referee, regulator, and first responder for the entire system.

The SolarWinds intrusion shows the propagation directly. A single supply-chain compromise reached, in one campaign, the Treasury’s role in financial oversight, the National Nuclear Security Administration’s custody of the nuclear stockpile, and the Commerce Department’s stewardship of communications policy — the governing functions of several sectors at once.14 The Typhoon campaigns make the same point from the opposite direction: an adversary embedded in the Communications Sector, or pre-positioned across energy, water, and transportation, is by definition within reach of the government that depends on those services to operate.19 The blast radius of a government compromise is the map of the state’s own responsibilities.

§ 5 — The defensive architecture

The federal cybersecurity stack

The defense of federal systems is one of the most heavily codified areas of cybersecurity in the world — a layered apparatus of law, standards, programs, and directives. Understanding it means reading from the statute upward.

The legal spine: FISMA

At the base sits the Federal Information Security Modernization Act of 2014, which amended the original Federal Information Security Management Act of 2002 and is codified at 44 U.S.C. § 3551 and following, Public Law 113-283. It requires every federal agency to develop, document, and implement an agency-wide information security program; NIST writes the standards, the Office of Management and Budget oversees compliance, and agency heads report their security status, with independent assessment from inspectors general.29 The 2014 modernization codified the Department of Homeland Security’s role administering security policy for civilian agencies, clarified OMB’s oversight, and required agencies to report major incidents to Congress.30

The method: the Risk Management Framework

NIST translates the statute into practice through the Risk Management Framework of Special Publication 800-37, which moves a system through a disciplined life cycle.31 Its seven steps run as follows:

  1. Prepare — establish the organizational risk strategy, roles, and system boundaries.
  2. Categorize — rate the system Low, Moderate, or High using FIPS 199, by the impact a breach would have on confidentiality, integrity, and availability.
  3. Select — choose a baseline of controls from the Special Publication 800-53 catalog and tailor it.
  4. Implement — deploy and document the controls.
  5. Assess — verify, through independent assessment, that the controls work.
  6. Authorize — have a senior official accept the residual risk and grant an Authorization to Operate, typically valid for three years.
  7. Monitor — maintain continuous oversight as the system and threats evolve.

The standards beneath these steps are themselves foundational: FIPS 199 (2004) governs categorization, FIPS 200 (2006) sets minimum security requirements, and Special Publication 800-53, now in Revision 5, is the control catalog from which every federal baseline is drawn.3233

The common language: CSF 2.0

Alongside the mandatory framework, NIST maintains the voluntary Cybersecurity Framework, whose version 2.0, published in February 2024, is the first major revision since 2014.34 It organizes cybersecurity outcomes into six functions — Govern, Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, and Recover — across 22 categories and 106 subcategories. The newly added Govern function elevates risk governance and supply-chain risk management, and the framework’s scope was widened from critical infrastructure to organizations of every kind, making it a common vocabulary that maps onto the federal regime and far beyond it.35

Cloud and continuous visibility

For cloud services, the Federal Risk and Authorization Management Program provides a standardized, government-wide approach to security assessment, authorization, and continuous monitoring, with Low, Moderate, and High baselines drawn from Special Publication 800-53 and assessments performed by accredited third-party organizations.36 Established in 2011 and administered by GSA, FedRAMP was written into permanent law by the FedRAMP Authorization Act, enacted within the fiscal-year 2023 National Defense Authorization Act.37

Three long-standing mandates structure most agency effort: FedRAMP for cloud, Continuous Diagnostics and Mitigation for network and data visibility, and Trusted Internet Connections for internet-based security.38 The Continuous Diagnostics and Mitigation program, run by CISA, gives agencies continuous visibility into their assets, identities, network activity, and data, reporting into a central dashboard.36 Guarding the network boundary itself, the EINSTEIN system — formally the National Cybersecurity Protection System — has since 2004 recorded the flow of traffic between federal civilian networks and the internet, with later phases adding signature-based intrusion detection and, at Trusted Internet Connection gateways, intrusion prevention.39 That intrusion-detection capability is now being restructured into a Cyber Analytics and Data System, in part because a signature-based approach can only block threats it has already seen.40 Finally, CISA wields Binding Operational Directives as compulsory instruments: BOD 22-01 requires agencies to remediate vulnerabilities on the Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog, and BOD 23-01 requires asset and vulnerability visibility, with results fed to the CDM dashboard on a fixed schedule.41

The post-SolarWinds turn: zero trust

The supply-chain and pipeline shocks of 2020 and 2021 produced Executive Order 14028, signed on 12 May 2021, which set the federal government on a path to zero-trust architecture, secure cloud adoption, and software-supply-chain security, and directed the Commerce Department to publish minimum elements for a Software Bill of Materials and a definition of “critical software.”42 The order charged NIST with developing secure-software-development guidance and required agencies to procure only from producers that attest to following it.43

The operational mandate followed in the Office of Management and Budget’s memorandum M-22-09, a Federal Zero Trust Strategy whose central premise is that agencies can no longer rely on network-perimeter protection to guard against unauthorized access.44 Among its concrete requirements is phishing-resistant multi-factor authentication, with the retirement of weaker methods such as one-time codes delivered by SMS or voice.45 CISA’s Zero Trust Maturity Model, now in its second version, structures the transition across five pillars — identity, devices, networks, applications and workloads, and data — threaded by three cross-cutting capabilities of visibility and analytics, automation and orchestration, and governance.46

Securing the operational layer

The control systems that run buildings and elections demand a different discipline, because in operational technology availability and physical safety often outrank confidentiality. Federal practice leans on NIST Special Publication 800-82, the guide to operational-technology and industrial-control-system security, which adapts the control catalog to those constraints.47 The international standard IEC 62443 contributes the architecture of “zones and conduits” — grouping assets into security zones and hardening the connections between them — a segmentation model well suited to the building-automation and access-control systems described earlier.48

The connective tissue: information sharing

Underneath the frameworks runs a network of Information Sharing and Analysis Centers. The Election Infrastructure ISAC, operated by the nonprofit Center for Internet Security, and the broader Multi-State ISAC distribute threat intelligence to state and local governments; since 2018 the Election Infrastructure ISAC has deployed intrusion-detection sensors known as Albert sensors to elections systems in all fifty states, and because the centers turn shared network data into threat signatures, the value of the system grows with every jurisdiction that joins.49

§ 6 — The unfinished work

Where the defense still lags

The apparatus is formidable, but it is not finished, and several of its gaps are structural rather than budgetary.

Yesterday’s signatures, today’s intruders

The most basic limitation is epistemic. A signature-based boundary system recognizes only threats it has already seen and analyzed; it cannot identify a genuinely novel attack at first encounter, which is the principal reason the EINSTEIN capability is being restructured.40 That is precisely the seam SolarWinds slipped through, and it is the same seam Volt Typhoon exploits by living off the land — using a system’s own legitimate tools so that almost nothing registers as malicious. Detecting that activity demands a shift from signatures to behavioral analytics, anomaly detection, and proactive hunting, an area where tooling and research still trail the adversary.19

The weight of legacy

Much of government runs on old code. Atlanta’s pre-attack audit counted as many as 2,000 vulnerabilities; Baltimore’s damage was magnified by unsupported software that had gone unpatched.1517 Technical debt at this scale is not a handful of missed updates but an accumulation that outruns the capacity to remediate it — and it is most acute precisely where budgets are thinnest.

The merging of worlds

The convergence of information and operational technology, and of physical and cyber security, widens the attack surface faster than governance adapts. A building’s ventilation controller or a card-reader network is now a potential path onto the enterprise, yet such systems were historically managed by facilities staff outside the security program, and the federal government was slow even to define the problem at the scale of its real estate.87

The resource asymmetry and a fragile partnership

State and local governments — which run elections and deliver most of the services the public depends on — confront nation-state adversaries with small security teams, constrained budgets, and a chronic shortage of skilled staff; federal support has historically narrowed that gap.23 That support, however, has lately become uncertain. In early 2025, CISA paused its election-security work pending an internal review and moved to cut funding for the Election Infrastructure ISAC, while the Critical Infrastructure Partnership Advisory Council — a protected forum for public-private threat sharing — was terminated.2423 Election officials have described a partnership built since 2017 as unraveling and a hard-won trust as broken.50 Because the value of a shared-defense network rises with participation, the erosion of the institution is itself a security risk.

The research agenda

The frontier work follows directly from these gaps. Detection must move decisively from known-bad signatures toward behavior and anomaly. The zero-trust transition depends on the automation and orchestration its own maturity model names as a cross-cutting capability, because manual enforcement cannot scale to the size of the federal estate.46 And software-supply-chain assurance needs the Bill of Materials to become routine and machine-checkable rather than a paper attestation, so that the next SolarWinds is caught in the build pipeline rather than in hindsight.43 None of these is solved; each is a place where the state’s defenders are still building the aircraft in flight.

§ — Coda

The defended center

The Government Services and Facilities Sector will never be “secured” in the sense of finished. It is defended continuously, against adversaries who have learned that the most efficient route to sixteen sectors runs through the one that serves them all — the courthouse and the clearance file, the voter roll and the building’s own thermostat. The frameworks are mature and the legal scaffolding is dense; the threats are maturing faster, and the remaining margin is held by people and partnerships at least as much as by technology. The work of defending the state is, in the end, the work of keeping the institutions that defend everything else worthy of the trust placed in them.
