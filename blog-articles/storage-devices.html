§ 01

Storage Fundamentals

Every computing system, from the simplest microcontroller to the largest hyperscale data centre, depends on its ability to retain information across time. Storage devices are the physical embodiments of that requirement: components that accept digital data, preserve it through power cycles, and return it reliably on demand. Understanding the engineering trade-offs that govern storage performance and longevity is foundational knowledge for anyone who builds, maintains, or troubleshoots computer systems.

Persistent Storage

Storage devices are classified as non-volatile — they retain data without continuous electrical power, distinguishing them from DRAM (volatile memory), which loses its contents the moment power is removed. This persistence is achieved through fundamentally different physical mechanisms depending on the technology: magnetised domains on a metal platter (HDD), trapped charge in a floating-gate transistor (NAND flash SSD), or optical phase-change material (optical disc).

In a modern PC the storage subsystem occupies a specific position in the memory hierarchy: it sits below system RAM in access speed, but above RAM in capacity and persistence. A typical workflow sees the operating system load programme code from storage into RAM, execute it at CPU speed, and write modified data back to storage for long-term retention.

Capacity Measurements

Storage capacity is described using two overlapping unit systems that are a persistent source of confusion. Drive manufacturers use decimal (SI) prefixes, while operating systems have historically reported sizes using binary-based units. The result is that a drive labelled 1 TB by its manufacturer is displayed as approximately 931 GiB by Windows or Linux.

Decimal (Manufacturer) Value Binary (OS) Value Difference
1 KB (kilobyte) 1,000 B 1 KiB (kibibyte) 1,024 B 2.4%
1 MB (megabyte) 1,000,000 B 1 MiB (mebibyte) 1,048,576 B 4.9%
1 GB (gigabyte) 1,000,000,000 B 1 GiB (gibibyte) 1,073,741,824 B 7.4%
1 TB (terabyte) 1012 B 1 TiB (tebibyte) 240 B 9.95%

The discrepancy grows with each order of magnitude. A 2 TB drive shows as roughly 1.82 TiB in Windows Explorer — not a hidden cost, but an artefact of unit definitions. IEC 80000-13 formalised the binary prefix names (KiB, MiB, GiB, TiB, PiB) in 2007 to eliminate ambiguity in technical documentation.

Performance Considerations

No single figure captures storage performance. Engineers use a battery of metrics, each illuminating a different behavioural facet:

Sequential throughput (MB/s or GB/s) measures the rate at which large, contiguous blocks of data are transferred — important for video editing, large file copies, and OS imaging.

Random IOPS (Input/Output Operations Per Second) measures how quickly a device handles many small, scattered reads and writes — the dominant workload for operating system booting, application launching, and database access. A drive with excellent sequential throughput may have poor random IOPS if its seek latency is high.

Latency (µs or ms) is the time from when a read or write command is issued to when the first byte is returned. Lower latency corresponds directly to a snappier user experience.

Queue depth describes how many commands a device can accept and process concurrently. HDDs are designed for low queue depths (1–4) while NVMe SSDs exploit the NVMe command set's 65,535 queue depth capacity for massive parallelism.

§ 02

Hard Disk Drives (HDD)

The hard disk drive is one of the oldest and most durable technologies in consumer computing. Introduced by IBM in 1956 in the form of the RAMAC 305 — a refrigerator-sized cabinet storing 5 MB — the HDD has since shrunk to palm-sized form factors while expanding capacity into the tens of terabytes. Despite the ascent of solid-state storage, HDDs remain the dominant technology for bulk storage, archival data, and any use case where cost-per-terabyte matters above all else.

Magnetic Storage and the Read/Write Head

An HDD stores data by magnetising microscopic regions on the surface of one or more spinning aluminium or glass platters coated with a ferromagnetic material (typically an iron-oxide compound). Each region can be magnetised in one of two orientations, encoding a binary 0 or 1. A read/write head — one per platter surface — rides on a cushion of air only a few nanometres thick, floating above the platter at speeds comparable to a jet aircraft flying one metre above the ground.

Two dominant recording technologies exist in current production drives:

CMR (Conventional Magnetic Recording) writes tracks side-by-side with a guard band between each. Reads and writes are independent and predictable in performance. CMR is the recommended technology for most use cases, particularly when drives will be used in RAID arrays or with mixed read/write workloads.[1]

SMR (Shingled Magnetic Recording) overlaps tracks like roof shingles to increase areal density, enabling higher capacities at lower cost. However, when data within an SMR zone must be rewritten, the drive must read, modify, and re-write the entire zone — a process that causes dramatic write performance degradation as the drive fills. SMR drives are generally unsuitable for RAID arrays or write-intensive workloads.[1]

"HDDs are also often gauged by rotations per minute (RPM), which is usually a direct indicator of performance — though not the only indicator."[1]

RPM Ratings and Their Practical Implications

The spindle speed — measured in revolutions per minute — is the single most important mechanical performance parameter of an HDD. It determines rotational latency (the average time for the target sector to rotate under the read/write head) and contributes to sequential throughput (since more rotations per second mean more data passes under the head per unit time).

Common spindle speeds and their use cases:

The seek time (time for the actuator arm to position the head over the correct track) adds further latency. Typical modern 7,200 RPM drives have average seek times of 8–12 ms. Combined with rotational latency, the total average access time is 12–17 ms — orders of magnitude slower than any SSD technology.

SATA Interface

Consumer and prosumer HDDs use the Serial ATA (SATA) interface for both data and power. The current revision — SATA III (also called SATA 6Gb/s) — provides a theoretical maximum interface bandwidth of 600 MB/s.[3] This ceiling is academic for HDDs: even the fastest 7,200 RPM drives reach only 285–295 MB/s sequential read under ideal conditions,[2] meaning the SATA interface is never the bottleneck in HDD performance.

Enterprise drives may use SAS (Serial Attached SCSI), which offers dual-port connectivity, higher sustained throughput, and superior error recovery, making it the preferred interface for servers and storage arrays. SAS is backward-compatible with SATA drives but not vice versa.

§ 03

Solid-State Drives (SSD)

A solid-state drive stores data entirely in semiconductor memory — specifically, NAND flash — with no mechanical components whatsoever. The absence of moving parts is the defining advantage: it eliminates seek time and rotational latency entirely, yields access times measured in microseconds rather than milliseconds, and makes the drive immune to physical shock during operation.

NAND Flash Storage: Cell Architecture

NAND flash memory stores data in cells built around floating-gate or charge-trap transistors. The number of bits stored per cell determines the type designation — and is the central engineering trade-off controlling density, speed, cost, and longevity:[6]

TypeBits/CellVoltage StatesP/E Cycles (typical)Use Case
SLC — Single Level Cell 1 2 50,000–100,000[7] Enterprise / industrial; highest cost
MLC — Multi Level Cell 2 4 3,000–10,000[7] Enterprise; rare in consumer products
TLC — Triple Level Cell 3 8 1,500–3,000[5] Consumer & prosumer — dominant technology
QLC — Quad Level Cell 4 16 ~150–500[6] High-capacity, read-intensive storage; lower cost

The industry transitioned from planar (2D) NAND — where cells are arranged side-by-side in a single layer — to 3D (vertical) NAND around 2015. By stacking cell layers vertically (current high-end NAND uses 200+ layers), manufacturers achieve much higher density than any 2D shrink could provide, while simultaneously relaxing the tight geometric tolerances that degraded reliability.[6]

⚙ SLC Cache (pSLC)

Many consumer TLC and QLC drives configure a portion of their NAND to operate in pseudo-SLC mode, accepting only one bit per cell at far higher speed. This SLC cache absorbs bursts of writes at near-MLC performance before the controller migrates data to the underlying TLC storage in the background. Once the SLC cache is exhausted — which can happen during sustained large writes — throughput drops to the native TLC write speed, which can be substantially lower.

Random performance, expressed as IOPS (Input/Output Operations Per Second at 4 KiB block size), is often the more practically important metric. A flagship PCIe Gen4 drive like the Samsung 990 PRO delivers up to 1.55 million write IOPS,[22] compared to a few hundred IOPS for a mechanical hard drive. This difference directly translates to operating system boot times, application launch speeds, and compile times.

A drive's DRAM cache further affects sustained random performance. Controllers use DRAM to maintain the Flash Translation Layer (FTL) mapping table — a directory of where logical blocks are physically located on the NAND. DRAM-less drives (common in budget SSDs) must store this mapping on the NAND itself, introducing additional read operations and latency spikes, particularly under sustained mixed workloads.[5]

Endurance Concepts: TBW and DWPD

Every NAND cell degrades with each Program/Erase (P/E) cycle. The oxide layer surrounding the floating gate weakens slightly with each erase operation; after enough cycles, the cell can no longer reliably hold charge and must be retired by the controller's bad-block management system.[8]

Manufacturers express endurance in two standardised metrics:[8]

TBW (Terabytes Written) — the total cumulative data volume the drive is warranted to accept before wear may affect integrity. A quality 1 TB TLC NVMe drive is typically rated between 300 and 1,200 TBW.[5]

DWPD (Drive Writes Per Day) — how many times the drive's full capacity can be rewritten per day across the warranty period. Consumer drives are typically 0.3–0.6 DWPD; enterprise drives may be 3–10 DWPD.

The relationship is: TBW = DWPD × Capacity (TB) × Warranty (years) × 365.[8]

📊 Real-World Endurance Perspective

A user writing 50 GB of data per day — a heavy workload — would take between 16 and 65 years to exhaust the rated endurance of a quality 1 TB TLC NVMe drive. In practice, drives are retired for other reasons (obsolescence, capacity, physical failure) long before their NAND wears out.[5]

The TRIM command (ATA Data Set Management) is essential to SSD endurance. Unlike HDDs, NAND flash cannot overwrite data in place — it must erase and rewrite entire blocks (typically 256–512 KiB). When the OS marks a file as deleted, TRIM notifies the drive that those blocks are free, allowing the controller to erase them proactively during idle time rather than on-demand during a future write. Operating systems that do not issue TRIM cause write amplification (rewriting pages unnecessarily), accelerating wear and degrading performance.[23]

§ 04

SSD Form Factors

The physical packaging of an SSD — its form factor — determines which slots and connectors it is compatible with. Form factor is independent of interface: both SATA and NVMe SSDs exist in the M.2 form factor, for example, and the interface must be confirmed separately from the physical size.

2.5-Inch SATA SSD

The 2.5-inch SSD (70 mm × 100 mm × 7 mm) was designed as a drop-in replacement for 2.5-inch laptop HDDs. It uses a standard SATA data connector and a 15-pin SATA power connector. Being limited to the SATA III interface, it reaches approximately 550 MB/s sequential read — substantially faster than any spinning disk but capped by the interface itself.[4] This form factor is ideal for upgrading older laptops or desktops that lack M.2 slots, and for adding secondary storage in desktop systems with available 2.5-inch bays.

M.2 SATA

The M.2 specification (formerly NGFF — Next Generation Form Factor) defines a small PCB-format card that slots directly into the motherboard, eliminating separate data and power cables. M.2 cards come in different lengths; 2280 (22 mm wide, 80 mm long) is by far the most common in consumer hardware.

An M.2 SATA drive occupies an M.2 slot but still communicates via the SATA protocol and is subject to the same 550 MB/s interface ceiling as a 2.5-inch SATA SSD. It uses the B+M keying on the edge connector. The advantage is purely physical — no cables, smaller footprint — not a performance improvement over 2.5-inch SATA.

M.2 NVMe

M.2 NVMe drives share the same physical M.2 connector but use the PCIe bus instead of SATA, providing dramatically greater bandwidth. The M-key edge connector differentiates them from B+M-keyed SATA M.2 drives, though most modern M.2 slots support both. The NVMe protocol (Non-Volatile Memory Express) was purpose-designed for flash storage — eliminating the legacy AHCI command overhead of SATA and supporting up to 65,535 command queues with 65,535 commands per queue, versus AHCI's single queue of 32 commands.

M.2 NVMe drives are the dominant high-performance storage choice in modern consumer and workstation systems, combining the cable-free form factor with PCIe bandwidth.

PCIe Expansion Card SSDs

Some high-capacity or enterprise-grade NVMe SSDs are packaged on a full-length PCIe add-in card that slots into a standard PCIe x4 or x16 expansion slot. This format offers excellent thermal management (the card can carry a large heatsink), uses the PCIe x4 interface at its full bandwidth, and is compatible with systems that may lack M.2 slots — including older workstations and servers. The format is also used for ultra-high-capacity enterprise drives (8 TB and above) that require multiple NAND chips too numerous to fit on a M.2 card.

§ 05

Storage Interfaces

The physical and logical interface determines how a storage device communicates with the host system — what connector it uses, what protocol it speaks, and what bandwidth ceiling applies. Understanding interface distinctions is critical both for hardware selection and for diagnosing mismatched installations.

SATA (Serial ATA)

Introduced in 2003 as a successor to Parallel ATA (PATA/IDE), SATA uses a thin, 7-pin data cable and a 15-pin power cable. The current SATA III revision runs at 6 Gbps, translating to a theoretical maximum of 600 MB/s with 8b/10b encoding overhead factored in.[3]

SATA is architecturally constrained by AHCI (Advanced Host Controller Interface), the command set originally designed for mechanical drives. AHCI supports a single command queue of 32 entries — a limitation that is academically manageable for HDDs but creates a bottleneck for fast SSDs. While SATA SSDs still dramatically outperform HDDs in latency and random IOPS, the protocol overhead prevents them from reaching their full potential. SATA remains appropriate for secondary and archival storage, and for systems where NVMe slots are unavailable.

PCIe (Peripheral Component Interconnect Express)

PCIe is the primary high-bandwidth interconnect in modern PCs, connecting CPUs to GPUs, SSDs, network cards, and other peripherals. Unlike SATA, PCIe provides scalable bandwidth that doubles with each generation and scales linearly with the number of lanes used.[9]

SSDs connect to the CPU via PCIe ×4 (four lanes), giving Gen4 NVMe SSDs access to 8 GB/s of raw bandwidth — enough for the ~7,400 MB/s sequential throughput of the fastest Gen4 drives after protocol overhead.[22] PCIe is backward and forward-compatible: a Gen4 SSD installed in a Gen3 slot operates at Gen3 speeds, and vice versa.

NVMe (Non-Volatile Memory Express)

NVMe is the logical protocol and command set — not a physical interface. It runs over PCIe and was designed from the ground up for flash storage, addressing the latency and queue depth limitations of AHCI. Key architectural advantages of NVMe include:

  • Up to 65,535 command queues, each supporting 65,535 commands (vs. AHCI's single queue of 32)
  • Streamlined command set: NVMe uses 2 commands to execute a read, AHCI uses 4
  • Direct CPU-to-storage path with minimal software stack overhead
  • Native support for multi-core parallelism and CPU NUMA awareness

USB (Universal Serial Bus)

USB is the standard interface for external storage, providing a portable, hot-pluggable connection without the need to open the system chassis. The relevant USB generations for storage are:

The bridge chip translates USB commands to SATA or NVMe commands, which introduces a small overhead. USB 4 / Thunderbolt 4 external NVMe enclosures can sustain over 3,000 MB/s — performance previously only available from internal drives.

§ 06

RAID Concepts

RAID — Redundant Array of Independent Disks — is a technology that combines multiple physical storage drives into a single logical unit, with the goal of improving performance, providing redundancy against drive failure, or both. RAID can be implemented in hardware (a dedicated RAID controller card), software (managed by the OS kernel), or as a hybrid (fake RAID via the motherboard firmware).

RAID is not a backup. A RAID array protects against drive failure — it does not protect against file deletion, ransomware, fire, flood, or simultaneous multi-drive failure. A separate, independent backup strategy remains mandatory.

RAID 0 — Striping

RAID 0 distributes data across two or more drives in stripes — alternating blocks of data are written to each drive in round-robin fashion. Both drives read and write in parallel, delivering performance roughly proportional to the number of drives in the array.[15]

The critical limitation: RAID 0 provides zero redundancy. The failure of any single drive in the array renders all data irrecoverable, because each drive holds only half (or a fraction) of each file's data. The more drives in the array, the higher the aggregate probability of failure at any given time.

RAID 1 — Mirroring

RAID 1 maintains an identical copy of all data on each drive in the array. Every write is committed to all member drives simultaneously; reads can be serviced by any member drive, which can improve read performance under some controllers. With two drives in a RAID 1 array, the usable capacity is equal to that of a single drive — 50% of total raw capacity is used for redundancy.[13]

RAID 1 can tolerate the failure of all but one member drive simultaneously, making it the simplest form of fault-tolerant storage and suitable for any system where uptime matters more than capacity efficiency.

RAID 5 — Striping with Distributed Parity

RAID 5 combines the performance of striping with a measure of redundancy, using a mathematical technique called XOR parity. Data is striped across all member drives, but for each stripe, a parity block is calculated and written to one of the drives. The parity is distributed across all drives in rotation, so no single drive becomes the parity bottleneck.[14]

RAID 5 requires a minimum of three drives and can survive the failure of exactly one drive. If a drive fails, its data can be reconstructed by XOR-ing the parity with the surviving data drives. During rebuild, array performance degrades significantly — and if a second drive fails during a rebuild (which is more likely because rebuild operations stress drives heavily), all data is lost.[14]

Usable capacity: (n−1) × drive_capacity, where n is the number of drives. A four-drive RAID 5 with 4 TB drives yields 12 TB of usable space.

RAID 10 — Stripe of Mirrors (RAID 1+0)

RAID 10 combines mirroring and striping, requiring a minimum of four drives. Data is first mirrored across pairs of drives (like RAID 1), then the mirrored pairs are striped together (like RAID 0). This architecture delivers the best read and write performance of the common RAID levels while providing strong redundancy: it can survive the loss of one drive in each mirrored pair simultaneously.[13]

Like RAID 1, half of total raw capacity is consumed by mirroring — a significant cost.[16] RAID 10 is the preferred configuration for mission-critical, I/O-intensive workloads such as relational databases, email servers, and virtualisation hosts.[16]

Benefits and Risks at a Glance

RAID delivers compelling benefits: performance improvements through parallelism (RAID 0, 5, 10), fault tolerance keeping systems operational through individual drive failures (RAID 1, 5, 10), and increased aggregate capacity. However, RAID introduces its own failure modes. A RAID 5 rebuild on multi-terabyte drives can take 24–72 hours; during that window, a second drive failure destroys all data. RAID controllers themselves can fail, and controller-specific RAID metadata may render drives unreadable on a replacement controller of a different model. Hardware RAID also obscures drive-level SMART data from the OS, potentially hiding early-warning indicators until it is too late to act.

§ 07

Installing Storage Devices

Physical installation of a storage device is a straightforward process, but attention to detail during installation prevents future problems with detection, stability, and connector wear.

Mounting Drives (3.5-inch and 2.5-inch)

Desktop cases provide drive bays — metal cages with either rubber-grommeted tool-free sleds or threaded screw holes. The industry standard mounting thread for 3.5-inch HDDs is UNC 6-32, while 2.5-inch SSDs use metric M3 screws. Four screws per drive are optimal for vibration damping; in NAS systems with many spinning drives, rubber mounts are highly recommended to prevent resonance between adjacent drives degrading performance and increasing acoustic noise.

2.5-inch SATA SSDs can also be mounted in 3.5-inch bays using an inexpensive adapter bracket. For tower cases that lack dedicated 2.5-inch bays, 3.5-to-2.5-inch sleds are widely available.

Connecting Power Cables

SATA drives receive power via a 15-pin SATA power connector, which provides three voltage rails: 3.3 V, 5 V, and 12 V. Most 2.5-inch SSDs and laptop HDDs operate from 5 V only; 3.5-inch HDDs use both 5 V (logic) and 12 V (spindle motor). The connector is keyed with an L-shaped lug and can only be inserted in one orientation. Never force a SATA power connector — misaligned insertion can damage the drive's power circuit.

Some older power supply units (PSUs) provide only Molex (4-pin) peripheral connectors. A SATA power adapter is required in this case; confirm that the adapter carries the required voltage rails for your drive type.

Connecting Data Cables

SATA data cables are thin, 7-pin cables that run between the drive's SATA data port and the motherboard's SATA controller port. Key installation notes:

  • SATA cables should ideally be kept shorter than 1 metre; the specification allows up to 1 m
  • Use cables with locking latches to prevent accidental disconnection — a very common cause of intermittent drive detection failures
  • Avoid sharp bends in SATA cables; the minimum bend radius is approximately 30 mm
  • Label cables when installing multiple drives to simplify future maintenance

M.2 Installation

M.2 drives install directly onto the motherboard's M.2 connector. The process is as follows:

# M.2 SSD Installation Procedure

Step 1: Confirm M.2 slot compatibility (SATA or NVMe; PCIe Gen3/4/5)
        — check motherboard documentation; not all M.2 slots support NVMe

        — standoff height adjusts for drive length (2242, 2260, 2280, 22110)

Many motherboards share PCIe lanes between M.2 slots and SATA ports. Populating a specific M.2 slot may disable one or more adjacent SATA ports. This is documented in the motherboard manual's lane configuration diagram. Always review this before planning your storage layout to avoid disabling ports used by existing drives.

§ 08

Drive Initialization and Formatting

A brand-new or blank drive is not immediately usable by an operating system. It must first be initialised with a partition structure, then each partition must be formatted with a file system that the OS understands. These are two distinct, sequential operations.

Partitioning

Partitioning divides a physical drive into one or more logical sections. Modern systems use one of two partitioning schemes:

MBR (Master Boot Record) — the legacy scheme, dating to 1983. Stored in the first 512 bytes of the drive. MBR supports a maximum of four primary partitions (extendable via logical partitions) and a maximum disk size of 2 TB. Systems booting from BIOS (legacy firmware) use MBR boot disks.

GPT (GUID Partition Table) — the modern standard, part of the UEFI specification. GPT supports up to 128 partitions (in Windows), disks up to 9.4 ZB, includes redundant copies of partition metadata at both ends of the disk for resilience, and stores a unique GUID for each partition. Any drive larger than 2 TB must use GPT. UEFI systems boot from GPT disks.

# Windows — Initialize a new disk (DiskPart)
diskpart
DISKPART> list disk
DISKPART> select disk 1
DISKPART> clean
DISKPART> convert gpt          # or: convert mbr
DISKPART> create partition primary
DISKPART> format fs=ntfs quick label="DataDrive"
DISKPART> assign letter=D
DISKPART> exit

# Linux — Initialize with GPT and format (fdisk + mkfs)
sudo fdisk /dev/sdb
# Press g (GPT), n (new partition), w (write)
sudo mkfs.ext4 /dev/sdb1

File Systems

A file system defines how the operating system organises, names, stores, and retrieves files within a partition. The three file systems most relevant to Windows users are NTFS, FAT32, and exFAT.[16b]

NTFS (New Technology File System)

NTFS has been the default Windows file system since Windows XP and is the only appropriate choice for Windows system drives. It supports file sizes and partition sizes far beyond any practical limit (the theoretical maximum file size is 16 EB), and offers advanced capabilities absent from FAT-based systems:[16b]

  • Journaling — records planned file-system operations before committing them, enabling recovery from interrupted writes caused by power loss or crashes
  • Access Control Lists (ACLs) — per-file and per-directory read/write/execute permissions with user and group assignment
  • Transparent compression and encryption (EFS) at the file and folder level
  • Shadow copies (Volume Shadow Service) for point-in-time snapshots
  • Hard links and symbolic links allowing a single file to appear at multiple paths

NTFS has limited cross-platform support: macOS can read NTFS volumes natively but requires third-party drivers (e.g., Paragon NTFS) for write access; Linux supports NTFS read/write through the ntfs-3g driver or native kernel support added in Linux 5.15.[17]

FAT32 (File Allocation Table 32)

FAT32 was introduced with Windows 95 OSR2 and is the most universally compatible file system in existence — supported by virtually every OS, game console, camera, and embedded device. Its two critical limitations make it unsuitable for modern general-purpose use: individual files cannot exceed 4 GB, and partition size is effectively limited to 2 TB. The 4 GB file limit is reached immediately by any single Blu-ray disc image, virtual machine disk, or video file exceeding that size.[16a]

exFAT (Extended File Allocation Table)

Microsoft introduced exFAT in 2006 as a modern replacement for FAT32, designed specifically for flash memory storage — USB drives, SD cards, and external SSDs. It eliminates the 4 GB file size ceiling (maximum: 128 PB) and supports partitions up to 128 PB, while maintaining broad cross-platform compatibility: natively supported by Windows, macOS, and Linux (kernel 5.4+).[16a] exFAT is the recommended file system for external drives intended to be shared across multiple operating systems or for storing files larger than 4 GB.[17]

Drive Letters (Windows) and Mount Points (Linux/macOS)

After partitioning and formatting, Windows assigns a drive letter (C: through Z:) to each accessible volume. Drive letters are a legacy of DOS and carry no fundamental significance — C: is the boot drive by convention only. Letters can be changed freely in Disk Management (diskmgmt.msc) or via DiskPart.

Linux and macOS use a mount point model: volumes are attached at arbitrary directories in the filesystem tree (e.g., /mnt/data, /home, /media/usb0), and their contents appear inline within the existing directory hierarchy. This approach allows, for example, a separate drive to provide the /home directory transparently to all applications.

§ 09

Storage Configuration in BIOS/UEFI

Before the operating system loads, the system firmware — either legacy BIOS or the modern UEFI — must be configured to correctly address and boot from storage devices. Several firmware settings directly affect storage performance and compatibility.

SATA Controller Mode: AHCI vs IDE

UEFI/BIOS exposes the SATA controller in one of two modes. IDE (or Legacy ATA) mode emulates the older parallel ATA interface for compatibility with very old operating systems; it disables AHCI features such as Native Command Queuing (NCQ) and hot-plug, reducing SSD performance. AHCI mode exposes the full SATA feature set and should always be selected on modern systems.

⚠ SATA Mode Change Warning

Changing the SATA mode from IDE to AHCI after Windows is already installed will cause a boot failure unless the appropriate driver is loaded first. On Windows 10/11, this can be performed without reinstallation by enabling AHCI in safe-mode registry edits before changing the BIOS setting.

NVMe drives bypass the SATA controller entirely — they connect directly to PCIe lanes — so AHCI mode is irrelevant to their operation. NVMe drives are automatically detected by UEFI systems without explicit configuration.

Boot Order

The boot order determines the sequence in which the firmware attempts to find a bootable device. In UEFI mode, the boot manager maintains a list of UEFI boot entries (stored in NVRAM) rather than polling devices sequentially. Setting the correct boot device priority ensures the system loads the intended OS and avoids confusion in multi-drive configurations.

When installing a new OS on a secondary drive, or when a drive with a bootloader is added to an existing system, the boot order should be reviewed and corrected in firmware settings (Del or F2 at POST on most systems) to prevent the system from attempting to boot from the wrong device.

UEFI Secure Boot and Storage

Secure Boot validates the cryptographic signature of every component in the boot chain — firmware, bootloader, kernel — before allowing execution. While Secure Boot itself does not restrict which storage device is used, it affects which OS installations are bootable: an unsigned Linux kernel or a legacy OS bootloader will be rejected unless Secure Boot is disabled or the relevant signing keys are enrolled. Storage technicians working with multiple OS environments frequently toggle Secure Boot in UEFI when switching configurations.

§ 10

Storage Troubleshooting

Storage failures range from silent data corruption to catastrophic drive death. A systematic diagnostic approach — moving from the physical layer up through firmware and OS layers — identifies the fault class efficiently and prevents wasted effort.

Drive Not Detected

When a drive fails to appear in the operating system or BIOS/UEFI, work through the following diagnostic hierarchy:

# Diagnostic hierarchy — Drive Not Detected

Layer 1: Physical
  ├─ Reseat all data and power connectors on both ends
  ├─ Try a different SATA port (controller port may have failed)
  ├─ Try a different SATA data cable (cables fail silently)
  ├─ Try a different power cable / PSU rail
  └─ For M.2: confirm slot supports the drive's key type (B, M, B+M)

Layer 2: Firmware
  ├─ Enter UEFI — does the drive appear in the storage device list?
  ├─ Confirm SATA mode is set to AHCI (not IDE)
  ├─ Check if M.2 population has disabled SATA ports (lane sharing)
  └─ Update UEFI firmware if NVMe drive is new-generation

Layer 3: OS
  ├─ Open Disk Management (Windows) — does drive appear as "Unknown"?
  ├─ If yes: drive needs initialisation (MBR/GPT) and formatting
  ├─ Run: lsblk or sudo fdisk -l  (Linux)
  └─ Check Device Manager for driver errors (Code 43, Code 10)

SMART Warnings

SMART (Self-Monitoring, Analysis, and Reporting Technology) is a built-in diagnostic system present in virtually all modern HDDs and SSDs. It continuously monitors dozens of drive health attributes and reports them to the host system, where they can be read by monitoring software.[18] SMART was introduced in the 1990s and was quickly adopted by all major drive manufacturers as an industry standard for detecting and reporting indicators of drive reliability.[19]

SMART distinguishes between two failure classes:[18]

Predictable failures — arising from gradual mechanical wear, surface degradation, or accumulated write cycles. SMART can detect these early, providing a warning window for data backup and drive replacement. Mechanical failures account for approximately 60% of all drive failures.[18]

Unpredictable failures — sudden electronic or mechanical catastrophic events with no precursor SMART indicators.

🔴 High-Risk SMART Attributes

Research analysing hundreds of thousands of drives identified specific SMART attributes as strong predictors of imminent failure. A drive with even a single reallocated sector (SMART ID 5) is 20–60× more likely to fail within the next 60 days than a drive with zero reallocated sectors.[20] Other critical attributes include uncorrectable sector count (ID 187), pending sector count (ID 197), and reported uncorrectable errors (ID 198).

On Windows, SMART data can be read with CrystalDiskInfo (free), Windows Management Instrumentation (Get-WmiObject -Class MSStorageDriver_FailurePredictData), or smartmontools. On Linux, smartctl from the smartmontools package provides comprehensive SMART reporting:

# Linux — Read SMART data (install: sudo apt install smartmontools)
sudo smartctl -a /dev/sda          # Full SMART report
sudo smartctl -t short /dev/sda    # Run short self-test (~2 min)
sudo smartctl -t long /dev/sda     # Run extended self-test (~hours)
sudo smartctl -l selftest /dev/sda # View test results

# Windows — PowerShell SMART check
Get-PhysicalDisk | Get-StorageReliabilityCounter | Select-Object *

Bad Sectors

A bad sector is a storage unit (typically 512 bytes or 4,096 bytes) that the drive cannot reliably read or write. In HDDs, bad sectors arise from physical damage to the platter surface, head crashes, or accumulated magnetic domain instability. In SSDs, "bad blocks" occur when NAND cells are worn beyond their P/E cycle rating or have experienced data retention loss.

HDD bad sectors fall into two categories. Hard bad sectors are permanently damaged and cannot be repaired — the drive firmware remaps logical sectors to spare sectors in the reallocated sectors pool. Soft bad sectors occur when data becomes unreadable due to magnetic instability but the physical surface is intact — a low-level format or write-zero operation can sometimes recover these. Any increasing count of reallocated or pending sectors is a serious warning sign requiring immediate data backup.

# Windows — Check disk surface for bad sectors
chkdsk D: /f /r   # /f fixes errors, /r locates and recovers bad sectors

# Linux — Extended SMART test then check
sudo smartctl -t long /dev/sdb
sudo badblocks -v -s /dev/sdb     # Non-destructive read scan

Performance Issues

Unexpected storage performance degradation has several common causes that warrant systematic investigation:

SSD performance throttling: NVMe SSDs generate substantial heat under sustained load. When junction temperature exceeds the drive's thermal threshold (typically 70–80°C), the controller reduces throughput to protect NAND. Installing a heatsink or improving case airflow resolves this. Monitor with smartctl -A /dev/nvme0 and observe the Temperature attribute.

SLC cache exhaustion: TLC and QLC drives that have filled their SLC write cache fall back to native TLC/QLC write speed, which can be 2–10× slower. Monitor sustained write throughput across a large file transfer to identify this pattern.

Drive fragmentation (HDD): Heavily fragmented HDDs suffer increased seek time because file fragments are scattered across the platter. Windows automatically defragments HDDs; SSDs should never be defragmented (Windows intelligently runs TRIM, not defragmentation, on detected SSDs).

SATA mode is IDE instead of AHCI: NCQ is disabled in IDE mode, eliminating the drive's ability to re-order queue commands for optimal head movement. Verify BIOS/UEFI settings.

Cable Failures

SATA data cables are among the most surprisingly common sources of intermittent storage problems. The SATA data connector is mechanically fragile — it uses a friction-based fit without the robust latching of USB or PCIe. Cable problems manifest as:

  • Drive disappearing and reappearing in the OS — intermittent connection loss; replace cable and use locking connectors
  • CRC errors in SMART (UDMA CRC Error Count, ID 199) — electrical noise on the SATA link, often from a marginal cable or a bent connector pin
  • Read errors or slow transfers — the controller retries failed transfers, appearing as a performance degradation before eventual total failure
  • Drive detected in BIOS but not OS — the drive initialises but loses connection during heavier data access

Always try a known-good SATA cable as the first diagnostic step when a drive behaves erratically. Cables cost under $5 and a cable swap takes 60 seconds — eliminating this cause early avoids extensive unnecessary investigation.
