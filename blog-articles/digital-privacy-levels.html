The First Four Levels of Digital Privacy

From default exposure to operational security: a technical guide to understanding where you stand, what the risks actually are, and what you can do about them.

by Nicholas Knapp May 7, 2026

The phrase "digital privacy" gets applied to an enormous range of situations and concerns — from password managers to operational security, from cookie banners to protecting a journalist's source. This breadth makes it difficult to reason about concretely. What does it actually mean to be private online? Private from whom? Against what kind of adversary? At what cost?

One useful framing is a ladder model: an informal but practical taxonomy of digital privacy levels, ranging from zero precautions to full operational compartmentation. This is not an official standard from any government body or standards organization — it is an emergent classification that has developed organically among security researchers, privacy advocates, and journalists who needed a shared vocabulary for talking about risk gradients. Think of it as analogous to the biosafety level (BSL) framework in laboratory settings: a shorthand for communicating how much protective infrastructure is warranted, based on what you are protecting against.

This article walks through Levels 0 through 3 in detail — their technical characteristics, the failure modes they close and the ones they leave open, the tools relevant to each, and the research and historical incidents that illuminate why each level matters. The goal is not to induce anxiety, but to give readers an accurate map of the territory.

↳ The Full Privacy Ladder
  • Level 0No precautions. Default settings, broad permissions, password reuse, and maximum passive exposure.
  • Level 1Basic hygiene. Unique passwords, MFA, software updates, and recovery-channel hardening against common attacks.
  • Level 2Consumer hardening. Tracker reduction, browser compartmentation, data-broker resistance, and better identity separation.
  • Level 3Targeted-risk protection. Stronger compartmentation and workflow discipline for people facing meaningful harassment, stalking, or professional risk.
  • Level 4High-risk adversarial environments. Dedicated devices, hardened endpoints, strict metadata minimization, and operational separation. Covered in the companion article Hostile Terrain: Digital Privacy at Levels 4 and 5.
  • Level 5Maximum-effort tradecraft. Studied mainly for defensive awareness of elite adversary capability, infrastructure rotation, and anti-forensic discipline. Covered in the Level 4 and 5 article.

A Note on Threat Modeling

All privacy decisions are implicitly threat-modeling decisions. The right level of protection is not the highest possible level — it is the level appropriate to your actual adversary. Researchers at Carnegie Mellon's CyLab have demonstrated that structured, user-centered threat modeling frameworks dramatically improve the identification of privacy shortcomings in digital systems.1 The same principle applies to individuals: knowing who or what you are protecting against shapes every tool and practice choice that follows.

0 Level
No Precautions
Default State · Maximum Exposure · The Population Majority

Level 0 is the default state of a large percentage of internet users. It is characterized not by any particular vulnerability but by the absence of intentional choices: default browser settings, no multi-factor authentication, passwords reused across services, broad app permissions, and essentially all digital activity consolidated under a single identity. The experience is frictionless, which is precisely why it is also the most comprehensively exposed state.

The Password Reuse Crisis

The most consequential failure mode at Level 0 is password reuse. Estimates from multiple large-scale studies place reuse rates between 60 and 84 percent of internet users, with 13 percent reporting they use the same password across all accounts.2 A 2024 analysis of 19 billion leaked passwords found that 94 percent were either reused or duplicated across services.3 The implications compound rapidly: every data breach anywhere on the internet contributes to a growing body of credential pairs that attackers can test against every other service. This attack pattern — known as credential stuffing — uses automation to replay stolen username-password combinations at scale across thousands of sites simultaneously.

The 23andMe breach of October 2023 is an instructive case study. Attackers did not break into 23andMe's systems directly — they used reused credentials from previous breaches at entirely unrelated platforms to authenticate against roughly 14,000 accounts. Through the platform's "DNA Relatives" feature, which linked accounts to biological relatives, they were able to access data on approximately 6.9 million users, including information on genetic heritage, health conditions, and family connections.4 The incident resulted in a proposed $30 million settlement and a £2.31 million fine from the UK Information Commissioner's Office. Roku disclosed that around 591,000 accounts were compromised in 2024 through similar credential stuffing attacks using breached credentials from unrelated sites.5

The pattern here is structural: a Level 0 user's password, once leaked anywhere, effectively leaks everywhere they use it. The database of breached credentials available to attackers grows continuously — IBM X-Force reported an 84 percent increase in infostealer malware delivery via phishing in 2024 compared to 2023, with billions of stolen passwords and session cookies now in active circulation.6

Ad-Tech Profiling and Identity Consolidation

The second major failure mode at Level 0 is not about account security at all — it is about the construction of a comprehensive identity profile across every digital interaction. When a user's shopping, health information searches, political reading, financial activity, and casual browsing all occur within a single browser profile, signed into a single dominant platform identity, the correlations between these activities are trivial to establish. The ad-tech industry was built to exploit exactly this.

Third-party tracking cookies — code silently embedded in pages that log activity across domains and report it back to centralized data aggregators — have been the primary mechanism of this surveillance for two decades.7 Shoshana Zuboff's foundational account of this system, The Age of Surveillance Capitalism (2019), describes it as a new economic logic in which human experience itself becomes raw material for behavioral prediction products.8 The architecture has evolved since then but not retreated: Google announced plans to phase out third-party cookies in Chrome multiple times — targeting 2022, then 2023, then 2024, then 2025 — before abandoning the plan altogether in July 2024, citing advertiser pressure and regulatory complexity.9 The cookies remain. Meanwhile, the ad-tech industry has developed alternative tracking mechanisms designed to survive cookie deprecation, including identity resolution graphs built on hashed email addresses and device fingerprinting.10

"Every data breach anywhere on the internet contributes to a growing body of credential pairs that can be tested against every other service a user has ever touched. The Level 0 user's exposure is not an isolated incident — it is a structural condition."

The Abstraction Problem

Perhaps the defining characteristic of Level 0 is not any specific technical exposure but a cognitive one. Privacy risk at this level tends to feel abstract until a concrete incident makes it personal — a fraud event, a password-reset chain failure, an embarrassing data leak, or a doxxing incident. This is not a failure of intelligence; it is the predictable result of a system designed to be convenient and opaque simultaneously. The costs of weak privacy practices are diffuse, delayed, and often invisible to the person bearing them. The costs of stronger practices are immediate and visible. Behavioral economics predicts exactly the pattern we observe.

· · ·
1 Level
Basic Hygiene
The Sensible Baseline · Closes the Easiest Pathways

Level 1 is the minimum sensible baseline for ordinary adults. It does not make anyone anonymous, does not prevent corporate profiling, and does not protect against a sophisticated or motivated adversary. What it does do is eliminate a large class of cheap, scalable, opportunistic attacks. It is sometimes described as "taking your car off the street" — the lock will not stop a determined thief, but it makes you a harder target than the unlocked car next to you.

Password Managers and Unique Credentials

The single highest-impact upgrade for most people is adopting a password manager. The function is straightforward: generate a random, unique password for every account, store them in an encrypted vault, and retrieve them through a single master password. The effect is to break the credential-stuffing attack chain entirely. If every password is unique, a breach on one platform reveals nothing about any other.

Password management is estimated to reduce the risk of credential-based breaches by 30 to 50 percent.2 Reputable options include Bitwarden (open-source, independently audited), 1Password, and KeePassXC (fully offline). The market has matured considerably; all major platforms now support autofill integration on mobile and desktop browsers, minimizing friction for the user.

↳ Core Level 1 Tools
  • BitwardenOpen-source password manager with E2E encryption, independent audits, and free tier. Self-hosting available for advanced users.
  • 1PasswordCommercial password manager with family/team features, strong audit history, and Travel Mode for border crossings.
  • Authy / AegisTOTP authenticator apps. Aegis is open-source and Android-only; Authy offers cross-device sync (at the cost of vendor dependency).
  • YubiKeyHardware security key supporting FIDO2/WebAuthn. The most phishing-resistant form of MFA for accounts that support it.
  • Have I Been PwnedTroy Hunt's free database of compromised credentials. Check whether your email appears in known breaches.

Multi-Factor Authentication: What Works and What Doesn't

Multi-factor authentication (MFA) adds a second verification step beyond the password — something you have (a code, a hardware key) or something you are (biometrics). Google's internal research found that SMS-based two-factor authentication stops 100 percent of automated bot attacks and 96 percent of bulk phishing attempts.11 Adoption has reached roughly 50 percent of individual users as of 2024, though this figure masks wide variation by demographics and platform.2

However, MFA is not a universal fix. The threat landscape around it has shifted considerably. Adversary-in-the-Middle (AiTM) phishing attacks — where a real-time proxy intercepts authentication tokens between the user and the legitimate service — can bypass time-based one-time passwords (TOTP) and SMS codes in real time.12 MFA fatigue attacks, in which users are bombarded with push-notification approval requests until they accept one, accounted for 14 percent of security incidents analyzed in the 2025 Verizon Data Breach Investigations Report.13 A notable example: in 2022, Cisco suffered a breach when an attacker used voice phishing to convince an employee to approve MFA push notifications after stealing VPN credentials.

Not All MFA Is Equal

SMS-based MFA is better than nothing but is vulnerable to SIM-swapping attacks, in which an attacker convinces a carrier to transfer a phone number to a new SIM they control. App-based TOTP codes are more secure but susceptible to real-time phishing proxies. FIDO2 hardware keys (YubiKey, Google Titan) are the most robust form of consumer MFA: they use asymmetric cryptography and domain binding, making them structurally immune to phishing because the authenticator cannot produce a valid response for a domain it was not registered with.14

Recovery Channels and the Identity Root Problem

A frequently overlooked aspect of Level 1 is the architecture of account recovery. Most platforms allow account access to be restored through a backup email address, a phone number, or a set of recovery codes. This means that security of the primary account is only as strong as security of the recovery pathway. Attackers routinely target the weakest link: rather than cracking a strong password, they compromise the recovery email, issue a password reset, and receive the reset link. Auditing and hardening recovery channels — ensuring they are actively monitored, not shared with other services, and not themselves vulnerable — is a foundational Level 1 practice that most guides underemphasize.

What Level 1 Does Not Do

Level 1 is effective against automated, scalable attacks driven by credential reuse, weak recovery flows, and unpatched vulnerabilities. It does not stop large platforms from building behavioral profiles of users. It does not prevent ISPs or mobile carriers from logging traffic metadata. It is not designed for people facing focused harassment, surveillance, or state-level adversaries. For those use cases, higher levels are necessary.

2 Level
Consumer Privacy Hardening
Profiling Reduction · Compartmentation · Breaking the Identity Graph

Level 2 is where privacy stops being purely a security question and begins functioning as an active choice about what information gets generated, linked, and sold. The practices here do not make someone anonymous — they reduce the quality and coherence of the data available to ad-tech systems and data brokers by introducing friction into the correlation process.

The Mechanics of Ad-Tech Surveillance

To understand Level 2, it helps to understand the infrastructure it is designed to frustrate. Empirical research has documented Google's omnipresent position in web tracking, with GAFAM companies (Google, Apple, Facebook, Amazon, Microsoft) forming a stratified surveillance architecture across the web.15 The mechanisms include third-party cookies, tracking pixels (single-pixel images that report load events to analytics servers), browser fingerprinting (which identifies browsers by the unique combination of fonts, plugins, screen size, and hardware characteristics — a fingerprint that persists even with cookies deleted), and increasingly, identity resolution graphs built from hashed email addresses and device identifiers.10

When a user browses in a single profile while signed into a dominant platform, they are effectively providing continuous, authenticated, first-party data to that platform's advertising system. Every search query, clicked article, shopping browse, and social interaction is attributed to a persistent, known identity. The resulting behavioral profile is extraordinarily detailed — and it is not solely used for advertising. Insurance pricing, credit decisions, and employment screening have all been linked to data broker profiles derived from this information.16

Browser Compartmentation

The core Level 2 technique is compartmentation — the deliberate separation of different identity contexts into different browser profiles, each with its own cookie store, history, and login state. A user might maintain a work profile (signed into work email and SaaS tools), a personal profile (social media, personal communications), a shopping profile (never signed into anything, uBlock Origin active), and a research profile (used for sensitive or potentially embarrassing searches). Each profile generates a different behavioral dataset. Cross-profile correlation still occurs via IP address and browser fingerprint, but the quality of the profile constructed is substantially lower than it would be in a unified context.

Firefox's Multi-Account Containers extension (developed by Mozilla) makes this accessible: different container tabs are color-coded and isolated from each other within the same browser window, without requiring separate profiles. Chromium-based browsers support full profile switching with separate cookie stores via the profile menu.

↳ Core Level 2 Tools
  • uBlock OriginOpen-source, highly efficient content blocker. Blocks third-party ad networks, tracking scripts, and malware domains using curated filter lists. Widely considered the most effective browser extension for reducing ad-tech exposure.
  • Firefox + ContainersMozilla's Multi-Account Containers extension provides tab-level isolation. Combined with Firefox's Enhanced Tracking Protection (Strict mode), it is among the strongest consumer-grade privacy browser configurations.
  • Brave BrowserChromium-based browser with built-in tracker blocking, fingerprint randomization, and optional Tor integration for specific windows.
  • Pi-hole / AdGuard HomeNetwork-level DNS filtering. Blocks ad and tracking domains at the router level, protecting all devices on a network including smart TVs and IoT devices that cannot run browser extensions.
  • SimpleLogin / Addy.ioEmail alias services. Generate unique per-service aliases that forward to a real inbox, keeping the actual email address private and allowing individual aliases to be disabled if compromised.
  • Privacy Badger (EFF)Learns to block trackers that appear across multiple sites. Developed by the Electronic Frontier Foundation as a companion to uBlock Origin.

The Data Broker Industry

Separate from ad-tech profiling, a parallel industry of data brokers aggregates personal information from public records, commercial databases, voter registration files, property records, and purchased consumer data to build people-search profiles available to anyone willing to pay a nominal fee. These profiles typically include home address, phone numbers, family relationships, employment history, and in some cases health data inferences.16

The regulatory environment here is in flux. California passed the Delete Act in 2023, and on January 1, 2026, launched the Delete Request and Opt-Out Platform (DROP) — the first public service allowing residents to submit a single deletion request to every registered data broker in the state at no cost.17 At the federal level, the CFPB proposed a rule in December 2024 to subject data brokers to Fair Credit Reporting Act obligations, which would have required them to honor deletion requests and restricted sale of Social Security numbers and phone numbers — but the rule was quietly withdrawn in May 2025.18

For manual opt-outs, independent analyses have found significant variation in effectiveness. A Consumer Reports investigation found that some removal services removed fewer than 10 percent of profiles after four months. The top-performing services (Optery and EasyOptOuts) achieved removal of over 65 percent of profiles in the same period.16 Critically, opt-out is not permanent: information reappears as brokers refresh their databases. A standing annual opt-out process is more realistic than a one-time request.

The Core Failure Mode at Level 2

Users at Level 2 often install impressive privacy tooling while continuing to log into the same primary identity everywhere. This is the level's defining failure mode: good tools reduce the surface area of third-party tracking considerably, but authenticated first-party data — the kind generated when you are logged into your Google account while using Google search, Google maps, and YouTube — bypasses most consumer privacy tools entirely. Compartmentation disciplines must be sustained, not merely established. The architecture of the tools and the architecture of behavior have to align.

3 Level
Targeted-Risk Protection
Operational Security · Adversarial Scenarios · Compartmented Identity

Level 3 is not a natural evolution of Level 2 — it represents a qualitative shift in the kind of threat being addressed. Levels 1 and 2 operate against opportunistic, scalable adversaries: automated systems, ad-tech platforms, data brokers, and script-kiddie attackers. Level 3 is designed for scenarios in which a motivated human adversary is specifically targeting a known individual — and is willing to expend significant resources to do so.

The population for whom Level 3 is relevant includes journalists, political activists and organizers, executives with high public profiles, researchers working on sensitive topics, domestic abuse survivors with known stalkers, and anyone subject to a documented doxxing or harassment campaign. For these users, the failure mode is not a data breach somewhere — it is a targeted investigation of their specific identity, network, and physical location. The Electronic Frontier Foundation's Surveillance Self-Defense project provides the most comprehensive publicly available guides for this population.19

From Settings to Operational Consistency

The shift at Level 3 is from tool selection to operational consistency. A journalist might have excellent privacy tools installed on their personal device and still compromise a source by using their personal phone to look up the source's name, or by logging into a work account on a device that shares a browser fingerprint with personal browsing. Level 3 requires what security professionals call OPSEC — operational security — which is the practice of identifying behaviors, rather than just tools, that could leak sensitive information.

Device separation is a common Level 3 practice: maintaining physically distinct devices for different identity contexts. A device used for sensitive communications is never used for casual browsing. Accounts are never accessed from the same network across different identity compartments. Operational phone numbers (VoIP numbers used for sensitive registrations) are separated from personal phone numbers. Email aliases, pseudonymous accounts, and ephemeral communications tools are used systematically rather than opportunistically.

↳ Core Level 3 Tools and Practices
  • SignalEnd-to-end encrypted messaging with disappearing messages. Uses forward secrecy (new encryption keys per message), so compromise of long-term keys does not expose past conversations. The protocol is open-source and has been independently audited.
  • Tor BrowserRoutes traffic through three layers of encrypted relays, making IP-based identification difficult. Provides the most robust browser-level anonymity available to the public, at significant speed cost. Do not log into identifying accounts inside Tor.
  • Tails OSA live operating system that runs from a USB drive, routes all traffic through Tor, leaves no persistent trace on the host machine, and amnesically resets on shutdown. Used by journalists handling sensitive documents.
  • GrapheneOSHardened Android fork with strong sandboxing, memory protections, and permission controls. Developed for Pixel hardware. Permits running Google Play services in an isolated sandbox without granting them system-level access.
  • ProtonMail / TutanotaE2E encrypted email providers with zero-access architecture (provider cannot read content). Tutanota encrypts subject lines; ProtonMail does not by default. Both are Switzerland/Germany-based with favorable legal jurisdictions.
  • VPN (selective)VPNs shift network-level trust from ISPs to the VPN provider — a trade-off, not an elimination of exposure. Useful for hiding traffic from a local network (e.g., public WiFi) or ISP logging. Mullvad and IVPN are the most audited providers. A VPN is not anonymization.

Public Exposure Reduction

At Level 3, managing one's data broker and public records footprint becomes urgent rather than optional. People search sites like Spokeo, Whitepages, BeenVerified, and dozens of others aggregate publicly available data — court records, voter files, property records, business registrations — and make it searchable by name. For someone facing a doxxing threat, these sites represent a direct physical safety risk, as they can expose a home address to anyone willing to pay a few dollars.16

A Senate investigation published in February 2026 found that the majority of registered data brokers do not audit the effectiveness of their opt-out processes, and that one company failed to process 80 percent of privacy requests from individuals.20 The implication is that manual opt-outs and automated removal services are an ongoing practice, not a solved problem. Domain privacy (using a privacy service to mask WHOIS registrant information on owned domains) and the use of a PO Box or registered mail forwarding service to keep residential addresses out of commercial records are additional Layer 3 practices.

Documented Response Planning

Level 3 also involves preparedness for the events that are specifically being guarded against. A documented incident response plan — covering what to do if a device is seized or lost, if accounts are compromised, if sensitive material is leaked, or if a phishing attack succeeds — is a Level 3 practice that most individuals at this risk level do not have. The Freedom of the Press Foundation's digital security resources provide structured guidance for journalists and activists on building and maintaining such plans.21

Level 3 Is Not for Everyone

Unnecessarily adopting Level 3 practices can create friction, cognitive load, and operational complexity that actually increases error rates and decreases security. For most people, Level 1 with selected Level 2 elements is the appropriate target. Level 3 practices should be adopted in proportion to documented, specific risk — not as a general response to abstract privacy concern.

The Adversarial Mindset Shift

The deepest change at Level 3 is epistemological: rather than asking "what tools should I install," the question becomes "what information would a motivated adversary need, and what pathways lead to that information?" This is threat modeling in earnest, applied to personal operational security rather than system design. It requires thinking through not just technical exposure but behavioral patterns, physical routines, social networks, and metadata generated by mundane activities. Who are you seen with publicly? What does your commute pattern reveal? What can be inferred about your sources from the metadata of your communications, even if the content is encrypted?

These questions do not have universal answers. They are the right questions to be asking at Level 3 — and largely unnecessary at Levels 1 and 2.

Where to Start

Most people reading this article are either at Level 0 or partway through Level 1. The research is unambiguous that the highest marginal returns on privacy investment come from the earliest steps: adopting a password manager, enabling MFA on email and financial accounts, and reducing the number of connected apps and browser extensions with unnecessary permissions. These interventions are available to anyone with thirty minutes and a willingness to change established habits.

Level 2 is the appropriate target for people who are motivated by more than account security — who want to actively reduce what is being inferred about them and limit the coherence of the identity graph that ad-tech and data broker industries construct from their behavior. The investment here is primarily cognitive rather than financial: the tools (Firefox, uBlock Origin, email aliases, browser compartmentation) are free. The cost is sustained behavioral discipline.

Level 3 requires a specific, concrete reason. The tools and practices involved are genuinely useful — and genuinely disruptive to ordinary digital life. They should be adopted in proportion to the threat, not as an aspirational status. For those who do need Level 3, the EFF's Surveillance Self-Defense,19 the Freedom of the Press Foundation's digital security curriculum,21 and Michael Bazzell's work on OSINT and personal privacy are among the most rigorous publicly available resources.

"Privacy is not a binary state. It is a set of specific protections against specific adversaries. The right question is not 'am I private?' but 'private from whom, against what, and at what cost?'"

The levels framework is ultimately a thinking tool: a way of making explicit the implicit trade-offs between convenience, exposure, and protection that every digital user is already making, whether consciously or not. Making those trade-offs consciously, with accurate information about what each level closes and what it leaves open, is itself the most important step any of these levels offers.
