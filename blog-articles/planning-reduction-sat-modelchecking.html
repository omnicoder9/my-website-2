Planning by Reduction:
SAT, SATplan, and Model Checking

A rigorous treatment of reduction-based methods in automated planning, covering propositional satisfiability encodings, the SATplan framework, DPLL and WalkSAT procedures, and the deep structural correspondence between planning and model checking.

Automated Planning · Combinatorial Search · Formal Methods

1. Introduction

Automated planning is the sub-field of artificial intelligence concerned with the synthesis of action sequences that transform a specified initial state of the world into a goal state, subject to constraints imposed by a formal domain model.[1] Classical formulations, rooted in the STRIPS framework of Fikes and Nilsson,[2] represent the world as a set of ground propositions, actions as operators with preconditions and add/delete effects, and problems as reachability queries over an exponentially large state space. Despite decades of algorithmic innovation—heuristic search, planning graphs, hierarchical decomposition—the fundamental computational barrier has remained: classical plan existence is PSPACE-complete in the general case, and NP-complete even for bounded-horizon variants.[3]

One of the most intellectually fruitful responses to this difficulty has been the strategy of reduction: rather than developing bespoke planning solvers, one systematically translates a planning instance into an instance of a well-studied combinatorial problem and then exploits the decades of engineering investment in solvers for that problem. This survey examines two such reductions in depth: reduction to the Boolean satisfiability problem (SAT), culminating in the SATplan family of planners; and reduction to model checking, a correspondence that is not merely pragmatic but structurally profound.

2. The Classical Planning Problem

A classical planning problem is formally specified as a tuple Π = ⟨F, A, I, G⟩, where F is a finite set of propositional fluents (facts), A is a finite set of actions, I ⊆ F is the initial state, and G ⊆ F is the goal condition.[4] Each action a ∈ A is a triple (pre(a), add(a), del(a)) of subsets of F, representing its preconditions and positive and negative effects respectively.

A state s ⊆ F satisfies the goal if G ⊆ s. Applying action a in state s is permissible when pre(a) ⊆ s, yielding successor state s′ = (s ∖ del(a)) ∪ add(a). A solution, or plan, is a sequence of actions π = ⟨a1, …, an⟩ such that ai is applicable in the state resulting from applying a1, …, ai−1 to I, and the final state satisfies G.[5]

The STRIPS Language

STRIPS (Stanford Research Institute Problem Solver), developed by Fikes and Nilsson in 1971,[2] remains the canonical formal language for classical planning. Its simplicity—no conditional effects, no numeric fluents, no durative actions—makes it the natural target for satisfiability reductions, since its semantics map cleanly onto propositional logic. Extensions such as ADL (Action Description Language) and PDDL (Planning Domain Definition Language) generalise STRIPS but preserve the core reduction-based machinery at the cost of larger encodings.[6]

3. The Boolean Satisfiability Problem

The Boolean satisfiability problem (SAT) asks: given a propositional formula φ over variables x1, …, xn, does there exist a truth assignment σ: {x1, …, xn} → {⊤, ⊥} such that σ(φ) = ⊤?[7] Cook's landmark 1971 proof that SAT is NP-complete—the first such proof—established it as the canonical hard problem of combinatorial search.[8]

In practice, SAT instances are almost universally expressed in conjunctive normal form (CNF): a conjunction of clauses, each clause being a disjunction of literals. The DIMACS CNF format is the standard interchange representation accepted by virtually all modern solvers.[9]

Conjunctive Normal Form (CNF) φ = C₁ ∧ C₂ ∧ … ∧ Cₘ, where Cᵢ = (ℓᵢ₁ ∨ ℓᵢ₂ ∨ … ∨ ℓᵢₖ), ℓᵢⱼ ∈ {xₚ, ¬xₚ}

3.1 The DPLL Algorithm

The Davis–Putnam–Logemann–Loveland (DPLL) algorithm, introduced in 1962,[10] is a complete, backtracking-based search procedure for SAT. It underpins virtually all modern conflict-driven clause-learning (CDCL) solvers and remains the workhorse of SAT-based planning backends.

Algorithm 1: DPLL Davis, Putnam, Logemann, Loveland · 1962
function DPLL(φ, σ): if all clauses in φ are satisfied by σ: return σ if any clause in φ is empty: return UNSAT -- Unit propagation -- while ∃ unit clause {ℓ} in φ: σ ← σ ∪ {ℓ = ⊤}; φ ← φ|ℓ=⊤ -- Pure literal elimination -- while ∃ pure literal ℓ in φ: σ ← σ ∪ {ℓ = ⊤}; φ ← φ|ℓ=⊤ -- Branching -- choose unassigned variable x ∈ φ return DPLL(φ|x=⊤, σ ∪ {x=⊤}) or DPLL(φ|x=⊥, σ ∪ {x=⊥})

3.2 WalkSAT

WalkSAT, introduced by Selman, Kautz, and Cohen in 1994,[11] is a stochastic local-search procedure for SAT that operates over complete variable assignments. Starting from a random assignment, WalkSAT iteratively selects an unsatisfied clause and flips one of its variables—either the variable minimising the number of newly unsatisfied clauses (greedy move) or a randomly chosen variable (random walk move) with probability p. WalkSAT is incomplete (it cannot certify unsatisfiability) but is highly effective in practice on satisfiable random instances and on the satisfiable planning instances generated by SATplan.[12]

Algorithm 2: WalkSAT Selman, Kautz, Cohen · 1994
function WalkSAT(φ, p, max_flips): σ ← random complete assignment for i = 1 to max_flips: if σ satisfies φ: return σ C ← randomly chosen unsatisfied clause in φ if random() < p: flip a random variable in C else: flip the variable in C with fewest break-count return UNKNOWN -- not found within budget

The break-count of a variable x in clause C is the number of currently satisfied clauses that would become unsatisfied if x were flipped. Selecting the variable with minimum break-count implements the GSAT heuristic embedded within the WalkSAT framework.[11]

4. Reduction of Planning to SAT

The central insight enabling SAT-based planning is that a bounded planning problem—"does a plan of length at most T exist?"—can be compiled into a propositional formula whose satisfying assignments correspond exactly to valid plans.[13] This is not a heuristic approximation; the reduction is exact, sound, and complete: a plan exists if and only if the formula is satisfiable.

4.1 Bounded Planning as SAT

Fix a planning problem Π = ⟨F, A, I, G⟩ and a horizon T ∈ ℕ. The propositional encoding Φ(Π, T) introduces the following variables:[14]

  • State variables: For each fluent f ∈ F and time step t ∈ {0, …, T}, a Boolean variable ft encoding whether f holds at step t.
  • Action variables: For each action a ∈ A and time step t ∈ {0, …, T−1}, a Boolean variable at encoding whether a is executed at step t.

The formula Φ(Π, T) = Φinit ∧ Φgoal ∧ Φpre ∧ Φeff ∧ Φframe, where:

Initial State Axioms Φinit = ⋀f∈I f⁰ ∧ ⋀f∉I ¬f⁰
Goal Axioms Φgoal = ⋀g∈G gT
Precondition Axioms (for each a, t) Φpre = ⋀a∈A, t<T aᵗ → ⋀f∈pre(a) fᵗ
Effect Axioms (for each a, t) Φeff = ⋀a∈A, t<T aᵗ → ( ⋀f∈add(a) ft+1 ∧ ⋀f∈del(a) ¬ft+1 )

The frame axioms Φframe are the most nuanced component: they encode the requirement that fluents do not change between steps unless some action is responsible. The classical explanatory frame axioms formulation by Reiter[15] introduces for each fluent f and each time step t:

Explanatory Frame Axioms (¬fᵗ ∧ ft+1) → ⋁a∈add(f) aᵗ (positive change requires an adding action)

(fᵗ ∧ ¬ft+1) → ⋁a∈del(f) aᵗ (negative change requires a deleting action)

This encoding avoids the classic frame problem by making every state transition explicitly justified. Translating precondition and effect implications to CNF is straightforward via Tseitin transformation,[16] preserving satisfiability while achieving linear growth in formula size.

5. SATplan: Planning as Satisfiability

"The key insight is that finding a plan is equivalent to finding a model for a propositional formula encoding the planning problem."

— Kautz & Selman, ECAI 1992[13]

SATplan, introduced by Henry Kautz and Bart Selman at ECAI 1992,[13] operationalises the encoding above into a complete planner: it iterates over horizon values T = 1, 2, 3, … constructing and submitting Φ(Π, T) to a SAT solver at each iteration, halting when a satisfying assignment is found (yielding an optimal-length plan) or when a theoretically established upper bound on plan length is exceeded (proving no plan exists).[17] The BLACKBOX variant, released in 1998, improved upon this by using the Graphplan planning graph[18] to extract a tighter lower bound on T and to generate a more compact formula with mutex constraints derived from the graph.[19]

5.1 The SATplan Algorithm

Algorithm 3: SATplan Kautz & Selman · 1992 / 1998
function SATplan(Π = ⟨F, A, I, G⟩): Tmin ← lower_bound(Π) -- e.g. from planning graph Tmax ← upper_bound(Π) -- existence guarantee for T = Tmin to Tmax: φ ← encode(Π, T) -- Φ_init ∧ Φ_goal ∧ Φ_pre ∧ Φ_eff ∧ Φ_frame result ← SAT_SOLVE(φ) if result = SAT(σ): return extract_plan(σ) -- read off aᵗ = ⊤ variables return NO_PLAN_EXISTS

5.2 Mutex Constraints from Planning Graphs

A critical refinement introduced in BLACKBOX was the derivation of mutex (mutual exclusion) constraints from the Graphplan planning graph.[18] Two actions a and b are mutex at step t if they interfere—one deletes a precondition or add-effect of the other, or they share a conflicting precondition. Adding the clause ¬at ∨ ¬bt for each mutex pair at each time step dramatically prunes the search space, producing a formula that is both smaller and easier for the SAT solver.[19]

Action Mutex Constraint Φmutex = ⋀t<T ⋀(a,b) mutex at t ¬aᵗ ∨ ¬bᵗ

5.3 SATplan'06 and Competition Performance

The SATplan'06 system,[20] submitted to the 5th International Planning Competition (IPC-5), used a sophisticated Tseitin-encoded planning graph combined with the zChaff CDCL solver[21] and emerged as a top performer on propositional planning tracks. Its success demonstrated that decades of engineering invested in SAT solvers for hardware verification and electronic design automation could be directly leveraged for planning, yielding competitive performance with domain-independent heuristic planners on many benchmark families.[20]

5.4 Implementation in Python

The following code implements a minimal SATplan encoder targeting the PySAT library,[22] which provides a uniform interface to Glucose, MiniSAT, Lingeling, and other industrial solvers. The example uses a simple blocks-world domain.

Python satplan_encoder.py — Minimal SATplan using PySAT 
from pysat.formula import CNF
from pysat.solvers import Glucose4
from itertools import product
from dataclasses import dataclass, field
from typing import FrozenSet, List, Optional, Tuple


# ── Domain representation ─────────────────────────────── #

@dataclass
class Action:
    name: str
    pre:  FrozenSet[str]
    add:  FrozenSet[str]
    dele: FrozenSet[str]

@dataclass
class PlanningProblem:
    fluents:  List[str]
    actions:  List[Action]
    init:     FrozenSet[str]
    goal:     FrozenSet[str]


# ── Variable mapping ──────────────────────────────────── #

class VarMapper:
    """Assigns unique positive integers to (name, timestep) pairs."""
    def __init__(self):
        self._map = {}
        self._counter = 1

    def var(self, name: str, t: int) -> int:
        key = (name, t)
        if key not in self._map:
            self._map[key] = self._counter
            self._counter += 1
        return self._map[key]

    def decode(self) -> dict:
        return {v: k for k, v in self._map.items()}


# ── CNF encoding ──────────────────────────────────────── #

def encode(prob: PlanningProblem, T: int) -> Tuple[CNF, VarMapper]:
    vm  = VarMapper()
    cnf = CNF()

    # Helper: implication p → (q1 ∧ q2 ∧ …) as CNF clauses
    def implies_all(p: int, consequents: List[int]):
        for q in consequents:
            cnf.append([-p, q])

    # Φ_init: initial state
    for f in prob.fluents:
        v = vm.var(f, 0)
        cnf.append([v] if f in prob.init else [-v])

    # Φ_goal: goal at horizon T
    for g in prob.goal:
        cnf.append([vm.var(g, T)])

    for t in range(T):
        for a in prob.actions:
            av = vm.var(a.name, t)

            # Φ_pre: action → preconditions at t
            implies_all(av, [vm.var(f, t) for f in a.pre])

            # Φ_eff: action → add-effects at t+1
            implies_all(av, [vm.var(f, t+1) for f in a.add])

            # Φ_eff: action → delete-effects at t+1 (negated)
            implies_all(av, [-vm.var(f, t+1) for f in a.dele])

        for f in prob.fluents:
            ft  = vm.var(f, t)
            ft1 = vm.var(f, t+1)

            # Φ_frame (explanatory): ¬fᵗ ∧ f^(t+1) → ∨ adding actions
            adders = [vm.var(a.name, t) for a in prob.actions if f in a.add]
            if adders:
                cnf.append([ft, -ft1] + adders)  # ¬(¬fᵗ∧f^t+1) ∨ ⋁adders

            # Φ_frame (explanatory): fᵗ ∧ ¬f^(t+1) → ∨ deleting actions
            deleters = [vm.var(a.name, t) for a in prob.actions if f in a.dele]
            if deleters:
                cnf.append([-ft, ft1] + deleters)

    return cnf, vm


# ── Plan extraction ───────────────────────────────────── #

def extract_plan(model: List[int], vm: VarMapper,
                   prob: PlanningProblem, T: int) -> List[str]:
    pos = set(model)
    decode = vm.decode()
    plan = []
    for t in range(T):
        for a in prob.actions:
            v = vm.var(a.name, t)
            if v in pos:
                plan.append(f"t={t}: {a.name}")
    return plan


# ── SATplan main loop ─────────────────────────────────── #

def satplan(prob: PlanningProblem, T_max: int = 20) -> Optional[List[str]]:
    """Iteratively deepen horizon T until a plan is found or T_max reached."""
    for T in range(1, T_max + 1):
        print(f"Trying horizon T={T} ...")
        cnf, vm = encode(prob, T)
        with Glucose4(bootstrap_with=cnf.clauses) as solver:
            if solver.solve():
                model = solver.get_model()
                plan  = extract_plan(model, vm, prob, T)
                print(f"Plan found at horizon {T}: {len(plan)} action(s)")
                return plan
    print("No plan found within T_max.")
    return None


# ── Example: simple two-block stacking ───────────────── #

if __name__ == "__main__":
    prob = PlanningProblem(
        fluents = ["on_A_B", "on_B_table", "on_A_table",
                   "clear_A", "clear_B", "holding_A", "holding_B", "arm_empty"],
        actions = [
            Action("pick_A_from_table",
                   pre  = frozenset(["clear_A", "on_A_table", "arm_empty"]),
                   add  = frozenset(["holding_A"]),
                   dele = frozenset(["clear_A", "on_A_table", "arm_empty"])),
            Action("stack_A_on_B",
                   pre  = frozenset(["holding_A", "clear_B"]),
                   add  = frozenset(["on_A_B", "clear_A", "arm_empty"]),
                   dele = frozenset(["holding_A", "clear_B"])),
        ],
        init = frozenset(["on_A_table", "on_B_table", "clear_A", "clear_B", "arm_empty"]),
        goal = frozenset(["on_A_B"]),
    )
    plan = satplan(prob, T_max=5)
    if plan:
        print("\nOptimal plan:")
        for step in plan:
            print(f"  {step}")

5.5 Parallel and Relaxed Encodings

The sequential encoding above places at most one action per time step (via implicit mutex constraints). A more efficient parallel encoding allows multiple non-interfering actions to execute simultaneously at each step, substantially compressing plan length.[23] Rintanen et al.[23] showed that with parallel semantics, the horizon T is bounded by the number of fluents rather than the state-space diameter, often yielding orders-of-magnitude smaller formulas. The trade-off is that parallel plans may not be directly executable in environments with strict temporal sequencing, requiring post-processing linearisation.

Encoding Variant Actions per Step Formula Size Plan Length Primary Tool
Sequential (STRIPS) ≤ 1 O(|A||F|T) Optimal-sequential SATplan
Parallel (Rintanen) Multiple non-mutex O(|A|²|F|T) Optimal-parallel Madagascar
BLACKBOX (Graphplan) Multiple + mutexes O(|A|²|F|T) Optimal-parallel BLACKBOX
Oversubscription / partial Multiple O(|A||F|T + objectives) Near-optimal OptiPlan

5.6 Tools and Frameworks

A mature ecosystem of software supports SAT-based planning. BLACKBOX[19] remains historically significant as the first planner to integrate planning-graph preprocessing with SAT solving. SATplan'06[20] (C implementation, IPC-5 competitor) is the most widely cited competitive system. Madagascar[24] (Rintanen, 2012–present) is the current state of the art for SAT-based planning, featuring parallel encodings, incremental solving, and support for PDDL 2.1. PySAT[22] is a Python meta-library providing a clean API over Glucose, MiniSAT, Cadical, Maplesat, and others, suitable for research implementations. For the underlying SAT backend, Kissat[25] (winner of SAT Competition 2022–2024 main track) and Cadical[25] represent the current engineering frontier.

Madagascar: State-of-the-Art SAT Planning

Madagascar (M, Mp, MpC variants)[24] by Jussi Rintanen encodes planning problems as SAT with Tseitin-encoded transition relations and exploits incremental SAT solving—reusing learned clauses across horizon increments—to substantially reduce redundant solver work. On IPC benchmark suites, Madagascar consistently solves problems within optimal or near-optimal solution lengths, often outperforming portfolio planners on purely propositional tracks. The planner accepts PDDL 2.1 input and is freely available as open-source software.

6. Reduction to Model Checking

"The connection between planning and model checking is not merely analogical—both problems are fundamentally about reachability in a state-transition system."

— Giunchiglia & Traverso, 1999[26]

The second major reduction strategy identifies automated planning with a subclass of model checking problems.[26] This connection is structurally profound: both disciplines are concerned with the traversal of a finite state-transition system, and both ask reachability queries—does there exist a path through the state space satisfying a given property?

6.1 Model Checking: Background

Model checking, introduced independently by Clarke and Emerson[27] and Queille and Sifakis[28] in 1981, is a formal verification technique that automatically determines whether a finite-state Kripke structure M satisfies a temporal logic specification φ, written M ⊨ φ.[29] A Kripke structure M = ⟨S, S₀, R, L⟩ consists of a set of states S, a set of initial states S₀ ⊆ S, a transition relation R ⊆ S × S, and a labelling function L: S → 2AP mapping states to the set of atomic propositions true in them.[29] Specifications are typically written in Computation Tree Logic (CTL) or Linear Temporal Logic (LTL).[30]

6.2 The Planning–Model Checking Correspondence

The correspondence is straightforward to state precisely.[26] Given a classical planning problem Π = ⟨F, A, I, G⟩, one constructs the Kripke structure M(Π) = ⟨2F, {I}, RA, id⟩ where:

  • The state space is the powerset 2F of fluents (each subset represents a world state).
  • The initial state set is the singleton {I}.
  • The transition relation RA encodes the STRIPS semantics: ⟨s, s′⟩ ∈ RA iff ∃a ∈ A: pre(a) ⊆ s ∧ s′ = (s ∖ del(a)) ∪ add(a).
  • The labelling function is the identity: L(s) = s.

Plan existence in Π then corresponds precisely to the CTL reachability query:

Planning as CTL Reachability M(Π) ⊨ EF(⋀g∈G g)

where EF is the CTL operator "there exists a path along which some future state satisfies the formula".[26] This is the simplest fragment of CTL—existential reachability—and is solvable in linear time O(|S| + |R|) via forward or backward BFS over M(Π).[29]

Why Not Just Use BFS?

The state space 2F is exponential in |F|. The practical utility of the model checking reduction is not that it makes planning easy in the worst case—it does not—but that symbolic model checking algorithms, particularly those based on Binary Decision Diagrams (BDDs)[31] and Bounded Model Checking (BMC),[32] can represent and manipulate exponentially large state spaces implicitly, often achieving tractability on structured instances that defeat explicit-state methods.

6.3 Bounded Model Checking and Planning

Bounded Model Checking (BMC), introduced by Biere et al. in 1999,[32] checks whether M ⊨ EF φ holds via a witness of length at most T by unrolling the transition relation T times and encoding the resulting formula as SAT. This is precisely the SATplan encoding! The formal equivalence between SATplan and BMC for reachability properties is not coincidental—both reduce bounded reachability to SAT by unrolling a transition system. The model checking community and the planning community independently discovered the same technique within a few years of each other, with cross-pollination occurring notably through the work of Cimatti et al.[33]

BMC Encoding (equivalent to SATplan) ⟦M, φ, T⟧ = I(s₀) ∧ ⋀t=0T-1 R(sₜ, sₜ₊₁) ∧ ⋁t=0T φ(sₜ)

6.4 Symbolic Planning with BDDs

Before BMC, the dominant symbolic approach used Ordered Binary Decision Diagrams (OBDDs),[31] a canonical representation of Boolean functions that supports polynomial-time Boolean operations. The BDD-based planner SymBA*[34] and its predecessors encode the set of reachable states as a BDD and compute the plan-reachability fixpoint by repeated image computation: if S is a set of states (represented as a BDD) and img(S) is the set of states reachable from S in one step, then plan existence is determined by iterating:

Forward BDD Image Iteration (Symbolic BFS) S₀ = {I}, Sₜ₊₁ = Sₜ ∪ img(Sₜ), until Sₜ ∩ ⟦G⟧ ≠ ∅ or Sₜ₊₁ = Sₜ (fixpoint)

The image of a BDD S under the transition relation R is computed as img(S) = ∃V. (R(V, V′) ∧ S(V)), where V and V′ are the current and next-state variable sets. Existential quantification over current-state variables is the crucial operation, efficiently supported by BDD packages such as CUDD[35] and BuDDy.[36]

Python symbolic_planning.py — BDD-based forward reachability with dd library 
from dd.autoref import BDD
from typing import List, Optional

def symbolic_plan(fluents: List[str],
                    actions: list,
                    init_clauses: str,
                    goal_clause: str) -> Optional[int]:
    """
    Compute the minimum plan horizon using BDD forward reachability.
    Returns the minimum number of steps, or None if goal is unreachable.

    Parameters
    ----------
    fluents      : list of fluent names (unprimed = current; primed = next)
    actions      : list of (precond_str, effect_str) tuples in dd syntax
    init_clauses : BDD expression for initial state
    goal_clause  : BDD expression for goal condition
    """
    bdd = BDD()
    primed = [f + "'" for f in fluents]
    bdd.declare(*fluents, *primed)

    # Encode transition relation as disjunction of action effects
    R = bdd.false
    for pre_expr, eff_expr in actions:
        pre = bdd.add_expr(pre_expr)
        eff = bdd.add_expr(eff_expr)
        R   = R | (pre & eff)

    S    = bdd.add_expr(init_clauses)  # current reachable set
    goal = bdd.add_expr(goal_clause)
    rename_to_curr = {f + "'": f for f in fluents}

    for step in range(1, 200):
        if S & goal != bdd.false:
            print(f"Goal reachable in {step-1} step(s).")
            return step - 1

        # Image: ∃curr_vars. R(curr, next) ∧ S(curr), then rename next→curr
        img  = bdd.exist(fluents, R & S)
        Snew = bdd.let(rename_to_curr, img)

        if Snew == S:
            print("Fixpoint reached: goal is unreachable.")
            return None
        S = S | Snew

    return None

6.6 Conformant and Contingent Planning

The model checking perspective becomes especially powerful for extensions of classical planning where classical SAT-based methods struggle. Conformant planning—finding a plan that achieves the goal from any state in a set of possible initial states, without sensing—corresponds to a universal reachability query in model checking: M ⊨ AF φ (on all paths, eventually φ).[33] Cimatti et al.[33] demonstrated that the MBP (Model-Based Planner) system, built on top of NuSMV, could solve conformant and contingent planning problems by exploiting BDD-based symbolic model checking algorithms designed for universal CTL operators. This was a significant advance that pure SAT-based planners could not straightforwardly achieve without major encoding extensions.[41]

7. Comparison and Synthesis

Analysis

The two reductions surveyed in this article—planning to SAT and planning to model checking—are not independent alternatives but deeply connected approaches. Bounded model checking is SAT-based planning; symbolic BDD-based model checking is an alternative algorithm for the same state-space traversal problem. The key differentiator lies in which algorithmic machinery is most advantageous for a given instance class.

Dimension SAT-Based (SATplan) Model Checking (BDD/BMC)
Horizon control Explicit iteration over T Fixpoint iteration (BDD) or k-induction (BMC)
Optimality Optimal plan length (bounded) Plan existence (any length); optimal requires additional structure
Uncertainty Classical deterministic only (base) Conformant, contingent, probabilistic via richer CTL/LTL queries
Solver backend CDCL SAT solver (Glucose, Kissat) BDD package (CUDD) or SAT/SMT solver (NuXMV)
Scalability Excellent on structured benchmarks Variable; BDDs can blow up; BMC matches SAT
State space representation Explicit (per time step) Symbolic (implicit set representation)
Plan extraction Read satisfying assignment Counterexample / witness path in model checker
Temporal reasoning Limited (durative actions need extension) Native via LTL/CTL operators
The Unification: Planning is Reachability

Both reductions ultimately encode the same mathematical object: a directed graph G = ⟨2F, EA⟩ where vertices are world states and edges are action applications, together with a reachability query from a designated source I to a set of goal states ⟦G⟧. The choice of reduction determines which graph-traversal machinery is brought to bear. SAT-based approaches (CDCL) exploit structured clause learning and non-chronological backtracking; BDD-based approaches exploit the compactness of shared function representations; explicit-state approaches exploit cache-efficient BFS. No single approach dominates across all problem families.[42]

7.1 Beyond Classical Planning: SMT-Based Approaches

A natural extension of SAT-based planning replaces the SAT solver with an SMT (Satisfiability Modulo Theories) solver, enabling direct treatment of numeric fluents, resources, and temporal constraints within the planning formula.[43] The SMTPlan system[44] encodes PDDL+ problems—including continuous numeric change and durative actions—as nonlinear arithmetic SMT instances and solves them with Z3.[45] This represents the current frontier of reduction-based planning, combining the engineering advantage of mature solver infrastructure with the expressivity needed for real-world temporal and resource-constrained planning problems.

from z3 import Solver, Bool, Real, And, Or, Not, Implies, sat

def smt_plan_numeric(T: int):
    """
    Sketch: numeric planning with a fuel constraint.
    Fluents: 'at_A', 'at_B'; numeric: fuel level.
    Actions: move_A_to_B (costs 3 fuel), move_B_to_A (costs 2 fuel).
    Goal: at_B, fuel >= 0.
    """
    s = Solver()

    # State variables at each timestep
    at_A   = [Bool(f"at_A_{t}")  for t in range(T+1)]
    at_B   = [Bool(f"at_B_{t}")  for t in range(T+1)]
    fuel   = [Real(f"fuel_{t}") for t in range(T+1)]
    act_ab = [Bool(f"move_AB_{t}") for t in range(T)]
    act_ba = [Bool(f"move_BA_{t}") for t in range(T)]

    # Initial state
    s.add(at_A[0], Not(at_B[0]), fuel[0] == 10)

    for t in range(T):
        # Preconditions
        s.add(Implies(act_ab[t], And(at_A[t], fuel[t] >= 3)))
        s.add(Implies(act_ba[t], And(at_B[t], fuel[t] >= 2)))

        # At most one action per step
        s.add(Or(Not(act_ab[t]), Not(act_ba[t])))

        # Effects on Boolean fluents
        s.add(Implies(act_ab[t], And(Not(at_A[t+1]), at_B[t+1])))
        s.add(Implies(act_ba[t], And(at_A[t+1], Not(at_B[t+1]))))

        # Numeric effects: fuel decreases
        s.add(Implies(act_ab[t], fuel[t+1] == fuel[t] - 3))
        s.add(Implies(act_ba[t], fuel[t+1] == fuel[t] - 2))

        # Frame axioms for no-op
        no_op = And(Not(act_ab[t]), Not(act_ba[t]))
        s.add(Implies(no_op, And(at_A[t+1] == at_A[t],
                                    at_B[t+1] == at_B[t],
                                    fuel[t+1] == fuel[t])))

    # Goal: at_B and fuel non-negative
    s.add(at_B[T], fuel[T] >= 0)

    if s.check() == sat:
        m = s.model()
        print(f"Plan found at T={T}:")
        for t in range(T):
            if m[act_ab[t]]: print(f"  t={t}: move_A_to_B")
            if m[act_ba[t]]: print(f"  t={t}: move_B_to_A")
        print(f"  Final fuel: {m[fuel[T]]}")
        return True
    return False

# Try increasing horizons
for T in range(1, 6):
    if smt_plan_numeric(T): break

8. Historical Trajectory

The history of reduction-based planning reveals a pattern of algorithmic cross-pollination between AI planning, formal verification, and combinatorial optimisation. The timeline below traces the major developments:

  • 1971 — Cook proves SAT is NP-complete;[8] STRIPS is published by Fikes and Nilsson.[2]
  • 1981 — Clarke–Emerson and Queille–Sifakis independently introduce model checking.[27],[28]
  • 1986 — Bryant introduces OBDDs, enabling symbolic model checking.[31]
  • 1992 — Kautz and Selman present SATplan at ECAI,[13] the first complete SAT-based planner.
  • 1994 — WalkSAT introduced,[11] enabling practical SATplan on satisfiable instances.
  • 1997 — Graphplan[18] provides compact planning graph that BLACKBOX uses for better bounds.
  • 1998 — BLACKBOX integrates Graphplan + WalkSAT/DPLL, wins AIPS planning competition.[19]
  • 1999 — Biere et al. introduce Bounded Model Checking,[32] independently arriving at the SAT-unrolling technique.
  • 1999 — Giunchiglia & Traverso formalise the planning–model checking correspondence.[26]
  • 2003 — Cimatti et al. demonstrate MBP for conformant/contingent planning via NuSMV.[33]
  • 2006 — SATplan'06 competes at IPC-5, demonstrating competitive performance.[20]
  • 2012 — Rintanen releases Madagascar with parallel encodings and incremental SAT.[24]
  • 2016 — SMTPlan extends the paradigm to numeric/temporal PDDL+ domains via Z3.[44]

9. Conclusion

Reduction-based approaches to automated planning represent one of the most fruitful intellectual strategies in AI: rather than solving a hard problem in isolation, one identifies its structural equivalence with problems that have attracted decades of algorithmic engineering. The reduction of bounded planning to SAT yields the SATplan family of planners, which exploit CDCL and WalkSAT solvers and have demonstrated competitive performance on the International Planning Competition benchmarks. The reduction to model checking reveals that planning is, at its core, a reachability problem in a state-transition system, enabling the application of BDD-based symbolic algorithms, bounded model checking, and richer temporal logics for extensions beyond classical planning. SMT-based extensions, currently under active research, promise to unify these threads within a single formal framework expressive enough to handle numeric, temporal, and probabilistic planning without sacrificing the solver-based reduction strategy.

The broader lesson is methodological: reduction is not a concession of difficulty but a strategy for amplification. By identifying the deep structural equivalences between planning and satisfiability, and between planning and model checking, researchers transformed isolated problems into instances of problems that hundreds of solver engineers had been optimising for decades—and the planning community has benefited immeasurably as a result.
