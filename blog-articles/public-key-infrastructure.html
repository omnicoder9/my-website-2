§1 The trust problem

A key is just a number until you know whose it is

In 1976, Whitfield Diffie and Martin Hellman opened their paper New Directions in Cryptography with a claim that now reads like prophecy: they were standing, they wrote, at the start of a revolution that would let people communicate privately and prove their identity without ever having shared a secret in advance.1 The mathematics they introduced — a public key that anyone may use to encrypt or verify, paired with a private key that only its owner can use to decrypt or sign — dissolved the oldest assumption in cryptography, that two parties must first meet on a secure channel to exchange a shared secret.

But the revolution carried a new problem in its luggage. If my public key is genuinely public, how do you know that the key you are about to use is mine, and not one an impostor has substituted along the way? Diffie and Hellman foresaw this. Their proposed answer was a Public File: a trusted directory, something like a telephone book, in which each name was listed beside its public key.1 Look up your correspondent, read off their key, and open a secure channel. The trouble is that the directory itself becomes a bottleneck and a target. Every conversation now depends on an online lookup against a single authority, and an attacker who can sit between you and that authority — or subvert it — can hand you whatever key they please.

The decisive move came two years later, and from an undergraduate. In his 1978 MIT bachelor's thesis, Towards a Practical Public-Key Cryptosystem, Loren Kohnfelder proposed replacing the live directory lookup with a signed, self-contained, offline-verifiable token. He called it a certificate.2 The same thesis introduced the idea of a certificate revocation list for withdrawing certificates that should no longer be trusted — two coinages that still name the load-bearing structures of the field nearly half a century later.2 The IETF's own record of the term's etymology traces it directly to Kohnfelder, responding to that very suggestion of Diffie and Hellman.3

The insight is deceptively simple. A trusted authority takes the binding it wishes to vouch for — this name owns this public key — and signs it with the authority's own private key, using a digital signature scheme such as the one Rivest, Shamir, and Adleman published, also in 1978.4 The result is a document anyone can check against the authority's public key, at any time, with no further contact with the authority. The directory has been turned inside out: instead of asking a server "what is Alice's key?", you receive Alice's certificate and verify the signature yourself. Trust has been made portable.

A certificate does not make a key trustworthy. It makes a claim about a key checkable.

That distinction is the whole subject. Public Key Infrastructure — PKI — is the assemblage of standards, roles, data formats, and procedures that lets these signed bindings be created, distributed, validated, and revoked at the scale of the entire Internet. NIST's foundational treatment defines it as the set of hardware, software, people, policies, and procedures needed to create, manage, store, distribute, and revoke certificates based on public-key cryptography.5 It is, in other words, not a single algorithm or product but an institution — one that happens to be expressed in cryptography rather than in law, and whose failures, as we will see, look less like broken mathematics and more like broken bureaucracy.

The chapters that follow walk the institution from the inside out. We begin with the certificate itself and the format, X.509, that almost everything now uses. We meet the authorities that issue certificates and the registration function that vouches for who is asking. We trace how individual certificates link into chains that terminate in a small set of trust anchors, and how a relying party walks and validates such a chain. We examine the two competing answers to the question "is this certificate still valid?" — revocation lists and online status checks. We look at how separate infrastructures are stitched together through cross-certification. And we close with the most instructive failures, because in PKI the failures are where the design is really written.

§2 Certificates

The signed binding at the centre of everything

Strip away the formats and acronyms and a public-key certificate is three things welded together: an identity, a public key, and a signature by some authority asserting that the first two belong together.6 Everything else — serial numbers, validity dates, extensions, policies — exists to qualify, scope, or make verifiable that central assertion.

The verification mechanism is the ordinary digital signature, applied recursively. To issue a certificate, an authority computes a cryptographic hash of the to-be-signed content and signs that hash with its private key. To check the certificate, a relying party recomputes the hash and verifies the signature using the authority's public key. If the signature verifies, two facts follow: the content has not been altered since signing, and the holder of the authority's private key really did sign it. Critically, the authority signs a binding, not the subject's private key — indeed a well-run authority never sees the subject's private key at all.5 The subject generates its own key pair, keeps the private half secret, and presents only the public half to be certified.

This recursion is what lets trust scale. The authority's public key is itself carried in a certificate, signed by a higher authority, and so on, until the chain reaches a key that the relying party already trusts without further proof. That terminal key is the trust anchor (§7); the links between are a certificate chain (§6). A single act of pre-established trust — the decision to trust one root key — is leveraged, through signatures, into the ability to verify millions of certificates that the relying party has never seen and whose subjects it has never met.

What a certificate is not

A certificate is not a secret; it is meant to be handed out freely, and it is routinely cached in untrusted storage and transmitted over untrusted networks.6 A certificate is not, by itself, proof of identity in any human sense — it is only as meaningful as the diligence of the authority that vetted the binding and the soundness of the policies under which it was issued. And a certificate is not permanent: it carries an explicit validity window, and it can be revoked before that window closes if the binding ceases to hold — most urgently, if the corresponding private key is compromised. Much of the machinery in the later sections of this guide exists to manage that last, awkward fact, that the world can change faster than a certificate's expiry date.

A note on terms

The party named in a certificate is the subject or subscriber. The party that signs it is the issuer, generally a Certificate Authority. The party that later receives the certificate and decides whether to act on it is the relying party — your browser, an email client, a server checking a client certificate. PKI's entire value accrues to relying parties: they get to make a trust decision about a stranger using only a signed document and a key they already hold.

§3 X.509

The format that won, and the directory that never came

Kohnfelder gave the world the idea of a certificate; the world needed a single way to write one down. That way is X.509, and its dominance today is so total that the two words are often used interchangeably — wrongly, because X.509 is one particular encoding of a far older idea, and it carries the marks of the environment it was born into.

X.509 first appeared in 1988 as one part of the ITU-T's X.500 series — an ambitious effort to build a single, global, hierarchical electronic directory, a kind of worldwide phone book for the emerging networked age.6 The certificate format in that 1988 standard is now called version 1. When X.500 was revised in 1993, two more fields were added, producing the version 2 format.6 Around the same time, the Internet's own Privacy Enhanced Mail (PEM) specifications attempted to build a public-key infrastructure on top of X.509 v1 certificates, defining a rigid hierarchy of certification authorities.7 Hard experience deploying PEM made one thing clear: v1 and v2 were too rigid. More fields were needed to carry the information that real deployments demanded.6

The answer, standardized in 1996, was version 3, which added an open-ended extensions mechanism — the single feature that let X.509 outlive the directory it was designed to serve.8 Where v1 was a fixed shape, v3 became a frame around which new fields could be defined for purposes nobody had anticipated. The standard is jointly maintained by the ITU-T and ISO/IEC as ISO/IEC 9594-8; its ninth edition was published in 2019.8

The "which directory" problem

X.500's global directory never materialized. This left an awkward inheritance, sometimes called the which-directory problem: a relying party holding a certificate may not know where to fetch the issuer's certificate or the relevant revocation information, because there is no universal directory to query.6 Early web servers, for instance, sent only their own certificate, and clients that lacked the intermediate CA certificate — and had nowhere to look it up — simply failed to build a valid chain. The pragmatic fix, still in use, is for servers to send the full chain of intermediate certificates alongside their own, and for certificates to carry pointers (the Authority Information Access extension) to where missing pieces can be retrieved over ordinary HTTP.6 The grand directory was replaced by the certificates carrying their own context.

The Internet's profile of X.509 was the work of the IETF's PKIX working group, chartered in 1995. Its first comprehensive profile, RFC 2459, appeared in January 1999;9 the current, much-refined profile is RFC 5280 (2008), the document a practitioner actually reaches for, because it pins down exactly how the open-ended ITU-T standard is to be used — and constrained — on the Internet.6

ASN.1, and why certificates are not text

An X.509 certificate is not, at heart, text. Its structure is described in Abstract Syntax Notation One (ASN.1), an interface-description language that defines data structures independently of how they are stored on the wire.8 For transmission, that abstract structure is serialized using the Distinguished Encoding Rules (DER), a canonical binary encoding chosen precisely because a digital signature requires one unambiguous byte sequence to sign and verify: DER guarantees that any given value has exactly one encoding.10 The familiar .pem file, with its -----BEGIN CERTIFICATE----- banner, is merely that DER blob wrapped in Base64 so it survives being pasted into a text field. Underneath, every field is a tag-length-value triple, nested like a Russian doll — which is why hand-editing a certificate is a fool's errand and why subtle parser disagreements over malformed encodings have been a recurring source of security bugs.

Anatomy of a certificate

The signed body of a certificate — the tbsCertificate, literally the "to-be-signed certificate" — carries a compact set of mandatory fields defined by RFC 5280:6 the version; a serial number unique to the issuer; the signature algorithm identifier; the issuer's distinguished name; a validity period of notBefore and notAfter; the subject's distinguished name; the subjectPublicKeyInfo, which carries the public key and its algorithm; and, in v3, the extensions. Outside the signed body sit the algorithm identifier repeated and the signature value itself — the issuer's signature over the hash of everything inside.

The extensions are where the format's longevity lives, and each is named by an Object Identifier, a globally unique dotted-number sequence drawn from the same hierarchical registry ASN.1 uses throughout. A handful do the heavy lifting in practice:

One rule governs every extension and is worth stating precisely, because it is a recurring source of both security and breakage. Each extension is flagged critical or non-critical. A validator that ignores an unknown critical extension is not lenient; it is broken, and it is exploitable.

§4 Certificate authorities

The institutions that sign

A Certificate Authority — CA — is the entity that issues certificates: it receives a request, satisfies itself that the binding is correct, and signs.5 Everything cryptographic about a CA is ordinary; what makes a CA a CA is institutional. It holds a private key whose corresponding public key is trusted by relying parties, it operates under published rules about who it will certify and how, and it accepts the consequences when it gets that wrong. The CA is the point where mathematics hands the problem back to governance.

Roots, intermediates, and the discipline of distance

In any serious deployment the CA is not one key but a hierarchy. At the top sits a root CA, whose certificate is self-signed — it has no higher authority to vouch for it, so it vouches for itself, and its trustworthiness comes entirely from being placed, by hand, into relying parties' trust stores (§7).6 Beneath the root sit one or more intermediate (or subordinate) CAs, each certified by the root or by another intermediate, and it is these that do the day-to-day work of issuing end-entity certificates.

This structure is not bureaucratic ornament; it is a security control. The root's private key is the most catastrophic single point of failure in the entire system — compromise it and every certificate beneath it becomes forgeable — so the overwhelming convention is to keep the root offline, its key material in a hardware security module in a vault, brought out only rarely to sign new intermediate certificates in audited ceremonies. The intermediates, which must be online to issue certificates continuously, are comparatively expendable: if an intermediate's key is compromised, the root can revoke that one intermediate and issue a replacement, without the far more disruptive surgery of replacing a root that is embedded in billions of devices.5 The hierarchy converts an existential risk into a merely serious one.

In practice

The Basic Constraints extension is what enforces the boundary between a CA and a leaf. A CA certificate has CA:TRUE, optionally with a pathLenConstraint capping how many further CAs may sit below it; an end-entity certificate has CA:FALSE, and a relying party that respects the rules will refuse to accept a signature on a child certificate from a key whose certificate is not marked as a CA.6 A single missing or unenforced constraint here is how an ordinary leaf certificate could, in a broken validator, masquerade as an authority.

Policy as the real product

What a CA actually sells is not bytes — the signing is trivial — but assurance, and that assurance is documented in two companion texts. A Certificate Policy (CP) states the rules a certificate is issued under and what it may be relied upon for; a Certification Practice Statement (CPS) describes how a particular CA implements those rules in its operations.5 These documents are where the abstract notion of "trust" acquires concrete, auditable content: how identity is checked, how keys are protected, how incidents are handled, how long records are kept. The policy under which a given certificate was issued is itself encoded into the certificate, as OIDs in the Certificate Policies extension, so that a relying party can in principle distinguish a casually issued certificate from a rigorously vetted one.6

Validation levels: DV, OV, EV

For the Web PKI, that assurance is stratified into three tiers, distinguished entirely by how hard the CA worked to verify the applicant. Domain Validation (DV) confirms only that the applicant controls the domain name — nothing about who they are. Organization Validation (OV) additionally verifies that a real, identified organization stands behind the request. Extended Validation (EV) applies a heavier, standardized identity-vetting process codified in the CA/Browser Forum's EV Guidelines, which grew out of an effort to halt a "race to the bottom" in which competing CAs had quietly stripped away validation checks to cut costs.11 All three produce technically identical certificates that encrypt traffic equally well; the difference is the strength of the claim about identity, not the strength of the cryptography.

Who watches the issuers

Because a relying party trusts every certificate a CA issues, the question of which CAs to trust is consequential, and it is not settled by the CA itself. It is settled by root programs — the curated trust stores maintained by Mozilla, Microsoft, Apple, and Google — and by the industry body where their requirements are negotiated, the CA/Browser Forum, founded in 2005. The Forum's Baseline Requirements, first effective in 2012, set the floor of technical and procedural rules that every publicly trusted CA must meet, verified through annual audits; a CA that fails them risks removal from the trust stores, which is a commercial death sentence.12

One trend in those requirements deserves note because it reshapes everything downstream: certificate lifetimes have been falling relentlessly. The permitted maximum validity for a TLS certificate dropped from over eight years to 60 months in 2012, to 39 months in 2015, and to 398 days by 2020; under a 2025 ballot it is set to fall to 47 days by 2029.12 A short-lived certificate limits the damage a compromised or misissued certificate can do — it expires before stale revocation information matters — but it makes manual certificate management impossible at scale, which is precisely why automation (§5) has moved from convenience to necessity.

The cryptography in a CA is the easy part. The hard part is everything a notary has always struggled with: proving that the person at the counter is who they say they are.

§5 Registration authorities

Who vouches for the applicant

Issuing a certificate is really two jobs. One is registration: establishing that the applicant is who they claim and controls what they claim. The other is certification: signing the certificate. In small systems one entity does both, but classical PKI architecture separates them, assigning the first job to a Registration Authority (RA).5 The RA performs identity proofing and approves or rejects requests; the CA, trusting the RA's verdict, performs the cryptographic act of signing. The RA never holds the CA's signing key.

The separation buys two things. It lets the sensitive signing key stay concentrated and protected in one well-guarded place while the messier, human-facing work of vetting is distributed to where the applicants actually are — a regional office, a corporate HR department, an enterprise's own identity system. And it draws a clean line of accountability: the question "was this binding properly verified?" is answered by the RA's records, separately from "was this certificate properly signed?", which is answered by the CA's.5 In large federations the RA function may itself be delegated through trusted agents who collect documentation on the RA's behalf, a structure used throughout government PKIs.

The request: a certificate signing request

The applicant's half of the transaction is a Certificate Signing Request (CSR), standardized as PKCS #10. The subject generates a key pair, places the public key and the desired names into a CSR, and signs the request with the corresponding private key — proving, before anything else, that it actually holds the private key for the public key it is asking to have certified.13 The CA extracts the public key and identity from the CSR, the RA validates the identity, and the issued certificate binds them. The private key itself is never transmitted; it never needs to be.

When the registration authority became a program

For decades, registration for web certificates was a manual ordeal: generate a CSR, paste it into a web form, prove control of the domain by responding to an email or placing a file, wait for a human, download the result, install it by hand, and remember to repeat the whole thing before expiry.13 This friction was, more than any other single factor, why most of the web ran on unencrypted HTTP for so long.

The fix was to turn domain-validation registration into a machine-to-machine protocol. The Automatic Certificate Management Environment (ACME), designed by the Internet Security Research Group for its Let's Encrypt CA and standardized as RFC 8555 in 2019, lets a piece of software prove control of a domain and obtain a certificate entirely over an API, with no human in the loop.13 The client requests an order for some names; the server issues a challenge for each — place this token at a well-known URL, or in a DNS record; the client provisions it; the server checks, from multiple network vantage points; and on success the client submits a CSR and downloads the signed chain. Every message from the client is signed with its account key, and the whole exchange carries anti-replay protection.13

The consequences were enormous. Let's Encrypt, which opened to the public in December 2015 and was built entirely around ACME, became the largest CA in the world by issuance volume, and free, automated certificates drove the share of encrypted web traffic from a minority to the overwhelming majority within a few years.14 In effect, the registration authority — once a clerk at a desk — became a published protocol, and the falling certificate lifetimes of §4 became survivable precisely because renewal no longer required a human.

The boundary that automation did not erase

ACME automates domain validation, which can be checked entirely online. It deliberately does not automate the deeper identity vetting behind OV and EV, which still rests on out-of-band human verification — documents, registries, callbacks. The protocol is explicit that its focus is proving control of an identifier, with extensions left open for other identifier types and PKI contexts.13 Some claims about the world can be checked by a server; others still need a person.

§6 Certificate chains

From a leaf to an anchor, link by link

A single certificate is rarely enough. The server you connect to presents a leaf certificate signed by an intermediate CA — but why should you trust that intermediate? Because it has a certificate, signed by another authority, and so on. This sequence is a certification path, or chain, and it exists for a structural reason: a relying party is initialized with only a small number of trusted CA public keys, so the path is the bridge from the certificate in hand to one of those few trusted keys.6

Building the chain: name chaining and key identifiers

Before a path can be validated it must be constructed, and the basic mechanism is name chaining: the issuer name in one certificate is matched against the subject name of a candidate issuing certificate.9 Names alone can be ambiguous — an authority may have re-keyed, so two CA certificates can share a subject name but carry different keys — so two extensions accelerate and disambiguate the search. Every conforming CA certificate carries a Subject Key Identifier; every certificate it issues carries an Authority Key Identifier whose value matches that SKI, giving the builder a direct pointer from a child up to its parent's key.6

A subtlety worth internalizing: these identifiers are an optimization for finding the path, not a security check. RFC 5280 is explicit that, while the AKI must be present to facilitate path construction, applications are not required to verify that an AKI and SKI actually match when validating — the real security comes from the signature, not the hint.6 Treating a key identifier as if it were a cryptographic guarantee is a category error.

Validating the chain: the algorithm

Construction finds a candidate path; validation decides whether to trust it. RFC 5280 specifies a precise path-validation algorithm, and although its full machinery is intricate, its obligations can be stated plainly. For each certificate, working from a trust anchor down to the leaf, the validator must establish that:6

RFC 5280 path validation, in essence

Signatures chain. Each certificate's signature verifies under the public key of the certificate above it, and the topmost links to a trusted anchor.

Time is valid. Each certificate is within its notBefore–notAfter window at the moment of validation.

Nothing is revoked. No certificate in the path has been revoked, checked via CRL or OCSP (§8–9).

Constraints hold. Every intermediate is genuinely a CA (Basic Constraints), within any path-length limit; Key Usage permits certificate signing; and any Name Constraints on a CA are honoured, so that a CA permitted only to issue under example.com cannot vouch for elsewhere.net.

Policies are consistent. Certificate-policy processing tracks a valid policy set down the chain, supporting policy mapping where it is allowed.

The order matters: validation proceeds from the anchor toward the leaf, because each step's authority derives from the step above it. Only if every certificate clears every test, all the way down, is the leaf accepted. A failure anywhere — an expired intermediate, a revoked certificate, a name-constraint violation, an unrecognized critical extension — invalidates the whole path. This is what your browser does, silently, in the milliseconds before it draws the padlock.

The chain the server must send

Because the global directory never arrived (§3), the relying party usually cannot look up missing intermediates. The working compromise is that the server sends the intermediate certificates along with its leaf, so the client can assemble the path without external fetches; where a piece is still missing, the Authority Information Access extension tells the client where to download the issuer's certificate over HTTP.6 A correctly configured server therefore presents leaf and intermediates but omits the root — the root must already be in the client's trust store, or no amount of sending it would make it trustworthy.

§7 Trust anchors

The axioms you never chose to examine

Every chain has to stop somewhere. The certificate at which validation terminates — the one the relying party trusts without asking for any further proof — rests on a trust anchor. A trust anchor is, at minimum, a public key together with the name of its owner and any constraints on what it may be used for; in the Web PKI it is almost always packaged as a self-signed root certificate.15 The anchors are the axioms of the system: the small set of trust decisions from which all other trust is derived.

It is tempting to think a self-signed root is trusted because its signature verifies. It is not. A self-signed certificate's signature proves only that whoever holds that private key produced it — which an attacker could do for their own forged root just as easily. What makes a root a trust anchor is entirely external to the certificate: the fact that it has been placed, by some out-of-band process you trust, into your trust store.6 RFC 5280's validation algorithm reflects this precisely — it does not assume that trust-anchor information arrives in a self-signed certificate at all, treating the anchor as an input to validation rather than a link to be verified.6 The self-signature is a convenient container; the trust comes from the store.

Where the anchors live

For most people, the set of trust anchors is inherited wholesale and invisibly. Operating systems and browsers ship a root store — a curated list of root certificates — maintained by the major root programs of Mozilla, Microsoft, Apple, and Google.12 When you install a browser, you are silently delegating to its vendor the decision about which few hundred organizations worldwide are allowed to vouch for the identity of any site you visit. That delegation is the true root of trust, and it is a human and organizational decision, not a cryptographic one. A modern root store is the most consequential access-control list almost nobody ever reads.

Because anchors are inputs, they can carry more than a key. Trust-anchor formats allow constraints to travel with the anchor — for example, name constraints declaring that paths beginning at this anchor should be trusted only within a particular namespace — so that a relying party can grant an authority real but bounded trust.15 An enterprise can install its internal root as an anchor constrained to its own domains, trusting it absolutely for those and not at all for anything else.

The blast radius of an anchor

Because a trust anchor underwrites every certificate beneath it without further check, a compromised or fraudulently used anchor is the worst failure in PKI. There is no higher authority to revoke a root; the only remedy is to remove it from every relying party's trust store — which, when the root is embedded in billions of shipped devices, can require software updates that take weeks to propagate and may break legitimate services that still depend on it. The 2011 DigiNotar collapse (§11) is the canonical demonstration of exactly this dynamic.

Hedging the axiom

Given that a single misbehaving CA in the trust store can vouch for any name, defenders have layered additional checks on top of plain path validation. Public-key pinning narrows trust for a specific service to a specific key or CA, so that a certificate for that service signed by any other trusted CA is rejected even though it would pass ordinary validation — the technique that first surfaced the DigiNotar attack, when a hard-coded pin in Chrome refused a fraudulent but otherwise valid certificate for Google.16 Pinning trades flexibility for safety and is brittle if a pinned key is lost, which is why it has been largely superseded for the open web by Certificate Transparency (§11), a mechanism that does not constrain which CA may issue but makes every issuance publicly visible. Both are admissions of the same uncomfortable truth: the trust anchors are powerful enough that the prudent course is to watch what they do.

§8 Revocation, part one — CRLs

Taking it back before it expires

A certificate carries a validity window, but the world does not respect it. A private key may be stolen, an employee may leave, a domain may change hands, a CA may discover it issued a certificate in error — any of which can make a binding false long before its notAfter date arrives. Revocation is the mechanism for declaring a certificate untrustworthy ahead of its natural expiry, and it is, by common consent, the hardest part of PKI to get right. The reason is structural: a certificate is designed to be verifiable offline, with no contact to the issuer — and revocation is exactly the inconvenient case where the relying party needs fresh information the certificate cannot itself contain.

The original answer, named in Kohnfelder's thesis and formalized in X.509, is the Certificate Revocation List (CRL): a signed, timestamped list of the serial numbers of certificates that have been revoked but have not yet expired, published periodically by the CA.6 A relying party downloads the list, checks whether the certificate's serial number appears on it, and treats a listed certificate as untrusted. The list is itself a signed object — an X.509 v2 CRL — so it can be fetched from untrusted mirrors and still be relied upon, exactly like a certificate.

Structure and distribution

A CRL carries its own metadata: the issuer, a thisUpdate time, a nextUpdate time by which a fresher list will be published, and, for each revoked certificate, its serial number, a revocation date, and optionally a reason code.6 Certificates point to the relevant list through the CRL Distribution Points extension, which names a URL from which the CRL can be retrieved — the same self-describing trick that compensates for the absent global directory.6 A CA may also delegate publication to a separate CRL issuer, keeping revocation data flowing without exposing the main signing key.6

The freshness–size dilemma

CRLs have a real virtue: once downloaded, they can be cached and checked offline, with no per-validation network call and no leak of which certificate is being checked. But they suffer two coupled problems. A CRL is only as current as its last publication, so there is an inherent latency between a revocation and the moment relying parties learn of it — bounded by the publication interval, which may be hours or days. And on a busy CA the list can grow large, since it must enumerate every unexpired revoked certificate, forcing every relying party to download and parse a sizable file merely to check one serial number.6

The standard mitigation is the delta CRL: a small supplementary list containing only the changes since a referenced full ("base") CRL, so that a relying party holding the base can stay current by fetching a much smaller delta.6 Delta CRLs ease the bandwidth problem but not the deeper tension — that a model built for offline verification is being asked to deliver near-real-time news. That tension is exactly what the next mechanism set out to resolve.

Reason codes

A CRL entry can state why a certificate was revoked — keyCompromise, cACompromise, affiliationChanged, superseded, cessationOfOperation, and others. One value, certificateHold, marks a temporary suspension that may later be lifted, the only reversible revocation state. The distinction matters operationally: a key compromise is permanent and urgent, while a superseded certificate is mere housekeeping.6

§9 Revocation, part two — OCSP

Asking about one certificate instead of downloading them all

If the problem with a CRL is that you must download a whole list to learn the fate of a single certificate, the obvious fix is to ask about just that one. The Online Certificate Status Protocol (OCSP) does exactly that: the relying party sends a small request naming a certificate's issuer and serial number to an OCSP responder, and receives back a signed answer of good, revoked, or unknown, bounded by its own freshness window.17 Originally specified in 1999 and revised as RFC 6960 in 2013, OCSP was designed to determine a certificate's status without requiring CRLs at all.17

The responder's answer is signed, so it can be trusted like any other PKI object. A CA may sign OCSP responses with its own key or delegate the task to a dedicated responder whose certificate carries the id-kp-OCSPSigning extended key usage — and RFC 6960 tightened the rules here, recommending that such a delegate be issued by the very CA whose certificates it reports on, signed with the same key, so that a relying party can be sure the responder truly speaks for that CA.17 A high-volume profile, RFC 5019, adapts OCSP for the web's scale by favouring pre-produced, cacheable responses over signing each query live.18

Two problems OCSP created

OCSP solved the size problem and sharpened freshness, but it introduced two of its own. The first is privacy: in its plain form, every time your browser checks a certificate it tells the responder — an entity operated by or for the CA — which site you are about to visit, and because OCSP does not mandate encryption, that disclosure may be observed by others in transit too.17 A mechanism meant to protect users quietly became a mechanism for watching them. The second is reliability: a live status check adds latency and a dependency, and responders are not always reachable. Rather than fail shut — refusing connections when status is unavailable, which would make the whole web fragile — browsers in practice "soft-fail," proceeding when an OCSP check times out. But an attacker exploiting a stolen key can simply block the OCSP traffic, and the soft-fail browser sails on, which substantially undermines OCSP's value as a defence against the very compromise it was built to catch.

Stapling, and must-staple

The privacy and latency problems share a fix: move the query off the client. With OCSP stapling — carried by the TLS status_request extension — the server periodically fetches a fresh, time-stamped OCSP response for its own certificate and "staples" it into the TLS handshake, so the client receives the proof of good standing directly and never contacts the responder.19 The responder no longer learns who is browsing, and the client incurs no extra round trip. Stapling is optional, however, so a server can simply decline to staple; the OCSP Must-Staple certificate extension closes that gap by committing a certificate to always being served with a stapled response, instructing clients to reject it if one is missing.20

The pendulum swings back

For all its engineering, OCSP's privacy cost ultimately turned the industry against it. In 2023 the CA/Browser Forum made operating an OCSP responder optional for CAs and returned to requiring CRLs;12 and in 2025 Let's Encrypt, the largest CA in the world, announced it would shut down its OCSP service entirely, citing the privacy leak as the decisive reason.14 The mechanism built to fix CRLs is being retired in favour of CRLs — now made tractable by modern, compressed, pushed revocation sets in browsers, and by the falling certificate lifetimes (§4) that shrink the window in which revocation matters at all. It is a rare and instructive case of a deployed Internet protocol being deliberately wound down because its cost to users outweighed its benefit.

§10 Cross-certification

Stitching separate infrastructures together

So far we have described a single tree of trust descending from one root. But the world is full of separate trees — a government's PKI, a company's internal CA, a commercial CA, each with its own root, each invisible to the others. Two users whose certificates descend from different roots have no basis for trust between them, even if both PKIs are perfectly sound, because neither root is an anchor in the other's world.21 Cross-certification is the set of techniques for building trust between infrastructures without collapsing them into one.

Three classical architectures

PKI architectures traditionally fall into three shapes, distinguished by where users place their trust and how CAs relate.21 A single CA serves one community from one trust point — simple, but it does not scale or interoperate. A hierarchy places all trust in a single root that delegates downward; path construction is easy because every path leads deterministically up to the root, but the whole edifice depends on that one root, and merging two established hierarchies after the fact is awkward because there is no agreed authority to sit above both.21 A mesh abandons the single root: peer CAs issue cross-certificates to one another, each becoming a trust point for its own community.

The mesh has genuine advantages — it absorbs new communities by simply adding a cross-certificate, and it is resilient, since the compromise of one CA does not topple the others, who merely revoke their cross-certificates to it.21 But it pays for this with complexity. Unlike a hierarchy, building a path in a mesh is non-deterministic: there are multiple candidate routes, some leading to dead ends, and it is even possible to construct endless loops of cross-certificates. In the worst case the longest path can be as long as the number of CAs in the mesh — scalability becomes, as one NIST analysis put it, a curse as much as a blessing.21

The bridge

The bridge CA is the architecture that resolves the mesh's combinatorial explosion. Rather than have every domain cross-certify every other — an arrangement whose cross-certificate count grows quadratically — each domain cross-certifies once with a central bridge, which acts as a hub and conduit of trust between them.21 A domain keeps its own root and internal structure; it simply establishes one peer relationship with the bridge, and through the bridge it reaches every other connected domain.

The canonical example is the United States government's Federal PKI, built around the Federal Bridge Certification Authority (FBCA). The federal effort began with hierarchical ambitions, but no single organization could be agreed upon to run a governmental super-root, and agencies had already deployed their own independent PKIs — so the plan shifted to integrating those autonomous infrastructures through a bridge.21 The FBCA issues cross-certificates only to the principal CAs that agencies and partners designate, and those cross-certified CAs are audited annually for conformance, extending the federation's reach well beyond the federal government itself.22

Mapping policy across the seam

Cross-certification raises a question hierarchies can dodge: two CAs that trust each other may run under different certificate policies, so what does a certificate's policy mean once it has crossed the boundary? The answer is policy mapping. A cross-certificate can assert, through the policyMappings extension, that a policy in the issuing domain is equivalent to a named policy in the subject domain, so that a relying party can translate assurance levels across the seam during path validation.6 The Federal Bridge does precisely this, mapping each connected entity's policies to its own assurance levels so that a certificate trusted at a given level in one domain is understood at the corresponding level in another.22

Cross-signing on the open web

Cross-certification also has a quieter, ubiquitous role in the Web PKI: bootstrapping new roots. A newly created root CA is trusted by nobody until root programs add it and that addition propagates into the installed base — a process that can take years. To be usable immediately, a new root's key is cross-signed by an older, already-trusted root, so its certificates chain up to the established anchor on devices that do not yet carry the new one. Let's Encrypt relied on exactly this when it launched, chaining to an established root until its own was widely trusted. One key, certified by two parents, presents whichever chain a given relying party can validate.6

§11 When trust breaks

DigiNotar, and the visibility that followed

The clearest way to understand what PKI is for is to watch one fail. In the summer of 2011 a relatively obscure Dutch certificate authority called DigiNotar — a trusted root in every major browser, and the issuer for parts of the Dutch government's own infrastructure — was thoroughly compromised. On 10 July an intruder with access to DigiNotar's systems issued a fraudulent wildcard certificate for *.google.com; DigiNotar detected an intrusion into its CA infrastructure on 19 July but did not disclose it.16 The forged certificate was real in every cryptographic sense — correctly signed by a CA the whole world trusted — and it needed no relationship with Google to be accepted, because any trusted CA may vouch for any name.

What the certificate enabled was a large-scale man-in-the-middle attack against Google users in Iran, intercepting the traffic of an estimated 300,000 Iranian Gmail accounts.23 The attack surfaced only because of a hedge against exactly this scenario: Chrome carried a hard-coded pin for Google's own certificates, so when an Iranian user was served the fraudulent certificate, the browser recognized it as not one of Google's expected certificates and refused it, prompting the help-forum post that unravelled the whole affair.16 Ordinary path validation would have accepted the certificate without complaint; only the additional check caught it.

The post-mortem, conducted by the security firm Fox-IT and code-named Operation Black Tulip, found a near-total compromise: all eight of DigiNotar's certificate-issuing servers had been under the intruder's control, log files had been tampered with, and the full extent of misissuance could not be reconstructed with confidence.23 Browser vendors moved to remove DigiNotar from their trust stores — but the removal was not clean. Because the CA's roots were embedded in shipped software, distrusting them required pushing updates that took time to reach users, and because DigiNotar underwrote legitimate Dutch government services, an immediate blanket ban risked breaking citizens' access to tax and identity systems.16 Within weeks the Dutch government had taken over operational management of the company, and DigiNotar declared bankruptcy.23 A single compromised authority had, briefly, broken the trustworthiness of the padlock for a whole country — and demonstrated that the catastrophic risk in PKI is not a broken cipher but a misbehaving issuer.

The certificate authority system was created in an era when the worry was credit-card thieves. Users now rely on it against nation-states. It is not obvious it can bear that weight.

— paraphrasing the post-DigiNotar consensus

The structural answer: make issuance visible

DigiNotar crystallized a problem that pinning could only patch: with hundreds of CAs each able to issue for any domain, how could anyone detect a misissued certificate before it was used in anger? The answer, developed at Google in the breach's immediate aftermath and standardized as RFC 6962, is Certificate Transparency (CT): an experimental protocol for publicly logging every certificate as it is issued, in append-only logs that anyone can audit.24 The logs are built on Merkle trees, whose cryptographic structure lets a log prove that a certificate is included and prove that the log is append-only — that it has never quietly deleted or altered an entry — without anyone having to trust the log operator.24

The mechanism is elegant in its incentives. When a certificate is logged, the log returns a Signed Certificate Timestamp (SCT), a promise to include the certificate; a relying party can require that the certificates it sees come with SCTs, which effectively forces CAs to log everything they issue.24 Domain owners and researchers then monitor the logs, watching for certificates issued for their names that they never requested — turning misissuance from an invisible event into a publicly observable one, usually within hours. CT does not prevent a CA from issuing a fraudulent certificate; it guarantees that doing so leaves an indelible public trace. The protocol was revised as Certificate Transparency version 2.0 in RFC 9162.25

Certificate Transparency reflects a philosophical shift visible across all of modern PKI. The early model tried to make trust preventive — vet carefully, and the system will be sound. The DigiNotar generation of failures taught that prevention is never perfect and that the leverage of a trusted CA is too great to leave unwatched. The newer model layers detection and accountability on top: log everything, monitor continuously, shorten lifetimes so mistakes expire quickly, and automate issuance and renewal so that short lifetimes are bearable.12 Transparency, automation (§5), and ever-shorter validity (§4) are three faces of the same lesson — that an institution this powerful must be designed to be audited, not merely trusted.

What is still coming

The next structural change on the horizon is cryptographic rather than procedural. The signature and key-exchange algorithms underpinning today's certificates — RSA and elliptic-curve schemes — would be broken by a sufficiently capable quantum computer, so standards bodies are profiling post-quantum signature algorithms for X.509 and planning hybrid certificates that carry both classical and quantum-resistant signatures during the transition.8 The format that survived the death of the X.500 directory by being extensible (§3) is now being asked to survive a change in the mathematics of trust itself.

§12 Coda

An institution wearing cryptographic clothing

It is easy to mistake PKI for a branch of mathematics, because it is built from signatures and hashes and keys. But every story in this guide points the other way. The cryptography is the part that works flawlessly; it is the institutional seams that strain — the vetting of an applicant, the protection of a signing key, the curation of a root store, the disclosure of a breach, the speed with which a mistake can be detected and undone. PKI is a way of manufacturing trust between strangers at planetary scale, and like every such system humans have ever built, its hardest problems are about authority, accountability, and what happens when authority is abused.

Seen this way, the field's whole trajectory becomes legible. The certificate inverted Diffie and Hellman's directory so that trust could travel offline. X.509 outlived the directory it was born to serve by being extensible. Hierarchies of CAs converted existential risk into survivable risk by keeping the most dangerous keys offline. Revocation, transparency, automation, and shrinking lifetimes are all responses to the same recurring discovery — that an issuer trusted to vouch for any name is too powerful to be left merely trusted, and must instead be constrained, watched, and, when necessary, removed. The padlock in the address bar is the visible tip of an enormous, mostly invisible apparatus for deciding whom to believe. That it works at all, billions of times a second, is one of the quiet engineering achievements of the age — and understanding how it can fail is the surest way to appreciate how carefully it has been made to succeed.
