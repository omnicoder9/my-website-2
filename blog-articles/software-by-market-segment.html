00Opening

An industry with no natural kinds

Ask a chemist what kinds of things exist and you will eventually arrive at the periodic table: a classification whose categories were, in a strong sense, waiting to be discovered. Ask a biologist and you will get a tree of life whose branches, for all their revisions, track something real about descent. Ask what kinds of software exist and you will get something stranger — not a discovery but a negotiation. The same program can be an application to one buyer, infrastructure to another, and a platform to a third, and none of them is wrong. Software is close to the purest product our economy makes: it is, almost literally, thought that has been written down and made to run, and thought does not fall into tidy bins on its own.1

And yet the software business is enormous, and enormous businesses cannot be discussed, sized, bought, sold, regulated, or invested in without categories. The market that analysts track runs into the hundreds of billions of dollars a year, and behind every headline figure is a taxonomy — a decision about what counts as one kind of thing and what counts as another. Those taxonomies are not laws of nature. They are instruments, built by particular people, at particular moments, for particular purposes: to add a market up without counting anything twice, to tell a chief information officer which vendors to compare, to let a statistician slot a company into a national accounts table, or simply to teach a first-year student the difference between the program that runs the machine and the program that does your taxes.

This essay is a field guide to those instruments. Its argument is that the categories of software — application, platform, infrastructure, and the finer divisions beneath them such as CRM, ERP, database, and operating system — are best understood not as facts about software but as a record of an industry learning to describe itself. We will look at why the classification of software is genuinely hard; how the categories we now take for granted emerged from the history of the market; the one distinction every engineer learns first, between system and application software, and where it breaks; the dominant commercial taxonomy used by analyst firms, which resolves that break into three tidy layers; the cross-cutting dimensions — horizontal versus vertical, on-premises versus cloud, proprietary versus open — that no single layered picture can capture; the formal statistical codes that governments use and why their tent always leaks; and, finally, why every one of these schemes is provisional, and how to read any software taxonomy with the right measure of suspicion.

01Why classification is hard

What a market taxonomy is for — and what it is fighting against

A classification is a machine for asking, of any object put in front of it, “which one of these is it?” The good ones share two properties that librarians and market researchers both prize. First, the categories should be collectively exhaustive: everything that belongs in the domain lands somewhere, with no orphans. Second, they should be mutually exclusive: nothing lands in two places at once, so that when you add the categories up you recover the whole and only the whole. When IDC, the oldest of the large technology-analyst firms, describes its own software taxonomy, it reaches for exactly this language, calling it a “collectively exhaustive and mutually exclusive” view of the market.2 The phrase is an aspiration disguised as a description, because software fights both properties at once.

It fights exhaustiveness because software will not stop being invented. New kinds of program — the spreadsheet, the relational database, the web browser, the container runtime, the large-language-model orchestration framework — arrive faster than any committee can name them, and each arrival forces a choice: bolt a new category onto the scheme, or stretch an old one until it no longer means much. And it fights exclusivity because software categories are defined by use, not by essence, and the same artefact can be used in incompatible ways. A relational database is a finished application to the person who administers it, a piece of raw infrastructure to the developer building an application on top of it, and a product to the company that sells it. The category depends on where you are standing.

This is why it helps to separate two ambitions that the word “taxonomy” runs together. A scientific taxonomy aspires to carve nature at its joints — to find categories that correspond to something mind-independent, such as chemical elements or evolutionary clades. A market taxonomy has a humbler and more honest goal: to be useful for a purpose while summing correctly. It does not claim that “application software” is a natural kind; it claims only that drawing the line there lets an analyst size a market, lets a buyer shortlist vendors, and lets everyone in the conversation mean roughly the same thing. Judged as science, every software taxonomy is false. Judged as an instrument, the question is not whether it is true but whether it is fit for what you are trying to do with it.

Different purposes pull toward different schemes, and much of the confusion in discussions of software categories comes from mixing them. At least five distinct jobs are in play:

  • Sizing and forecasting. Analyst firms need a partition fine enough to model demand for each slice yet coarse enough to roll up into a defensible total. This is the pressure that produced the layered analyst taxonomy examined below.
  • Procurement and comparison. A buyer wants a category to mean “a set of products that can substitute for one another” — a shortlist. This is what the analyst firms’ vendor-ranking grids are for, and it pulls toward narrow, competitor-defined markets.
  • Competitive strategy. A vendor uses categories to stake a claim: to say “we own this market” or, more ambitiously, to name a market that did not previously exist and appoint itself the leader of it.
  • Statistics and regulation. A government statistician needs categories stable enough to compare across years and countries, which pulls toward slow-moving, establishment-based codes such as the North American Industry Classification System.
  • Engineering pedagogy. A teacher needs the smallest number of categories that make the architecture of a computer intelligible, which is why the very first cut every student learns has only two boxes.

Hold these purposes in mind, because the rest of this guide is largely a tour of the schemes each of them produced, and of the seams where they fail to line up. One theme recurs. Because software categories are defined by use and enforced by convention rather than by nature, the act of naming a category is never quite neutral. To call a class of products “enterprise resource planning” or “software as a service” is not only to describe the market but, in part, to make it — to tell vendors what to build toward and buyers what to ask for. We will meet this performativity repeatedly, and confront it directly at the end. A taxonomy of software is not a photograph of the terrain. It is more like a surveyor’s grid, which does not merely record the land but decides where the property lines will run.

02A short history of the categories

How the market invented its own kinds

To see that software categories are historical, it helps to visit a time before they existed. In the 1950s, software was not a product at all. Computers were monstrously expensive machines sold or leased by their manufacturers, and the programs that made them useful came bundled with the iron at no separate charge, generally handed over in source form. Users who needed something the manufacturer did not supply wrote it themselves and, increasingly, shared it. In 1955 a group of IBM customers founded SHARE, a cooperative whose members swapped documentation and programs the vendor did not offer; the manufacturer’s own free library grew fat on this communal effort.3 In such a world the question “what kind of software is this?” had no commercial stakes, because nobody was buying or selling kinds of software. Software was something you traded with peers, not something with a market to be segmented.

The first commercial category to appear was therefore not a category of products but of services. A handful of firms — the earliest of them dating to the 1950s — made their living writing bespoke programs under contract for the few organisations that owned machines. The historian Martin Campbell-Kelly, whose business history of the industry remains the standard account, calls this the era of the software contractor, the first of three great sectors his book traces.1 Contracting was a service business with the economics of a consultancy: each program was built once, for one client, and the notion of a shrink-wrapped “product” that could be sold many times over was still to come.

The event that turned software from a giveaway into a product has a precise date, and it is worth dwelling on because so many later categories descend from it. Under mounting antitrust pressure, IBM announced in December 1968 that within six months it would begin pricing software and services separately from hardware; the United States Department of Justice filed its antitrust suit the following month, in January 1969; and on 23 June 1969 the company duly announced separately priced offerings for systems engineering, maintenance, education, custom programming, and seventeen distinct software products.4 This is the famous “unbundling.” In the same stroke IBM introduced a distinction that would echo for decades: it split its programs into System Control Programming, which remained free because the machine could not run without it, and Program Products, which customers now had to pay for.5 Here, in embryo, is the deepest fault line in every subsequent software taxonomy — the line between the software that operates the machine and the software you buy to do something with it.

A caution about origin stories

It is tempting, and common, to say that antitrust action “created” the software industry at a stroke in 1969. The reality is more tangled. Independent software products existed before 1969, and economists who later studied IBM’s records argue that the motives for unbundling were as much about the spiralling cost of supporting the System/360 operating system as about the courts, finding no direct evidence tying the announcement to the lawsuit.6 Unbundling is best read not as the big bang of the industry but as the moment a diffuse activity acquired a price, and with a price, a market — and with a market, the need for categories.

What unbundling did unambiguously accelerate was the second of Campbell-Kelly’s sectors: the corporate software product. IBM’s System/360, introduced in 1964, had become so dominant that its architecture was a de facto industry standard, which meant that for the first time a program could be written once and sold to thousands of essentially identical installations.7 A firm no longer had to choose between building bespoke systems and building nothing; it could build a package. The early landmark products — Applied Data Research’s flowcharting tool Autoflow, Informatics’ file-management system Mark IV — proved that software could be a repeatable product with the margins of manufacturing rather than the margins of consulting.1 Roughly a decade later a third sector arrived, the mass-market software product, born of the personal computer, whose shrink-wrapped spreadsheets and word processors reached a public the mainframe vendors never touched.1

Campbell-Kelly’s central insight for our purposes is that these sectors did not merely succeed one another; each was born at the precise moment when the prevailing technology created a business opportunity for a new mode of software, and each brought its own way of slicing the market. By the 1970s, industry observers were classifying software along two axes at once — by supplier (computer manufacturers, independent vendors, service bureaux) and by category, where the categories already included the seeds of the modern picture: systems software such as databases and IBM’s CICS transaction monitor; industry-specific software for banking or manufacturing; and cross-industry software such as accounting and office automation. Campbell-Kelly makes the striking observation that this “increasingly fine classification of software was itself significant” — that the proliferation of categories was not incidental bookkeeping but a sign of the market maturing.1 The taxonomy was growing because the industry was.

The people whose job is to draw the market’s categories are almost as old as the market itself.

If categories are what a maturing market produces, then a market this large will eventually support a profession devoted to producing them. That profession is the technology industry analyst, and its defining activity, in the words of one working definition, is precisely to “create segment taxonomies, size markets, prepare forecasts, and develop industry models.”8 The lineage is old. The oldest continuously operating analyst firm dates to 1959; International Data Corporation, the IDC whose taxonomy anchors the next several sections, was founded in 1964 by Patrick McGovern; the Yankee Group followed in 1970, and it was a Yankee Group alumnus, George Colony, who later founded Forrester Research.8

The most consequential firm for the vocabulary we now use was founded in 1979, when Gideon Gartner — a former IBM man and Wall Street technology analyst — left the securities world to sell independent research to the buyers and sellers of computers.9 Gartner’s methods institutionalised the category as a commercial object. His analysts were trained to distil complex markets into brief, disciplined research notes; the firm’s early notes were famously held to a single page or two; and in the early 1980s Gartner analysts devised the graphical vendor-ranking grid, the Magic Quadrant, that would become the standard instrument for defining a market and appointing its leaders.9, 10 It is not an accident that Gartner’s whole enterprise grew out of IBM’s dominance of the applications-software market: the founder’s advantage was insider knowledge of how one giant’s products related to its rivals’, and the business he built was, at bottom, a business of classifying and comparing.11 The categories of software, in other words, were not handed down by engineers; they were, to a remarkable degree, manufactured by the analysts whose living depended on selling them.

03The foundational axis

System software, application software, and the thing between them

Before any market analyst drew a market, engineers had already drawn the most durable line in software, and it has only two boxes. On one side is system software, defined in the standard pedagogy as software designed to operate and control the computer hardware and to provide a platform on which other software can run. On the other is application software, defined by contrast as software that causes the computer to perform useful tasks beyond the mere running of the computer itself. The relationship is a chain of service: the system software serves the application, and the application serves the user.12 This is the distinction a student meets in the first week of a computing course, and it is the same distinction IBM stumbled toward in 1969 when it separated the System Control Programming the machine could not live without from the Program Products a customer chose to buy.

The canonical members of each box are familiar. System software includes the operating system — z/OS, Windows, macOS, Linux — which lets the parts of a machine work together and offers a platform for everything above it, together with the device drivers that let the operating system talk to hardware and the utilities that maintain and optimise the machine. Application software includes the accounting package, the word processor, the media player, the browser, and the enterprise suite: everything a person actually opens in order to get something done. Drawn this way, the model is clean, memorable, and, for teaching how a computer is organised, entirely adequate.

It is also, as a taxonomy of the software market, quietly broken, because a great deal of the most valuable software in the world does not sit comfortably in either box. Consider a relational database, a message queue, an application server, or the toolchain a programmer uses to build and ship code. None of these operates the hardware, so they are not obviously system software. None of them is opened by an end user to do a job, so they are not obviously application software. They are a third thing, and the industry has a name for it: middleware, memorably described as “software glue” — software that provides services to applications beyond those the operating system offers, so that developers can get on with the specific purpose of their program instead of reinventing communication, storage, and coordination each time.12 Middleware is the ghost in the two-box model. It is enormous, it is lucrative, and the classical distinction has nowhere to put it.

The usual repair is to stop thinking in boxes and start thinking in layers — a stack, with the hardware at the bottom, the operating system just above it, a broad middle band of platforms and glue, and the applications on top. The stack is the mental model behind almost every modern picture of software, and it is worth being explicit about what changed in moving from two boxes to a layered stack: the middle was promoted. What the two-box model treated as an embarrassing remainder — the software that is neither operating system nor end-user application — becomes, in the layered view, a first-class region with its own vendors, its own buyers, and its own economics. That single move, promoting the middle, is the hinge between the engineer’s taxonomy and the market’s.

The line keeps moving

Even the two original boxes are less stable than they look. Functions migrate across the boundary as the platform absorbs them: a networking stack, a windowing system, a package manager, and increasingly a container runtime were once separate purchases and are now expected parts of an operating system. What counts as “system” software is itself a moving convention, which is a first hint that no software taxonomy can be fixed for long — a point we return to at the end.

One further caution before we build on this. The engineer’s distinction and the market’s distinction rhyme, but they are asking different questions. The engineer asks about role: does this code operate the machine, or use it? The market asks about the buyer and the job: who purchases this, and to solve what problem? The two usually agree — an operating system is system software by role and infrastructure by market — but they can come apart, and much of the difficulty in classifying a specific product comes from letting the two questions blur into one. Keep them separate, and the layered market taxonomy we turn to now becomes far easier to read.

04The industry-standard scheme

Three layers: applications, development and deployment, infrastructure

The taxonomy that governs how the software market is sized and discussed is IDC’s, and it is built exactly as the previous section anticipated: it takes the engineer’s two boxes and promotes the middle. It is a strict hierarchy. In its 2025 edition, IDC’s worldwide software taxonomy resolves the entire market into seventy-nine individual functional markets, grouped within twenty secondary markets, which in turn aggregate into three primary segments: applications, application development and deployment, and systems infrastructure software.2 Those three primary segments are the load-bearing categories of the whole industry, and each corresponds to one band of the stack.

The genius of the three-segment scheme is that it answers a different question at each layer. Applications answer “what does the organisation actually do?” Application development and deployment — universally abbreviated AD&D — answers “what do we build and run those applications on?” And systems infrastructure software answers “what keeps the machine underneath alive, managed, and secure?” AD&D is precisely the promoted middle: it is where the databases, application platforms, integration tools, and developer toolchains that the two-box model could not place finally get a home of their own.

Applications: software for what a business does

The applications segment is the part of the market closest to the user and to business value, and it is the layer people usually mean when they say “software” without qualification. It contains the great enterprise categories — customer relationship management, enterprise resource planning, supply-chain and procurement, human-capital management, content and collaboration, and analytics and business intelligence — alongside engineering and design tools and a long tail of industry-specific suites. The archetype of the segment is ERP, and its history is a perfect illustration of how an application category comes into being. ERP grew out of two older manufacturing ideas, material requirements planning and manufacturing resource planning, and the term “enterprise resource planning” was coined by the Gartner Group around 1990 — credited to the analyst Lee Wylie — as a criterion for how thoroughly a package integrated across the functional silos of a business.13 The name recognised that integrated back-office software was no longer just for manufacturers; it now spanned finance, sales, engineering, and human resources as a single source of truth.14 The company that would define the category, SAP, had been founded years earlier, in 1972, by five former IBM engineers in Germany, and it moved from its mainframe R/2 system in the 1980s to the client-server R/3 in 1992, riding the wave as firms retired their in-house legacy systems.15 The category did not stop evolving once named: Gartner rechristened its internet-enabled successor “ERP II” in 2000 and, in 2013, described a looser, best-of-breed “postmodern ERP” assembled from interchangeable parts.16 An application category, in short, is a name an analyst gives to a bundle of business functions at the moment the market is ready to buy them together.

Application development and deployment: software for building software

The AD&D segment is the workshop of the industry — the tools, platforms, and data-management systems on which applications are constructed and operated. IDC’s own breakdown of this segment reads like a tour of the promoted middle: development languages, environments, and tools; software quality and life-cycle tooling; application platforms and application servers, including cloud deployment platforms and transaction-processing monitors; business-rules management; modelling and architecture tools; and robotic process automation.17 Beneath and beside these sit databases and data management, the segment’s largest and oldest neighbourhood. It is no coincidence that this is where the deepest computer science in the commercial stack lives: the entire database market descends from a single 1970 paper in which E. F. Codd proposed a relational model of data grounded in set theory and predicate logic, an idea abstract enough to seem academic and practical enough to become a multi-billion-dollar market.18 Where the applications segment is defined by business function, AD&D is defined by technical capability — it is bought by the people who build and run systems rather than by the people who use them.

Systems infrastructure software: software for the machine itself

The systems infrastructure segment is the bedrock: the software that manages, provisions, and secures the computing environment regardless of what runs on top of it. It comprises the operating systems, the virtualisation and container platforms, storage and data-protection software, the whole of the security-software market, and the system, network, and IT-operations management tools that keep large estates of machines running — the region IDC labels system and service management, IT operations management, and IT automation and configuration management.17 This is the layer that most closely matches the engineer’s original “system software” box, now expanded to include the sprawling security and operations markets that barely existed when the two-box model was drawn. Its buyer is neither the business user nor the application developer but the operator — the administrator responsible for keeping the lights on.

A market taxonomy earns its keep not by being true but by summing correctly.

Two features of the scheme are easy to miss and important to grasp. The first is the discipline of the hierarchy: because the seventy-nine functional markets are meant to be mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive, every product is supposed to land in exactly one, so that the pieces add up to a market total with nothing double-counted — the property that makes the taxonomy usable for forecasting at all. The second is the escape valve. A rigid partition cannot follow a theme like “artificial intelligence” or “cybersecurity” that cuts across many functional markets at once, so IDC layers a second construct on top: competitive markets, which are combinations of whole or partial functional markets assembled to reflect a particular problem being solved or a particular technology, rather than a fixed slot in the tree.17 This is how the taxonomy has it both ways — a clean, additive base partition for sizing, and flexible overlays for the cross-cutting themes that would otherwise shatter it. It also quietly concedes the point of this entire essay: that a single layered picture is never enough, and that the categories which matter to strategy often run across the ones that matter to accounting.

05What the layers leave out

Three axes that cut across the stack

The three-layer taxonomy is powerful, but it is a single dimension — a vertical one — and it silently drops information that buyers and vendors care about intensely. Two products can sit in the very same functional market and still be radically different animals depending on who they are built for, how they are delivered, and how they are licensed. These are not sub-categories within the layer scheme; they are independent axes that run across all of it. A serious description of a piece of software is therefore less like a slot in a tree and more like a set of coordinates in a space with several dimensions. Three of those dimensions matter enough to treat in turn.

Horizontal versus vertical: the breadth of the market served

The oldest of the cross-cutting distinctions divides software by the shape of its audience. Horizontal software addresses a business function common to every industry — accounting, customer management, analytics, security — and is sold to banks and bakeries alike. Vertical software addresses one industry in depth, encoding the workflows, regulations, and vocabulary of a single sector. The distinction is not new; by the 1970s observers already separated cross-industry software such as accounting and office automation from industry-specific software for banking or manufacturing, and even then the proliferation of these distinctions was read as a sign of a maturing market.1 It survives in the way software firms describe themselves today: the national statistical definition of a software publisher notes explicitly that publishers segment their market both by industry vertical — healthcare, finance, manufacturing — and by the size of the organisation served.19

The horizontal/vertical axis interacts with the layer stack in an instructive way. It bites hardest in the applications segment, where a general-purpose CRM competes against a wealth-management CRM built only for private banks, or a horizontal ledger competes against a hospital revenue-cycle system. But it is not confined there. Verticalisation reaches down into infrastructure whenever an industry’s constraints are severe enough — operational-technology security for factory floors, for instance, is systems-infrastructure software specialised for one vertical. The lesson is that “which layer?” and “how broad an audience?” are separate questions, and a product’s market is only pinned down once both are answered.

Delivery model: where the software runs and how it is paid for

The second axis is the one that has reshaped the industry most violently in the past two decades: the model by which software is delivered and consumed. For most of the history sketched earlier, software was something you acquired and ran on your own machines — on-premises, under a perpetual licence. The alternative, delivering software as a metered service over the network, was given its authoritative definition by the United States National Institute of Standards and Technology, whose model describes cloud computing in terms of five essential characteristics, four deployment models — private, community, public, and hybrid — and, most relevant here, three service models: software as a service, platform as a service, and infrastructure as a service.20 These are usually abbreviated SaaS, PaaS, and IaaS, and their names are a near-perfect echo of the three market layers.

The echo is not a coincidence, and the reason is illuminating. NIST’s own guidance for telling the three service models apart says they are best distinguished by two things: the computing capability being provided, and the primary customer — an end user, a developer or deployer, or an IT-operations team.21 That is exactly the customer distinction that separates IDC’s three segments: applications for the end user, application development and deployment for the developer, systems infrastructure for the operator. The market taxonomy and the cloud taxonomy converge because both are ultimately organised around the same three kinds of buyer.

Two warnings keep this correspondence honest. First, NIST is careful to say the service models are not a simple three-layer cake stacked neatly on top of one another; it draws them as interlocking “L-shaped” regions, because a service in one model can be built and consumed independently of the others rather than always depending on the layer below.21 Second, and more importantly, the delivery model is orthogonal to function. The very same customer-relationship-management application can be shipped as an on-premises package or rented as SaaS; the choice changes the business model, the buyer’s risk, and the vendor’s economics, but it does not change which functional market the software is in. That software delivery had become an axis in its own right was the great insight of Salesforce, which launched in 1999 with a browser-delivered, subscription CRM and a deliberately provocative “No Software” campaign, and in doing so turned how software reaches you into a competitive battleground independent of what the software does.22

Source model: proprietary versus open

The third axis concerns not what software does or how it is delivered but the terms on which its source code may be used. Here the pivotal history is the free-software movement. In September 1983 Richard Stallman announced the GNU Project, an effort to build a complete operating system that users would be free to run, study, modify, and share; he founded the Free Software Foundation in 1985 and enforced those freedoms through the copyleft mechanism of the GNU General Public Licence.23 The missing piece, a kernel, arrived when Linus Torvalds released Linux in 1991 and placed it under the same licence in 1992, completing a wholly free operating system. Late in the decade, at a 1998 strategy meeting in Palo Alto, the movement was rebranded — Christine Peterson proposed the term “open source” to make the idea more palatable to business, a shift catalysed by Netscape’s decision to release its browser’s code.23

What makes the source model a true axis rather than a category is that it is independent of everything else. Open-source software spans all three layers of the stack at once: Linux is systems infrastructure, PostgreSQL is application development and deployment, and there are open-source applications in nearly every functional market. It is equally independent of delivery: open-source code can be run on-premises or offered, by its author or by a third party, as a hosted service — which is exactly how much of today’s SaaS is built. And the once-clean line between proprietary and open has itself blurred into a spectrum of commercial-open-source and source-available arrangements. A product’s licence, in other words, is a coordinate you must read off separately; knowing that something is “a database” tells you nothing about whether it is open, and knowing that it is open tells you nothing about which layer it occupies.

A software product is not a point on a line. It is a set of coordinates in a space.

Step back and the shape of the problem is clear. “A typology of software by market segment” is really the projection of a multi-dimensional object onto a single axis — the vertical, layer axis — because that axis is the most useful one for sizing the market and organising a conversation. But the same product also has a position on the horizontal/vertical axis, a position on the delivery axis, and a position on the source axis, and those positions are largely independent of one another. To locate a product fully is to give all four coordinates: a vertical, on-premises, proprietary application is a different market proposition from a horizontal, SaaS, open-core platform, even if a crude single-axis taxonomy would file them both under “software.” The layered market segment is the headline, but it is never the whole address.

06Statistics and the state

How governments classify software — and why the tent leaks

The analyst taxonomy is optimised for timeliness: it changes every year, splitting and merging markets as fast as the industry moves. Governments need the opposite. To compare an industry across years and across countries, a statistical agency needs categories that hold still, defined by the durable characteristics of a business rather than the shifting fashions of its products. In North America that scheme is the North American Industry Classification System, and software has a slot in it: Software Publishers. The category covers establishments primarily engaged in publishing software — designing, documenting, installing, and supporting it, and distributing it by subscription or download — and it sits inside the broad Information sector rather than anywhere near manufacturing.19

What is most revealing about the official code is not what it includes but what it deliberately pushes out. Reselling someone else’s packaged software is classified as wholesale or retail trade, not publishing. Designing custom software to meet the needs of one specific client is classified separately, as custom computer programming services. Mass-duplicating software is a manufacturing activity. And — the exclusion that matters most in a cloud era — providing customers with access to software published by others from a central host site is classified not as software publishing at all but under data processing, hosting, and related services.19 The statistical system, in other words, was built around the shipped package, and it treats the act of hosting software as a fundamentally different business from the act of publishing it.

A category out of step with its object

The consequence is a genuine seam between the two great classification systems. To an analyst, a pure-SaaS customer-relationship-management vendor is unambiguously in the applications market. To the letter of the statistical code, that same vendor — delivering software it wrote, but as a hosted service — can look less like a software publisher and more like a data-processing and hosting company. This is one reason cloud revenue is so hard to reconcile across sources, and a vivid demonstration of the essay’s theme: the categories are instruments built for a purpose, and when the object moves faster than the instrument, the instrument mismeasures.

The divergence is not a mistake by either side; it is the predictable result of optimising for different purposes. The analyst scheme is function-first: it asks what problem a product solves, so that buyers can compare substitutes and markets can be sized this quarter. The statistical scheme is activity-first: it asks what an establishment principally does, so that economic output can be compared across decades. A function-first scheme must change constantly to stay accurate; an activity-first scheme must change rarely to stay comparable. Each is correct on its own terms, and the two will never perfectly align, because timeliness and comparability are, past a point, in direct tension. The same logic governs the international counterpart used for cross-country statistics, which likewise trades granularity and currency for the stability that makes national economies commensurable at all.

The practical upshot for anyone reading a number about the software industry is to ask which classification produced it. A figure for “the applications software market” from an analyst firm and a figure for “software publishers” from a statistical agency are not two measurements of the same thing; they are measurements of two differently drawn things that happen to share a name. Neither is wrong. But they answer to different masters, and treating them as interchangeable is the single most common error in discussions of the size and shape of the software business.

07The instrument and the terrain

Why every software taxonomy is provisional

Everything so far points to a single conclusion: a software taxonomy is a lens, not a fact, and lenses are ground for particular eyes and particular tasks. This is not a counsel of despair — the lenses are genuinely useful — but it does mean that any scheme should be read with an awareness of its limits, of which there are three worth naming explicitly.

The first is convergence. Categories that are cleanly separate on paper tend to merge in the market, because customers prefer to buy fewer, broader things. Enterprise resource planning began as manufacturing software and steadily swallowed finance, human resources, supply chain, and customer management; the same integrative pressure that first justified the ERP label eventually turned it into a suite whose edges overlap half a dozen other functional markets, which is precisely why the category has had to be renamed and re-theorised more than once, from ERP to ERP II to the loosely coupled “postmodern” assemblage of interchangeable parts.16 Convergence strains the mutually-exclusive ideal at exactly the seams where the biggest vendors operate: a modern hyperscale cloud provider sells across all three layers at once — infrastructure, development platforms, and finished applications — so that the single most important companies in the industry can no longer be located in a single segment at all. The partition still works product by product, but it frays at the level of vendors and platforms, which is where strategy actually happens.

The second limit is the deepest, and it is the reason a software taxonomy can never be a neutral map. Categories are performative: naming one helps bring it into being. When an analyst firm coins “enterprise resource planning” or the industry rallies around “software as a service,” the name does not merely label a market that already existed — it tells vendors what to build toward and buyers what to demand, and the market reshapes itself to fit. The instrument most associated with this effect is the vendor-ranking grid, which does not only report on a market’s leaders but helps decide who they will be, since a position on the grid moves procurement shortlists and therefore revenue. Scholars who have studied the analyst industry closely argue that these firms do not just observe the technology sector but actively help shape its future.11 A taxonomy of software, then, is caught in a feedback loop with its object: the categories describe the market, the market rearranges itself around the categories, and the next revision of the taxonomy describes a market its predecessor helped create.

The third limit is simply time. Because software will not stop being invented and delivery models will not stop shifting, any partition is a snapshot of a moving target — which is why the analyst taxonomy is republished every year, why the statistical codes are periodically renumbered, and why even the bedrock distinction between system and application software keeps drifting as platforms absorb what were once separate products. A taxonomy is best understood as accurate as of a date, and stale by a schedule.

None of this makes the categories useless; it makes them tools to be handled with judgement. A short set of questions is enough to read almost any software taxonomy well:

  • Whose purpose is it built for? Sizing, procurement, strategy, statistics, and teaching pull toward different schemes; a scheme is only “wrong” relative to a purpose it was never meant to serve.
  • What is the base unit? Products, vendors, revenue, and establishments are counted differently, and a total means little until you know what is being totalled.
  • What does it double-count or exclude? Every partition has an escape valve for cross-cutting themes and a set of quiet exclusions; find them, because that is where the number is soft.
  • Which axis is it projecting? Layer, horizontal/vertical, delivery, and source are independent; a single-axis label is a headline, not a full address.
  • How old is it, and who benefits from its shape? A taxonomy is accurate as of a date and is rarely disinterested in who its categories crown.

Ask those five questions and the categories stop being a source of false confidence and become what they always were: useful, contestable, and provisional instruments for talking about an object that has no natural kinds of its own.

08Coda

The map that redraws the land

We began with an industry that, at its birth, had no categories, because it had no market: in the 1950s software was swapped between peers, not sold, and there was nothing to segment. It took a price — supplied, almost by accident, by IBM’s unbundling of software from hardware in 1969 — to turn a diffuse activity into a market, and a market, once it exists, cannot be discussed without categories. Everything after that is the story of an industry inventing the kinds it needed: the engineer’s two boxes of system and application software; the analyst’s three layers, which rescued the awkward middle by promoting it into a segment of its own; the cross-cutting axes of breadth, delivery, and source that no single layered picture can hold; and the slow statistical codes that trade currency for the stability nations need to count themselves.

The typology of software by market segment — the layered stack of applications, application development and deployment, and systems infrastructure, answering respectively to the user, the developer, and the operator — is the most useful single lens we have, which is why it anchors this guide. But it is one projection of an object with several dimensions, drawn by a profession whose business is drawing it, and it acts on the market even as it measures it. That is not a weakness peculiar to software classification; it is what happens whenever you try to impose discrete categories on a continuous, invented, and rapidly moving thing made of pure thought. The categories are real in the only sense that matters — people build companies, sign contracts, and allocate billions on the strength of them — but they are real the way property lines are real, not the way rivers are.

So the next time you meet a confident diagram sorting software into neat, exhaustive boxes, treat it as a surveyor’s grid rather than a photograph: an instrument that is genuinely useful for finding your way, that was drawn by someone for a reason, and that, in the act of marking where the lines fall, quietly helps decide where they will run. Read it that way and it will serve you well. Mistake it for the territory, and it will lead you exactly as far astray as any map that has forgotten it is a map.
