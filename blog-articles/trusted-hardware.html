Abstract. The concept of trust in computing systems has evolved from a purely software-defined property into one that is increasingly anchored in dedicated silicon. Modern threat models assume that operating system kernels, hypervisors, firmware, and even cloud infrastructure operators cannot be unconditionally trusted — yet computation must still proceed, often over highly sensitive data. Trusted hardware technologies address this challenge by establishing hardware-rooted trust anchors, enforced isolation boundaries, and cryptographically verifiable claims about platform state. This article surveys the principal technologies in this domain — from the Trusted Platform Module's measurement and attestation model, through Intel SGX's enclave paradigm, AMD's confidential VM stack, ARM TrustZone's secure/normal world split, and the hardened key custody provided by Hardware Security Modules — before examining the rich literature of microarchitectural attacks that reveals the limits of these guarantees, and concluding with the emerging confidential computing ecosystem in public cloud environments.
01

Historical Context: The Problem of Trust in Computing

The question of what it means to trust a computing system long predates the silicon solutions we examine today. The seminal 1975 paper by Saltzer and Schroeder on information protection in computer systems articulated a set of design principles — least privilege, complete mediation, separation of privilege, and economy of mechanism — that remain foundational to trusted systems design decades later. The Trusted Computing Base (TCB), a term crystallised during the U.S. Department of Defense's work on the Orange Book (DoD 5200.28-STD, 1985), described the set of all hardware, firmware, and software components whose correct functioning is necessary and sufficient to enforce a security policy. A central, enduring observation from that era was that the TCB had to be as small as possible: every component included is a potential point of failure or subversion.

Yet the personal computing revolution of the 1980s and 1990s built systems with essentially no hardware trust anchor whatsoever. A BIOS could be replaced, an OS kernel subverted by a rootkit, and an application binary swapped for a Trojan — and the resulting machine would happily execute malicious code with full privilege, with no means for a remote party to distinguish it from a legitimate system. Boot-time rootkits, firmware implants, and supply-chain attacks against hardware became serious threats. The problem was not merely theoretical: by the late 1990s, sophisticated adversaries were demonstrably capable of infecting systems below the OS level where conventional antivirus and integrity checkers were blind.

The response came from an industry consortium. In 1999, the Trusted Computing Platform Alliance (TCPA) was formed, bringing together Compaq, HP, IBM, Intel, and Microsoft, with the explicit goal of embedding a hardware root of trust into every personal computer.[2] TCPA's core insight was that a small, tamper-resistant co-processor — soldered to the motherboard, possessing its own cryptographic key pair, and incapable of being reset or reflashed by software — could serve as a bootstrap for measured and verified system state. The TCPA published its Main Specification Version 1.1b in 2001, defining what would become the Trusted Platform Module.

In 2003, the TCPA reorganised and expanded into the Trusted Computing Group (TCG), a non-profit consortium that continues to maintain TPM specifications alongside complementary specifications for mobile, network, and storage devices. The TCG's influence extended beyond the TPM itself: its PC Client Platform Firmware Profile specification defines how UEFI firmware should measure boot components, its Software Stack (TSS) defines how software interfaces with the TPM, and its Trusted Network Connect work extended the measurement model into network access control.

Concurrently, academic work was advancing the theoretical foundations. Arbaugh, Farber, and Smith published their influential 1997 paper on a secure and reliable bootstrap architecture, formalising the notion of a chain of trust that begins in read-only silicon and extends upward through successive software layers — each layer verifying the integrity of the next before transferring control.[4] This chain-of-trust model underpins virtually every trusted hardware design discussed in this article.

The 2000s saw parallel developments in the mobile and embedded space. ARM introduced its TrustZone technology in the ARMv6 architecture around 2004, providing hardware isolation between a rich operating environment and a secure enclave directly within the main application processor — no separate co-processor required. This approach has since dominated mobile device security, enabling features from secure boot and DRM key storage to biometric verification and mobile payments. Meanwhile, the HSM market matured from bespoke military-grade appliances into commercial offerings with well-defined certifications — most notably the NIST FIPS 140 series — serving the payment industry, certificate authorities, and enterprise key management.

The 2010s brought a new threat model: the cloud. When computation migrated to shared infrastructure operated by third parties, even a fully verified OS and application could be undermined by a privileged hypervisor or a malicious cloud provider with physical access to the host. Meeting this threat required hardware isolation mechanisms that could function even when the hypervisor itself was considered adversarial — exactly the capability that Intel SGX, AMD SEV, and eventually Intel TDX were designed to provide.

Trusted Platform Module: Architecture and Cryptographic Services

The Trusted Platform Module is a standardised cryptographic co-processor that serves as a hardware root of trust for measurement, storage, and reporting. At the time of writing the dominant version is TPM 2.0, defined by the TCG's multi-part TPM Library Specification.[1] Understanding the architecture requires examining both the physical form factor and the abstract functional model.

Physical Form Factors

The TPM exists in several physical implementations. The traditional discrete TPM (dTPM) is a dedicated microcontroller soldered to the motherboard's LPC, SPI, or I²C bus, communicating with the host via a simple command-response protocol. It possesses its own CPU, ROM for the firmware, NVRAM for persistent storage, and a hardware random number generator. Vendors include Infineon (SLB96xx series), STMicroelectronics (ST33), Microchip (ATTPM20), and Nuvoton.

Intel's Platform Trust Technology (PTT) and AMD's firmware TPM (fTPM) implement the TPM 2.0 interface within the management firmware — the Intel Management Engine (ME) or AMD Platform Security Processor (PSP) respectively — without a discrete chip. This reduces BOM cost but narrows the trust boundary: the firmware TPM shares silicon with more complex code, and its isolation guarantees are weaker than a discrete device.

TPM 1.2 vs. TPM 2.0

TPM 1.2 (finalized 2003, widely deployed from ~2006) had several design limitations that prompted a complete redesign.[3] Algorithm support was fixed: SHA-1 for measurement, RSA-2048 for asymmetric operations, and AES-128 for symmetric operations. As SHA-1 weakened, this became an architectural liability. Key management used a single Storage Root Key (SRK) rooted in a hierarchical storage tree, with an Endorsement Key (EK) — a device-unique RSA key pair generated at manufacture — serving as the attestation anchor. The Authorization protocol was based on HMAC-SHA-1 sessions (OIAP, OSAP). Platform Configuration Registers (PCRs) were twenty-four 160-bit SHA-1 registers.

TPM 2.0, published in 2014 and mandatory for Windows 11 systems, introduced sweeping changes.[1] Algorithm agility allows the TPM to support any combination of hash algorithms (SHA-1, SHA-256, SHA-384, SHA-512, SM3) and asymmetric schemes (RSA, ECC P-256, P-384, BN-256, SM2), selected at provisioning time. PCR banks exist for each supported hash algorithm, each containing 24 registers (though the number is implementation-defined; TPM2_GetCapability reveals the actual count). The key hierarchy was restructured into four independent hierarchies, each with its own primary seed stored in NVRAM:

TPM 2.0 Key Hierarchies

Endorsement Hierarchy (EH): Seeds the device's Endorsement Key pair. The EK certificate, provisioned by the manufacturer and stored in NV index 0x01C00002 (RSA) or 0x01C0000A (ECC), vouches for the device's authenticity. This hierarchy is used exclusively for privacy-preserving attestation.

Owner/Storage Hierarchy (SH): The general-purpose hierarchy, reset to a new seed when the TPM is provisioned by an owner. Used for application keys, sealed storage, and general cryptographic services.

Platform Hierarchy (PH): Controlled by the firmware; used for PCR-based measurements and sealed firmware secrets. Reset only by the TPM manufacturer.

Null Hierarchy: Ephemeral; its seed is randomised at every boot. Useful for session keys with no persistence requirement.

Platform Configuration Registers

PCRs are the central data structure for measured boot. A PCR cannot be written directly; it can only be extended with a hash value using the operation:

Sealing and Key Derivation

Sealing binds a secret — a symmetric key, a private key blob, or arbitrary data — to a specific set of PCR values and an optional policy. Only when the TPM's PCR state matches the expected values at sealing time will it release the sealed object. This is the mechanism behind BitLocker's TPM-only unlock, systemd-cryptenroll, and many enterprise disk encryption deployments.

Bash tpm2-tools — sealing a secret to PCR state 
# Create a PCR policy that requires PCR[0,1,7] to match their current values
tpm2_createpolicy \
  --policy-pcr \
  --pcr-list sha256:0,1,7 \
  --policy pcr_policy.dat

# Create a primary key in the owner hierarchy
tpm2_createprimary -C o -g sha256 -G rsa -c primary.ctx

# Seal a secret file (e.g., a LUKS key file) under that policy
tpm2_create \
  -C primary.ctx \
  -G keyedhash \
  -i /path/to/secret.key \
  -L pcr_policy.dat \
  -u sealed.pub -r sealed.priv

# Load the sealed object and unseal it (only works if PCRs match)
tpm2_load -C primary.ctx -u sealed.pub -r sealed.priv -c sealed.ctx
tpm2_unseal -c sealed.ctx -p "pcr:sha256:0,1,7"

Quoting and Attestation Keys

A TPM Quote is the mechanism by which the TPM cryptographically asserts the current values of a set of PCRs, binding them to a nonce provided by a verifier. The quote is generated using a special-purpose Attestation Key (AK) — derived under the endorsement hierarchy and certified by the manufacturer EK certificate — so that only a genuine TPM can produce a valid quote.[1]

Bash tpm2-tools — creating an AK and generating a quote 
# Create an Endorsement Key
tpm2_createek -G rsa2048 -c ek.ctx -u ek.pub

# Create an Attestation Key, certified under the EK
tpm2_createak -C ek.ctx -G rsa2048 -g sha256 -c ak.ctx -u ak.pub -n ak.name

# Generate a quote over PCRs 0-7 with a fresh verifier nonce
NONCE=$(openssl rand -hex 20)
tpm2_quote \
  -C ak.ctx \
  -l sha256:0,1,2,3,4,5,6,7 \
  -q "${NONCE}" \
  -m quote.msg \
  -s quote.sig \
  -o pcrs.dat \
  -f serialized

# Verify the quote signature against the AK public key
tpm2_checkquote \
  -u ak.pub \
  -m quote.msg \
  -s quote.sig \
  -f pcrs.dat \
  -q "${NONCE}"

The resulting quote contains a TPMS_ATTEST structure: the magic constant TPM_GENERATED_VALUE (preventing cross-protocol attacks), the qualifying data (verifier's nonce), the PCR selection list, the digest of the selected PCRs, and a timestamp. The signature over this structure, verifiable against the AK's certified public key, creates a cryptographically unforgeable assertion of platform state.

TPM Software Stack

The TCG's TPM Software Stack (TSS) 2.0 specification defines three layers of API: the Feature API (FAPI), the Enhanced System API (ESAPI), and the System API (SAPI). The reference open-source implementation, tpm2-tss, is maintained at tpm2-software/tpm2-tss on GitHub and ships in most major Linux distributions. The companion tpm2-tools package provides the command-line interface illustrated above. For production software, the C FAPI provides the highest-level abstractions, including key management policies, callbacks for authorization, and PKCS#11 integration via tpm2-pkcs11.

UEFI Secure Boot: Establishing the Boot-Time Chain of Trust

Secure Boot is the UEFI specification's mechanism for verifying the cryptographic integrity of every executable loaded during the pre-OS boot process.[6] It instantiates the chain-of-trust model described by Arbaugh et al.: if the firmware is assumed trustworthy (rooted in ROM), it can verify the bootloader, which verifies the kernel, which verifies kernel modules, and so on up the stack. Without Secure Boot, an attacker who gains temporary physical access or exploits a pre-OS vulnerability can install a bootkit — a form of persistent malware invisible to the running OS and surviving full OS reinstallation.

Key Hierarchy and Database Variables

UEFI Secure Boot uses a four-level key hierarchy stored as authenticated EFI variables in NVRAM.[7] These variables are cryptographically protected and can be updated only by entities holding the appropriate signing keys:

KEKKey Exchange KeyAuthorises updates to the db and dbx databases. On consumer hardware this typically contains both the OEM's KEK and Microsoft's KEK certificate.
dbSignature DatabaseA list of trusted certificates and hash values. Any EFI binary signed by a key in db, or matching a hash in db, is permitted to load.
dbxForbidden Signature DatabaseRevocation list. Binaries matching hashes or certificates here are blocked, even if they also appear in db. Used to revoke compromised bootloaders.

The Boot Sequence Under Secure Boot

When the firmware initialises with Secure Boot active, it reads the current PCR[7] into the TPM (recording the Secure Boot policy), then validates every EFI executable before executing it. The typical boot sequence on a Linux system with Microsoft signing is: UEFI firmware → shim (signed by Microsoft's UEFI CA) → GRUB2 (signed by the distribution's key, embedded in shim's MOK database or shim itself) → Linux kernel (signed by the distribution's key) → kernel modules (optionally signed via CONFIG_MODULE_SIG).

The shim bootloader deserves particular attention. Since Canonical, Red Hat, SUSE, and Debian are not direct signatories on the UEFI db database on consumer hardware — Microsoft controls that — they instead have Microsoft sign a small, auditable shim binary. shim contains its vendor's certificate (or more precisely, it embeds a hash or certificate that identifies the distribution's signing key), and when it executes, it validates GRUB against that embedded key before transferring control. The Machine Owner Key (MOK) database, stored in NVRAM and managed via the mokutil tool, allows system administrators to enrol additional keys after confirming them physically at a MOK manager prompt during boot.

Secure Boot and measured boot are complementary, not redundant: Secure Boot prevents malicious code from running; measured boot records what did run so that a remote verifier can detect policy violations even after the fact.

Conceptual distinction between enforcement and attestation models

Measured Boot vs. Verified Boot

A critical distinction separates verified boot (Secure Boot's model) from measured boot. Verified boot actively blocks the loading of any unverified binary — the system will refuse to boot if the chain of trust breaks. Measured boot, by contrast, is agnostic: it unconditionally records the hash of every loaded component into TPM PCRs but does not itself block execution. The policy enforcement is deferred to a remote attestation verifier that examines the PCR values after the fact. The two approaches are often combined: Secure Boot provides enforcement, while measured boot provides an audit trail for remote attestation. Microsoft's Windows Device Health Attestation service is a large-scale deployment of this combined model.

UEFI Secure Boot Vulnerabilities

Despite its design, Secure Boot has been repeatedly bypassed. A notable 2023 vulnerability, "BlackLotus" (CVE-2022-21894), exploited a known-vulnerable Windows bootloader that remained signed in the db database — demonstrating that dbx update hygiene is critical but difficult to maintain in practice. Supply-chain attacks against OEM PKs have also been documented, where private keys were inadvertently included in firmware packages posted publicly. The UEFI Secure Boot mechanism is only as strong as its revocation infrastructure and the secrecy of the OEM's PK private key.

Remote Attestation: Concepts, Protocols, and the RATS Framework

Remote attestation is the process by which a computing platform cryptographically convinces a remote party — who cannot physically inspect the system — that the platform is in a particular, expected state. It is the bridge between the local hardware measurement infrastructure described above and the distributed applications that depend on trustworthy counterparts. Coker et al. (2011) provide a rigorous formalisation of the principles underlying remote attestation, defining it in terms of evidence generation, transport, and appraisal within a trust model.[8]

The RATS Architecture (RFC 9334)

The IETF's Remote ATtestation procedureS (RATS) working group published RFC 9334 in January 2023, defining an architecture that generalises attestation across heterogeneous platforms and TEE technologies.[9] The architecture defines three primary roles:

RATS Roles and Data Types

Attester: The device or software system whose trustworthiness is to be established. It produces Claims — assertions about its state — and packages them as Evidence using a hardware-backed signing key (the Attesting Environment, typically the TPM's AK or the TEE's attestation key).

Verifier: A trusted service that appraises the Evidence against Endorsements (manufacturer-supplied facts, such as EK certificates or reference measurements) and Appraisal Policy, producing an Attestation Result — a signed statement declaring the Attester's trustworthiness and the basis for that determination.

Relying Party: The entity that uses the Attestation Result to make an access-control or trust decision — for example, a key management service that will release a secret key only to attested VMs.

Reference Values: Expected PCR values, expected firmware hashes, expected MRENCLAVE values (for SGX), etc. Published by the Reference Value Provider (hardware vendor or software vendor) and consumed by the Verifier.

Topology Models

RFC 9334 distinguishes two topology models. In the background-check model, the Attester sends Evidence directly to the Relying Party, which then forwards it to the Verifier and receives an Attestation Result before deciding. In the passport model, the Attester obtains an Attestation Result from the Verifier in advance and presents it directly to the Relying Party — analogous to presenting a pre-issued credential. The passport model decouples the Verifier from real-time interactions with the Relying Party, enabling offline or cached attestation.

Evidence Formats and Transport

The IETF has defined several interoperable evidence formats under the RATS umbrella. Entity Attestation Tokens (EAT, RFC 9444) define a CBOR- or JSON-encoded token format for conveying Claims. TCG DICE (Device Identifier Composition Engine) provides a layered certificate chain rooted in immutable silicon — useful for attestation in constrained IoT devices. For TEE platforms, Intel's SGX quote, AMD's SNP attestation report, and ARM's CCA attestation token each serve as platform-specific Evidence formats.

Transport is typically handled over TLS, with attestation flowing as part of a handshake extension (the IETF RATS WG is standardising TLS attestation handshakes) or as a HTTPS-transported JSON/CBOR payload. The VERAISON project (part of the CCC) provides an open-source Verifier and policy framework that can ingest multiple Evidence formats and issue Attestation Results in EAT format.

Privacy Considerations: EPID

A recurring tension in attestation is between verifiability and privacy. A naive attestation scheme uses the device's unique EK to sign quotes — but this allows the Verifier to track a specific physical device across attestation sessions. Intel's Enhanced Privacy ID (EPID), used in TPM 1.2's DAA (Direct Anonymous Attestation) and early SGX, addresses this through a group signature scheme in which any member of a large manufacturer-defined group can produce a valid signature, but no individual member can be identified. The Verifier learns that the hardware is authentic and part of a known production batch, but not the identity of the specific device. EPID has been largely superseded in SGX by ECDSA-based DCAP attestation, which provides pseudonymity via the Platform Certificate Key (PCK) certificate hierarchy rather than cryptographic unlinkability.

Intel SGX: Isolated Enclaves and the Attestation Model

Intel Software Guard Extensions (SGX) represent one of the most architecturally ambitious trusted hardware technologies yet deployed at scale. Introduced in Intel's Skylake generation (6th-generation Core processors) in 2015, SGX exposes new ISA instructions that allow user-mode software to create hardware-enforced isolated memory regions — enclaves — that are inaccessible to all other software on the system, including the operating system kernel, the hypervisor, SMM code, and device DMA. The design assumes that the threat model includes a compromised OS or hypervisor, and that the enclave must protect its code and data even in that context.

The foundational architecture was described by McKeen et al. at the HASP 2013 workshop,[10] and the attestation model by Anati et al. in the same venue.[13] The Intel SGX Developer Reference provides the authoritative specification.[11]

Enclave Page Cache and Memory Encryption

SGX reserves a region of physical DRAM called the Processor Reserved Memory (PRM), configured via Processor Reserved Memory Range Registers (PRMRRs). Within the PRM, the Enclave Page Cache (EPC) holds the actual enclave pages. In Skylake and Kaby Lake, the EPC is typically 128 MB as seen by the OS, though the Memory Encryption Engine (MEE) imposes overhead, leaving roughly 93 MB of usable enclave memory per platform — a severe constraint for memory-intensive workloads that shaped much subsequent research and the SGX2 extensions.

All data leaving the LLC (Last-Level Cache) and entering DRAM is transparently encrypted by the MEE using AES-128 in CTR mode, with per-cache-line integrity tags (AES-CMAC) and a Merkle tree for replay protection. The encryption key is hardware-generated and never exposed to software. This protects against cold-boot attacks on enclave memory and hardware bus sniffing.

Each EPC page is described by a corresponding entry in the Enclave Page Cache Map (EPCM) — a hardware-maintained table in protected SRAM that records the owning enclave, page type, and permissions. The EPCM enforces ownership invariants: an EPC page allocated to enclave A cannot be mapped into enclave B's address space.

Enclave Lifecycle

Enclave creation proceeds through a sequence of privileged instructions:

SGX ISA Enclave lifecycle instructions (ring 0 unless noted) 
ECREATE   – Initialises a new enclave control structure (SECS) in EPC
EADD      – Adds a page from untrusted memory into the enclave's EPC range
EEXTEND   – Extends the MRENCLAVE measurement with 256-byte page chunks
EINIT     – Finalises the enclave; verifies SIGSTRUCT signature; locks measurement

--- After EINIT, the enclave can be entered from user mode ---

EENTER    – Transitions from untrusted app into enclave (sets TCS.STATE = active)
EEXIT     – Returns from enclave to untrusted caller
ERESUME   – Resumes enclave after an asynchronous exit (e.g., interrupt, page fault)

--- Privileged management ---

EREMOVE   – Removes an EPC page and invalidates its EPCM entry
EPA       – Adds a Version Array page for SGX2 dynamic paging
EMODPR    – SGX2: modifies EPC page permissions (must be acked by enclave via EACCEPT)
EAUG      – SGX2: dynamically adds a new EPC page post-EINIT

Enclave Identity: MRENCLAVE and MRSIGNER

SGX provides two distinct identity measurements, each enabling a different trust model. MRENCLAVE (256-bit SHA-256) is computed deterministically by SHA-256ing the ECREATE, EADD, and EEXTEND operations in sequence — it is a complete cryptographic description of the enclave's code and initial data layout. No two distinct enclave binary images can share an MRENCLAVE. MRSIGNER (256-bit SHA-256) is computed from the public modulus of the RSA key used to sign the SIGSTRUCT that accompanies the enclave. It identifies the enclave's developer/vendor rather than its specific version.

This distinction matters enormously for key derivation and sealed storage. Sealing to MRENCLAVE means sealed data can only be accessed by the exact same enclave build — a hard update barrier. Sealing to MRSIGNER allows any enclave bearing the same developer signature (and an equal or greater ISV_SVN — Independent Software Vendor Security Version Number) to access the sealed data, enabling smooth update semantics. The ISV_PROD_ID and ISV_SVN fields within the SIGSTRUCT provide additional granularity for product and patch-level versioning.

Attestation: EPID (Legacy) vs. DCAP

SGX provides two attestation paths. The original EPID-based path used Intel's Internet Attestation Service (IAS) as a centralised verifier: the Quoting Enclave signs the quote using an EPID group signature, the relying party submits it to IAS over HTTPS, and IAS returns a verification report. This model requires network connectivity to Intel's infrastructure and has deprecation in progress as of 2024.

The Data Center Attestation Primitives (DCAP) framework replaces the online IAS dependency with a certificate-chain-based scheme.[12] The Quoting Enclave signs the quote using ECDSA P-256 with a Platform Certificate Key (PCK). The PCK is issued by Intel's Provisioning Certification Service (PCS) in a certificate chain: PCK → SGX Intermediate CA → SGX Root CA. Organisations running DCAP deploy a Provisioning Certificate Caching Service (PCCS) that pre-fetches PCK certificates from Intel's PCS and serves them to local attestation infrastructure — enabling air-gapped and high-volume deployments. The Quote Verification Library (QVL) and the Intel Trust Authority service provide the appraisal layer that validates the full certificate chain, checks Certificate Revocation Lists, and verifies the TCB level reported in the quote.

SGX Ecosystem: LibOS and SDK Options

Porting applications to run inside SGX enclaves is significantly complicated by the OCALL boundary: system calls must be re-implemented as calls out to the untrusted OS, increasing both complexity and attack surface. Several approaches address this:

The Intel SGX SDK provides the lowest-level toolchain, requiring developers to explicitly partition their application into trusted and untrusted components using the EDL language shown above. The Open Enclave SDK[41] provides a hardware-agnostic API layer that targets both SGX and OP-TEE. Gramine (originally Graphene-SGX)[42] implements a library OS that runs inside the enclave, providing a POSIX-compatible environment that allows largely unmodified Linux applications to run with minimal porting effort. Enarx, a CNCF project, takes a WebAssembly-based approach: the workload is compiled to Wasm and executed by a Wasm runtime inside the enclave, providing hardware-agnostic deployment across SGX and AMD SEV.

SGX2 and Intel TDX

SGX2 (available in Ice Lake and later Xeon Scalable processors) introduced Enclave Dynamic Memory Management (EDMM), allowing enclaves to dynamically request additional EPC pages at runtime and modify page permissions using the EAUG, EMODPR, and EACCEPT instruction triplet. This eliminates the static EPC sizing constraint of SGX1 and enables practical deployment of large enclave workloads. Ice Lake Xeons support EPC sizes up to approximately 512 GB, a qualitative change enabling in-enclave databases and ML inference workloads.

Intel Trust Domain Extensions (TDX), introduced in 4th-generation Xeon Scalable (Sapphire Rapids) processors, applies the TEE paradigm at the VM granularity rather than the process level.[40] A Trust Domain (TD) is a hardware-isolated VM whose memory is encrypted and integrity-protected by the new Multi-Key Total Memory Encryption (MKTME) engine, and whose state is verifiable by remote attestation using ECDSA quotes similar to SGX DCAP. TDX allows confidential VMs without requiring application code modification, positioning it as the cloud-native successor to SGX for workloads that cannot easily be refactored into the enclave model.

AMD SEV, SEV-ES, and SEV-SNP: Confidential VM Memory Protection

AMD's approach to confidential computing targets the VM granularity — protecting entire virtual machines from an untrusted hypervisor — rather than the process-level enclave model of SGX. The SEV family comprises three generations of successively stronger guarantees, each addressing limitations exposed in the previous version. The foundational architecture was described by Kaplan, Powell, and Woller in AMD's 2016 white paper.[14]

SEV: Memory Encryption per VM

Secure Encrypted Virtualization (SEV), introduced with EPYC Naples (first generation) in 2017, provides per-VM AES-128 memory encryption. The AMD Platform Security Processor (PSP) — an ARM Cortex-A5 embedded in the CPU die, running AMD-signed firmware — generates and manages a unique Key Encryption Key (VEK) for each VM. Each VM is assigned an Address Space Identifier (ASID) tag, and the memory controller routes encryption through the appropriate key based on the ASID accompanying each cache-line transaction. The VEK is generated by the PSP in response to a LAUNCH_START command from the hypervisor; it never leaves the PSP or the memory controller hardware path.

The encryption uses AES-128 in XEX (XOR-Encrypt-XOR) mode, with the physical address serving as the tweak. This ensures that identical plaintext stored at different physical addresses produces different ciphertext, thwarting aliasing attacks. The hypervisor can read the encrypted memory but sees only ciphertext, preventing data exfiltration via memory access.

The limitation of basic SEV is significant: while memory contents are encrypted, the VM's register state (general-purpose registers, control registers, SIMD state) is visible to the hypervisor in the VM Save Area (VMSA) during VMEXIT. An attacker with hypervisor access can observe or modify register values, intercept system call arguments, and inject values into computation, severely limiting the security guarantee.

SEV-ES: Encrypted Register State

SEV Encrypted State (SEV-ES), available since EPYC Rome (2nd generation, 2019), extends the encryption to cover the VMSA — the CPU state saved during VMEXIT transitions. The VMSA is encrypted with the VM's VEK, preventing the hypervisor from reading or modifying register contents on VM exits. This closes the register-state exposure window.

However, SEV-ES introduces a new challenge: the hypervisor must still communicate with the guest to handle VMEXITs (e.g., for I/O, hypercalls, CPUID emulation). The Guest-Hypervisor Communication Block (GHCB) protocol[16] provides a structured, intentional interface for this. The guest explicitly marks which information it is willing to share with the hypervisor by placing it in the GHCB — a shared unencrypted page. A VMMCALL or VMGEXIT instruction triggers the VMEXIT, and the hypervisor reads only the GHCB fields the guest has explicitly populated. This preserves confidentiality while preserving functionality.

SEV-SNP: Integrity Protection and Attestation

SEV Secure Nested Paging (SEV-SNP), available since EPYC Milan (3rd generation, 2021), adds memory integrity protection — the critical missing guarantee in SEV and SEV-ES. AMD's 2020 whitepaper on SEV-SNP[15] describes the primary threat addressed: a malicious hypervisor could, in SEV and SEV-ES, remap guest physical pages to point at attacker-controlled physical addresses (a replay or aliasing attack), or could modify guest page table entries to redirect memory accesses — none of which would be detectable from within the encrypted memory itself.

SEV-SNP addresses this by introducing the Reverse Map Table (RMP), a hardware data structure that maps every 4 KB physical page of system DRAM to a single owning entity (hypervisor or a specific ASID-identified guest). The RMP is maintained in system memory and protected by hardware — only the PSP can assign pages, and the memory controller enforces ownership by checking every guest page access against the RMP. An attempt by the hypervisor to map a guest-assigned page into another VM or its own address space is blocked at the hardware level.

Two new instructions support the RMP model. The hypervisor uses RMPUPDATE to transfer page ownership to a guest; the guest uses PVALIDATE to confirm the assignment, preventing the hypervisor from surreptitiously swapping pages after validation. A page is either validated (guest-owned, RMP entry consistent) or not; the guest's page fault handler aborts on any inconsistency.

SEV-SNP provides a hardware-generated attestation report, requested by the guest via the SNP_GUEST_REQUEST MSR protocol (or via the SEV guest driver in Linux). The PSP generates and signs the report using ECDSA P-384. The report structure includes:

VM Privilege Levels (VMPL)

SEV-SNP introduces a four-level VMPL hierarchy within a guest. VMPL0 (highest privilege) can manage page permissions for lower VMPLs via the RMPADJUST instruction. This enables a trusted guest kernel or a thin firmware layer (e.g., COCONUT-SVSM, an AMD-endorsed framework for guest-level security services) to run at VMPL0 while the main guest OS runs at VMPL1, providing an additional isolation layer within the confidential VM.

AMD SEV-SNP's Reverse Map Table enforces a key invariant: every physical page has exactly one owner. This hardware primitive eliminates an entire class of hypervisor-based aliasing and remapping attacks that were invisible to the encrypted VM.

ARM TrustZone: Secure and Normal World Isolation

ARM TrustZone is a system-wide security architecture integrated into the ARM processor core and AMBA AXI bus fabric, providing a hardware-enforced partitioning of the system into two execution environments: the Normal World (also called the Rich Execution Environment, REE) where the general-purpose OS and applications run, and the Secure World (the Trusted Execution Environment, TEE) where trusted applications execute with protected memory, peripherals, and storage.[17]

Hardware Mechanism: The NS Bit

The fundamental mechanism is elegantly simple. A single Non-Secure (NS) bit accompanies every memory transaction on the AXI bus. When the processor executes in Non-Secure state, it asserts NS=1 on all memory and peripheral accesses; when executing in Secure state, NS=0. Memory controllers, peripheral buses (TZPC — TrustZone Protection Controller), and SRAM controllers are configured to honour this bit — Secure-tagged regions reject NS=1 accesses at the hardware level, making them invisible to Normal World software regardless of privilege level. A Normal World kernel running at EL1 in AArch64 cannot read or write a Secure-tagged SRAM bank, even with root privileges, because the memory controller will respond with a bus error.

The ARM exception level model in AArch64 maps cleanly to the TrustZone model:[18]

EL0Normal user applicationsTrusted Applications (TAs)

EL2Hypervisor (KVM, Xen)Secure hypervisor (S-EL2, AArch64 only)

World Transitions and the Secure Monitor

Transitions between Normal and Secure World are controlled exclusively by EL3, the Secure Monitor layer. The SMC (Secure Monitor Call) instruction, executed in the Normal World at any privilege level, causes a trap to EL3. The Secure Monitor inspects the arguments, dispatches to the appropriate handler, and may then switch the processor to Secure World (S-EL1) for TEE code execution, before returning control to Normal World upon TEE completion. The ARM Trusted Firmware-A (TF-A) project provides the open-source reference implementation of the Secure Monitor for Cortex-A processors, implementing the Power State Coordination Interface (PSCI) for CPU power management and the SMC Calling Convention (SMCCC) for dispatching calls between layers.

OP-TEE: Open-Source TEE Implementation

OP-TEE (Open Portable Trusted Execution Environment) is the most widely used open-source TEE implementation for TrustZone-enabled platforms.[19] Maintained by the Linaro Community Projects Division, it implements the GlobalPlatform TEE specifications[20] and runs at S-EL1 on ARM processors. OP-TEE supports a broad range of SoCs (i.MX, Raspberry Pi, QEMU for development), provides a hardened memory management unit configuration for Secure World, and implements the TEE supplicant daemon (tee-supplicant) in Normal World Linux for storage and cryptographic service delegation.

GlobalPlatform TEE API

The GlobalPlatform consortium publishes the TEE System Architecture[20] and TEE Internal Core API[21] specifications that define the portable programming interface for Trusted Applications. The Internal Core API covers cryptographic operations (AES, RSA, ECDSA, SHA family — all exposed through a unified TEE_Crypto* interface), secure storage (TEE_CreatePersistentObject, keyed and integrity-protected by the TEE), time and communication primitives, and panic handling. The Client API is the corresponding interface for Normal World Client Applications (CAs) to open sessions to and invoke commands in TAs.

Deployment: Mobile and Embedded Use Cases

TrustZone is essentially ubiquitous in modern ARM-based mobile devices. Every flagship Android and iOS smartphone contains TrustZone, used for: biometric sensor data processing (fingerprint, face recognition — the raw sensor data never leaves the Secure World); mobile payment credentials (Android Protected Confirmation, Apple Secure Enclave — though the latter is a discrete chip on Apple silicon, not a TrustZone TA); eSIM provisioning and remote SIM management; Digital Rights Management (DRM) key storage for Widevine L1 (highest quality video streaming); and TLS private key storage for enterprise applications.

TrustZone-M, introduced in the ARMv8-M architecture for Cortex-M microcontrollers, brings the security model to deeply embedded and IoT devices. Unlike Cortex-A TrustZone which uses the Secure Monitor as a context switch mechanism, Cortex-M TrustZone-M uses a simpler state-machine model with SG (Secure Gateway) and BXNS/BLXNS instructions for efficient Secure/Non-Secure world transitions without the overhead of an EL3 monitor.

Limitations and Attack Surface

TrustZone's threat model has well-known limitations. The Secure World is trusted by definition within the hardware model, but the software in that world — the TEE OS and all TAs — expands the TCB substantially. A vulnerability in the TEE kernel (OP-TEE, Qualcomm QTEE, etc.) or in any signed TA can compromise the security guarantees for all other TAs. In contrast to SGX, where each enclave is isolated from all other enclaves, in a TrustZone system all TAs share the TEE's single address space partition; a memory safety bug in one TA can affect others. Academic researchers have demonstrated repeated privilege-escalation vulnerabilities in commercial TEE implementations, including Qualcomm's QTEE (which manages SoC fuse data and secure boot keys).

Hardware Security Modules: Certified Key Custody

A Hardware Security Module (HSM) is a purpose-built cryptographic appliance that provides a physically hardened, tamper-detecting environment for generating, storing, and operating on cryptographic keys. Unlike TPMs (which are embedded co-processors serving a single host) or TEEs (which provide software isolation within a general-purpose processor), HSMs are dedicated systems with their own processors, RNG hardware, and independent power supplies, designed to resist sophisticated physical attacks including microprobing, power analysis, and invasive disassembly.

FIPS 140 Certification Levels

The dominant certification framework for HSMs is FIPS 140, published by NIST. FIPS 140-2 (published 2001) defines four security levels,[23] and FIPS 140-3 (adopted 2019, based on ISO/IEC 19790:2012) refines and extends this framework.[22]

LevelPhysical RequirementsRepresentative Use
Level 1 No physical security beyond production-grade components. Software-only implementations qualify. Software crypto libraries, developer test environments
Level 2 Tamper-evident seals or coatings; role-based authentication required (not operator-specific). Low-assurance enterprise HSMs, USB crypto tokens
Level 3 Tamper-resistant enclosure; physical intrusion attempts must be detected and cause key zeroisation. Identity-based authentication. Private key export prohibited in plaintext. Enterprise CAs, payment HSMs, cloud HSM services
Level 4 Complete envelope of physical protection; environmental attack protection (temperature, voltage, EM). Active tamper response with immediate zeroisation. Military-grade, payment network root keys, satellite operations

The vast majority of commercial deployments use Level 3 devices. Level 4 validation is rare and expensive; Thales (formerly Safenet) Luna PCIe 7 and the IBM 4769 are Level 4 validated. FIPS 140-3 introduces enhanced self-test requirements, updated approved algorithm lists (deprecating SHA-1 and transitioning to CNSA 2.0 algorithms for post-quantum readiness), and tightened documentation requirements aligned with ISO 19790.

PKCS#11 and the Cryptoki Interface

The dominant application programming interface for HSMs is PKCS#11, defined by the OASIS PKCS Technical Committee (currently at version 3.0).[24] Also known as the Cryptoki (Cryptographic Token Interface) specification, PKCS#11 defines a C API for platform-agnostic access to cryptographic tokens — HSMs, smart cards, software tokens, and TPMs. The core abstraction is the slot/token model: a PKCS#11 library exposes one or more slots (physical or virtual), each containing a token (the HSM partition). Operations on the token include key generation (C_GenerateKey, C_GenerateKeyPair), signing (C_Sign), verification (C_Verify), encryption/decryption, and object management.

Form Factors and Vendors

HSMs exist in three primary form factors. Network HSMs are appliances connected via Ethernet, providing cryptographic services to multiple hosts simultaneously; the Thales Luna Network HSM 7 and Entrust nShield Connect series are market leaders. PCIe HSMs are expansion cards installed directly in a server, offering lower latency than network HSMs at the cost of host-specific deployment; Thales Luna PCIe 7 and Utimaco SecurityServer are representative. All major cloud providers offer managed HSM services. Attacking Trusted Hardware: Side-Channel and Microarchitectural Attacks

Trusted hardware technologies do not exist in a vacuum; they are realised in silicon that shares microarchitectural resources with adversarial workloads, and they are operated by firmware and software whose complexity introduces vulnerabilities. The years since SGX's deployment in 2015 have witnessed a remarkable and sustained series of attacks, demonstrating that hardware trust boundaries are not impermeable and that the gap between formal security models and physical implementations can be exploited with sophisticated but realistic techniques. This section surveys the major attack classes, examining both the underlying mechanisms and their implications for trusted hardware deployments.

Taxonomy of Hardware Attacks

Hardware attacks on TEEs fall into two broad categories. Passive side-channel attacks observe covert information leakage through shared microarchitectural resources — CPU caches, translation lookaside buffers, branch predictors, prefetchers, power draw, electromagnetic emissions — without modifying system state. Active fault injection attacks deliberately disturb system state — via voltage glitching, clock manipulation, or DRAM disturbance — to cause computation errors that leak secrets or bypass integrity checks. A third category, software-based vulnerabilities, exploits implementation flaws in TEE firmware or OS without requiring hardware-level access.

Cache-Based Side-Channel Attacks

The CPU cache is shared between the enclave and untrusted code in all current TEE architectures that do not partition the cache (SGX does not; TrustZone implementations vary). Two fundamental primitives enable cache-based attacks:

Flush+Reload exploits shared pages (typically executable code or shared libraries). The attacker flushes a target cache line using CLFLUSH, allows the victim to execute, then measures the reload time. A fast reload indicates the victim accessed that cache line, revealing access patterns — sufficient to recover AES keys from T-table implementations, or RSA modular exponentiation from square-and-multiply sequences.

Prime+Probe requires no shared pages. The attacker fills a cache set with its own data (prime), allows the victim to execute, then measures the eviction of its own data (probe). Eviction indicates the victim accessed a memory address mapping to that cache set. Brasser et al. (2017) demonstrated Prime+Probe against SGX enclaves, recovering RSA private keys from an SGX-protected mbedTLS instance.[36]

Spectre and Meltdown

The Spectre and Meltdown vulnerabilities, disclosed in January 2018, represent the most significant microarchitectural security discoveries in computing history. Kocher et al.[26] and Lipp et al.[27] demonstrated that speculative execution — the processor's optimistic execution of predicted code paths before knowing whether they will be needed — can be exploited to read arbitrary memory across hardware-enforced isolation boundaries.

Meltdown (CVE-2017-5754) exploited an Intel-specific microarchitectural race condition: when a user-mode instruction attempted to read a kernel virtual address, the processor would speculatively execute subsequent instructions using the (supposedly inaccessible) kernel data before the permission fault was raised and architectural state was rolled back. The speculative execution window was long enough to encode the kernel data into the cache state, where it could be recovered via a Flush+Reload side channel. Meltdown was mitigated by Kernel Page-Table Isolation (KPTI), which maps the kernel into a separate page table not accessible from user mode — at a performance cost of 5–30% for system-call-heavy workloads.[27]

Spectre (CVE-2017-5753, CVE-2017-5715) is architecturally broader and fundamentally harder to mitigate. Rather than exploiting a permission check race, Spectre exploits the Branch Prediction Unit (BPU): the attacker mistrains the branch predictor to cause the victim to speculatively execute a code gadget (a sequence of instructions) that leaks victim data through the cache. The attack requires finding a suitable gadget in the victim's code, but the Linux kernel, Windows NT, and most JIT-compiled runtimes contain abundant such gadgets.[26] Mitigations include retpoline (indirect branch trampoline to prevent branch prediction), microcode updates for Indirect Branch Restricted Speculation (IBRS, STIBP, IBPB), and compiler-based serialisation with LFENCE.

Foreshadow (L1 Terminal Fault)

Van Bulck et al.'s Foreshadow attack (2018)[28] — also known as L1 Terminal Fault (L1TF, CVE-2018-3615/3620/3646) — demonstrated a Meltdown-style attack specifically targeted at SGX enclaves. The key insight was that Intel's memory abort semantics on a page table walk that encounters a Not-Present page do not prevent speculative execution from using the physical address embedded in that PTE — even when the physical address points to enclave EPC memory. By carefully constructing a PTE pointing to a target EPC page and causing a speculative access, a Non-Secure process (or hypervisor) could transiently read the decrypted contents of any enclave page present in the L1 data cache, regardless of EPCM ownership checks.

Foreshadow was particularly devastating because it could extract SGX attestation keys from the Quoting Enclave — entirely undermining the attestation guarantee for all SGX deployments on affected microarchitectures. Intel's response was microcode updates that flush L1D on VMEXIT (for the hypervisor variants L1TF-VMM and L1TF-OS) and a hypervisor-side mitigation requiring disabling hyperthreading when untrusted enclaves share a physical core with the Quoting Enclave. The SGAxe attack (2020)[29] subsequently showed that even post-Foreshadow mitigations were insufficient on certain systems, allowing attestation key extraction via a cache-based variant, prompting Intel to revoke the affected TCB versions in its attestation infrastructure.

⚠ Critical: Attestation Key Compromise

Foreshadow and SGAxe demonstrated that SGX attestation keys — the root of the entire attestation trust chain — could be extracted by a privileged local attacker. This implies that attestation quotes from a compromised platform cannot be distinguished from genuine ones, absent post-compromise key revocation. Remote attestation provides no value without a functioning revocation and TCB versioning infrastructure that allows verifiers to reject quotes from out-of-date or compromised platforms.

PlundervVolt: Fault Injection via Undervolting

Murdock et al.'s PlundervVolt attack (2020)[30] demonstrated a novel fault-injection path requiring no physical access: the Intel MSR_IA32_VOLTAGE interface (accessible from Linux root via /dev/msr) allows software-controlled undervolting of the CPU's core voltage. By transiently reducing supply voltage during a cryptographic computation inside an SGX enclave, the attackers induced bit flips in the computation — causing the MEE to store an encrypted version of the faulty result while the enclave's integrity checks (running on faulty silicon) failed to detect the error. By carefully timing the voltage glitch relative to AES rounds or RSA multiplication, they recovered the AES-256 key from a protected enclave in minutes.

Intel's mitigation was to lock the voltage offset from unprivileged software (effectively removing undervolting capabilities on SGX-enabled systems), delivered via a microcode update and BIOS changes. This mitigation was controversial, as undervolting is widely used for power efficiency and thermal management on laptops.

LVI: Load Value Injection

Van Bulck et al.'s LVI attack (2020)[31] inverted the direction of Meltdown: rather than reading values out of an enclave, the attacker injects attacker-controlled values into the enclave's transient computation. By manipulating page tables and exception handlers to cause a particular load within the enclave to transiently return attacker-controlled data (via a microarchitectural fault that later causes a redo with the correct value), an attacker can hijack enclave control flow during the speculative window. LVI is theoretically very powerful but practically limited by strict timing requirements and the need for enclave-specific gadget chains. Mitigation requires serialising every potentially vulnerable load with LFENCE, causing up to a 19× performance overhead — largely prohibitive without substantial compiler and application co-design.

PLATYPUS: Power Side-Channel via RAPL

Lipp et al.'s PLATYPUS attack (2021)[33] demonstrated that Intel's Running Average Power Limit (RAPL) interface — which exposes real-time power consumption data to userspace via the powercap Linux kernel interface — provides sufficient resolution to distinguish individual AES rounds and recover keys from SGX enclaves. The RAPL interface, originally designed for power management, was accessible without root privileges on many Linux systems. Mitigation was straightforward: restrict RAPL access to root (now the default in Linux 5.10+). The attack highlighted the danger of exporting fine-grained hardware telemetry to unprivileged software.

TPM-FAIL: Timing Attacks on TPM ECDSA

Moghimi et al.'s TPM-FAIL (2020)[32] discovered that multiple commercial TPM chips — specifically STMicroelectronics ST33 and Infineon SLB96xx — implemented ECDSA signature generation with execution time dependent on the secret ephemeral nonce (k). By collecting timing observations of TPM ECDSA signatures over a network (the attack required roughly 45,000 queries for STM, 40,000 for Infineon), they applied lattice reduction techniques (LLL and BKZ algorithms) to recover the ECDSA private key — compromising the TPM's signing key without physical access. The attack is significant because it targets the one component that was supposed to be the hardware root of trust. Affected vendors issued firmware updates.

Cold Boot Attacks

Halderman et al.'s seminal 2009 cold boot attack demonstrated that DRAM retains its contents for seconds to minutes after power is removed — with retention times extending to hours when DRAM is cooled with liquid nitrogen.[35] An attacker who can briefly freeze and remove memory DIMMs can image the DRAM contents on another system and recover cryptographic keys from running memory. This attack is relevant to TPM-sealed volume encryption (BitLocker, LUKS): if the volume key is present in host RAM — which it must be for the running system — it can be extracted via a cold boot attack. Mitigations include memory encryption (AMD SME/SEV, Intel TME) and OS-level techniques to scrub memory before shutdown, though these are difficult to implement completely.

Rowhammer and DRAM-Based Attacks

Kim et al. (2014) demonstrated that repeatedly accessing DRAM rows at high frequency causes capacitive coupling that induces bit flips in adjacent rows — the Rowhammer vulnerability.[34] While not a side channel in the traditional sense, Rowhammer has been weaponised to break process isolation and, in some configurations, to corrupt page table entries — causing a process to gain write access to arbitrary physical memory. Rowhammer-based attacks have been demonstrated against SGX enclaves: by hammering rows adjacent to EPC pages, an attacker can potentially flip bits in enclave code or data, causing computation errors or bypassing integrity checks. DRAM vendors have implemented Target Row Refresh (TRR) as a mitigation, though researchers have repeatedly shown TRR bypass techniques (TRRespass, Blacksmith).

AMD SEV Vulnerabilities

AMD SEV (pre-SNP) has been extensively attacked. Since basic SEV provides no integrity protection, an attacker with hypervisor access can perform chosen-plaintext attacks on the encrypted memory: by remapping guest physical memory to an attacker-controlled address and observing the ciphertext that results from known guest writes, the attacker can learn the per-page encryption tweaks and perform arbitrary plaintext manipulation. The "SEVered" attack (Hetzelt and Buhren, 2018) demonstrated API extraction from an Apache web server running inside a basic SEV VM. SEV-ES closed some of these gaps but the missing integrity protection remained until SNP.

Confidential Computing in the Cloud: Architecture and Deployment

Confidential computing is the protection of data in use — computation over sensitive data in a hardware-isolated environment that is verifiable by remote attestation. Whereas cryptography comprehensively protects data at rest (encrypted storage) and data in transit (TLS), data in use — loaded into processor registers and memory during computation — has historically been exposed to the operating system, hypervisor, and physical infrastructure operator. Confidential computing addresses this final frontier.

The Confidential Computing Consortium

The Confidential Computing Consortium (CCC) was established in 2019 under the Linux Foundation to accelerate adoption and standardisation.[36] Its founding members included Alibaba, Arm, Baidu, Google, IBM, Intel, Microsoft, and Red Hat. The CCC provides a governance framework for open-source projects — including the Gramine LibOS, the Enarx deployment framework, the Kata Containers runtime, and the VERAISON attestation service — alongside non-profit technical working groups on attestation, certification, and use-case documentation. Its 2022 Technical Analysis of Confidential Computing report[36] provides the canonical definition and taxonomy adopted by most cloud providers.

AWS Nitro Enclaves

Amazon Web Services introduced Nitro Enclaves in 2020.[37] The Nitro architecture is foundational: AWS uses dedicated Nitro Security Chips (custom ASICs) to offload virtualisation, networking, and storage I/O from the host CPU, dramatically reducing the hypervisor's attack surface. Nitro Enclaves are isolated virtual machines spawned from a parent EC2 instance, connected only via a local VSOCK channel — they have no persistent storage, no external network access, and no interactive console access. The isolation boundary is enforced by Nitro's hypervisor and the Nitro Security Chip; the parent instance and AWS operators cannot access enclave memory.

Attestation is provided by the Nitro Hypervisor, which generates a signed attestation document for each enclave — a CBOR-encoded structure containing the enclave's PCR values (PCR0: enclave image file hash; PCR1: Linux kernel and bootstrap; PCR2: application; PCR8: enclave image file certificate), a user-provided nonce, and a certificate chain rooted in the AWS Nitro root CA. AWS KMS can enforce a policy that releases a key only to enclaves with specific PCR values, enabling sealed secret delivery — the canonical use case for Nitro Enclaves in data processing workflows.

Google Confidential VMs

Google Cloud's Confidential VMs,[38] available since 2020, use AMD SEV (initially) and AMD SEV-SNP (from late 2022 onwards) to provide memory-encrypted virtual machines on N2D instance types (EPYC Rome/Milan). The offering is notable for its simplicity: customers select the "Confidential VM" option and receive an otherwise standard GCE VM whose memory is hardware-encrypted under a guest-controlled key. Google cannot access plaintext memory contents.

Attestation is provided via the Confidential Computing API and the go-tpm-tools library, which retrieves and validates the AMD SEV-SNP attestation report from inside the confidential VM. Google integrates confidential computing with its Key Management Service (Cloud KMS) and its Workload Identity Federation to enable attestation-conditional secret release. The Confidential Space product extends this to multi-party computation scenarios: multiple organisations provide encrypted inputs to a jointly-audited workload running in a Confidential VM, with attestation ensuring the code running on their data matches the publicly committed code hash.

Microsoft Azure Confidential Computing

Azure's confidential computing portfolio[39] is the broadest among the major cloud providers, reflecting Microsoft's early investment in SGX through the Azure DCsv2 and DCsv3 instance families (SGX-enabled Xeon Ice Lake), the DCasv5/ECasv5 families (AMD SEV-SNP), and the new DCesv5/ECesv5 families (Intel TDX). Azure's deployment of Intel SGX at scale — with tens of thousands of SGX-enabled VMs — made it a proving ground for SGX DCAP attestation infrastructure.

Azure Attestation is a managed service that implements the background-check attestation model: the attesting workload submits its quote (SGX, SEV-SNP, or TDX) to the Azure Attestation endpoint, which validates it and returns a signed JWT (JSON Web Token) acting as an Attestation Result. The JWT contains claims derived from the attestation evidence — for SGX, this includes MRENCLAVE, MRSIGNER, ISV_SVN, and the enclave's user data; for SEV-SNP, it includes the measurement and TCB level. Azure Key Vault and Azure Managed HSM can be configured with access policies that evaluate these JWT claims, enabling conditional key release to attested workloads.

Intel TDX Deployment

Intel Trust Domain Extensions[40] entered cloud production with 4th-generation Intel Xeon Scalable (Sapphire Rapids) processors. TDX creates hardware-isolated VMs (Trust Domains) using the new Multi-Key Total Memory Encryption (MKTME) engine, which supports multiple independent AES-XTS keys simultaneously — each assigned to a Trust Domain. The TDX module, a software module loaded and measured during platform initialisation, manages TD lifecycle and enforces memory access controls via the Secure-EPT (Extended Page Tables) mechanism. From an operator perspective, TDX confidential VMs are operationally similar to regular VMs: they use standard cloud management APIs, support unmodified guest OS images, and can run full Linux distributions without application modification.

TDX attestation uses ECDSA quotes generated by the platform's TDX Quoting Enclave, structurally similar to SGX DCAP quotes. The TD Quote contains a measurement of the TD's initial state (analogous to MRENCLAVE) computed by the TDX module during TD boot. Google Cloud, Microsoft Azure, and Alibaba Cloud have deployed TDX instances.

Confidential Containers

Confidential containers bring the TEE model to Kubernetes-native workloads. The Kata Containers runtime, a CCC hosted project, spawns each pod in a lightweight VM; when combined with a TEE (SGX via Gramine Shielded Containers, or AMD SEV-SNP via AMD-SEV-enabled Kata), the pod's memory is hardware-isolated from the Kubernetes host and container runtime. The CNCF's Confidential Containers (CoCo) project provides an operator-level framework for deploying TEE-isolated pods with integrated attestation — allowing pod images to be decrypted only after the TEE's identity has been verified against a remote attestation service.

Use Cases and Open Challenges

The primary business drivers for confidential computing fall into three categories. Regulated-industry compliance: financial services firms can run analytics on sensitive customer data in the cloud without the cloud provider's legal access to that data; healthcare organisations can run ML inference on genomic data under strict HIPAA and GDPR constraints. Multi-party computation: multiple organisations contribute proprietary data to a jointly-audited workload (e.g., fraud detection, clinical trials analytics) with attestation ensuring no party can observe another's inputs. Intellectual property protection: software vendors can deploy proprietary model weights or algorithms in customer-controlled environments with assurance that the customer cannot extract the IP.

Open challenges remain substantial. The TCB of a confidential computing deployment extends beyond the hardware to include the TEE firmware (PSP firmware for AMD SEV-SNP, TDX module for TDX, SGX microcode for SGX) — all of which have had vulnerabilities. Side-channel attacks, as surveyed in the previous section, can often bypass hardware isolation boundaries without triggering attestation failures. Performance overhead is non-trivial: AMD SEV-SNP incurs 1–5% overhead for most workloads due to RMP checks; SGX EPC page swapping (when enclave memory exceeds EPC) causes severe performance degradation. Finally, the attestation infrastructure itself is a complex trust dependency: the verifier must trust Intel's, AMD's, or ARM's PKI and revocation mechanisms — creating a new form of cloud dependency on silicon vendor infrastructure.

Remote attestation does not eliminate trust — it concentrates and formalises it. Instead of trusting a cloud operator's operational procedures, you trust the silicon vendor's attestation PKI, the TEE firmware update process, and the verifier's appraisal policy.

Future Directions: Post-Quantum Attestation and Formal Verification

Current TEE attestation chains rely on RSA-2048/3072 and ECDSA P-256/P-384 — algorithms vulnerable to Shor's algorithm running on a cryptographically relevant quantum computer. NIST's post-quantum standardisation process, which finalised ML-KEM (Kyber), ML-DSA (Dilithium), and SLH-DSA (SPHINCS+) in 2024, presents a migration challenge for attestation infrastructure: TCG has initiated work on post-quantum TPM algorithms, Intel and AMD have begun planning DCAP and SEV-SNP certificate chain migrations, and the IETF RATS WG is examining algorithm agility in CBOR-encoded attestation tokens. Formal verification of TEE security properties using tools such as ProVerif, Tamarin, or CryptoVerif has produced verified models of isolated execution guarantees for SGX's formal properties, though full formal verification of complete TEE implementations at scale remains an open research challenge.

Conclusion

Trusted hardware technologies have matured from the TCPA's initial vision of a measurement chip in every PC to a rich ecosystem of interoperating mechanisms — the TPM's measured boot and remote attestation, UEFI Secure Boot's enforcement of the boot chain, Intel SGX's enclave isolation, AMD SEV-SNP's confidential VMs with integrity protection, ARM TrustZone's pervasive mobile TEE, and HSMs' certified key custody. Together, these technologies enable computing systems to make cryptographically verifiable claims about their own trustworthiness, reducing reliance on organisational trust in cloud operators and creating the technical foundation for confidential computing in multi-party and regulated-industry scenarios.

Yet the attack literature reviewed here should instil epistemic humility about what hardware trust boundaries actually guarantee. Spectre, Meltdown, Foreshadow, PlundervVolt, LVI, and PLATYPUS demonstrate that the gap between the security model assumed by a hardware specification and the guarantees achievable on physical silicon remains dangerously exploitable. Defences are available — microcode updates, software mitigations, hardware revisions — but they often carry performance costs, require careful deployment, and are typically reactive rather than proactive.

For practitioners, the operational lesson is that trusted hardware is a powerful but not unconditional guarantee. Its value depends entirely on the quality of the attestation verification infrastructure, the timeliness of TCB updates and key revocation, the correctness of the software running inside the TEE (which remains the responsibility of the developer), and the absence of undiscovered microarchitectural vulnerabilities. A well-designed confidential computing deployment treats the TEE as one layer in a defence-in-depth strategy — necessary but not sufficient — alongside principle of least privilege in API surface design, comprehensive side-channel-resistant cryptographic implementations, and continuous monitoring of CVE disclosures from CPU and TEE vendors.

The trajectory is clear: hardware trust mechanisms will become more pervasive, more standardised, and more tightly integrated with cloud and network infrastructure. The Confidential Computing Consortium's governance work, the IETF RATS framework, and post-quantum attestation migration will define the next decade of this field. For architects and engineers building systems over sensitive data, fluency in these technologies is no longer optional — it is a core competency of secure systems design.
