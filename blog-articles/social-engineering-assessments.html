There is a recurring joke among red teamers: why spend three weeks chaining exploits when you can call the help desk and ask nicely? It is funny because it keeps working. The most resilient stack of firewalls, EDR agents, and patched servers can be undone by a single employee who wants to be helpful at the wrong moment. A social engineering assessment is the discipline of measuring exactly how undone — safely, under contract, before a real adversary does it for free.

The numbers are not subtle. Verizon's 2025 Data Breach Investigations Report — the industry's most-cited, vendor-neutral breach study — found that roughly 60% of breaches involved the human element: a person clicking, approving, mistyping, or being talked into something.1 The prior year's report put the figure at 68% and added a detail that should worry every security leader: in simulated campaigns, the median time for a user to click a malicious link was 21 seconds, followed by another 28 seconds to hand over their credentials.2 The gap between an email landing and a domain admin being compromised can be measured with a stopwatch.

And the human path is the expensive one. IBM's Cost of a Data Breach study, conducted by the Ponemon Institute across hundreds of breached organizations, pegged the global average breach at USD 4.88 million in 2024, with phishing the second-most-common initial vector at 15% of breaches and an average cost of USD 4.76 million. Breaches that began with stolen or compromised credentials — the usual prize of a social engineering attack — took the longest of any vector to identify and contain, an average of 292 days.3 Nearly ten months of an attacker living inside the building.

This guide walks the full surface a professional engagement covers — human security as a concept, then phishing simulations, vishing, smishing, pretexting, and physical intrusion — and closes with the standards, regulations, tooling, defenses, and training that hold the work together. It is written for the people who run these assessments and the people who buy them.

PART 00

The human attack surface

Before tooling, before pretexts: why people are exploitable, and what "human security" actually means as an assessable thing.

"Human security" is the recognition that an organization's people form a control plane as real as its network — and a far less predictable one. You cannot apply a patch to a person, you cannot enforce a uniform configuration across thousands of them, and their behavior shifts with fatigue, deadlines, and emotion. A social engineering assessment treats that plane the way a network test treats a subnet: enumerate it, probe it, measure the response, and report where it gives way.

What makes the surface so reliable for attackers is that it runs on cognitive shortcuts that are usually adaptive. We trust authority because deferring to legitimate authority is efficient. We reciprocate favors because cooperation built civilization. We act under urgency because hesitation can be dangerous. Social engineering is the practice of triggering these shortcuts on demand, in contexts where they backfire.

00.1The psychology that does the work

Nearly every modern social engineering playbook traces back to the work of psychologist Robert Cialdini, whose 1984 book Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion distilled decades of research into six principles of compliance: reciprocity, commitment and consistency, social proof, authority, liking, and scarcity (he later added a seventh, unity).4 Cialdini studied salespeople; attackers simply read his work as a manual. Microsoft's security team has explicitly mapped each principle onto live social engineering campaigns, noting that receiving even a small unsolicited gift triggers a neurological obligation to reciprocate — the same reflex a "free" IT favor exploits.5 Academic work continues to confirm that these principles measurably shift trust and risk-taking in social engineering scenarios, independent of the target's personality.6

In the intercept at the top of this page, every annotation is one of Cialdini's levers. The apology and small talk build liking. "My VP needs it in ten minutes" fuses authority with scarcity. "You'd really be saving me" invites reciprocity. None of it is technical. All of it works.

FIELD NOTE · ELICITATION VS. INTERROGATION
The best operators never seem to ask

Skilled social engineers rely on elicitation — steering a conversation so the target volunteers information without ever feeling questioned. A complaint ("your competitor's portal is so much faster") invites correction. A deliberate wrong guess invites a fix. The target leaves the conversation feeling helpful and informed, never realizing they were the source. Training people to recognize the feeling of being elicited is harder, and more valuable, than teaching them to spot a misspelled URL.

00.2What the data says people actually do

The uncomfortable finding for anyone who sells awareness training: in Verizon's 2025 dataset, the click-through (failure) rate in phishing simulations appeared largely unaffected by whether users had been trained, and "prompt bombing" — flooding a user with multi-factor push requests until they approve one — appeared for the first time as a top social engineering action.1 Meanwhile, the SANS Institute's analysis of the DBIR highlighted that over 40% of successful social engineering breaches were business email compromise (what the report classifies as pretexting) — financially motivated attacks with no malicious link or attachment at all — and that non-malicious human error alone now drives roughly 28% of breaches as systems grow more complex and people work faster.7

This does not mean awareness is worthless; it means a click rate is a thin metric. A mature assessment measures the things that actually bound risk: how fast a phish is reported, whether the report reaches a SOC that acts, whether a single skeptical employee can inoculate a whole department, and whether technical controls catch what humans miss. The point of testing the human is rarely to shame the human — it is to find out which compensating controls have to exist because humans are human.

A social engineering assessment is not a test of whether your people are gullible. It is a test of whether your organization survives the fact that everyone, eventually, is.

00.3Where the assessment sits

Social engineering rarely stands alone. In a full-scope red team engagement it is the initial access phase — the way operators get a foothold before pivoting to internal networks, just as Scattered Spider phoned its way into casino networks before ALPHV's ransomware ever ran. 00.4The six levers, lever by lever

Listing Cialdini's principles is easy; recognizing them mid-attack is the skill an assessment is really testing. Each lever maps to a recognizable operational pattern and, more usefully, to a specific weakness a tester should probe and a defender should harden. Microsoft's security researchers, who mapped the principles directly onto observed campaigns, are blunt about it: the techniques are not exotic, they are the ordinary mechanics of human cooperation turned against the person using them.5 The table below is the working translation most red teams carry in their heads.4

The levers are not used singly. The help-desk call in the intercept above fuses liking (the apology and small talk), authority (an unseen VP), and scarcity (ten minutes) into a few seconds of dialogue — which is exactly why isolated "spot the red flag" training fails against it. Critically, the academic work finds these effects operate largely independent of the target's personality: the susceptibility is structural, not a character flaw in particular employees.6 That single finding reshapes the defensive goal. You cannot screen for skepticism and hire your way to safety; you have to build a process that forces verification regardless of how legitimate, friendly, or urgent a request feels in the moment.

PART 01

Frameworks & standards

A test without a methodology is just someone improvising in your lobby. These are the standards that make the work repeatable, defensible, and auditable.

The reason standards matter is coverage and credibility. Without a framework, testers gravitate to the techniques they enjoy and skip the ones they don't, and a client has no way to judge whether an engagement was thorough. A named methodology forces systematic coverage and gives the tester, the buyer, and the auditor a shared vocabulary. No single standard owns social engineering, so professional teams typically layer several.

01.1OSSTMM — the one that names the human channel

The Open Source Security Testing Methodology Manual, published by the Institute for Security and Open Methodologies (ISECOM) and first released in 2001, is the standard most directly relevant here because it organizes all testing into five channels — and one of them is Human Security, alongside Physical Security, Wireless, Telecommunications, and Data Networks.8 OSSTMM is metrics-driven by design: rather than a binary pass/fail, it produces a quantified RAV (Risk Assessment Value) for an environment's operational security. For an organization that wants social engineering measured on the same scientific footing as its network, OSSTMM is the anchor.

01.2PTES — the engagement lifecycle

The Penetration Testing Execution Standard is a community-built framework (published at ptes.org) that defines an engagement as seven phases: pre-engagement interactions, intelligence gathering, threat modeling, vulnerability analysis, exploitation, post-exploitation, and reporting.9 Its strength is process: it covers scoping and rules of engagement through to a report that speaks to both executives and remediation teams, and it applies cleanly to social engineering and physical work, not just network tests. PTES is not a certification or a legal mandate — its authority comes from professional adoption.

01.3NIST SP 800-115 — the regulated baseline

For US government, contractors, and regulated industries, the authoritative reference is NIST Special Publication 800-115, the Technical Guide to Information Security Testing and Assessment. It defines a four-stage methodology — planning, discovery, attack, reporting — and explicitly treats social engineering as a target vulnerability validation technique, distinguishing three assessment methods: testing, examination, and interviewing.10 It is deliberately less prescriptive than PTES on technique, functioning as a policy-level standard auditors recognize rather than a step-by-step playbook.

01.4MITRE ATT&CK and PCI DSS

To make findings legible to defenders, mature teams map social engineering actions to the MITRE ATT&CK knowledge base — for example, the Phishing technique (T1566) and its sub-techniques for spearphishing links, attachments, and services, plus Phishing for Information (T1598) during reconnaissance.11 On the compliance side, PCI DSS v4.0 Requirement 11.4 mandates periodic penetration testing for organizations handling cardholder data, while leaving the execution methodology open — exactly the procedural gap PTES and NIST SP 800-115 fill.12

FIELD NOTE · HOW TO READ A VENDOR
One question exposes a weak provider

Ask a prospective tester: "Which standards do you follow, and how do you cover what they leave out?" A strong answer names two or three and explains how they stack — PTES for process, OSSTMM's channels for coverage, NIST for the regulated paper trail, ATT&CK for reporting. A vague answer — "we use our own methodology" — tells you the engagement will be ad hoc, and coverage will depend on whichever finding happened to interest the tester that week.

PART 02

Phishing simulations

The workhorse of the discipline — and the one most often run badly. Done right, it measures resilience; done wrong, it just generates a shaming spreadsheet.

A phishing simulation sends benign-but-realistic lure emails to employees and measures what happens. It is the most scalable social engineering test, which is precisely why it is the easiest to reduce to a vanity metric. The mature practitioner's first job is to decide what the exercise is actually for.

02.1The lifecycle of a campaign

A defensible campaign follows the same arc as any engagement: scope and authorize (who is in scope, what data is collected, how results are handled), reconnaissance (build target lists, study the organization's real email styles and vendors), pretext design (craft the lure and the landing page), delivery and tracking (send, then capture opens, clicks, data submission, and — most importantly — reports), and debrief (feed results back into training and controls). The metrics that matter are not just the click rate. They include the report rate, the time-to-first-report, and the repeat-clicker population — the small group that fails repeatedly and needs targeted intervention rather than another all-hands email.

02.2The Phish Scale — why click rates lie

A 30% click rate means nothing without context: was the lure a clumsy "you've won a prize" or a pixel-perfect clone of the company's real expense tool, sent the week expenses were due? To solve this, NIST researchers Steves, Greene, and Theofanos published the Phish Scale in the Journal of Cybersecurity in 2020, a method for rating a phishing email's human detection difficulty built from over four years of real enterprise training data.13 It scores two dimensions: the number of observable cues in the email (spelling, mismatched links, odd sender) and how strongly the email's premise aligns with the recipient's actual work context. The companion User Guide gives practitioners a step-by-step rubric to place each campaign on a difficulty scale, so a high click rate on a genuinely hard lure is read very differently from the same rate on an easy one.14

The Phish Scale also reframes what "improvement" looks like. A 2025 large-scale reproduction study grounded in the Phish Scale delivered a sobering pair of results: phishing training interventions showed no statistically significant effect on click rates or reporting rates, while the Phish Scale itself reliably predicted behavior — click rates climbed from 7.0% on easy lures to 15.0% on hard ones. The same study found that in 36–55% of campaigns, a security-conscious employee reported the threat before a colleague interacted with it — an "inoculation" effect that operates independently of individual training.15 The lesson for assessors: stop optimizing for a lower click rate and start measuring whether your organization detects and reports fast enough to matter.

If your phishing program's only output is a list of people who clicked, you have built a tool for blame, not a tool for defense.

02.3The tooling

The open-source ecosystem is mature, and choosing tools sets the realism ceiling of the test.

GoPhish · campaign engine SET · cloning & payloads Evilginx · MFA bypass King Phisher · campaign mgmt Maltego · OSINT recon

GoPhish, an MIT-licensed framework created by Jordan Wright, is the default starting point: a clean web interface, a REST API, scheduled sending, and built-in tracking that turns campaign data into dashboards. It runs as a single binary on Windows, macOS, or Linux.16 The Social-Engineer Toolkit (SET), written in Python by Dave Kennedy of TrustedSec, is the Swiss-army knife for the technical side — spear-phishing emails, on-the-fly website cloning, credential harvesting, malicious QR codes, and payload generation.17

The tool that changed the threat model is Evilginx, created by Kuba Gretzky. Rather than serving a static fake login page, Evilginx operates as a reverse proxy sitting between the victim and the real site — so the login works normally while the framework captures not just the password but the post-authentication session cookie. With that cookie, an attacker resumes the victim's authenticated session and bypasses multi-factor authentication entirely.18 This class of "adversary-in-the-middle" (AiTM) attack is why, as we'll see in the defenses section, one-time codes and push notifications no longer count as phishing-resistant. An assessment that still tests only against static credential-harvesting pages is testing last decade's attacker.

02.4The statistics of a click rate

A click rate is the number executives most want to benchmark and the one they almost always misread. Comparing one organization's rate against another's — or against a vendor's published "industry average" — is close to statistically meaningless, for three reasons that a competent report should make explicit. The first is the denominator problem: a rate depends entirely on who was counted (the whole company or one targeted department), on how a "failure" was defined (opening the email, clicking the link, or actually submitting credentials), and on the send window. Two providers both quoting "12%" may be measuring different events on different populations over different timeframes, and the figures simply do not compare.

The second is difficulty. As the Phish Scale establishes, a rate is uninterpretable without the difficulty of the lure that produced it.13 The 2025 reproduction study quantified this precisely — click rates rose from 7.0% on easy lures to 15.0% on hard, context-aligned ones — which means a 15% failure on a pixel-perfect, perfectly-timed lure is a better result than 7% on an obvious "you've won a prize" email.15 A board shown a bare percentage, with no difficulty score attached, is being shown noise. The third is timing: the same study found that in a third to half of campaigns a security-conscious colleague reported the threat before others interacted with it, so the click rate alone conceals the detection capability that actually bounds the organization's risk.

What is defensible is an internal trend line measured against a stable population, with each campaign's Phish Scale difficulty recorded, tracking the report rate and the time-to-first-report alongside the click rate.14 Presented to leadership, the story collapses to three honest questions: how hard was the lure, how fast did we detect and report it, and did that report reach someone who acted? Anchored against the population-level reality the DBIR documents — the human element in roughly 60% of breaches in 2025, and a median 21-second click time the year before12 — those three numbers tell a leadership team something true. A vendor leaderboard does not. The assessor's deliverable is never a percentage; it is a defensible answer to whether the organization detects faster than the attacker exploits.

FIELD NOTE · CONSENT & CARE
How you run it is part of the result

Phishing simulations touch real people and real emotions. Lures that exploit genuine anxieties — fake layoff notices, bogus bonus announcements, spoofed messages from a named executive — generate high click rates and equally high resentment, and they can do lasting damage to the security team's credibility. Coordinate with HR and legal, avoid weaponizing personal hardship, lead the debrief with education rather than punishment, and make the reporting button trivially easy to find. A program that erodes trust between employees and the security team has made the organization less safe.

PART 03

Vishing

Voice phishing. A live human, a believable story, and a phone line — the technique that took down two of the largest casinos in the world in under ten minutes.

Vishing — a portmanteau of "voice" and "phishing" — is social engineering conducted over a phone call. It is more potent than email for a simple reason: a real voice in real time can improvise. It can answer a hesitant question, project authority, manufacture urgency, and apply the gentle social pressure of not wanting to seem unhelpful — all in the moment, in ways a static email cannot. The data bears this out starkly. IBM's X-Force Threat Intelligence Index found that while the average targeted phishing campaign achieved a click from 17.8% of victims, campaigns that added a phone call were three times as effective, succeeding against 53.2% of targets.19 The channel is also growing fast: Zimperium's 2025 mobile threat research recorded a 28% rise in vishing over its measured period.20

CASE FILE · MGM & CAESARS, SEPTEMBER 2023
A ten-minute phone call, a nine-figure bill

The threat group Scattered Spider (also tracked as UNC3944) reportedly identified an MGM Resorts employee on LinkedIn, then called the company's IT help desk impersonating that person and requested a password reset and a new device registration — enough to bypass multi-factor authentication. Initial access took roughly ten minutes. Partnered with the ALPHV/BlackCat ransomware-as-a-service operation, the intrusion crippled slot machines, digital room keys, and reservations.21

MGM disclosed a USD 100 million hit to its third-quarter results and declined to pay; Caesars Entertainment, struck in the same campaign, reportedly paid roughly USD 15 million. In January 2025, MGM agreed to a USD 45 million settlement with affected customers. The entire chain hinged on a single help-desk agent who couldn't verify a caller.21

This was not an isolated tactic. In January 2024 the American Hospital Association warned of a validated scheme in which attackers impersonated revenue-cycle employees to IT help desks, supplied enough stolen personal detail to pass security questions, then had a password reset and a new MFA device registered — after which they redirected payment-processor instructions to fraudulent bank accounts.22 The help desk has become the soft underbelly of identity security.

03.1How an assessment runs vishing

A professional vishing engagement mirrors the attacker's kill chain under controlled conditions. Operators conduct OSINT to learn names, reporting lines, vendor relationships, and internal jargon; build a pretext with a plausible character and a reason to call; spoof or acquire a caller-ID consistent with the story; and run the call toward a defined objective — extracting a piece of information, getting a target to visit a URL, or persuading a help-desk agent to perform an action like a reset. Calls are recorded (within the rules of engagement and local law) so the debrief can show leadership not just that a control failed but exactly how the conversation unfolded.

03.2What good defense looks like

The defense is procedural, not technological. The single highest-value control is a caller verification process the help desk cannot be talked out of: out-of-band callback to a number on file, a one-time verification code pushed to a registered device, or a manager attestation — never identity confirmed by information an attacker can buy or scrape. Because caller ID is trivially spoofed, it must be treated as untrusted. High-risk actions like MFA re-enrollment and password resets should carry elevated verification by policy, and agents must be explicitly empowered — and rewarded — for refusing a plausible, urgent request from someone they cannot verify.

PART 04

Smishing

SMS phishing — the fastest-growing fraud channel against consumers, and an increasingly serious enterprise threat as work moves onto personal phones.

Smishing is phishing delivered by text message, and it exploits a different psychology than email: texts feel personal and urgent, arrive on a device people check reflexively, and strip away most of the visual cues — sender domains, formatting, hover-to-preview links — that train people to spot email phishing. A shortened link in a terse "your package is held, pay the fee" text gives the target almost nothing to evaluate.

The losses are now enormous. The US Federal Trade Commission reported that consumers lost USD 470 million to text-message scams in 2024 — five times the 2020 figure — and the FTC describes text as the channel scammers now favor over calls or email.23 The FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3) recorded 859,532 complaints and over USD 16.6 billion in losses in 2024, with phishing and spoofing the single most-reported category, and combined business email compromise losses of USD 2.77 billion.24 Its 2025 report showed losses climbing again to roughly USD 20.9 billion, with phishing and spoofing still the top complaint type and a first-ever AI-fraud category accounting for USD 893 million.25 The Anti-Phishing Working Group logged over one million phishing attacks in a single quarter of 2025,26 and Zimperium measured a 22% rise in smishing specifically.2020 Survey data from Pew Research in 2025 found that 73% of US adults have experienced an online scam or attack, most of them receiving scam texts, calls, or emails at least weekly.27

04.1Quishing and the QR-code twist

A fast-rising variant is quishing — embedding a malicious QR code in an email, a poster, a parking sign, or a text. The QR code launders the destination URL out of the reach of email security gateways and onto the victim's personal phone, which is often unmanaged and outside corporate controls. SET and similar tools generate malicious QR codes natively, and an assessment that covers mobile-era threats should include them where the rules of engagement allow.

04.2Running and defending smishing

Smishing assessments carry extra ethical and legal weight because texts often land on personal, employee-owned devices. Scope must be explicit about consent, the numbers used, and data handling, and many programs limit enterprise smishing tests to corporate-issued devices or to employees who have opted in. On defense, the controls echo vishing: treat any unsolicited text requesting action or credentials as hostile, verify through a known channel rather than the number that messaged you, disable SMS as a fallback for accounts already enrolled in stronger authentication, and report scam texts to carriers (via the 7726 / "SPAM" short code in the US) and to the FTC. Critically, organizations should reduce reliance on SMS one-time codes for authentication, which carry their own SIM-swap and interception risks.

FIELD NOTE · THE CONVERGENCE
Real attacks rarely use one channel

The neat boxes in this guide — email, voice, text — are an analyst's convenience, not an attacker's constraint. Modern operators chain them: a smishing text primes the target, a vishing call "from IT" follows up to resolve the very problem the text raised, and a phishing link closes the deal. Each channel lends the next a borrowed legitimacy. A serious assessment should test the seams between channels, not just each channel in isolation, because that is precisely where real intrusions happen.

PART 05

Pretexting

The story underneath every other technique. A fabricated scenario, a borrowed identity, and a reason the target should comply — engineered, rehearsed, and in many jurisdictions, a crime.

Pretexting is the construction and use of a fabricated scenario — the pretext — to win a target's trust and move them to act against their interest. It is less a separate channel than the engine inside the others: a vishing call needs a pretext, a spear-phishing email needs a pretext, a tailgater at the loading dock needs a pretext. As the practitioner literature frames it, every pretext is built from two parts: a character (the role the operator plays — an IT technician, a delivery driver, a panicked executive) and a situation (the plot that explains why the character needs the target to do something now).28 The most effective characters carry authority the target is inclined to defer to, and the most effective situations are specific, time-bound, and emotionally charged.

05.1Business email compromise — pretexting at scale

The most financially devastating form of pretexting needs no malware at all. Business email compromise (BEC) impersonates a trusted figure — typically an executive or a known vendor — to trick an employee into wiring funds or changing payment details. Verizon's data classifies these imposter attacks as pretexting and finds they make up over 40% of successful social engineering breaches, with pretexting involved in roughly a quarter of all financially motivated attacks and a median fraudulent transaction near USD 50,000.72 Because organizations are not legally required to disclose stolen money the way they must disclose stolen data, BEC stays out of the headlines despite costing more, in aggregate, than most ransomware.

05.2Pretexting is regulated — and often illegal

Unlike most social engineering techniques, pretexting is named and prohibited in law, which has direct consequences for how assessments are scoped. In the United States, the Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act of 1999 (Subtitle B of Title V, 15 U.S.C. § 6821) makes it illegal to obtain or attempt to obtain a financial institution's customer information by false pretenses or deception — the so-called Pretexting Rule — and the FTC enforces it.29 GLBA also obliges financial institutions to train employees to recognize and resist pretexting, and its Safeguards Rule, significantly strengthened by the FTC in 2021 with full compliance from 2023, codifies the surrounding security-program requirements.30

The legal net is wider than finance. The Telephone Records and Privacy Protection Act of 2006 specifically criminalized pretexting to obtain a customer's telephone records — passed largely in response to the Hewlett-Packard board-spying scandal — and a more recent FTC rule formally bans the impersonation of any government agency or business, empowering the agency to pursue tactics like spoofed business emails, unauthorized logo use, and lookalike websites.28

CASE FILE · THE HP PRETEXTING SCANDAL, 2006
When the board hired the social engineers

Seeking the source of boardroom leaks, Hewlett-Packard's leadership engaged investigators who used pretexting to obtain the private telephone records of HP directors and journalists — impersonating those individuals to their phone carriers. The scandal forced the resignation of chairwoman Patricia Dunn, drew felony charges (later dismissed), and became a catalyst for the federal telephone-records pretexting statute passed that same year.28 The lesson endures: pretexting that feels like clever investigation to the person ordering it can be a crime to the person performing it.

For the assessor, the practical takeaway is unambiguous: a pretext that would be lawful as a test of your own employees can become a serious offense the moment it targets a third party — a bank, a telecom, a vendor — to extract their protected data. This is why rules of engagement, scope, and legal review are not bureaucratic friction but the difference between a professional assessment and a prosecutable act.

PART 06

Physical Intrusion

Where the human operating system has a front door. Tailgating, cloned badges, bypassed locks, and dropped devices — the assessment that ends with an operator sitting at a desk inside your building.

Every control discussed so far assumes the attacker is remote. Physical social engineering removes that assumption. Here the operator walks through the door, and the target is not a mouse-click but a receptionist, a security guard, or the colleague who holds the door open out of ordinary courtesy. A physical intrusion assessment tests the full stack — perimeter, access control, and the human reflexes that override both — by attempting to gain unauthorized entry to a facility and reach a defined objective: a server room, an executive's desk, a sensitive document left on a printer. The standards already in play apply directly. The Open Source Security Testing Methodology Manual treats physical access as one of its five testable channels, and PTES folds physical attacks into its execution phase alongside their digital counterparts.89

06.1Tailgating and the courtesy exploit

Tailgating — also called piggybacking — is the simplest and most reliable physical technique: the operator follows an authorized person through a controlled door before it closes, relying on social convention to suppress the challenge. Holding a door for the person behind you is a deeply ingrained courtesy, and badge readers do nothing to stop a second body slipping through on one valid scan. Operators amplify the effect with props that manufacture belonging: a coffee in each hand so they physically cannot badge in, a large box that invites help, a high-visibility vest and a clipboard that read as contractor, or a phone held to the ear mid-conversation that makes interruption feel rude. The defense is not technological but cultural — a workforce that treats access control as everyone's responsibility and is trained, without embarrassment, to ask an unfamiliar face to badge in independently.

06.2Badge cloning — the hardware of impersonation

Many organizations rely on proximity cards for door access, and a large share of deployed badges use low-frequency 125 kHz RFID chips — families such as the EM4100/EM410x — that were designed for convenience, not security. These cards broadcast a fixed identifier with no encryption and no challenge-response, which means the credential can be read at close range and replayed. In documented real-world physical assessments, testers have approached a target carrying a concealed reader, captured a badge's data in a brief moment of proximity — in an elevator, a lunch queue, a crowded doorway — and written that identifier to a blank card to produce a working clone.31 The cloned badge then opens every door the original did, with the intrusion indistinguishable from legitimate access in the logs.

FIELD NOTE · THE TEN-CENTIMETRE PROBLEM
Why low-frequency badges are a finding on their own

When an assessment report flags 125 kHz proximity cards, the recommendation is rarely "train your staff." The credential technology itself is the weakness: a fixed, unencrypted number that can be harvested from a pocket. Mitigation means migrating to high-frequency smart cards with mutual authentication and encrypted communication, or to credentials bound to a cryptographic secret that cannot be replayed from a passive read. Until then, every employee badge is a password printed on the outside of the building.

06.3Bypass, pick, and the gap under the door

Where social entry fails, physical operators turn to the lock itself. Lockpicking opens conventional pin-tumbler locks; bypass tools defeat them without picking at all. A surprising number of commercial doors are vulnerable to under-door tools that reach through the gap at the threshold to pull a lever handle from the inside, or to latch-slipping ("loiding") that retracts a spring bolt that was never backed by a deadbolt. Request-to-exit motion sensors mounted inside can sometimes be triggered from outside through a gap, releasing a maglock. None of this is exotic; it is the standard toolkit of a physical assessor, and its relevance to a client is that expensive electronic access control is often undermined by the cheapest mechanical detail.

06.4Drop devices — leaving something behind

The objective of a physical intrusion is not always to take something out; sometimes it is to leave something in. A drop device is a small, network-aware implant — a modified USB device, a credit-card-sized single-board computer with a cellular or Wi-Fi uplink, or a rogue access point — planted on the internal network to give the remote team a persistent foothold. An operator who reaches an unattended desk or a conference-room network jack for sixty seconds can convert a physical intrusion into ongoing digital access. The companion technique is the baiting drop: USB devices left in parking lots, lobbies, and smoking areas, relying on curiosity to carry them past the perimeter and into a machine on the inside. Field studies of dropped media consistently find that a meaningful fraction are picked up and plugged in.

06.5The physical operator's toolkit

Modern physical engagements are supported by a compact set of well-known tools, and naming them in a report helps a client understand the threat model concretely rather than abstractly.

Proxmark3 — RFID research / badge read & clone Flipper Zero — multi-protocol RF, sub-GHz, NFC, iButton Lockpick set — picks, rakes, tension tools Under-door tool — lever-handle bypass USB implant — keystroke injection / network drop SBC implant — single-board computer with remote uplink

Professional bodies have formalized this practice. ISACA's guidance on physical penetration testing frames it as a structured discipline with reconnaissance, planning, execution, and reporting phases that mirror a digital engagement, and stresses that the most dangerous findings usually live at the intersection of weak physical controls and helpful human behavior.32 Specialist assessment firms describe the same arc: open-source reconnaissance of the target's buildings, badges, uniforms, and routines; a pretext to explain the operator's presence; entry; objective; and a clean, well-evidenced exit.33

Yet of all the assessment types in this guide, physical intrusion carries the highest personal risk to the operator — not of failure, but of arrest. That risk is not hypothetical, and it is governed entirely by the quality of the authorization the operator carries. Which brings us to the part of the discipline that is less about technique than about staying out of jail.

PART 07

Ethics, Rules of Engagement & the Law

The discipline that separates a professional assessment from a felony. Written authorization, tightly drawn scope, and a plan for the moment a security guard calls the police.

Social engineering is, by definition, the practice of deception, manipulation, and unauthorized access carried out against real people. The only thing that distinguishes a sanctioned assessment from a crime is authorization — and that authorization must be explicit, documented, and carried by the operator at all times. This is not legal theatre. Every technique in this guide, performed without permission, maps to a real offense: pretexting to a bank violates federal law, planting a device is unauthorized access, and walking into a building you were not invited into is trespass or burglary. The rules of engagement are the instrument that converts those acts into a lawful, contracted service.

07.1The authorization letter — the operator's lifeline

The single most important artifact in a physical or in-person social engineering engagement is the written authorization document, known colloquially as a "get-out-of-jail-free letter." It is a signed statement from someone with genuine authority over the target facility attesting that the bearer is conducting an authorized security test, with the dates of the engagement, the scope, and a named, reachable point of contact who can confirm the operation in real time, day or night. When a guard detains an operator, this letter — and the phone call to the authorizing contact — is what ends the encounter peacefully. Its value depends entirely on three things: that it is signed by someone who actually owns the risk, that the point of contact answers the phone, and that the operator carries it on their person rather than leaving it in the car.

07.2Scope is a safety control, not paperwork

Rules of engagement define what the team may and may not do, and the boundaries matter as much for the client's safety as for the operator's. A well-drafted scope specifies which buildings, systems, and people are in bounds; which techniques are permitted (may the team pick locks? clone badges? impersonate employees?) and which are forbidden (forcing doors, disabling alarms, physically restraining anyone); the hours of operation; data-handling rules for anything captured; and explicit exclusions — the executive who must not be targeted, the production system that must not be touched, the alarm that must never be defeated. Crucially, scope should also identify who at the client is aware of the test. If front-line security and local law enforcement have not been notified, the team must plan for a genuine confrontation, because to everyone on the ground the intrusion is real.

The consequences of leaving these boundaries ambiguous are not theoretical. The most instructive cautionary tale in the profession is also one of its most public.

CASE FILE · COALFIRE & THE DALLAS COUNTY COURTHOUSE, 2019
Authorized to test, arrested anyway

In September 2019, two penetration testers from the security firm Coalfire — Gary De Mercurio and Justin Wynn — were arrested inside a county courthouse in Adel, Iowa, during a physical security assessment they had been contracted to perform for the state's judicial branch. Their contract authorized them to test the building's defenses, including after-hours entry, but a jurisdictional gap proved disastrous: the state court administration had engaged them, while the building itself was owned by the county, whose sheriff had not been informed. Responding to an alarm, deputies found two men inside a government building at night; the testers produced their authorization, but the county did not recognize it. They were booked on felony burglary charges, held on six-figure bail, and spent the night in jail.34

The criminal charges were eventually dropped in early 2020, but the fallout ran for years. In January 2026, more than six years after the arrest, Dallas County agreed to settle the testers' related civil lawsuit for USD 600,000 — and the episode left the industry with a body of hard-won guidance and freely shared legal templates so that no engagement is ever again undone by an authorization that one stakeholder simply did not accept.35

The Coalfire episode crystallized several rules now treated as non-negotiable. Authorization must come from a party with genuine jurisdiction over every element in scope — including, for a physical test, the actual owner of the building. All affected stakeholders, including local law enforcement where appropriate, should be informed that an authorized test may occur. And the contract should pre-arrange a clear escalation path so that a real-world detention resolves with a phone call rather than a cell. The lesson is not that physical assessments are too dangerous to run; it is that the legal and procedural scaffolding around them is part of the engineering, not an afterthought to it.

07.3The duty of care toward the target

There is a second ethical dimension that has nothing to do with the operator's legal exposure and everything to do with the people on the receiving end. The employee who clicks the simulated phish, answers the vishing call, or holds the door for the tester is a colleague, not an adversary — and an assessment that humiliates them produces fear, not security. Responsible programs treat results as organizational findings rather than individual failings, never publicly name the people who fell for a simulation, and pair every test with constructive education rather than punishment. They also weigh the psychological impact of the pretext itself: scenarios that exploit grief, threaten employment, or impersonate emergencies can cause real distress and should be avoided or carefully bounded. The objective is a workforce that feels equipped and trusted, because a culture that punishes honest mistakes simply teaches people to hide them — including the next real breach.

An assessment that ends with employees afraid to report mistakes has made the organization less safe, not more — whatever the click-rate says.
PART 08

Defenses & Best Practices

What the findings should drive. Phishing-resistant authentication, email authentication, verification culture, and human risk management — the controls that actually move the numbers.

An assessment that only produces a click-rate is a thermometer, not a treatment. The purpose of testing the human operating system is to identify which controls will blunt the attacks it is vulnerable to — and the evidence is now clear that the highest-leverage defenses are technical controls that make human error survivable, paired with a culture that makes verification routine. Awareness alone is necessary but demonstrably insufficient: as the phishing data showed, training reduces but never eliminates the click. The strategy that works assumes some clicks are inevitable and removes their payoff.

08.1Phishing-resistant MFA — the highest-leverage control

The single most effective defense against credential phishing is authentication that cannot be phished. Most multi-factor methods can be defeated by a determined operator: one-time codes by SMS or authenticator app can be relayed through an adversary-in-the-middle proxy, and push notifications can be worn down by MFA-fatigue bombing. The reverse-proxy toolkits discussed earlier exist precisely to harvest the session token after a valid second factor is entered, neutralizing conventional MFA entirely.18 The U.S. Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency draws a sharp line here: it identifies only two categories as genuinely phishing-resistant — the FIDO/WebAuthn family of authenticators (including passkeys and hardware security keys) and PKI-based smart cards such as government PIV credentials — and recommends them as the gold-standard target for any organization, prioritizing high-value accounts first.36

What makes these methods resistant is cryptographic origin-binding: a FIDO credential is mathematically tied to the legitimate website's domain, so the authenticator simply will not release a signature to a lookalike phishing domain, no matter how convincing it appears to the human. The relayed-token attack fails because there is no shared secret to relay. This is why authentication standards bodies place FIDO2 and PIV at their highest assurance levels, and why federal policy has moved decisively in this direction: the U.S. Office of Management and Budget's zero-trust strategy directs agencies toward phishing-resistant authentication as a baseline requirement, explicitly steering them away from factors that can be intercepted.37

That federal push is part of a broader, vendor-wide migration toward passkeys — FIDO credentials synced across a user's devices — as a usable replacement for passwords entirely. CISA has urged software manufacturers to make strong authentication the default rather than an opt-in, naming passkeys among the capabilities customers should expect built in.38 The technology is no longer niche: the underlying Web Authentication standard is a published, browser-native specification stewarded by the FIDO Alliance and the World Wide Web Consortium, and adoption has reached enormous scale across the major platforms.39

FIELD NOTE · "WE HAVE MFA" IS NOT THE END OF THE CONVERSATION
The question every assessment should force

When a client says they have multi-factor authentication, the assessor's job is to ask which kind. SMS codes, authenticator apps, and push approvals all raise the bar — but each can be defeated by an adversary-in-the-middle proxy or fatigue attack, and a successful assessment will often prove it. Only origin-bound FIDO/WebAuthn or PKI credentials remove the phishing payoff entirely. "We have MFA" and "we have phishing-resistant MFA" are different security postures, and the gap between them is exactly where modern intrusions live.

08.2Authenticate the email itself — SPF, DKIM, and DMARC

Many phishing and business-email-compromise attacks succeed by spoofing a trusted sending domain. Three complementary email-authentication standards exist to make that harder. SPF (Sender Policy Framework) lets a domain publish which mail servers are authorized to send on its behalf. DKIM (DomainKeys Identified Mail) attaches a cryptographic signature that lets a receiver verify a message was authorized by the domain and not altered in transit. DMARC (Domain-based Message Authentication, Reporting and Conformance) ties the two together, tells receiving servers what to do with messages that fail — quarantine or reject — and returns reporting that reveals who is sending mail in your name. Deployed together with an enforcing policy, they sharply reduce direct domain spoofing and are now a baseline expectation rather than an advanced control. They do not stop lookalike domains or compromised legitimate accounts, which is why they complement, rather than replace, the authentication and verification controls around them.

08.3Build a verification culture

Every social engineering attack in this guide ultimately asks the target to act on trust without verifying. The corresponding organizational defense is a culture in which verification through an independent channel is normal, expected, and never penalized. Concretely, that means a standing rule that any request to move money or change payment details is confirmed by calling the requester back on a known, pre-existing number — never one supplied in the message itself — which defeats most business email compromise outright. It means help desks that authenticate callers through something stronger than knowledge an attacker can buy or guess, a direct response to the help-desk vishing that produced some of the most damaging breaches on record.21 And it means giving every employee a fast, blame-free way to report a suspicious message and a clear signal that doing so is valued — because the reporting employee is a sensor, and the data shows reporting rates remain far lower than they should be.2

08.4From awareness to human risk management

The field is moving away from annual, one-size-fits-all "security awareness training" toward continuous human risk management: identifying which roles and individuals are most exposed, delivering targeted and timely education, and measuring behavior change rather than course completion. The phishing-simulation research is the cautionary backdrop — generic training showed little durable effect, and embarrassment-based approaches can backfire — so the better programs personalize to risk, reinforce in the moment a mistake is made rather than months later, and treat metrics as a guide to coaching rather than a stick.15 The aim is not a workforce that has watched a video, but one whose default behaviors — verify, report, pause before acting on urgency — are strong enough to hold up under a real attacker's pressure.

PART 09

Resources, Training & Certifications

Where to learn the craft properly. Hands-on courses, a dedicated certification track, the standards to read in full, the canonical books, and the conference channels worth your time.

Social engineering assessment is a practitioner's discipline, learned through structured training and supervised practice rather than from a single text. The resources below are the ones the professional community consistently points to — vendor-neutral where possible, and weighted toward material that teaches the methodology and ethics, not just the tricks.

09.1Formal training and certification

For a rigorous, hands-on grounding, the SANS Institute's dedicated course on social engineering for penetration testers teaches the full assessment lifecycle — reconnaissance, pretext development, phishing and vishing execution, and physical entry — within a professional, ethics-first framework, and is widely catalogued as a core offering for this specialty.40 The other pillar of the field is the body of work built by Christopher Hadnagy and the Social-Engineer organization, which maintains a structured framework for human-focused testing, runs a well-regarded practical course, and offers a profession-specific credential — the Social Engineering Practical Professional certification — alongside extensive free educational material.41 The same group founded the social engineering community at the DEF CON conference, including its long-running capture-the-flag competition, which has done as much as any single venue to professionalize and document the field.41

For staying current, the most valuable free resource is the recorded talks from the security conference circuit. The annual breach and incident reports cited throughout this guide are themselves a discipline of study — Verizon's Data Breach Investigations Report in particular is published openly each year and remains the field's most-referenced barometer of how the human element is being exploited in practice.44 Reading each new edition against the last is the simplest way to keep a social engineering practice aligned with the real threat rather than last year's version of it.

CODA

The Assessment as a Mirror

A social engineering assessment is, in the end, a structured way of asking an organization an uncomfortable question: when someone applies pressure to the right person at the right moment, what happens? The answer is never a single number. It is a portrait of how authority is deferred to, how urgency overrides procedure, how courtesy holds a door, and how a help desk treats a stranger in a hurry. The techniques in this guide — the phishing email, the vishing call, the cloned badge, the fabricated pretext — are merely the instruments that surface that portrait. The data is unambiguous that the human element remains the dominant factor in real breaches, and equally unambiguous that the fix is not to demand that people stop being human.1

The organizations that get the most from this work treat the assessment not as an exam to be passed but as a mirror to be read. They invest in the controls that make human error survivable — phishing-resistant authentication above all — and in a culture where verifying a request and reporting a mistake are signs of competence, not weakness. They run their assessments ethically, with airtight authorization and genuine care for the people tested, because an assessment that breeds fear corrodes the very thing it was meant to measure. Done that way, testing the human operating system is not an act of catching people out. It is the most honest form of preparation an organization can undertake — a rehearsal, on its own terms, for the day the pressure is real.
