Vegan Activism · Strategy & Markets

The Market for Mercy

The most powerful forces ending factory farming may be venture capital firms, insurance actuaries, and SaaS entrepreneurs.

April 24, 2026 Long Read by Nicholas Knapp

The conventional story of vegan activism runs on moral suasion: show people the suffering, appeal to their conscience, and wait for behavior to change. That story is running out of road. Per capita meat consumption in the United States has remained stubbornly elevated for decades, and the global picture is worse — the UN Food and Agriculture Organization projects that animal protein demand will continue rising well into mid-century as incomes grow in the developing world. Changing individual hearts is not, on its own, sufficient. The food system is a machine of industrial scale, and the machine is held in place not merely by consumer preference but by capital flows, government policy, infrastructure dependencies, and the simple fact that cruelty is, at present, cheap. The most urgent work of this generation's food advocates may not be the persuasion of individuals — it may be the rewiring of incentives.

This article maps six strategic vectors where business model innovation, financial engineering, and policy entrepreneurship are doing precisely that: making it harder and more expensive to run the old system, while making the new one easier, cheaper, and more attractive to the people who actually control supply chains — procurement officers, institutional investors, insurance underwriters, and legislators.

01 Infrastructure & B2B — Selling Picks and Shovels

During the California Gold Rush, the miners who got rich were not usually the prospectors. They were the merchants who sold shovels, pickaxes, and denim work trousers. A structurally similar dynamic governs the alternative protein industry today: the companies capturing the most reliable value are not necessarily the ones with their name on the package, but the ingredient, manufacturing, and logistics companies underneath them.

Consider the ecosystem surrounding Impossible Foods and Beyond Meat. Their ascent required precision fermentation platforms for heme and protein ingredients, contract manufacturers with bioreactor capacity, cold-chain logistics adapted for novel products, and specialized QA and regulatory consultancy. None of these are branded consumer plays. All of them are monetizable, scalable, and relatively insulated from the consumer sentiment swings that have buffeted retail plant-based sales — which fell 7% in the US in 2024 for meat and seafood alternatives, even as the infrastructure sector continued to attract capital.[5]

Live examples

Perfect Day illustrates this pivot sharply. The Berkeley-based precision fermentation company, which has raised approximately $840 million from investors including Temasek and Horizons Ventures, began as a consumer brand but has deliberately repositioned as a B2B ingredient supplier of animal-free whey proteins — partnering with ADM for industrial-scale fermentation capacity and pursuing large CPG licensing deals rather than fighting on retail shelves.[6] The global precision fermentation market, valued at roughly $5.82 billion in 2025, is projected to reach $151 billion by 2034 — a compound annual growth rate of 43.6%.[7]

The opportunity for founders and investors here is not about convincing carnivores. It is about being the indispensable plumbing behind every plant-based product that reaches market. Supply chain tools, ingredient platforms, manufacturing-as-a-service — these are infrastructure bets on an industry that is growing regardless of any individual product's success.

02 Default-Switching Systems — Change the Environment, Not the Person

Behavioral economics has established a powerful insight: defaults matter enormously. The organ donation rate in countries with opt-out systems dramatically exceeds those with opt-in systems — not because preferences differ, but because friction does. The same logic applies to food. When a hospital cafeteria, university dining hall, or corporate campus makes the plant-based option the default — presented first, most visually prominent, easiest to grab — consumption shifts without anyone being lectured about animal welfare.

This creates an extraordinary opportunity for software entrepreneurs. A SaaS product that helps institutional food service operators optimize menus to reduce food costs, lower emissions, and meet ESG reporting targets — while defaulting to plant-forward options — would sell to CFOs and sustainability directors, not vegans. The pitch is not moral; it is operational and financial.

Institutional levers

The targets are not trivial: K-12 schools, universities, hospital systems, military dining operations, corporate campuses, and prisons collectively feed tens of millions of Americans daily. Procurement tools that shift even a fraction of those meals toward plant-based options — by auto-generating compliant menus, tracking emissions per calorie served, and surfacing the cheapest plant-protein options from supplier catalogs — would produce aggregate impact at a scale that no individual consumer campaign could match.

The Meatless Monday initiative documented measurable dietary shifts in school cafeteria settings simply by relabeling and repositioning plant-forward options. Technology that systematizes and deepens those nudges — tied to supply chain data, ESG dashboards, and cost analytics — transforms an advocacy insight into a recurring revenue business.

03 Financial Engineering — Capital Allocation Drives Everything

Money does not merely follow the food system — it shapes it. The U.S. Department of Agriculture spent at least $72 billion supporting livestock and seafood producers between 1995 and 2023, according to an Environmental Working Group analysis. Over the same period, the USDA spent less than $30 million supporting plant-based and alternative proteins.[8] This is not a failure of consumer demand. It is a capital allocation choice, repeated annually through legislation, which artificially suppresses the price of animal products relative to what a fully-costed market would produce.

The UN FAO has found that over two-thirds of the approximately $540 billion in annual global agricultural subsidies are "price-distorting and largely harmful to the environment."[2] Faunalytics researchers have calculated that if factory farming's true externalities — environmental pollution, public health costs, and antibiotic resistance — were priced into retail, consumers would pay an additional $1.70 for every dollar of animal product purchased. A gallon of milk would cost closer to $11 than $4.[9]

"If factory farming's true externalities were priced in, consumers would pay an extra $1.70 for every dollar of animal product purchased."[9]

The investor turn

Private capital is beginning to realign. The FAIRR Initiative — a collaborative investor network founded in 2015 by private equity investor Jeremy Coller — now represents over 400 institutional members managing more than $90 trillion in combined assets.[4] FAIRR engages directly with the world's largest meat, dairy, and aquaculture companies through its Coller FAIRR Protein Producer Index, pressing them on climate risk, antibiotic stewardship, and supply chain transparency. In 2023, FAIRR released a report warning that climate risks could push half of the world's largest livestock producers into operating losses by 2030 under a 2°C warming scenario.

Separately, investors managing $11.5 trillion in assets — coordinated through the FAIRR network — have called on twenty major food companies including Walmart, Amazon, Danone, and Kraft Heinz to diversify toward plant-based protein as a supply chain resilience strategy.[10] This is not ethics-driven activism. It is fiduciary risk management — and it is how capital allocation actually changes.

For-profit vehicles that formalize this logic — ESG funds excluding or underweighting animal agriculture, alt-protein venture funds with dedicated mandates, and green bonds tied to food system transition — represent the next frontier. Governments committed $510 million in public funds to alt-protein research and development in 2024 alone, a figure GFI's advocacy helped unlock.[11] The infrastructure for redirecting capital exists. The task is building the financial products that channel it.

04 Making Cruelty Expensive — Insurance, Litigation, and Transparency

Most vegan advocacy tries to make plant-based living more attractive. An underexplored complementary strategy is making factory farming more expensive — through insurance pricing, legal liability, and supply chain transparency tools that force the externalization of reputational costs.

The zoonotic risk premium

The scientific literature on factory farming and pandemic risk is unambiguous. Three-quarters of emerging infectious diseases in humans are zoonotic in origin.[12] Intensive animal production — with its high-density confinement, chronic stress-induced immunodeficiency, and live transport — creates ideal conditions for pathogen mutation and amplification. Lancet Infectious Diseases and multiple NIH-indexed studies identify factory farming as a major structural driver of pandemic risk.[13] An estimated 66% of all antibiotics used globally are administered in livestock — a practice the Alliance to Save Our Antibiotics warns could drive 10 million antimicrobial resistance-related deaths annually by 2050.[14]

Yet insurance products do not currently price this systemic risk into premiums for concentrated animal feeding operations (CAFOs). A business model opportunity exists for actuarially sophisticated insurance products that accurately price zoonotic spillover risk — making factory farming insurance-expensive in proportion to its actual hazard profile. Academic economists have proposed exactly this via a "zoonotic Pigouvian tax," and a peer-reviewed paper in Environmental and Resource Economics outlines the framework in detail.[15]

Supply chain transparency as leverage

Legal-tech and supply chain transparency tools represent another lever. If consumers, investors, and journalists could easily trace the provenance of individual meat products back through processing plants and specific farms — with their animal welfare violation records, environmental citations, and antibiotic usage logs attached — the reputational costs of the worst actors would be significantly higher. Companies like Sourcemap and others in the supply chain traceability space have demonstrated the commercial viability of this model in adjacent industries. An equivalent platform focused specifically on animal agriculture would function as a market mechanism for ethical pricing.

05 Category Creation — Stop Competing, Start Inventing

The framing of "fake meat" is a trap. It positions every alternative protein as a lesser substitute for a real thing — and substitutes compete on parity, which means cost and taste, which means the challenger must win on the incumbent's terms. The stronger strategic play is category creation: building new food experiences that are not trying to simulate anything, and that colonize consumer occasions that animal products do not currently own.

Fermented proteins are one such category. Mycelium-based products from companies like Meati Foods — which secured $100 million in Series C funding in 2024, the largest single fermentation deal of the year — do not attempt to replicate beef.[5] They offer a distinct texture and nutritional profile that positions them as novel, rather than imitative. Biomass fermentation, precision fermentation of functional ingredients, and hybrid food formats (plant plus biotech) all create product categories that consumers can adopt without the psychological friction of "giving something up."

The Perfect Day model, extended

Perfect Day's pivotal insight was that the relevant consumer proposition was not "animal-free" — a negation that requires a belief system — but "whey from fermentation" — a production method that can appeal to sustainability-conscious consumers who are not vegan and will never be.[6] Brands built on this logic expand the addressable market dramatically. A food entrepreneur building in this space is not competing for the 3% of Americans who identify as vegan. They are competing for the 100% of Americans who will eventually buy dairy, protein powders, egg-based products, or fats — and who might, given comparable price and equivalent taste, prefer a product with a lower emissions footprint produced without a cow in the supply chain.

Fermentation-derived ingredients now represent the fastest-growing subcategory in alternative proteins by VC investment, up 43% year-over-year in 2024 even as plant-based brands saw funding fall 64%.[5] The market is voting with capital for category creation over category competition.

06 Government-Adjacent Business — The Lever Most People Ignore

The most powerful structural force distorting food markets is not consumer preference — it is public subsidy. Why can a fast-food burger cost less than a head of broccoli? Partly because of taste and convenience, yes. But substantially because of the $72 billion the USDA routed to livestock producers between 1995 and 2023, the $540 billion in annual global agricultural subsidies that the UN FAO has found to be predominantly price-distorting, and the implicit subsidy of externalized costs — environmental, public health, antibiotic resistance — that factory farms offload onto the general public without appearing on any income statement.[1,2]

Changing these subsidies is how you change the food system at scale. And unlike a consumer campaign, it requires persuading not millions of individuals but hundreds of legislators and regulators. This is a far more tractable influence problem — and it is the kind of problem that professional policy shops, lobbying firms, and public-private partnership architects are built to solve.

Where the money is going

The Good Food Institute — the most prominent nonprofit operating in this space — has built a policy workstream that helped secure $510 million in government commitments to alt-protein R&D in 2024 across multiple countries, including Canada's C$150M for Protein Industries Canada, Germany's €38M sustainable protein budget allocation, and a $40M US commitment to fermentation research.[3,11] GFI's advocacy has also challenged labeling laws, supported regulatory frameworks for cultivated meat, and engaged directly with trade bodies.

The for-profit equivalent would be lobbying firms with a specific alt-protein and plant-agriculture mandate, policy consulting practices that advise governments on food system transition strategy, and public-private partnership vehicles that help municipalities and national governments direct existing agricultural transition funds toward regenerative and plant-forward production. Investors in FAIRR's network have already filed statements representing $7 trillion in assets calling on governments to repurpose agricultural subsidies in line with climate commitments under the Global Biodiversity Framework.[4] The policy infrastructure exists. The commercial layer above it — the firms that get paid to navigate and leverage it — is underdeveloped.

"Subsidies currently favor factory farming by orders of magnitude. Changing those subsidies does not require changing consumer behavior. It requires changing fifty votes in a committee."

Behavioral and loyalty systems

At a more granular level, behavioral technology can gamify the incentive structure for individual consumers without requiring moral conversion. Apps that offer cashback on plant-based grocery purchases, insurance premium discounts tied to dietary carbon footprints, or loyalty rewards for choosing certified ethical food options all work by making the "good guy" identity financially attractive — aligning hedonic motivation with ethical outcomes without relying on altruism alone. This is the architecture of decarbonization applied to diet: make green choices cheaper, track them automatically, and reward consistency.

The System View

None of these strategies requires its practitioners to be vegan, or even particularly sympathetic to animal welfare as a primary motivation. The insurance actuary pricing zoonotic risk into CAFO premiums is doing their job. The SaaS founder selling cafeteria optimization software to hospital procurement departments is building a business. The policy consultant advising a government on agricultural subsidy reform is advising a client. The venture capitalist backing a fermentation infrastructure company is making a bet on a market.

This is the point. The most durable systemic changes in history have generally not been won purely by moral persuasion. They have been won when moral imperatives aligned with economic interests — when the cost of the status quo exceeded the cost of change, when new industries made old industries less competitive, when legislators could point to constituents and donors with aligned interests. The abolition of slavery was accelerated by industrialization. The decline of leaded gasoline was driven in part by the cost of catalytic converters that did not work with lead. Clean energy is winning where it is winning because solar and wind have become the cheapest forms of new electricity generation in most of the world.

The task for this generation of food system advocates — and the entrepreneurs, investors, and policy professionals among them — is to assemble the same convergence around animal agriculture. Make the infrastructure for plant-based and fermentation-based food cheaper and more capable. Make the financing for conventional animal agriculture more expensive and more scrutinized. Make the default institutional choice the plant-forward one. Make the regulatory environment one that accurately prices the externalities of factory farming. And build the businesses that profit from each of these transitions.

When cruelty becomes expensive and alternatives become effortless, the market will do a great deal of the rest.
