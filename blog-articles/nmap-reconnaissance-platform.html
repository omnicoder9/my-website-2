Nmap is usually introduced as a port scanner, which is a little like introducing a stethoscope as a noise-detector. The description is technically true and entirely misses the point. A practitioner who has only ever run nmap -sS 10.0.0.0/24 has seen the tool's first sentence; the rest of the book is about what you do once the network starts answering back — how you read filtered ports against unfiltered ones, how you tell a stateful firewall from a stateless ACL by the shape of a reset packet, how you turn three hundred Lua scripts loose on a discovered service, and how you keep all of that legible across a sixteen-thousand-host engagement without drowning in false positives.

This piece assumes you already know the basics: that a SYN scan is half-open, that -Pn skips host discovery, that -sV fingerprints services. The goal here is narrower and more useful — to treat Nmap as a reconnaissance platform during authorized assessments rather than as a one-shot scanner. That means understanding the protocol-level mechanics well enough to choose a scan deliberately, to interpret an ambiguous result instead of trusting the label, and to chain discovery into enumeration into vulnerability validation. Everything below is framed for work you are authorized to perform; the section on authorization is not boilerplate.

01 / OriginsA tool with a thesis

Why a 1997 magazine article still shapes how the tool thinks.

Nmap was first released on 1 September 1997, not as a download but as source code printed inside issue 51 of Phrack, in an article by Gordon Lyon — writing as Fyodor — titled The Art of Port Scanning.1 The original was roughly two thousand lines of C with no version number, because no further releases were planned.2 The stated ambition, recalled later in the preface to the Nmap Network Scanning book, was to consolidate a fragmented field of single-purpose scanners into one flexible tool with a consistent interface and an efficient implementation.3 That consolidation thesis is why Nmap accumulated scan types — SYN, FIN, ACK, Window, Maimon — rather than settling on one. Each type exists because some class of target, or some class of firewall, responds differently, and a serious operator wants the whole instrument.

The next decade filled in the platform. OS detection arrived with version 2.00 in December 1998; service/version detection landed in 2003; the ultra_scan engine that still drives timing was rewritten for 3.70 in 2004.4 The decisive moment came on 13 December 2007, when Nmap 4.50 — the tenth-anniversary release — shipped the Nmap Scripting Engine alongside the Zenmap GUI and second-generation OS detection.5 NSE is what turned a scanner into a framework: an embedded Lua interpreter that runs purpose-built scripts in parallel against discovered services. By the current 7.x line — 7.98 was released in August 20254 — the distribution ships several hundred of them. The point of the history is not nostalgia. It is that Nmap's design rewards an operator who knows why each capability exists, because the labels the tool prints are deductions, not ground truth.

02 / FoundationsThe state model is the whole game

Everything advanced follows from how Nmap collapses a probe response into a label.

Nmap reports six port states, and the advanced material only makes sense once you treat them as inferences from probe behavior rather than as facts about ports. Open means an application is accepting connections. Closed means the port is reachable but nothing is listening. Filtered means a packet filter prevented the probe from eliciting a usable response — Nmap could not determine whether anything listens, often because probes were silently dropped, which forces retransmissions and slows the scan.6 Two compound states matter enormously for the advanced scans: open|filtered, used when the absence of a response is itself ambiguous, and unfiltered, used when a port is reachable but the scan type cannot decide open versus closed.6

A port state is not solely a property of the port. The same port can be "filtered" to one scan type and "unfiltered" to another against the same host.

That observation, drawn directly from the Nmap book's firewall chapter, is the hinge for the entire discipline.7 A SYN scan and an ACK scan ask the target two different questions and therefore get two different answers; combining them tells you something neither could alone. Hold that idea — that you are running differential experiments, not reading a register — and the rest of the scan zoo organizes itself.

03 / ProbesAdvanced scan types

Six TCP scans that exploit RFC ambiguity, stack quirks, and firewall logic.

The RFC 793 loophole: FIN, NULL, and Xmas

The stealth trio — -sF (FIN), -sN (NULL), and -sX (Xmas) — all exploit a single subtle reading of the TCP specification. Page 65 of RFC 793 states that a segment arriving at a closed port that does not contain RST must provoke a RST in response, while the following page says that a segment arriving at an open port without the SYN, RST, or ACK bits set should simply be dropped.8 The consequence: against an RFC-compliant stack, any probe lacking SYN/RST/ACK draws a RST from a closed port and silence from an open one.9 As long as those three bits are absent, the other three (FIN, PSH, URG) may be set in any combination. The FIN scan sends a bare FIN; the NULL scan sends a header with no flags at all; the Xmas scan lights FIN, PSH, and URG so the packet appears "lit up like a Christmas tree."9

stealth scans
# FIN / NULL / Xmas — silence == open|filtered, RST == closed
$ sudo nmap -sF -T4 -p 1-1024 192.168.56.10
$ sudo nmap -sN --reason 192.168.56.10
$ sudo nmap -sX -p- 192.168.56.10

The reward is stealth and a real chance of slipping past stateless, SYN-focused packet filters; the penalty is interpretive. Because an open port stays silent, Nmap cannot distinguish "open" from "dropped by a filter," so it reports open|filtered.9 Worse, the loophole only holds where the stack obeys the RFC. Microsoft Windows, many Cisco devices, and IBM OS/400 reply with RST regardless of port state, which makes every port look closed.9 That failure is itself a fingerprint: a host that returns "all closed" to a NULL scan but shows open ports under SYN is very likely a Windows or Cisco stack. Truly advanced users go further with --scanflags, specifying arbitrary TCP flag combinations — a SYN/FIN scan, for instance — to build probes whose signatures were never added to an IDS ruleset written by paging through the Nmap man page.10

The Maimon scan: a BSD quirk in plain sight

The Maimon scan (-sM) is mechanically identical to the FIN/NULL/Xmas family except that its probe carries FIN/ACK. By RFC 793, a FIN/ACK to either an open or a closed port should draw a RST. The technique is named for Uriel Maimon, who described in Phrack issue 49 (November 1996) that many BSD-derived stacks violate this by silently dropping the packet when the port is open — Nmap shipped the technique two issues later.1112 On a vulnerable BSD-lineage target it therefore behaves like a FIN scan; everywhere else it reports closed. It is a niche probe, but it is the canonical example of Nmap weaponizing a specific implementation deviation, and worth keeping in the kit for older network appliances and BSD-derived embedded systems.

The ACK scan: a firewall mapper, not a port finder

The ACK scan (-sA) is different in kind from everything above: it never reports a port open, or even open|filtered. Its probe carries only the ACK flag, and its purpose is to map firewall rulesets — to learn which ports are filtered and whether the firewall is stateful.13 Against an unfiltered system, both open and closed ports return RST, because an unsolicited ACK matches no connection and the stack resets it. Nmap labels these unfiltered: reachable by the ACK packet, open-versus-closed undetermined.6 Ports that return nothing, or that answer with ICMP unreachable errors of type 3 code 0, 1, 2, 3, 9, 10, or 13, are labeled filtered.6 The logical trick that follows is powerful: if a SYN scan calls a port open|filtered and an ACK scan calls it unfiltered, the port must be open — though, as we'll see, that deduction has limits.13

The Window scan: reading the reset's window

The Window scan (-sW) sends the same bare ACK probe as the ACK scan but, instead of always printing "unfiltered" on a returned RST, it inspects the TCP window field of that RST. On certain stacks, open ports advertise a positive window even in a reset while closed ports use zero, so the window value betrays the port state.14 When it works it is elegant; in practice it is fragile, since most modern stacks no longer exhibit the quirk and may even invert it, producing exactly wrong results. Treat -sW as a corroborating probe against unusual targets, never as a primary scan.

04 / InferenceFirewall and ACL analysis

Turning differential scan results into a map of the perimeter.

The single most useful application of the ACK scan is distinguishing stateful from stateless filtering. The Nmap book's worked example uses the public host scanme.nmap.org, which is protected by a stateful iptables rule that accepts only packets belonging to or related to an established connection: iptables -A INPUT -m state --state ESTABLISHED,RELATED -j ACCEPT. Against that host, an ACK scan finds the same handful of explicitly permitted ports unfiltered and everything else filtered, because the unsolicited ACK packets match no connection and are dropped.7 A stateless filter, by contrast, has no notion of connection state and will pass the ACK to closed and open ports alike, returning RSTs and reporting them unfiltered. The difference in the result set is the diagnosis.

stateful detection — ACK scan
$ sudo nmap -sA -T4 scanme.nmap.org
PORT    STATE      SERVICE
22/tcp  unfiltered ssh
25/tcp  unfiltered smtp
80/tcp  unfiltered http
Not shown: 994 filtered ports   # → stateful: only permitted ports answer

Packet-filter identification & ACL discovery

Filtered ports rarely announce themselves cleanly. Sometimes a firewall that blocks a probe answers with an ICMP destination-unreachable error — type 3 code 13 means "communication administratively prohibited," a near-confession that an ACL is dropping the traffic — but far more often filters simply drop probes silently, which is why Nmap retries before committing to a "filtered" verdict.6 ACL discovery, then, is a differential exercise: scan the same target with SYN and with ACK, scan it from inside and outside the perimeter if scope allows, vary the probed ports, and reconstruct the rule set from the pattern of which ports answer which probe. The --reason flag is indispensable here because it prints the actual stimulus behind each verdict — reset, no-response, admin-prohibited — rather than just the label.

Two cautions keep this honest. First, the logical-deduction trick (open|filtered ∩ unfiltered ⇒ open) assumes that different scan types return a consistent state for a given port, and that assumption is frequently wrong: TCP-stack idiosyncrasies and firewall behavior routinely make scans disagree, so deduction is a hypothesis to confirm, not a conclusion.13 Second, firewall forgery is always possible; an ACK scan showing packets reaching a host establishes only that they probably arrived. The Nmap book itself notes that such reachable-but-unconnectable hosts can be useful for OS detection, for IP-ID tricks, and as zombies in an idle scan — a reminder that perimeter mapping feeds the rest of the engagement.7 When you need to attribute filtering to a specific hop rather than the host, the NSE firewalk script reconstructs rules with a TTL-expiration technique layered on traceroute.

05 / EvasionEvasion & spoofing

Primitives for testing whether a sensor actually sees what it claims to see.

Framing

Evasion techniques are how you measure detection efficacy on an authorized engagement — do the IDS rules fire, does the firewall reassemble fragments, does the SOC alert? They are diagnostic instruments for blue-team improvement, not a recipe for going unnoticed. Use them to produce findings and detection content, not to defeat monitoring you are meant to validate.

Fragmentation — -f, -f -f, --mtu

The -f option splits the requested scan into tiny fragmented IP packets, the idea being to spread the TCP header across several fragments so a naïve packet filter or IDS cannot see the whole thing at once.15 A single -f fragments into 8-byte data chunks; specifying it twice (-f -f) doubles that to 16. For explicit control, --mtu sets the fragment size directly and must be a multiple of eight (do not combine it with -f).15 The technique has real limits the book is candid about: filters that queue and reassemble all fragments — the Linux kernel's CONFIG_IP_ALWAYS_DEFRAG, or iptables connection tracking — defeat it outright, and some source hosts defragment on the way out. If the host OS interferes, --send-eth bypasses the IP layer with raw Ethernet frames, and fragmentation is only available for Nmap's raw-packet scans (so not connect or FTP-bounce).15 Always confirm with a sniffer that packets are actually leaving fragmented.

Decoys — -D

The decoy option (-D) cloaks a scan by forging probes that appear to originate from additional source addresses, so the target sees several systems scanning at once and cannot easily pick out the real one.16 You can list decoy IPs explicitly, generate them with RND:5 for five random non-reserved addresses, and place the literal token ME to control where your real address sits in the sequence. Decoys raise the cost of attribution and can swamp simple alert logic, but they are not invisibility: decoys must be live or you risk SYN-flooding the target, and a sensor doing upstream path analysis or correlating which "source" actually completes connections can still surface the real origin.

Source-port manipulation — -g / --source-port

A surprising number of firewall rule sets trust traffic by source port — leaving DNS (53), or the old FTP-DATA port (20), or Kerberos (88) effectively whitelisted on the assumption that such packets are benign replies. Nmap's -g (alias --source-port) sets a fixed source port so probes inherit that misplaced trust.16 A scan that comes back empty from the standard source can light up when sourced from port 53 — and that delta is itself a finding worth writing up, because it reveals a fragile ACL.

Packet padding & checksum tricks — --data-length, --data, --badsum

Nmap's probes have characteristic, signature-able sizes. The --data-length option appends random bytes to change the packet length and slip past length-based signatures; --data and --data-string append a custom hex or ASCII payload when you want probes to resemble a specific protocol.16 Related spoofing controls in the same family include -S to forge a source address, --spoof-mac to set a MAC by address or vendor prefix, --ttl to set the IP time-to-live, --ip-options for crafted IP options, --proxies to relay through HTTP/SOCKS4 proxies, and --badsum, which sends a deliberately bad TCP/UDP checksum so that only a system not validating checksums (often an IDS or firewall, rather than a real host) will respond — a clean way to flush out inline inspection devices.16

Timing as evasion

The most effective evasion is often just patience. Slowing the probe rate below a sensor's correlation window keeps a scan under rate-based alert thresholds. The -T0 (paranoid) template serializes the scan and waits five minutes between probes; -T1 waits fifteen seconds; both are explicitly intended for IDS evasion.17 Finer control comes from --scan-delay, --max-rate, and --max-retries. A realistic detection-validation command stacks these primitives — and tests the SOC's ability to reconstruct a slow, fragmented, decoyed scan from telemetry:

stacked evasion (detection test)
$ sudo nmap -sF -f -g 53 -D RND:3,ME -T1 --data-length 28 \
       --reason -p 1-200 10.10.20.5
# FIN flags avoid SYN logging · fragments split the header
# source port 53 inherits DNS trust · 3 random decoys + you
# T1 stays under rate thresholds · padding breaks size signatures

06 / NSEThe Scripting Engine

The feature that makes Nmap a platform rather than a scanner.

The Nmap Scripting Engine lets users write and share scripts in Lua to automate networking tasks, executed in parallel with the speed expected from Nmap.18 The design space the authors had in mind spans host and network discovery, more sophisticated version detection, vulnerability detection — and, explicitly, vulnerability exploitation.18 Scripts run in one of four phases: prerule scripts before any scanning, host scripts after host discovery, service scripts against specific open services, and postrule scripts after all targets are done. Each script declares one or more of fourteen categories, indexed in a script.db for fast selection: auth, broadcast, brute, default, discovery, dos, exploit, external, fuzzer, intrusive, malware, safe, version, and vuln.19

Selecting scripts

Selection accepts script names, categories, directories, and shell-style wildcards, and supports boolean expressions with and/or/not at Lua precedence. The convenience flag -sC is equivalent to --script=default.19 A few patterns cover most real work:

nse selection idioms
$ nmap -sV -sC target                       # default scripts + versions
$ nmap --script "http-*" -p 80,443 target     # every http- script
$ nmap --script "default and safe" target      # boolean expression
$ nmap --script "vuln and not dos" target      # vuln checks, no DoS
$ nmap --script ssh-brute \
       --script-args userdb=users.txt,passdb=pass.txt target
$ nmap --script-help smb-enum-shares            # read before you run

The four families that matter on an assessment

Vulnerability scripts (vuln) check for known flaws without exploiting them — smb-vuln-ms17-010 for EternalBlue, or the third-party vulners script that maps detected versions to CVEs and supports a mincvss argument to suppress low-severity noise. Authentication scripts (auth) deal with credentials or their bypass: examples in the category include ftp-anon (anonymous FTP), x11-access, and ssh-auth-methods; the book draws a deliberate line between these and brute force, placing credential-guessing scripts in their own bucket.19 Brute-force scripts (brute) attempt credential guessing against services — ssh-brute, ftp-brute, ms-sql-brute — and take userdb/passdb arguments. Exploit-related scripts live in the exploit category, reflecting that NSE can move beyond detection into proof-of-impact; because they are by nature intrusive, they belong only inside an authorized, scoped window.

Risk & scope

The intrusive, dos, brute, and exploit categories can crash services, lock accounts, or trip lockout and alerting. Read the script source or --script-help before running anything outside safe, and confirm each is permitted by your rules of engagement. A brute-force run that locks out a production directory is a self-inflicted incident, not a finding.

07 / ScaleEnumeration across enterprise networks

Staging, timing, and discipline for sixteen-thousand-host engagements.

Scanning a large enterprise space the way you'd scan a single box — every port, every script, all at once — produces a scan that never finishes and a report nobody can act on. The professional approach is staged: a fast, lightweight discovery sweep to find live hosts and obvious open ports; a triage pass over the survivors to identify services worth attention; and only then targeted deep scans (version detection, NSE) against the hosts and ports that matter. Each stage narrows the next.

Timing templates

The -T templates trade stealth for speed across six levels. -T0 through -T2 serialize and slow the scan for evasion; -T3 is the parallelized default; -T4 caps RTT timeouts and scan delays for fast, reliable networks (its definition includes --max-rtt-timeout 1250ms, --min-rtt-timeout 100ms, --initial-rtt-timeout 500ms, --max-retries 6); -T5 is more aggressive still and risks accuracy.17 The book's standing recommendation is -T4 for reasonably modern, reliable networks, and fine-grained options override the template where you set them.20

Host groups, parallelism, rate

For parallel scanning, Nmap divides the target space into groups and scans one group at a time; larger groups are more efficient but delay per-host results, so by default Nmap starts as low as five hosts and ramps as high as 1024. --min-hostgroup and --max-hostgroup set those bounds explicitly — a large minimum speeds big subnet sweeps.17 Probe parallelism is tunable with --min-parallelism/--max-parallelism (rarely needed, since Nmap adjusts dynamically), and absolute pacing with --min-rate/--max-rate packets per second.16 On flaky or filtered networks, --host-timeout abandons hosts that hang and --max-retries trades thoroughness against speed.

staged enterprise sweep
# Stage 1 — discovery: who's alive across a /16
$ nmap -sn -T4 --min-hostgroup 256 10.0.0.0/16 -oA disco

# Stage 2 — triage: top ports on live hosts only, open ports only
$ nmap -sS -T4 --top-ports 200 --open \
       -iL live-hosts.txt -oA triage

# Stage 3 — deep dive: versions + safe scripts on what matters
$ nmap -sS -sV -O --script "default and safe" \
       -p 22,80,139,443,445,1433,3389 \
       -iL interesting.txt --reason -oA deep

Reducing false positives

A SYN scan infers "open" from a SYN/ACK, but firewalls forge resets, intermediate devices answer for hosts, and congested networks drop probes that then read as filtered. Three habits cut the error rate. Verify with -sV: version detection actually connects and confirms a service is really there, correcting ports that a raw scan mislabeled. Use --reason so every state carries its evidence, letting you spot the implausible (a "closed" port whose RST has a TTL inconsistent with the host). Tune retries and timeouts: too few retries on a lossy link manufactures false "filtered" results, while overly aggressive timing produces both false negatives and noise. And keep the structural caveats in mind — that scans of the same port can legitimately disagree, and that a returned packet only probably reached the host.13

Output formats & prioritization

At scale, machine-readable output is mandatory. -oA writes normal, greppable, and XML simultaneously; the XML (-oX) feeds reporting pipelines and tools, while greppable (-oG) is unbeatable for quick grep/awk triage on the command line. Prioritization is then an exposure-times-exploitability judgment: rank by what is reachable from where the attacker sits, weighted by what is actually exploitable. Pairing -sV with the vuln category and a CVSS floor (the vulners script's mincvss) turns a flat port list into a ranked queue — internet-facing hosts with high-severity, network-exploitable findings first, low-severity informational items last.

08 / DepthService-specific enumeration

Once a service is found, NSE is how you read it.

The payoff of disciplined discovery is targeted enumeration. Each of the services below maps to a cluster of NSE scripts that extract configuration, identity, and version data — the raw material of an attack path. Run them only against in-scope hosts, and prefer the information-gathering scripts before anything intrusive.

SMB (139/445)

SMB is the richest enumeration surface on a Windows network. smb-enum-shares lists shares and their access, smb-os-discovery pulls OS and domain details, and smb2-security-mode reveals whether message signing is required.21 For vulnerabilities, smb-vuln-ms17-010 detects whether an SMBv1 server is exposed to the EternalBlue remote-code-execution flaw (CVE-2017-0143, disclosed 14 March 2017) — the bug actively exploited by the WannaCry and Petya ransomware families. The script works by connecting to the IPC$ tree and inspecting the error code returned, classifying unpatched systems without exploiting them; it is categorized both vuln and safe.22

smb enumeration
$ nmap -p 445 --script smb-os-discovery,smb-enum-shares,smb2-security-mode target
$ nmap -p 445 --script smb-vuln-ms17-010 target
| smb-vuln-ms17-010:
|   VULNERABLE: Remote Code Execution (ms17-010) — CVE:CVE-2017-0143

The rest of the estate

LDAP (389/636) exposes directory structure through ldap-rootdse (naming contexts and capabilities) and ldap-search (querying objects, often Active Directory users and groups). Kerberos (88) yields valid usernames via krb5-enum-users, which probes principals and reads the KDC's differing responses to existing versus non-existent accounts — the foundation of AS-REP and pre-auth attacks downstream. MSSQL (1433, UDP 1434) is served by ms-sql-info and ms-sql-ntlm-info for instance and host details, ms-sql-brute for credential testing, and ms-sql-xp-cmdshell for command execution where xp_cmdshell is enabled and you hold credentials. Oracle (1521) requires a valid SID first: oracle-sid-brute guesses it against the TNS listener, oracle-tns-version fingerprints the listener, and oracle-brute tests accounts. PostgreSQL (5432) is handled by pgsql-brute. RDP (3389) reveals identity and crypto posture through rdp-ntlm-info (hostname, domain, build) and rdp-enum-encryption (supported security layers), with BlueKeep-class checks available for legacy hosts. NFS (111/2049) starts at the portmapper: rpcinfo lists RPC programs, nfs-showmount enumerates exports, and nfs-ls/nfs-statfs inspect exported filesystems for world-readable data.

09 / AuthorizationThe line that isn't optional

Why "I was only scanning" is not a defense.

Before any packet leaves your host

Obtain written authorization that names the in-scope address ranges, the permitted techniques (especially intrusive, brute-force, and exploit scripts), the testing window, and a point of contact. Port scanning sits in a genuinely unsettled legal area, and the safest posture is explicit, recorded permission for every target you touch.

The Nmap project devotes an entire chapter of its book to legal issues precisely because the legality of scanning is jurisdiction-dependent and contested, and because civil or criminal exposure can attach to activity an operator considered harmless.23 The practical rules are simple. Scan only what your scope authorizes, in writing. Treat the rules of engagement as binding — if brute-force is not listed, you do not run brute. When you need a target to practice against, the project maintains scanme.nmap.org for exactly that purpose, within its posted terms. Authorization is not a compliance checkbox bolted onto the front of an engagement; it is the thing that distinguishes a professional assessment from an offense.

10 / CodaNmap is a discipline, not a button

The thread running through every section is the same one Fyodor pulled in 1997: a scanner is only as good as the operator's understanding of what the network is actually saying. The advanced scan types are not party tricks; they are different questions, each surfacing a different truth about a stack or a firewall. NSE is not a vulnerability button; it is a programmable lens that rewards reading the script before you run it. Scale is not about turning the timing up; it is about staging the work so the results stay legible and the false positives stay low. And evasion is not about hiding; on an authorized engagement it is about measuring whether the defenders can see you at all.

The canonical references remain the best: the freely available Nmap Network Scanning book for the conceptual depth, the per-script documentation at nmap.org/nsedoc for the engine, and scanme.nmap.org for a lawful place to break things. Read the man page not once but periodically — it changes — and let every scan you run be one you could explain, packet by packet, to the person who authorized it.

Reconnaissance is the cheapest phase of an assessment and the one that determines everything after it. Spend the time here.
