Protocol-Oriented Design:
How Communication Rules Shape the Internet

From the three-way handshake of TCP to the zero-RTT resumption of QUIC, every engineering tradeoff in networked systems begins with a question of protocol design.

by Nicholas Knapp  ·  Networks & Distributed Systems  ·  2025
Contents 1. Foundations 2. TCP vs UDP 3. HTTP/2 vs HTTP/3 4. QUIC Architecture 5. API Design Guidance 6. Challenges & Frontiers References

Protocols are the constitutions of networked systems — invisible legal codes that govern every byte in transit. They dictate not merely how data travels, but what counts as a valid conversation, how failure is handled, and which tradeoffs are baked permanently into the architecture. Understanding protocol-oriented design means understanding why engineers in 2025 still argue about choices made in the 1970s, and why a protocol Google prototyped to speed up YouTube ended up rewriting the foundational transport layer of the entire web.

This article traces the intellectual lineage of network protocol design from its ARPANET origins through the QUIC revolution, examines the key engineering tradeoffs between TCP and UDP, unpacks what changed between HTTP/2 and HTTP/3, and translates these principles into concrete guidance for engineers designing APIs or optimizing system performance.

§ 01Foundations of Protocol Design

A Brief History: From ARPANET to the Modern Stack

The story begins in 1969, when the first node of ARPANET came online at UCLA. Networks existed, but they could not talk to each other. Each used different interfaces, different data rates, and different error controls.[1] The solution came from two DARPA scientists. In 1974, Vint Cerf and Bob Kahn proposed a new method for sending data in digital "datagrams" — packets whose addresses could be read by any machine, but whose contents could only be opened by the intended recipient.[2] They called it the Transmission Control Protocol. It allowed computers to speak the same language, and it set the ARPANET on the path to becoming the internet.[3]

The protocol was formally installed on ARPANET on January 1, 1983, after the US Department of Defense made it standard for all military computer networking.[4] By then, TCP had been split into two layers: TCP for transmission control and IP for routing — the TCP/IP model that still underlies virtually all internet communication today.

What Is a Protocol, Exactly?

A protocol is a set of rules governing how data is formatted and transmitted across a network.[5] These rules operate at different layers of the OSI (Open Systems Interconnection) model, from physical bit transmission at Layer 1 up through application-level semantics at Layer 7. Protocol-oriented design is the discipline of consciously choosing — and often composing — these rules to satisfy specific requirements around reliability, latency, throughput, security, and operational complexity.

Every protocol design decision encodes assumptions about the environment. The National Cyber Security Centre notes that protocols operating in high-latency or constrained environments — such as IoT devices — must balance security guarantees against battery constraints and processing power in ways that protocols running in data centres simply do not.[6] This is not a bug in protocol design — it is the entire point. A protocol is a contract, and like any good contract, it must be explicit about what it promises and what it does not.

A protocol is a contract. The engineering tradeoffs aren't accidents — they are the document.

§ 02TCP vs UDP: The Foundational Tradeoff

TCP (Transmission Control Protocol) and UDP (User Datagram Protocol) are the two core protocols of the Transport Layer — Layer 4 in the OSI model. Both handle end-to-end communication between applications, but they make radically different promises about how that communication happens.[7]

TCP: Reliability at a Cost

TCP is a connection-oriented protocol. Before any data is sent, TCP establishes a formal connection between client and server using a process called the three-way handshake: the client sends a SYN, the server replies with SYN-ACK, and the client acknowledges with ACK.[8] Only then does data transfer begin.

This handshake is not mere ceremony. It enables the core guarantees that make TCP indispensable for so much of the internet: reliable delivery, ordered packet reassembly, flow control to prevent overwhelming receivers, and congestion control that throttles transmission rates when network saturation is detected.[9] TCP's header carries 20 bytes of metadata per packet to support these mechanisms — sequence numbers, acknowledgment numbers, window size, and checksum fields.[8]

The cost is latency and overhead. Every lost packet triggers a retransmission and a wait. Every new connection requires at least one round trip before data flows. On high-latency links — intercontinental connections, satellite, mobile — these overheads accumulate into perceptible delays.

UDP: Speed as a Feature

UDP is connectionless. It sends datagrams from one host to another without establishing a formal connection first — a "fire-and-forget" model.[8] Its header is a mere 8 bytes, compared to TCP's 20, reducing per-packet overhead significantly. UDP performs no error-checking, no reordering, and offers no delivery guarantee: packets may arrive out of sequence, duplicated, or not at all.

For real-time applications, this is a feature, not a bug. In a live video call, a retransmitted packet that arrives 500ms late is useless — worse than useless, since it consumes bandwidth that current data could use. A tiny glitch from a dropped UDP packet is far preferable to the buffering stall that TCP's error correction would impose.[8] For online gaming, where low latency determines whether a player's inputs register in time, UDP is similarly dominant.

The Ossification Problem

One of the most consequential facts about TCP is that it is implemented in operating system kernels and network middleboxes. This makes evolving it extraordinarily difficult. As researchers from the RIPE Atlas measurement study found, attempts to deploy new transport protocols like SCTP were persistently blocked by NATs and firewalls that only understood TCP and UDP — a phenomenon called protocol ossification.[10] This is precisely why QUIC chose UDP as its carrier: UDP "works on most networks," and impairments are largely confined to access networks where simple fallback strategies can work around them.[10]

Modern Protocol Variants

The TCP/UDP binary is not the whole story. The ecosystem includes TCP Fast Open (TFO) for reducing handshake latency, Multipath TCP (MPTCP) for using multiple network paths simultaneously, DTLS for adding TLS-like security to UDP streams, and hybrid approaches like QUIC that build reliability on top of UDP at the application layer.[11]

§ 03HTTP/2 vs HTTP/3: Rewriting the Application Layer

Why HTTP/1.1 Needed to Change

HTTP/1.1, standardized in 1997, required a new TCP connection for each request. Browsers worked around this with connection pooling and domain sharding, but the fundamental inefficiency remained. Slow-loading pages and high connection overhead were structural problems, not implementation bugs.

HTTP/2: Multiplexing Over TCP

HTTP/2 (RFC 7540, 2015) addressed HTTP/1.1's core inefficiency by introducing multiplexing: multiple requests and responses can be interleaved over a single TCP connection simultaneously. It also added binary framing (replacing HTTP/1.1's text-based protocol), header compression via HPACK (reducing the repetitive overhead of sending identical headers on every request), and server push for proactively sending resources a client will need.[12] LinkedIn reported 34% faster page load times after transitioning to HTTP/2.[13]

But HTTP/2 inherited TCP's fatal flaw for multiplexed workloads: head-of-line (HoL) blocking. Because TCP requires packets to be processed in order, a single lost packet blocks all streams on the connection — even those whose data arrived intact. The application layer cannot read any results until the missing packet is retransmitted and received.[12] In a multiplexed environment with many concurrent streams, this makes packet loss disproportionately damaging.

HTTP/3: QUIC Changes Everything

HTTP/3 (RFC 9114, 2022) is not HTTP/2 over UDP. It is HTTP/2 reimagined for the current internet, running over QUIC — a transport protocol that uses UDP as its carrier but builds its own reliability, encryption, and performance stack on top.[14]

The Core Architectural Shift

HTTP/2 runs over TCP, which means transport and TLS negotiation require separate handshakes — typically 2–3 round trips before any data flows. HTTP/3 integrates TLS 1.3 directly into the QUIC transport layer, collapsing these into a single 1-RTT handshake, with 0-RTT resumption for returning clients.[15]

Eliminating Head-of-Line Blocking

QUIC handles each HTTP stream as an independent data stream with its own flow control. A lost packet on one stream does not affect others — CSS and JavaScript files continue downloading while an image retransmits.[16] This architectural separation is the single most important improvement over HTTP/2 for high-concurrency workloads.

Connection Migration

TCP connections are identified by a 4-tuple: source IP, source port, destination IP, destination port. When a mobile user switches from Wi-Fi to a cellular network, their IP address changes and the connection breaks. HTTP/3 over QUIC uses opaque connection IDs instead, allowing connections to migrate seamlessly between network interfaces. This is particularly significant for mobile applications.[16]

Integrated Security

While HTTP/2 strongly recommends but does not strictly require TLS, HTTP/3 mandates TLS 1.3. Encryption is integrated directly into the QUIC layer — not layered on top — which eliminates redundant handshakes and makes it impossible to deploy HTTP/3 without encryption.[15]

Where HTTP/3 Wins — and Where It Doesn't

HTTP/3's advantages are real but situational. It delivers its strongest gains for mobile users on unstable networks, high-latency connections, websites with many concurrent resources (such as e-commerce stores with large product catalogues), and returning visitors who benefit from 0-RTT resumption.[16] A synthetic benchmark comparing intercontinental US–Germany connections found HTTP/3 delivering 25% faster downloads on average, rising to 52% for mobile users on unstable networks.[13]

On stable, low-latency LAN connections or for simple pages with few resources, the performance difference between HTTP/2 and HTTP/3 is often negligible. Moreover, HTTP/3 consumes more CPU and memory than HTTP/2, because encryption and packet processing happen in userspace rather than in the kernel — a cost that is apparently offset by performance gains for companies like Google, Cloudflare, and Akamai, but one that smaller deployments must weigh carefully.[19]

§ 04QUIC: Architecture of a Modern Transport

Origins and Standardization

QUIC was initially designed by Jim Roskind at Google and first deployed in 2012. Google disclosed it to the IETF in 2013, describing it as "an early-stage network protocol experimenting with stream multiplexing over a new flavor of TLS on top of UDP instead of TCP."[21] By 2017, QUIC was being used by nearly all Chrome users for connections to Google services.[22]

The IETF established a QUIC working group in 2016. Critically, the IETF version of QUIC diverged substantially from Google's original implementation — most notably by replacing Google's proprietary QUIC Crypto with standard TLS 1.3 for the cryptographic handshake, and adopting a modular design separating transport, security, and application concerns.[23] In October 2018, the HTTP and QUIC Working Groups jointly named the HTTP mapping over QUIC "HTTP/3." In May 2021, RFC 9000 was published: the complete, formally standardized QUIC specification.[24]

QUIC cuts across the traditionally siloed transport, security, and application spaces in protocol design — a more ambitious undertaking than any single-layer protocol had attempted before.

Technical Architecture

QUIC runs over UDP but is emphatically not "just UDP." It implements its own reliability, congestion control, and stream multiplexing — all inside encrypted packets that middleboxes cannot inspect or interfere with. This is intentional: because QUIC's internal state is encrypted, routers and firewalls cannot ossify it the way they did TCP extensions.[25]

Key Architectural Properties (RFC 9000)

From RFC 9000 — Core Guarantees

Flow-controlled streams for structured communication, enabling multiple independent data channels within a single connection.

Low-latency connection establishment — 0 RTTs in the common case for returning clients.

Network path migration — connections survive IP address changes.

Confidentiality, integrity, and availability — security is not optional; it is intrinsic.[26]

Congestion Control

Unlike TCP, where congestion control algorithms (CUBIC, BBR) are kernel-level choices difficult to update, QUIC's congestion control lives in userspace. RFC 9002 specifies loss detection and an exemplary congestion control algorithm, but implementations can substitute alternatives without kernel changes. This modularity allows rapid iteration on congestion algorithms — a significant advantage for deployment environments where network conditions vary widely.[27]

QUIC in Production

Major technology companies have deployed QUIC commercially at scale. Google, Akamai, Meta, Microsoft, and Cloudflare are among those who have adopted QUIC for production traffic, signalling broad confidence in its practical benefits.[20] Akamai tested HTTP/3 during a live European football streaming event in April 2023, which peaked at 4.16 Tb/s of traffic — one of the largest HTTP/3 deployments in history at that point.[13]

§ 05Applying Protocol Knowledge to API Design

Protocol selection is not just an infrastructure concern — it directly shapes API design, performance characteristics, and client-server contracts. Engineers designing APIs or optimizing networked systems should reason about protocols explicitly, not treat them as someone else's problem.

Latency vs Throughput: The Core Tension

Latency and throughput are not simply traded off against each other in a linear way. Measures that boost throughput — increasing bandwidth, optimizing hardware — can increase latency. Conversely, reducing latency by creating more direct network paths can reduce maximum data transfer capacity.[28] Protocol design makes these tradeoffs structural and often irreversible, which is why understanding them at design time matters more than optimizing at runtime.

Protocol Selection Guide — API Engineering

Choose TCP / HTTP/2 when:

  • Data integrity is non-negotiable (financial transactions, database replication, file transfer)
  • You control both client and server in a stable network environment
  • You need ordered, reliable delivery and can absorb the handshake overhead
  • Your API follows a request-response pattern with moderate concurrency

Choose UDP / QUIC / HTTP/3 when:

  • Low latency is more important than perfect reliability (real-time data, gaming, VoIP)
  • You serve mobile users who frequently switch networks
  • You have many concurrent requests to many resources (high multiplexing benefit)
  • Your clients are geographically distributed with high-latency connections
  • You need 0-RTT resumption for returning users (e.g., authenticated API sessions)

Consider gRPC over HTTP/2 when:

  • You need efficient binary serialization (Protocol Buffers) for microservice communication
  • You need bidirectional streaming within a single connection
  • You have polyglot service environments requiring strongly-typed contracts

General API latency optimization principles:

  • Minimize round trips — batch requests or use bulk transfers instead of multiple individual calls
  • Use connection pooling and persistent connections to amortize handshake cost
  • Apply header compression where headers are repetitive (HPACK in HTTP/2, QPACK in HTTP/3)
  • Implement CDN or edge nodes to reduce physical client-server distance
  • Prefer asynchronous processing over synchronous blocking on the critical path

Protocol Measurability

Measurability has historically been a secondary concern in protocol design, yet it is essential for operational monitoring and debugging.[29] Designing APIs without considering observability — latency histograms, connection failure rates, packet loss metrics — is designing blind. For QUIC-based deployments, this is especially important: because QUIC encrypts its headers, traditional packet inspection tools (tcpdump, Wireshark) cannot decode connection state without decryption keys, and many enterprise firewalls may block QUIC traffic entirely, necessitating HTTP/2 fallback mechanisms.[14]

§ 06Challenges, Innovations, and the Road Ahead

Open Challenges in Protocol Design

Middlebox Interference

Enterprise firewalls and network middleboxes frequently block or degrade QUIC traffic on UDP port 443. This is particularly visible in Asia-Pacific and African networks, where HTTP/3 connections may fail and require transparent fallback to HTTP/2.[30] Protocol designers must build robust negotiation and fallback mechanisms — the Alt-Svc header and DNS HTTPS records are the current mechanisms for signalling HTTP/3 availability without breaking clients that cannot use it.

Resource Cost of Userspace Encryption

Because QUIC handles TLS in userspace rather than delegating to kernel-level TCP+TLS, its CPU and memory costs are higher per connection. This is a significant concern for high-connection-count servers at smaller scale, though major CDNs have determined the benefits outweigh the costs at their traffic volumes.[19]

Congestion Control Uncertainty

QUIC's new transport protocol means getting congestion control right requires experimentation and tuning. As Cloudflare noted during HTTP/3's early rollout, the choice of congestion control algorithm materially affects performance, and new implementations need time to mature to the reliability of decades-old TCP implementations.[17]

Security and Denial-of-Service

QUIC's use of UDP introduces denial-of-service risks that TCP's stateful design partially mitigates. UDP amplification attacks — where a small spoofed request triggers a large server response — are a known attack vector. RFC 9000 includes address validation mechanisms to limit this exposure, but the threat landscape for QUIC-based services differs from that of TCP services and requires careful threat modelling.[31]

Innovations on the Horizon

Multipath QUIC (MPQUIC)

Experimental extensions to QUIC, such as MPQUIC, allow simultaneous use of multiple network paths — similar to Multipath TCP but with QUIC's lower ossification risk. This is particularly promising for mobile devices that can leverage both Wi-Fi and cellular simultaneously for improved throughput and resilience.[20]

QUIC Extensions

The IETF QUIC working group continues standardizing extensions to the core protocol. RFC 9221 defines an Unreliable Datagram Extension to QUIC — essentially native UDP semantics inside a QUIC connection, enabling applications like gaming and video conferencing to use QUIC's connection management without forcing reliability on latency-sensitive streams.[32]

DNS-over-QUIC and Beyond

QUIC is increasingly being used as a transport for protocols beyond HTTP. DNS-over-QUIC (RFC 9250) brings the latency and privacy benefits of QUIC to DNS resolution — a domain historically dominated by UDP due to DNS's single-packet request-response pattern. The modular design of RFC 9000 was explicitly intended to facilitate exactly this kind of extension.[24]

Protocols that are designed with measurability, modularity, and explicit threat models outlast those designed only for the happy path. The internet's next fifty years will be built on choices made today.

Practical Takeaways for Engineers

Protocol-oriented design is not about choosing between TCP and UDP in the abstract. It is about making explicit, documented, measurable decisions about what guarantees your system provides and at what cost. The most resilient networked systems are those whose engineers understood that every protocol is a policy document — and treated it with the same rigor they would apply to any other architectural decision.

When designing an API, ask: What is the worst-case behaviour when a packet is lost? When the client switches networks? When the server is under load? The answers to these questions should drive your protocol selection — not convention, not the path of least resistance, and not the assumption that TCP's reliability makes these questions irrelevant.
