The Overlooked Trillions: Insects, Small Animals, and the Frontiers of Vegan Ethics

From farmed crickets to shellac-coated jelly beans, from mosquito foggers to silk boiled in the cocoon — billions upon billions of small animals are killed in industries most vegans never think about. This guide examines the evidence, the ethics, and what activists can do.

by Nicholas Knapp · Long Read · ~6,000 words · May 7, 2026

Back to all articles

When most people picture the victims of animal agriculture, they think of cows, pigs, chickens, or fish. They rarely picture a mealworm, a cochineal beetle, or a silkworm boiled alive in its cocoon. Yet invertebrates — animals without a backbone — vastly outnumber vertebrates in the scale of human exploitation. One analysis estimates that at least 29 to 59 trillion invertebrates are killed or used by humans every year, and that this figure could be as high as a quadrillion (1015) once agricultural pesticide deaths are included.[1] That is not a typo.

For the vegan movement, this raises hard questions that do not have easy answers. Are insects morally considerable? How do we weigh genuine uncertainty about sentience? What distinguishes avoidable exploitation from unavoidable harm? And crucially: where should activist attention go, given finite time and resources? This article works through the evidence, the ethics, and the practical landscape for those who care about doing the most good for the most animals.

The Science of Insect Sentience

This is not a settled question — but the evidence has shifted dramatically in recent years, and the scientific consensus is moving in a direction that should trouble any animal advocate who has not yet engaged with it.

Insects have long been assumed to be mere biological automata — responsive to stimuli but not truly feeling anything. This assumption, convenient for every industry that exploits them, is increasingly challenged by neuroscience and behavioural research. A 2022 landmark review in Advances in Insect Physiology systematically assessed the neural and behavioural evidence for pain in insects, finding that many species satisfy multiple criteria associated with sentience in vertebrates.[4] Then, in 2023, a striking study published in PLOS ONE inserted human capsaicin (pain-receptor) genes into fruit fly larvae — and the receptors functioned seamlessly, producing the expected pain behaviours. The researchers celebrated this as evidence that pain mechanisms are "highly conserved" across species, inadvertently strengthening the very case they were not explicitly making: that insect pain is real.[5]

In 2024, the New York Declaration on Animal Consciousness, endorsed by researchers from institutions worldwide, explicitly acknowledged a "realistic possibility" of consciousness in invertebrates, including honeybees (Apis mellifera).[6] That same year, a study in Veterinary Sciences argued that honeybees' exclusion from EU animal welfare legislation is "both scientifically unfounded and ethically problematic," given that they demonstrate behavioural and neurological traits comparable to protected vertebrates.[6]

A 2024 survey of animal behaviour scientists found that 67% attributed emotions — including something resembling sentience — to at least some insects, with 25% extending this to most or nearly all insects.[7] Crucially, even professional entomologists largely lack training on insect welfare, suggesting that institutional inertia, not evidence, is driving the prevailing indifference.[7]

The evidence quality for sentience in some insects now matches or exceeds that for lobsters and crabs — animals many people readily grant moral consideration.

What does "evidence of sentience" actually look like for insects? Researchers point to several lines of converging data:

Evidence Type Key Findings Species
Nociception & pain behaviour Withdrawal, guarding, sensitisation after injury; behavioural analogues to chronic pain in Drosophila Fruit flies, crickets
Analgesic self-administration Honeybees will self-administer analgesics after painful stimuli (2017 study)[9] Honeybees

Emotional states / affect Pessimistic cognitive bias after stressors; play behaviour documented in bees Bees, bumblebees
Neuropathic sensitisation Heightened vigilance and sensitisation after nerve injury[10] Fruit flies

None of this proves insects are conscious in the full phenomenological sense. But for a movement that generally applies the precautionary principle to animal welfare — erring on the side of moral caution under uncertainty — the implication is clear: we should treat the possibility of insect sentience seriously, especially at scales involving trillions of individuals.

Insect Farming: The "Sustainable Protein" Question

Insect farming has been marketed as an ethical alternative to conventional meat — low land use, low emissions, efficient protein conversion. The reality is more complicated, and the welfare implications are almost entirely unaddressed.

Between 1 trillion and 1.2 trillion insects are raised on farms every year for food and animal feed, with 79 to 94 billion alive at any one time.[2] These numbers dwarf the global count of farmed vertebrates. The global edible insect market was valued at approximately $3.2 billion in 2022 and is projected to reach $17.6 billion by 2032.[11] One forecast from Grand View Research projects annual sector growth of 16.9% from 2024 to 2030.[11]

Despite this expansion, welfare standards for farmed insects are virtually nonexistent. There are no comprehensive, species-specific standards in any major jurisdiction.[13] The European industry body, the International Platform of Insects for Food and Feed (IPIFF), has published vague welfare guidance that "vaguely attempts to claim that insects might not feel pain" and calls for more research without mandating any protections.[2] This mirrors, in miniature, the early decades of factory farming: an industry scaling rapidly before the ethical and regulatory infrastructure exists to govern it.

Killing Methods and Their Welfare Implications

Farmed insects are typically killed by boiling, blanching in hot water, steam, grinding alive, or being blasted with hot air. Freezing is sometimes used and may be relatively more humane, though research on the relative experience of these methods is almost entirely absent. The scale matters enormously here: a single large black soldier fly facility processes hundreds of billions of individual animals annually. One planned Tyson Foods / Protix joint venture in Nebraska — since suspended — projected the killing of approximately 300 billion individual larvae per year at a single site.[14]

Insect farms also experience significant non-harvest mortality. Mass die-offs from bacterial and viral infections, cannibalism, starvation, and drowning are common; these deaths are typically either discarded or fed back to surviving insects as supplementary chitin.[2] The welfare implications of these deaths have received no systematic study.

For Animal Feed: A Compounded Problem

The majority of the farmed insect industry is not aimed at human food — it supplies insect meal as feed for farmed fish, poultry, and livestock.[14] This creates a moral compounding problem: insects raised and killed to feed other animals being raised and killed. For vegans who have moved away from fish and chicken in part to reduce feed-chain suffering, insect-based feed represents a new, largely invisible link in the same chain.

Insect Protein for Humans

Products aimed at human consumers include cricket flour energy bars, mealworm protein powders, grasshopper-topped snacks, and insect-based pet foods. EU regulation now permits several insect species for human food, and food manufacturers increasingly blend insect protein into mainstream products with minimal labelling emphasis.[11] For vegans, this creates a labelling vigilance challenge distinct from the conventional animal product landscape.

Insect-Derived Products: Hidden Exploitation

Long before insect "protein" became a trend, insects were quietly embedded in the global supply chain — in cosmetics, confectionery, textiles, and food. Most consumers have no idea.

Carmine (E120 / Cochineal Extract / Natural Red 4)

Carmine is a vivid red dye extracted from the dried, crushed bodies of female cochineal beetles (Dactylopius coccus), which feed on prickly pear cacti in South America. It takes approximately 70,000 beetles to produce just 100 grams of carminic acid — the active pigment.[3] Some estimates suggest up to 90 billion cochineal insects are killed annually for this purpose.[15] The dye is found in red fruit juices, yoghurts, candies, strawberry-flavoured products, lipsticks, and blushers. In the US, FDA labelling rules introduced in 2011 now require carmine to be declared on food labels, but it can still appear under aliases: Cochineal Extract, Crimson Lake, E120, Carminic Acid, C.I. 75470.[16]

Shellac (E904 / Confectioner's Glaze)

Shellac is a resin secreted by the female lac bug (Kerria lacca), primarily found in India and Thailand. Used as a food-grade glaze on confectionery (jelly beans, chocolate eggs, pharmaceutical pill coatings), shellac requires between 50,000 and 200,000 individual insects to produce one kilogram of raw resin.[17] According to PETA, producing a single pound of shellac flakes kills approximately 100,000 insects.[15] Shellac is also used as a coating on some fresh fruit and vegetables to extend shelf life, where it may not be declared prominently.

Silk

Silk production (sericulture) involves raising silkworm caterpillars (Bombyx mori), which spin cocoons of continuous filament as part of their metamorphosis. To unwind the single thread, the pupa inside must be killed — typically by boiling or blasting with steam — before it can break the filament by emerging as a moth. China and India account for over 98% of global silk production.[18] Each silk garment may involve thousands of silkworm deaths. So-called "peace silk" (ahimsa silk) allows the moth to emerge first, but is a niche product.

Beeswax & Honey

The vegan position on honey and beeswax is long-established: both involve the exploitation of bees, a species now supported by significant evidence of sentience and emotional states. Honeybee welfare concerns include queen clipping and wing-clipping, artificial insemination, colony culling at season's end, and the replacement of nutritious honey with sugar syrup. The science on honeybee cognition — including evidence of play, pessimistic affect, and pain self-administration[9] — makes the case for avoiding these products considerably stronger than it was a decade ago.

Insect Oils and Pet Foods

Emerging commercial categories include insect-derived oils (from black soldier fly larvae) marketed in functional foods and cosmetics, and insect-based pet foods (marketed as "sustainable" alternatives to conventional pet food proteins). These sectors are growing rapidly and sit below the radar of most animal advocates.

Pest Control and Extermination: The Ethics of Defensive Killing

Pest control is where vegan ethics confronts genuine tension between competing moral goods: human health, safety, and wellbeing on one side; animal suffering on the other. The answers here are far more contested than in the world of consumer products.

The scale of pesticide use is staggering. Global pesticide application kills invertebrates on an almost incomprehensible scale: one Rethink Priorities analysis estimates that including insecticide deaths, the total annual human-caused invertebrate kill count could reach 10 quadrillion (1016).[1] Ground-dwelling insect populations have declined by as much as 98% in some monitored locations since the 1970s, driven significantly by pesticides and habitat loss.[19]

Methods and Their Welfare Profiles

Insecticides. Chemical insecticides cause a wide range of modes of death — neurotoxic paralysis, respiratory failure, desiccation — many of which are slow and may involve considerable distress if insects can suffer. Neonicotinoids, in addition to their devastating effects on pollinator populations, are associated with sublethal welfare harms including impaired navigation, feeding disruption, and physiological stress in bees.[4]

Rodenticides. Anticoagulant rodenticides cause death over several days by preventing blood clotting, resulting in internal bleeding. This is widely considered one of the least humane pest-control methods for vertebrates. Secondary poisoning — where raptors, owls, foxes, and domestic pets consume poisoned rodents — extends the harm significantly through the food chain.[20]

Glue traps. Arguably the most ethically indefensible method in common use. Animals caught in glue traps can remain alive for hours or days, experiencing dehydration, starvation, and severe physical injury as they attempt to escape. Glue traps have been banned in Scotland, and campaigns to ban them elsewhere are ongoing.[21] They also catch non-target animals — birds, lizards, hedgehogs — indiscriminately.

Snap traps. When functioning correctly, snap traps kill rodents quickly, though trap failure can cause significant non-lethal injury and prolonged suffering. They represent a less harmful option than glue traps or slow-acting poisons for situations where lethal control is genuinely necessary.

Mosquito control. Large-scale mosquito control programmes — including aerial insecticide spraying, larviciding of water bodies, and sterile insect technique — involve both direct invertebrate killing and significant non-target species impacts. The calculus here is complicated by the fact that mosquitoes transmit diseases responsible for hundreds of thousands of human and animal deaths annually.

Agricultural pest management. Crop protection is one of the most morally complex areas for vegans. Plant-based diets are frequently and correctly cited as reducing animal deaths, but the calculation is incomplete without acknowledging that crop monocultures, mechanical harvesting, and pesticide regimes kill invertebrates at scale. The key ethical lever is relative harm reduction: a plant-based diet still causes dramatically fewer animal deaths than an omnivorous one, even accounting for crop deaths, because of how many crops go into animal feed under conventional agriculture.[22]

A Framework for Thinking About Pest Control

Vegan ethics commonly distinguishes between several categories of harm that are useful here:

1 Avoidable Exploitation

Deliberately farming or harvesting insects for non-essential purposes (carmine in cosmetics, silk in fashion). The clearest case for vegan rejection: suffering caused for trivial human benefit when alternatives exist.

2 Defensive Action

Killing animals that pose genuine threats to human health (disease vectors, structural pests). Most ethical frameworks permit some defensive harm, though the method and proportionality matter.

3 Ecological Necessity

Harm caused as an unavoidable side-effect of human habitation and agriculture. The vegan imperative is minimisation, not impossible perfection. Systemic advocacy for less harmful methods is more productive than individual guilt.

4 Minimising Suffering

Where harm cannot be fully avoided, choosing the least painful method — live traps over glue traps, exclusion over rodenticide — reflects genuine ethical seriousness about animal welfare.

Veganism does not require moral perfection — it requires moral seriousness. The question is not whether one can live entirely without any insect deaths, but whether one is making genuine efforts to reduce unnecessary harm.

Activist Strategies: Where to Focus

Given the scale, the scientific uncertainty, and the diversity of contexts, where should vegan activists and advocates invest attention? The following strategies are grounded in what is most tractable, most neglected, and most impactful.

  1. Insect Farming Welfare Reform The insect farming sector is currently scaling rapidly without any species-specific welfare standards. This makes it an exceptionally tractable target for early regulatory intervention — as the cage-free egg campaign showed, welfare standards are far easier to establish before an industry has entrenched itself. Advocates should engage with food safety regulators, academic researchers, and the Effective Altruism-aligned organisations (such as Rethink Priorities and the Insect Institute) already working in this space to push for mandatory welfare requirements as a condition of operating licences.
  2. Labelling Transparency Campaigns Carmine, shellac, cricket flour, and mealworm protein are frequently hidden behind technical names or inadequately disclosed on food labels. Campaigns modelled on the US FDA's 2011 cochineal labelling rule — which forced explicit disclosure — can increase consumer awareness and give ethical consumers the information they need to choose alternatives. Target food standards agencies and consumer protection bodies.
  3. Research Funding for Insect Welfare Science The evidence base for insect sentience is growing but remains thin relative to vertebrate welfare science. Directing philanthropic funding toward peer-reviewed research on insect nociception, affect, and welfare interventions would both strengthen the scientific case for moral consideration and create pressure for regulatory attention. Key researchers include those at Rethink Priorities, Queen Mary University of London, and the Effective Altruism Forum's insect welfare research community.
  4. Glue Trap Bans Glue traps are one of the most indefensible tools in common pest control use — they cause extreme suffering, are indiscriminate, and can be easily replaced by less cruel alternatives. Scotland has banned them; campaigns in England, Wales, and across North America and Australia have built significant momentum. Local ordinances, retailer pledges (several UK retailers have voluntarily phased out glue traps), and legislative petitions are all viable levers.
  5. Vegan-Framed Consumer Education Many committed vegans are unaware of carmine in their yoghurt, shellac on their jelly beans, or silk in their scarf. Simple, non-judgmental educational content about hidden insect-derived ingredients — articles, social media guides, shopping apps — serves both the immediate consumer goal and the broader aim of building a culture that takes insect welfare seriously.
  6. Challenging the "Sustainable Protein" Narrative The insect farming industry's sustainability claims are increasingly contested. Life cycle assessments show that insect protein production can have higher environmental impacts than conventional plant-based feed ingredients when insects are fed on cereals rather than waste streams.[13] Activists should challenge greenwashing in insect product marketing, draw attention to the welfare vacuum, and push for honest comparative analysis.
  7. Promote Integrated Pest Management (IPM) For agricultural contexts, Integrated Pest Management — which prioritises biological controls, habitat management, and targeted interventions over broad-spectrum pesticide application — significantly reduces invertebrate deaths without sacrificing crop yields. Advocacy for public procurement of organic and IPM-certified produce, especially in schools and hospitals, can drive systemic change at scale.
  8. Build Bridges with Mainstream Science The most effective long-term strategy for insect welfare may be scientific legitimisation. The parallel with cephalopod and crustacean welfare is instructive: once the UK's Animal Welfare (Sentience) Act (2022) included lobsters and crabs following the LSE Birch review, commercial and regulatory behaviour shifted rapidly. Peer-reviewed science → legal recognition → industry accountability is a proven pathway. Supporting and publicising insect sentience research builds the first link in that chain.

Conclusion: Ethics at Scale

The vegan movement was built on a foundational insight: that the capacity to suffer, not species membership, is the morally relevant criterion for ethical consideration. Applied consistently, this principle extends far beyond the charismatic megafauna of factory farming into a world of small, largely invisible animals — insects, crustaceans, molluscs — that die in numbers that beggar human imagination.

Insect sentience is not proven. But it is plausible, supported by a rapidly growing body of peer-reviewed science, and taken seriously by a substantial minority of the world's leading animal behaviour researchers. Given that the industries built on insect exploitation operate at a scale of trillions of individuals — with no welfare standards, no regulatory oversight, and almost no public awareness — the precautionary principle demands attention.

This does not mean treating an ant and an elephant as moral equals. It does not mean paralysis in the face of unavoidable harm. And it does not mean that vegans who eat a plant-based diet peppered with carmine-dyed yoghurt are frauds. What it means is that a movement serious about reducing suffering should face these questions honestly — and begin, strategically, to act on them.

The insects are already being farmed by the trillion. The question is whether we will notice in time to shape what that industry becomes.
