Greedy algorithms make the locally optimal choice at each step and never revisit a decision. When that reckless-sounding strategy is correct, it collapses problems that look like search into a single linear sweep. This is a field guide to when it works, why it works, and how to prove it.

Section 01

The greedy contract

A greedy algorithm builds a solution one piece at a time, and at every step it commits to whatever looks best right now — the largest coin, the earliest-finishing meeting, the farthest reachable square — under some fixed, local rule. It never stores alternatives, never backtracks, and never reconsiders a choice once made.

That is the whole contract, and it is what makes greedy both fast and dangerous. Where dynamic programming explores an exponential space of decisions and memoises the overlap, and where backtracking enumerates and prunes, a correct greedy simply walks forward. The reward is dramatic: most problems on this page run in O(n) or O(n log n) time and O(1) extra space. The risk is equally dramatic: a greedy rule that is almost right is simply wrong, often on inputs you would never think to test.

The classical theory12 frames correctness around two properties a problem must have before greed is safe. The first is the greedy-choice property: there exists an optimal solution that is consistent with the locally optimal choice — you never have to look ahead or revise to reach an optimum. The second is optimal substructure: once you have made that first greedy choice, what remains is a smaller instance of the same problem, and an optimal solution to the whole contains optimal solutions to the parts.

The mental model

Dynamic programming asks “what is the best I can do from every state?” and remembers all the answers. Greedy makes a bet that one choice is always safe and pays nothing to remember the rest. DP is a search that has been compressed; greedy is a search that has been eliminated.

Because elimination is so much cheaper than compression, the interesting question is never “how do I code a greedy?” — the loop is usually trivial — but “am I allowed to?”. The rest of this guide is organised around that question. We first learn to recognise the smell of a greedy problem, then learn the two proof techniques that turn a hunch into a guarantee, and then work through ten canonical problems where the greedy rule is subtle enough to be worth seeing.

Section 02

Recognising a greedy problem

There is no decision procedure for “is this greedy?” — if there were, P would look very different — but there is a reliable set of signals.35 None is sufficient alone; together they justify trying a greedy and then proving it.

  • You are asked for an optimum over a sequence of choices. A maximum, a minimum, the fewest steps, the largest subset. Greedy is an optimisation technique; if there is nothing to optimise, the question does not arise.
  • A local quantity totally orders the choices. Intervals have an earliest end; items have a value-to-weight ratio; jumps have a reach. When the candidates can be sorted by a single key that captures “goodness”, sorting plus a sweep is the natural shape.
  • An exchange argument feels available. If you can imagine taking any optimal solution and swapping one of its choices for the greedy choice without making it worse, you are most of the way to a proof. We formalise this below.
  • The future does not punish a locally good choice. Taking the best option now must not poison your remaining options. When a choice can strand you — as in 0/1 knapsack — greedy fails and you reach for DP.

Two cheap habits save enormous time. First, try to break your own rule with a tiny adversarial input before writing a line: a three-element array, a coin system like {1, 3, 4}, an interval that is contained inside another. A single counterexample is a complete disproof. Second, if you cannot break it in a few minutes, look for the exchange argument — the act of searching for the proof usually reveals whether one exists.

Section 03

Proving a greedy correct

Two proof techniques cover almost every greedy you will meet in competitive programming. Both are worth internalising as templates, because once you can fill in the template the correctness argument writes itself.

The exchange argument

The exchange argument shows that the greedy choice is never a mistake by transforming an arbitrary optimal solution into the greedy one, one swap at a time, never decreasing its quality.2 The skeleton is always the same:

  1. Let G be the greedy solution and O any optimal solution, and suppose they first differ at some choice.
  2. Show you can replace O's choice at that point with G's choice, producing a solution O' that is still feasible and no worse than O.
  3. Since O was optimal, O' is optimal too. Repeat; each step makes the optimal solution agree with the greedy in one more place.
  4. After finitely many exchanges, O has been turned into G without ever losing optimality — so G is optimal.
Worked skeleton — interval scheduling

To maximise the number of non-overlapping intervals, greedy keeps the interval with the earliest finish time. Suppose an optimal solution's first interval finishes later than the greedy's first interval. Swap it for the greedy one: it finishes no later, so it cannot collide with anything the optimal solution scheduled afterwards. The swapped solution is still valid and has the same count — still optimal. Induct on the remainder. The earliest-finish choice is therefore always safe.

Greedy stays ahead

The second technique proves an invariant: after each step, the greedy's partial solution is at least as far along as any other solution's partial solution by the same measure.1 You define a notion of progress, show greedy leads after step one, and show that if it leads after step k it still leads after step k+1. Because greedy is never behind at any prefix, it cannot lose overall.

This is the cleaner lens for the Jump Game family: the measure of progress is “farthest index reachable using at most j jumps,” and you show greedy maximises it at every j. We use it explicitly when we reach Jump Game II.

Discipline

“It passed the examples” is not a proof. Greedy failures hide in structure, not in size — the coin system {1, 3, 4} breaks the obvious change-making greedy at the value 6, which a casual test set sails right past. Either exhibit the exchange/stays-ahead argument, or distrust the rule.

Section 04 · Problem

Jump Game

LeetCode 55 Medium Pattern: farthest reach

You are given an integer array nums. You start at index 0, and nums[i] is the maximum jump length from position i — from i you may land on any index up to i + nums[i]. Return true if you can reach the last index.8

The greedy choice

The naive instinct is to search: from each cell, try every jump and recurse. That is exponential, and memoising it gives an honest O(n²) DP. Greedy throws all of that away by tracking a single number — the farthest index reachable so far. Sweep left to right; at each index i that is still within reach, relax the frontier to max(farthest, i + nums[i]). If you ever stand on an index that lies beyond the frontier, no earlier cell could launch you here, and the answer is false.

Why one number suffices

Reachability is an interval, not a set of scattered islands. If index i is reachable and you can reach i + nums[i] from it, then every index in between is reachable too — you can always stop short. So the reachable region is exactly [0, farthest], and a single high-water mark describes it completely.

Correctness is a one-line stays-ahead argument: after processing index i, farthest equals the maximum index reachable using only cells in [0, i]. No alternative bookkeeping can reach further, because it would have to use a launch cell we already considered. If the loop never finds i > farthest, the frontier reaches n − 1.

Variations & pitfalls

Early exit. You can return true the instant farthest ≥ n − 1; there is no need to walk the tail. Off-by-one on the guard. The check is i > farthest, not ≥ — standing exactly on the frontier is fine. Reachable-from-end variant. Some phrasings ask whether you can reach index 0 from the end by jumping left; mirror the sweep. Minimum jumps is the natural follow-up — that is the next problem.

Section 05 · Problem

Jump Game II

LeetCode 45 Medium Pattern: BFS frontier / farthest reach

Same array, same jump rule, but now you are guaranteed you can reach the last index and asked for the minimum number of jumps to get there. The reachability sweep becomes a counting sweep.

The greedy choice

Think of it as breadth-first search collapsed into a line. With zero jumps you can reach [0, 0]. With one jump you can reach everything launchable from that range; with two jumps, everything launchable from that range, and so on. Each “level” is a contiguous interval, and the answer is the number of levels needed to cover index n − 1.

Implement it with two markers: curEnd, the right edge of the current level, and farthest, the best reach discovered while scanning it. Sweep i from 0 to n − 2, always relaxing farthest = max(farthest, i + nums[i]). When i hits curEnd, you have exhausted the current level: increment the jump count and advance curEnd to farthest.

Why this is optimal — greedy stays ahead

Let R(j) be the farthest index reachable using at most j jumps. A simple induction shows the algorithm's curEnd after its j-th increment equals exactly R(j): every cell in level j−1 is scanned before the jump is counted, so farthest has already absorbed the best launch in that whole range. No strategy can exceed R(j) with j jumps by definition, so the first level whose interval covers n−1 gives the minimum jump count.

func jump(nums []int) int {
    jumps, curEnd, farthest := 0, 0, 0
    for i := 0; i < len(nums)-1; i++ {
        if i+nums[i] > farthest {
            farthest = i + nums[i]
        }
        if i == curEnd {
            jumps++
            curEnd = farthest
        }
    }
    return jumps
}

    for (let i = 0; i < nums.length - 1; i++) {

Variations & pitfalls

Stop at n − 2. Iterating to the last index can over-count by one: arriving at the final cell needs no further jump. The guard against a redundant jump is folded into the loop bound here, which is why no if i < n-1 check is needed. Reconstruct the path by recording which index supplied farthest at each level. Beware usize in Rust: the backward-style variants underflow, so the forward two-marker form shown here is the safe one.

Section 06 · Problem

Gas Station

LeetCode 134 Medium Pattern: running balance & reset

There are n gas stations in a circle. Station i offers gas[i] units of fuel, and driving from i to i + 1 costs cost[i]. With an empty tank and unlimited capacity, return the index of the station from which you can complete the full loop, or −1 if none exists. The answer is guaranteed unique when it exists.

The greedy choice

Two observations make this linear. First, a feasibility check: if the total fuel is less than the total cost, no start can work, full stop. Second, and less obvious, a reset rule: scan once accumulating a running tank; whenever the tank dips below zero at station i, no station in the stretch you just drove can be the answer either — so jump the candidate start to i + 1 and zero the tank.

Why the reset skips a whole range

Suppose you start at s and first go negative arriving at i + 1. For any intermediate station k with s < k ≤ i, the partial tank from s to k was non-negative (otherwise you would have failed earlier), so starting at k instead gives you less or equal fuel on reaching i — it also fails. Hence none of s..i can start a successful loop, and the next viable candidate is i + 1. One forward pass tries every maximal candidate exactly once.

If the summed surplus over the whole array is non-negative, the last candidate you reset to is guaranteed to work — so you return it without a second loop. The two facts combine into a single sweep that maintains both a global total and a resettable tank.

func canCompleteCircuit(gas []int, cost []int) int {
    total, tank, start := 0, 0, 0
    for i := 0; i < len(gas); i++ {
        diff := gas[i] - cost[i]
        total += diff
        tank += diff
        if tank < 0 {
            start = i + 1
            tank = 0
        }
    }
    if total < 0 {
        return -1
    }
    return start
}

Variations & pitfalls

Don't conflate the two accumulators. total never resets — it answers feasibility; tank resets — it finds the start. Uniqueness is what licenses returning the first surviving candidate; without it you would need more care. Brute force tries every start in O(n²); the reset insight is the whole game. Circular indexing is avoided entirely here because feasibility plus a single linear candidate sweep proves the loop closes.

Section 07 · Problem

Partition Labels

LeetCode 763 Medium Pattern: farthest reach on last-occurrence

Given a string s, partition it into as many contiguous pieces as possible so that each letter appears in at most one piece. Return the list of piece sizes.

The greedy choice

This is the Jump Game frontier wearing a different costume. Precompute the last index at which each character occurs. Then sweep, maintaining the right edge end of the current piece as the maximum last-occurrence of every character seen so far. The moment the sweep index i equals end, every character inside the current window has all of its occurrences inside that window — so you cut, record the length, and start the next piece.

The frontier, again

“Farthest index this piece must still extend to” is exactly the reachability frontier of Jump Game, with i + nums[i] replaced by lastOccurrence[s[i]]. The cut condition i == end is the analogue of the frontier going no further. Recognising the shared skeleton is more valuable than memorising either problem.

Greedy maximises the number of pieces because any cut must occur at an index i where end == i: cutting earlier would split some character's occurrences across two pieces, and cutting later would needlessly merge two valid pieces. So every i == end is a forced, and therefore optimal, boundary.

func partitionLabels(s string) []int {
    var last [26]int
    for i := 0; i < len(s); i++ {
        last[s[i]-'a'] = i
    }
    result := []int{}
    start, end := 0, 0
    for i := 0; i < len(s); i++ {
        if last[s[i]-'a'] > end {
            end = last[s[i]-'a']
        }
        if i == end {
            result = append(result, end-start+1)
            start = i + 1
        }
    }
    return result
}

function partitionLabels(s: string): number[] {
    const last = new Array(26).fill(0);
    const base = "a".charCodeAt(0);
    for (let i = 0; i < s.length; i++)
        last[s.charCodeAt(i) - base] = i;
    const result: number[] = [];
    let start = 0, end = 0;
    for (let i = 0; i < s.length; i++) {
        end = Math.max(end, last[s.charCodeAt(i) - base]);
        if (i === end) {
            result.push(end - start + 1);
            start = i + 1;
        }
    }
    return result;
}
Variations & pitfalls

Alphabet assumption. A fixed 26-slot array is O(1) space for lowercase input; for arbitrary Unicode use a hash map and the bound becomes O(k). Track only the right edge. You need the running end, not the full set of seen characters. Return boundaries instead of sizes if a downstream step needs the actual substrings. The skeleton recurs in “merge intervals”-style problems where each element induces a reach.

Section 08 · Problem

Non-overlapping Intervals

LeetCode 435 Medium Pattern: sort by endpoint

Given a set of intervals, return the minimum number you must remove so that the rest do not overlap. Touching at an endpoint ([1,2] and [2,3]) does not count as overlap.

The greedy choice

Removing the fewest is the same as keeping the most, which is the classic activity-selection problem. Sort by right endpoint and greedily keep an interval whenever its start is at least the end of the last kept interval. Everything you do not keep is a removal.

Why earliest-finish is safe — the exchange argument in full

Among all intervals, the one that finishes earliest belongs to some maximum non-overlapping set: take any optimal set, look at its earliest-finishing member, and swap in the globally earliest-finishing interval. It ends no later, so it cannot overlap anything the optimal set scheduled after it; the swap preserves both feasibility and size. Fix that first choice, delete every interval that overlaps it, and recurse on the rest — the same argument applies. Greedy keeps a maximum-size compatible set, so its complement is the minimum removal.

Sorting dominates the cost at O(n log n); the sweep that counts kept intervals is linear. Track only the end of the last kept interval — no data structure beyond a single variable is required.

LeetCode 452 Medium Pattern: sort by endpoint

Balloons are given as horizontal intervals [start, end]. An arrow shot vertically at coordinate x bursts every balloon whose interval contains x. Return the minimum number of arrows that bursts all of them.

The greedy choice

This is the same endpoint-sorting skeleton as the previous problem, read as a covering rather than a packing. Sort by right endpoint. Shoot the first arrow at the end of the earliest-finishing balloon; it bursts that balloon and every later one that starts at or before that coordinate. When you meet a balloon whose start lies strictly beyond the current arrow, you need a fresh arrow, placed at its end.

Why the earliest end is the best arrow position

The earliest-finishing balloon must be hit by some arrow, and placing that arrow at its right endpoint is the most rightward — hence most generous — position that still bursts it. Any arrow that bursts it sits at or before that coordinate, so the rightmost choice bursts a superset of what any other choice would. Greedy therefore never uses more arrows than necessary; the exchange argument mirrors interval scheduling exactly.

The structural twin of “non-overlapping intervals” is worth stating plainly: the number of arrows equals n minus the number of removals there, because each maximal group of mutually overlapping balloons is exactly one arrow and exactly one kept interval. Two problems, one idea.

Best Time to Buy & Sell Stock II

LeetCode 122 Medium Pattern: sum of positive deltas

Given daily prices, you may buy and sell as many times as you like — but you must sell before buying again (you hold at most one share). Maximise total profit.

The greedy choice

Forget about picking peaks and valleys. The maximum profit is simply the sum of every positive day-to-day gain: Σ max(0, prices[i] − prices[i−1]). Whenever tomorrow is higher than today, pocket the difference; whenever it is lower, do nothing.

Why capturing every up-tick is optimal

Any profitable trade from a low day to a higher day later telescopes into a sum of consecutive daily differences along the way: buying at p₃ and selling at p₄ earns (p₃₃₁−p₃) + (p₃₃₂−p₃₃₁) + … + (p₄−p₄₃₁). Negative terms in that chain only hurt, and the no-overlap rule lets us choose each daily segment independently. So the optimum keeps exactly the positive daily steps and discards the negative ones — precisely what the sum does.

Because each daily difference is an independent yes/no, there is no interaction between decisions and nothing to search. The transaction-fee and cooldown variants do couple decisions and cross over into dynamic programming — a clean illustration of the boundary between the two techniques.

Section 11 · Problem

Candy

LeetCode 135 Hard Pattern: two-pass greedy

Children stand in a line, each with a rating. Every child gets at least one candy, and any child must receive strictly more candy than an immediate neighbour with a lower rating. Return the minimum total candy.

The greedy choice

One constraint looks left, the other looks right, so satisfy them in two independent passes. Give everyone one candy. Sweep left to right: if a child outranks the child on their left, give them one more than that neighbour. Sweep right to left: if a child outranks the child on their right, raise them to at least one more than that neighbour — taking the maximum of the two demands rather than overwriting.

Why the max of two passes is exactly minimal

Each pass enforces one directional constraint with the smallest legal increment: after the left pass, every left-side rule holds with the least candy; after the right pass, every right-side rule holds. Taking the elementwise maximum satisfies both simultaneously, and it cannot be reduced anywhere — lowering any child below that maximum would violate whichever constraint produced it. Minimality is therefore pointwise, hence global.

Both passes are linear and the bookkeeping is an array of counts, so the whole solution is O(n) time and O(n) space. A constant-space variant exists by counting up/down run lengths, but the two-pass version is the one worth remembering.

Section 12 · Problem

Task Scheduler

LeetCode 621 Medium Pattern: most-constrained first

You are given task labels and a cooldown n: two runs of the same task must be separated by at least n intervals, during which the CPU may run a different task or stay idle. Each task takes one interval. Return the minimum number of intervals to finish them all.

The greedy choice

The most frequent task is the bottleneck — it forces the skeleton of the schedule. If it occurs maxFreq times, lay those copies down first, separated by gaps of length n, forming maxFreq − 1 frames of width n + 1 plus a final copy. Other tasks (and, if necessary, idles) fill the gaps. This yields the closed form:

answer = max( (maxFreq − 1) × (n + 1) + numMax , totalTasks ) maxFreq = count of the most frequent task numMax = how many distinct tasks share that maximum frequency
Why the formula is both a lower and an upper bound

The frame structure is unavoidable: the most frequent task alone needs (maxFreq−1)×(n+1)+numMax intervals just to respect its own cooldown, so that value is a lower bound. It is also achievable — filling the gaps with the remaining tasks in decreasing-frequency order never violates a cooldown, because each task is placed at most once per frame. When tasks are so plentiful that the gaps overflow, no idles are needed and the answer is simply totalTasks. The maximum of the two is therefore exact.

The whole computation is a frequency count and an arithmetic expression — O(total) time, O(1) space over a fixed alphabet. The greedy “schedule the most constrained element first” is the same instinct that drives graph colouring heuristics and exam timetabling.

Section 13 · Problem

Fractional Knapsack

Classic Pattern: ratio / unit value Contrast with 0/1 DP

Given items with values and weights and a knapsack of capacity W, maximise the value carried. Crucially, items are divisible — you may take any fraction of an item and earn the same fraction of its value.

The greedy choice

Divisibility is exactly the property that makes greed optimal here. Compute each item's value-to-weight ratio, sort items by that ratio in descending order, and pour them into the sack greedily — whole items while they fit, then a fraction of the next item to top off the capacity precisely.

Why density ordering is optimal — exchange argument

Suppose an optimal packing includes some weight of a lower-density item while leaving capacity-equivalent weight of a higher-density item unused. Swap an infinitesimal slice: remove a unit of the lower-density item and add a unit of the higher-density one. Total weight is unchanged but value strictly rises — contradicting optimality. So no optimal solution can prefer a lower density while a higher one remains, which is precisely the greedy ordering. Fractions make the swap always possible; that is the hinge.

The boundary: 0/1 knapsack is NOT greedy

Remove divisibility — items must be taken whole — and the same density-first greedy fails. With capacity 50 and items (value 60, weight 10), (100, 20), (120, 30), greedy by density takes the first two for 160, but the optimal whole-item choice is the last two for 220. The integrality constraint creates exactly the “a good local choice strands you” situation greedy cannot see, and 0/1 knapsack needs dynamic programming.

// Items are divisible; sort by value/weight density, take greedily.
func fractionalKnapsack(values, weights []float64, capacity float64) float64 {
    n := len(values)
    idx := make([]int, n)
    for i := range idx {
        idx[i] = i
    }
    sort.Slice(idx, func(a, b int) bool {
        return values[idx[a]]/weights[idx[a]] > values[idx[b]]/weights[idx[b]]
    })
    total := 0.0
    for _, i := range idx {
        if capacity <= 0 {
            break
        }
        if weights[i] <= capacity {
            capacity -= weights[i]
            total += values[i]
        } else {
            total += values[i] * (capacity / weights[i])
            capacity = 0
        }
    }
    return total
}

        Arrays.sort(idx, (a, b) ->
            Double.compare(values[b] / weights[b], values[a] / weights[a]));
        double total = 0.0;
        for (int i : idx) {
            if (capacity <= 0) break;
            if (weights[i] <= capacity) {
                capacity -= weights[i];
                total += values[i];
            } else {
                total += values[i] * (capacity / weights[i]);
                capacity = 0;
            }
        }
        return total;
    }
}

function fractionalKnapsack(values: number[], weights: number[], capacity: number): number {
    const n = values.length;
    const idx = Array.from({ length: n }, (_, i) => i);
    idx.sort((a, b) => values[b] / weights[b] - values[a] / weights[a]);
    let total = 0;
    for (const i of idx) {
        if (capacity <= 0) break;
        if (weights[i] <= capacity) {
            capacity -= weights[i];
            total += values[i];
        } else {
            total += values[i] * (capacity / weights[i]);
            capacity = 0;
        }
    }
    return total;
}
Variations & pitfalls

Use floating point for the final fraction, or scale to keep exact rationals if the judge demands precision. Sort by the ratio, not by value or weight alone — either single key is a classic wrong answer. Capacity exhaustion ends the loop: once a fraction is taken the sack is full. The contrast with 0/1 is the single most important lesson on this page about when greedy is licensed at all.

Section 15

When greedy fails

The discipline of this whole subject is knowing the boundary. Three recurring failure modes account for nearly every case where a tempting greedy is simply wrong.15

  • Integrality strands you. 0/1 knapsack is the archetype: density-first greed takes a good light item and then cannot use the capacity it leaves behind. With whole items the local choice poisons the global one, and only DP — trying both “take” and “skip” for each item — recovers the optimum.
  • The local key does not capture the future. The coin system {1, 3, 4} breaks “always take the largest coin”: making 6 greedily gives 4 + 1 + 1 (three coins) while the optimum is 3 + 3 (two). Canonical coin systems happen to be greedy-friendly; arbitrary ones are a DP.
  • Constraints couple the decisions. Add a transaction fee or a cooldown to Stock II and the independence that justified summing positive deltas disappears — each trade now affects the next, and the problem becomes a small state machine solved by DP.
The decision, in one line

If you can exhibit an exchange or stays-ahead argument, use greedy and enjoy the linear runtime. If you can build a tiny input where a locally optimal choice forecloses the global optimum, reach for dynamic programming. The two techniques are partners, not rivals: greedy is what DP becomes when one choice is provably always safe.

Section 16

A practice ladder

A rough order of ascent. Each rung reuses a pattern from the table above; the goal is to state the proof before coding, not merely to pass the tests.4

  1. Warm-up — frontier & deltas. Jump Game, Best Time to Buy & Sell Stock II, Maximum Subarray (Kadane as a reset-greedy). Cement the “one running number” habit.
  2. Interval craft. Non-overlapping Intervals, Minimum Arrows to Burst Balloons, Merge Intervals, Meeting Rooms II. Internalise sort-by-endpoint and the exchange argument until it is automatic.
  3. Counting sweeps. Jump Game II, Partition Labels, Gas Station. Frontiers and resets that produce a count rather than a boolean.
  4. Construction & ordering. Task Scheduler, Candy, Queue Reconstruction by Height, Assign Cookies. Most-constrained-first and multi-pass reasoning.
  5. The boundary. Fractional vs 0/1 Knapsack, Coin Change (general vs canonical), Stock with fee/cooldown. Practise proving that greedy fails — the most useful skill of all.

Work each rung in whichever of the six languages you are sharpening — the switcher in the top bar exists precisely so you can compare idioms across Go, Rust, C++, Java, C# and TypeScript on identical logic.
