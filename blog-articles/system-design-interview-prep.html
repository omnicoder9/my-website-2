System Design Interview Preparation: Requirements, Scale, Tradeoffs, and Common Architectures

System design interviews test how candidates turn vague product prompts into clear requirements, quantified constraints, credible architectures, bottleneck analysis, and reasoned tradeoffs. This article provides a practical preparation framework with examples for chat systems, URL shorteners, social media feeds, and video streaming systems.

1. Executive summary

A system design interview is a structured conversation about how to build a software system under constraints. The interviewer usually cares less about one “perfect” answer and more about the candidate’s reasoning: how they gather requirements, clarify assumptions, estimate scale, select components, identify bottlenecks, evaluate tradeoffs, and communicate design decisions.

Strong candidates do not immediately draw boxes. They first define the scope, users, core actions, data model, access patterns, latency expectations, consistency requirements, availability goals, security boundaries, and growth assumptions. Then they produce an initial architecture, stress it with traffic and failure scenarios, and improve the design iteratively.

Real production architecture frameworks help interview preparation. The AWS Well-Architected Framework organizes architecture evaluation around operational excellence, security, reliability, performance efficiency, cost optimization, and sustainability [1]. Google SRE literature defines service level objectives as measurable reliability targets based on service level indicators, and recommends concrete latency targets rather than vague statements such as “fast” [2,3]. CAP theorem research shows that distributed systems cannot simultaneously provide perfect consistency, availability, and partition tolerance under network partitions, which is why consistency and availability discussions are central to many design interviews [4].

2. How system design interviews work

A typical system design interview lasts around 45 to 60 minutes and begins with an intentionally broad prompt such as “Design Twitter,” “Design a URL shortener,” “Design WhatsApp,” or “Design YouTube.” The interviewer expects the candidate to drive the conversation. The exact scoring rubric varies by company and level, but most interviews evaluate scope control, requirement analysis, architecture quality, tradeoff reasoning, operational awareness, and communication.

A common interview flow

PhaseGoalCandidate output
Clarify the problemTurn a broad prompt into a bounded design problem.Users, use cases, assumptions, non-goals.
RequirementsSeparate functional behavior from quality constraints.Functional requirements, non-functional requirements, APIs.
Scale estimationQuantify traffic, storage, bandwidth, and hot paths.Back-of-the-envelope calculations.

The interview is interactive. A strong candidate periodically checks whether the interviewer wants a deeper dive into data modeling, APIs, reliability, scale, or a specific subsystem. That behavior matters because real architecture work involves aligning stakeholders around constraints and tradeoffs, not silently producing diagrams.

3. Requirement gathering techniques

Requirement gathering is the foundation of the interview. Functional requirements describe what the system must do. Non-functional requirements describe quality attributes and constraints such as latency, availability, reliability, scalability, security, durability, privacy, observability, cost, and maintainability. Martin Fowler notes that architectural characteristics are often called non-functional requirements or “ilities,” and that teams should communicate the value of those characteristics rather than treating them as abstract technical preferences [5].

3.1 The “users, actions, objects, qualities” method

A useful interview technique is to identify four things before drawing architecture:

  • Users: who uses the system, and are there admins, content creators, consumers, bots, or internal systems?
  • Actions: what must each user do, such as send a message, shorten a URL, follow a user, upload a video, or read a feed?
  • Objects: what domain objects exist, such as users, messages, links, posts, feeds, videos, comments, or sessions?
  • Qualities: what must be fast, durable, consistent, secure, highly available, or cheap?

3.2 Clarifying assumptions

Interview prompts are deliberately incomplete. Clarifying assumptions demonstrates product thinking and prevents designing the wrong system. Good assumptions should be explicit, reasonable, and revisited when they drive architecture.

Question typeExample questionsWhy it matters
ScopeShould the chat app support one-to-one only, or also groups, attachments, search, and read receipts?Prevents overbuilding and helps prioritize the core design.
ScaleHow many daily active users, requests per second, writes per second, or videos per day?Determines storage, traffic, partitioning, caching, and queueing needs.
LatencyWhat is the expected p95 latency for message delivery, feed load, redirect, or video startup?Turns “fast” into measurable design constraints.
ConsistencyIs it acceptable for counters, feeds, or recommendations to be eventually consistent?Influences database, cache, queue, and replication choices.
DurabilityCan messages, videos, or analytics events be lost?Determines write path, acknowledgments, replication, and backups.
Abuse and securityDo we need rate limits, spam detection, privacy controls, or access control?Protects system integrity and user data.

Interview phrase that works

“I’ll first scope the product to the essential flows, then quantify traffic and data volume. After that I’ll draw a high-level design and deep dive into the highest-risk path.”

4. Functional vs. non-functional requirements

Functional requirements specify externally visible behavior. Non-functional requirements specify how well the system must behave and under which constraints. For interview purposes, the best non-functional requirements are quantified and tied to a product path. “Low latency” is weak; “URL redirects should complete under 100 ms p95 from edge locations” is stronger.

SystemFunctional requirementsNon-functional requirements
ChatSend messages, receive messages, maintain conversations, show presence, support offline delivery.Low message-delivery latency, high durability, ordering within a conversation, availability, abuse prevention.
URL shortenerCreate short links, redirect users, manage link metadata, track analytics.Extremely low redirect latency, high availability, high read-to-write ratio, collision avoidance, rate limiting.
Social feedCreate posts, follow users, generate feed, like/comment/share.Low feed-read latency, high fanout scalability, eventual consistency for feeds and counters, ranking quality.
Video streamingUpload video, transcode, store, search, play, resume, recommend.High bandwidth efficiency, low startup time, adaptive bitrate streaming, CDN distribution, durable storage.

4.1 SLIs, SLOs, and interview-quality metrics

Google SRE defines an SLO as a target value or range for a service level measured by an SLI [2]. The SRE Workbook recommends multiple latency thresholds when long-tail latency matters, such as measuring both 90th and 99th percentile behavior [3]. In interviews, this means candidates should talk in terms of p50, p95, and p99 latency, availability targets, error rates, queue lag, and data durability rather than vague adjectives.

Good NFR

“Feed reads should return the first page in under 300 ms p95 for active users.”

Weak NFR

“The feed should be fast and scalable.”

5. Whiteboarding architectures

Whiteboarding is a communication exercise. The diagram should show the main components, data flow, external dependencies, storage systems, caches, queues, and failure-sensitive boundaries. Avoid drawing every class, table, and microservice. The best interview diagrams are layered and easy to narrate.

5.1 A reusable high-level template

Clients | v DNS / CDN / Load Balancer / API Gateway | v Application Services | | | | | +--> Async Workers / Stream Processors | | | +--> Cache | +--> Primary Database / Search Index / Object Storage | +--> Replicas / Backups / Analytics Pipeline

5.2 API-first thinking

APIs make requirements concrete. HTTP semantics, status codes, and caching behavior are standardized by RFC 9110, which defines the general architecture and semantics of HTTP [6]. In interviews, API sketches do not need to be exhaustive, but they should show request shape, response shape, idempotency, authentication, pagination, and rate limits where relevant.

Example: URL shortener APIs

POST /api/links

5.3 Data model sketches

Data models should be shaped by access patterns. For a feed system, reads may dominate and precomputed timelines may matter. For a URL shortener, redirect lookup by short code is the primary hot path. For chat, fetching recent messages by conversation and delivering unread messages are central.

6. Estimating scale

Back-of-the-envelope estimation helps determine whether a simple design is enough or whether the system needs partitioning, caching, asynchronous processing, or specialized storage. The goal is not perfect arithmetic; the goal is to reason from product assumptions to architecture.

6.1 Estimation checklist

  • Users: daily active users, monthly active users, peak concurrency.
  • Reads and writes: requests per second, read/write ratio, peak-to-average multiplier.
  • Storage: records per day, bytes per record, retention period, replication factor.
  • Bandwidth: upload and download volume, media size, CDN offload.
  • Indexes: search, lookup, ranking, and secondary access paths.
  • Growth: expected 10x path and which dimension grows first.

6.2 Example estimation: URL shortener

Assumptions:
- 10 million new links per month.
- 100 redirects per created link on average.
- Short code metadata is about 500 bytes.
- Retain links for 5 years.

Storage:
10M links/month * 12 months/year * 5 years = 600M links
600M * 500 bytes ≈ 300 GB raw metadata

Redirect traffic:
10M links/month * 100 redirects/link = 1B redirects/month
1B / 30 days / 86,400 seconds ≈ 386 redirects/second average
If peak is 10x average, design for roughly 4,000 redirects/second.

Interview warning

Always state assumptions before calculations. If the interviewer gives different numbers, update the design rather than defending the original estimate.

7. Bottleneck analysis

A bottleneck is the limiting resource or coordination point that prevents the system from meeting its requirements. Good candidates identify likely bottlenecks, explain why they matter, and propose mitigations with tradeoffs.

BottleneckSymptomsMitigationsTradeoffs
Database hot keyOne shard receives disproportionate traffic.Hash partitioning, key salting, caching, denormalization.More complex reads, rebalancing, possible consistency issues.
Fanout explosionCelebrity user posts generate millions of feed writes.Hybrid push/pull feeds, special handling for high-follower accounts.More complex feed generation and freshness behavior.
Cache miss stormDatabase overload after cache expiration or failure.TTL jitter, request coalescing, stale-while-revalidate, prewarming.Staleness and complexity.
Queue backlogDelayed processing, increasing consumer lag.More consumers, partitioning, batching, backpressure.Ordering constraints and duplicate processing.
Network egressHigh cost or latency for media delivery.CDN, regional replication, compression, adaptive bitrate.Cache invalidation, content propagation delay.

7.1 Observability as a design concern

Observability is not an afterthought. A system design answer should mention logs, metrics, traces, dashboards, alerts, SLOs, and failure-mode visibility for critical paths. The SRE Workbook includes chapters on monitoring and alerting on SLOs, reflecting the production importance of measuring service behavior rather than merely deploying components [7].

8. Tradeoff discussions

System design interviews heavily reward explicit tradeoff reasoning. Distributed systems often force choices between latency, durability, availability, consistency, cost, operational complexity, and development speed.

8.1 CAP theorem in interviews

Gilbert and Lynch formalized Brewer’s conjecture by proving limits on simultaneously achieving consistency, availability, and partition tolerance in asynchronous networks [4]. In interview language, this does not mean systems simply “choose two” forever. It means that when partitions or communication failures occur, designers must decide which operations preserve consistency, which continue serving, and which degrade or fail safely.

8.2 Common tradeoffs

ChoiceBenefitsCostsTypical interview use
SQL databaseTransactions, relational integrity, mature tooling.May need sharding or replicas at high scale.Payments, metadata, URL mappings, user accounts.
NoSQL key-value storeHigh throughput and horizontal scaling for simple access patterns.Limited joins, weaker query flexibility, consistency tradeoffs.Sessions, feed entries, short-code lookup, message inboxes.
CacheLower latency and database load.Staleness, invalidation, cache stampedes.Feeds, profiles, URL redirects, video metadata.
Message queue / logDecouples producers and consumers; smooths bursts.Duplicates, ordering, retries, operational complexity.Notifications, feed fanout, video transcoding, analytics.
CDNReduces origin load and improves global latency.Invalidation complexity, propagation delay, cache-control strategy.Video streaming, images, static assets, public redirects.

Apache Kafka is an example of a distributed publish-subscribe messaging system designed for log processing, and its early paper describes architecture and design principles for high-volume data flows at LinkedIn [8]. Such systems are useful in interview designs when asynchronous processing, event history, or fanout pipelines are more appropriate than synchronous request chains.

9. Communicating architecture decisions

Communication is part of the score. The interviewer must be able to follow your reasoning, understand what is in scope, and see why your design choices match the requirements. Strong candidates narrate transitions: “Now that we know redirects dominate writes, I’ll optimize the read path.”

9.1 Decision framing

Decision statement format:

Given:
- Requirement or constraint.
- Scale assumption.
- Failure or performance risk.

I choose:
- Architecture decision.

Because:
- Benefits tied to requirements.

Tradeoffs:
- Costs, limitations, and mitigation.

9.2 Example

“Given that URL redirects are read-heavy and must be very low latency, I’ll cache popular short-code mappings at the edge and in an in-memory cache. The tradeoff is staleness if a link is edited or deleted, so I’ll use short TTLs for editable links and immediate invalidation for abuse takedowns.”

9.3 Handling disagreement

If the interviewer challenges a design, treat it as signal, not failure. Ask what constraint they want to stress: “Are you concerned about hot partitions, consistency, cost, or operational complexity?” Then adapt the design. This demonstrates senior-level collaboration.

10. Common system example: chat systems

Chat systems test realtime delivery, durability, ordering, presence, notifications, and offline behavior. They often use persistent connections such as WebSockets or long polling for delivery, a message store for durability, and asynchronous notification pipelines for offline users.

10.1 Requirements

FunctionalNon-functional
Send and receive messages; create conversations; fetch message history; support online/offline users; optionally support group chat, attachments, typing indicators, read receipts.Low delivery latency, durable messages, ordered messages per conversation, high availability, privacy, abuse prevention, push notification support.

10.2 High-level architecture

Mobile/Web Clients | +--> WebSocket Gateway / Realtime Gateway | +--> Auth Service | +--> Chat Service | +--> Message Store partitioned by conversation_id | +--> Presence Store / Cache | +--> Message Queue | +--> Delivery Workers +--> Push Notification Service +--> Search / Analytics Pipeline

10.3 Deep-dive topics

  • Message ordering: assign monotonic sequence numbers per conversation or use server timestamps plus tie breakers.
  • Delivery acknowledgement: distinguish sent, stored, delivered, and read states.
  • Offline users: persist unread messages and send push notifications through platform providers.
  • Presence: use ephemeral state with heartbeats and TTLs, because presence can tolerate staleness.
  • Groups: large group fanout may require batching, partitions, and separate delivery tracking.

11. Common system example: URL shorteners

URL shorteners are classic because they appear simple but test key design skills: API design, ID generation, read-heavy scaling, caching, redirects, analytics, abuse prevention, and storage estimation.

11.1 Requirements

FunctionalNon-functional
Create short URLs; redirect to long URLs; support custom aliases; expire links; track click analytics; manage malicious links.Very low redirect latency, high availability, high read throughput, collision resistance, rate limiting, durable mappings.

11.2 Architecture

Client | v CDN / Edge Cache | v Load Balancer | +--> Redirect Service ----> Cache ----> Link Mapping Store | +--> Link Creation Service ----> ID Generator | | | +--> Link Mapping Store | +--> Analytics Event Queue ----> Stream Processor ----> Analytics Store

11.3 Short-code generation strategies

StrategyBenefitsTradeoffs
Auto-increment ID encoded in Base62Compact, deterministic, easy to generate.Can reveal scale and requires coordination or ranges in distributed systems.
Random codeHarder to enumerate, simple to distribute.Requires collision detection and retry.
Snowflake-style distributed IDHigh-throughput generation without central database bottleneck.Requires clock and worker coordination.

11.4 Bottlenecks and mitigations

  • Redirect latency: use edge caching and in-memory caches.
  • Database overload: cache hot links and partition by code hash.
  • Analytics write amplification: write click events asynchronously.
  • Abuse: rate limit creation and integrate malware/phishing detection.

12. Common system example: social media feeds

Social feed systems test the candidate’s ability to handle fanout, ranking, freshness, caching, denormalization, eventual consistency, and high read volume. Feed systems are rarely just database queries at scale; they usually need precomputation, caching, or hybrid strategies.

12.1 Requirements

FunctionalNon-functional
Create posts; follow/unfollow users; generate home feed; like/comment/share; fetch profile feed.Low feed-read latency, high availability, scalable fanout, eventual consistency for counts, abuse controls, ranking freshness.

12.2 Push vs. pull vs. hybrid feed generation

ApproachHow it worksBest forTradeoffs
Fanout on writeWhen a user posts, copy the post ID into followers’ home timelines.Fast reads for normal users.Expensive writes for celebrity accounts.
Fanout on readWhen a user opens the feed, fetch posts from followed accounts and rank them.Users who follow few accounts or need fresh ranking.Slow reads and many queries for highly connected users.
HybridPrecompute normal follow relationships; pull from high-follower accounts at read time.Large-scale social feeds.More complex implementation and ranking logic.

12.3 Architecture

Client | v API Gateway | +--> Post Service ----> Post Store ----> Media/Object Store | +--> Social Graph Service ----> Graph Store | +--> Feed Service ----> Feed Cache / Timeline Store | +--> Ranking Service | +--> Event Log / Queue | +--> Fanout Workers +--> Notification Workers +--> Analytics / ML Feature Pipeline

12.4 Deep-dive topics

  • Hot users: handle celebrities separately to avoid fanout storms.
  • Ranking: separate candidate generation from ranking and hydration.
  • Pagination: use cursor-based pagination rather than offset pagination for dynamic feeds.
  • Consistency: tolerate delayed feed updates, but keep user-generated post creation durable.
  • Caching: cache first-page feeds and post metadata, while defining invalidation for deletes and privacy changes.

13. Common system example: video streaming systems

Video streaming systems test large-object storage, upload workflows, transcoding, metadata, search, CDN delivery, adaptive bitrate, playback state, recommendations, and bandwidth cost. HTTP Live Streaming (HLS) is an HTTP-based protocol for transferring multimedia streams and is commonly used with ordinary web servers and content delivery networks; Apple’s HLS documentation notes that HLS adapts playback to available network conditions [9,10].

13.1 Requirements

FunctionalNon-functional
Upload video; transcode to multiple formats; generate thumbnails; search/browse videos; stream playback; record watch progress; serve recommendations.Durable upload storage, low startup time, high playback availability, CDN delivery, adaptive bitrate support, cost-efficient storage and bandwidth.

13.2 Architecture

Uploader Client | v Upload Service ----> Raw Video Object Storage | +--> Metadata Service ----> Metadata DB | +--> Transcoding Queue | +--> Transcoding Workers | +--> Encoded Video Segments / Manifests in Object Storage | +--> CDN | +--> Viewer Client Viewer Client | +--> Playback API / Metadata API +--> Search Service / Recommendation Service +--> Watch History Events ----> Stream Processor ----> Analytics Store

13.3 Deep-dive topics

  • Upload path: use resumable uploads, virus scanning, metadata validation, and object storage.
  • Transcoding: process asynchronously with a queue because video encoding is CPU-intensive and slow relative to request latency.
  • Adaptive bitrate: store multiple encodings so clients can switch quality according to network conditions.
  • CDN delivery: place video segments near viewers to reduce origin load and latency.
  • Cost: optimize storage tiers, retention, CDN cache hit ratio, and egress patterns.

StorageRelational databases, key-value stores, document stores, object storage, search indexes.Match storage to access patterns, consistency, and scale.

Cost and performance considerations should be part of architecture discussion. The AWS Cost Optimization pillar describes designing workloads to achieve business outcomes at the lowest price point [11]. In interviews, this can translate to using CDNs for media, asynchronous pipelines for expensive work, compression, tiered storage, cache hit-rate improvements, and avoiding unnecessary synchronous dependencies.

15. Reusable interview templates

15.1 Opening template

1. Restate the problem.
2. Identify users and core use cases.
3. Ask clarifying questions.
4. Define functional requirements.
5. Define non-functional requirements with numbers.
6. State non-goals.
7. Estimate scale.
8. Draw high-level architecture.
9. Deep dive into the hardest part.
10. Discuss bottlenecks, tradeoffs, failures, and monitoring.

15.2 Requirement template

Functional:
- Users can ...
- System must ...
- Admins/internal services can ...

Non-functional:
- Latency: p95 / p99 target for critical path.
- Availability: target and graceful degradation.
- Durability: what cannot be lost.
- Consistency: strong vs eventual by operation.
- Scale: users, reads, writes, storage, bandwidth.
- Security/privacy: authentication, authorization, abuse.
- Cost/operations: caching, async processing, observability.

15.3 Wrap-up template

Final summary:
- The core read/write paths are ...
- The design scales by ...
- The main bottlenecks are ...
- The most important tradeoffs are ...
- The next improvements would be ...

16. Common mistakes and how to avoid them

MistakeWhy it hurtsBetter behavior
Starting with microservices immediatelyShows solution-first thinking.Start with requirements, access patterns, and scale.
No numbersArchitecture cannot be evaluated without load assumptions.Estimate users, requests, storage, bandwidth, and peak factors.
Ignoring non-functional requirementsMisses the reason system design differs from feature design.Discuss latency, availability, consistency, reliability, security, and cost.
Drawing too much detailConsumes time and hides important flows.Draw major components first, then deep dive where needed.
Not discussing failureProduction systems fail; interviews test resilience thinking.Discuss retries, timeouts, failover, queues, backpressure, and monitoring.
Using buzzwords without tradeoffs“Kafka,” “Redis,” or “Kubernetes” alone does not prove judgment.Explain why the component fits and what complexity it adds.

17. Practice plan

Preparation should combine concept review with timed practice. The goal is to become fluent in a repeatable structure while staying flexible enough to adapt to interviewer prompts.

Week 1: Foundations

Review HTTP, databases, caching, queues, replication, CAP, sharding, load balancing, and SLOs.

Week 2: Core problems

Practice URL shortener, rate limiter, chat, feed, file storage, notification system, and search autocomplete.

Week 3: Deep dives

Run timed mock interviews and focus on bottlenecks, consistency, failure handling, observability, and tradeoffs.

17.1 Self-review rubric

  • Did I clarify scope before designing?
  • Did I state assumptions and quantify scale?
  • Did my architecture match the requirements?
  • Did I identify the hottest path and likely bottleneck?
  • Did I explain tradeoffs instead of listing technologies?
  • Did I discuss failure modes and observability?
  • Did I summarize the design clearly at the end?

19. Conclusion

System design interview preparation is not memorizing diagrams. It is practicing a way of thinking: clarify the problem, quantify the constraints, design the simplest credible architecture, stress the bottlenecks, explain tradeoffs, and adapt when assumptions change.

The strongest answers connect product behavior to engineering constraints. A chat system prioritizes low-latency delivery and message durability. A URL shortener optimizes read-heavy redirect latency and availability. A social feed balances fanout, ranking, freshness, and eventual consistency. A video streaming system optimizes storage, transcoding, CDN delivery, startup latency, and bandwidth cost. In every case, the interview rewards clear reasoning over architectural ornamentation.
