A Theory of Innovation

Where New
Things Come From

Nothing comes from nothing. So where does genuine novelty hide — and how does the world keep reaching it?

38 min read 8,689 words Seven parts 33 sources

● Each amber node is one recombinant step away — the adjacent possible. Tap one to actualize it, and watch the frontier move.

In the winter of 1610, almost no one in Europe believed that mathematics could be independently discovered twice. Genius was singular, providential, a lightning-strike upon a chosen mind. And yet, within a span of a few years on either side of 1670, Isaac Newton in Cambridge and Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz in Hanover each assembled — separately, from different motives, in different notations — the calculus. It was not a fluke. In 1858, Charles Darwin, who had been sitting on his theory for two decades, received a letter from a feverish naturalist in the Malay Archipelago named Alfred Russel Wallace that laid out natural selection almost exactly as Darwin himself had framed it. On the 14th of February, 1876, Alexander Graham Bell filed his telephone patent; on that very same day, Elisha Gray filed a caveat for a strikingly similar device.1

These are not curiosities. In 1922, the sociologists William Ogburn and Dorothy Thomas set out to test whether invention is fundamentally a matter of lone genius, and compiled a list of roughly one hundred and fifty discoveries and inventions made independently by two or more people at nearly the same moment.1 A generation later, the sociologist of science Robert Merton went further, arguing that such "multiples" are not the exception in the history of discovery but the rule — that singletons, the truly solitary breakthroughs, are the anomalies most in need of explanation.2 The pattern is uncomfortable for our mythology. If Newton had never been born, we would still have the calculus. If Bell had overslept, we would still be making telephone calls. The romantic story — the isolated genius conjuring the unprecedented from the void — describes almost nothing about how new things actually arrive.

This essay is about what the romantic story leaves out. It asks a question so basic that it usually goes unasked: where do new things come from? Not new to a person, but new to the world — the first engine, the first proof, the first vaccine. The question has an ancient shadow. Ex nihilo nihil fit, the philosophers insisted: nothing comes from nothing. If that is true — if there is no creation from the void — then every new thing must be made of old things, and novelty is not conjuring but rearrangement. The task of a theory of innovation is to say precisely how rearrangement produces genuine surprise, why the surprise clusters in time and place the way it does, and why most attempts at newness quietly die.

The answer this essay defends has a simple spine and a few crucial complications. The spine is a cycle with three movements. New candidates are generated, most often by combining things that already exist; the promising candidates are varied and refined against the friction of the real; and the variants are selected by an environment that keeps a few and discards the rest — whereupon the survivors become raw material for the next turn of the wheel. Onto that spine we must graft two subtler sources of newness that pure combination and variation miss. The first is exaptation: the theft of a thing from its original context to serve a purpose it was never built for. The second, and deepest, is abstraction: the rare move that changes not the pieces on the board but the rules of the game — and in doing so redraws the entire space of what can be built next. Around all of it sits a single governing idea, borrowed from the study of life itself: that at any moment the world stands at the edge of an adjacent possible, a set of next steps reachable from here, and that every step taken quietly enlarges the set of steps that become reachable after.

Part I

New arrangements of old things

The oldest and sturdiest theory of innovation says novelty is combinatorial: to make a new thing is to wire together things that already exist.

The most influential single sentence in the economics of innovation was written by Joseph Schumpeter, and it is deceptively plain. Development, he argued, consists in the carrying out of new combinations.3 Schumpeter's insight was that economic novelty rarely arrives as a brand-new element dropped into the world. It arrives as a recombination of elements already present: a new product is old materials arranged in a new way, a new method of production is known operations in a new order, a new market is an existing good carried to an untried place. He enumerated five such forms of new combination, and he insisted on a distinction we will need throughout — between the invention, which is the technical fact of the new combination, and the innovation, which is its actual introduction into economic life. The person who works the combination into the world he called the entrepreneur, and the entrepreneur's function was not to be clever but to get the new combination adopted against the inertia of the old.

From this seed grew Schumpeter's most quoted image. The introduction of new combinations does not gently augment the existing order; it attacks it. The railway annihilates the canal, the power loom guts the handweaver, the automobile empties the stables. He called this process creative destruction — "the perennial gale" that "incessantly revolutionizes the economic structure from within, incessantly destroying the old one, incessantly creating a new one."4 The phrase has been so thoroughly absorbed into business cliche that its analytical content is easy to miss. The content is this: novelty and obsolescence are the same event seen from two sides. A new combination does not merely add a possibility; it subtracts the possibilities it renders pointless. Every innovation is also a small extinction.

Schumpeter described the economics of combination. It fell to the complexity economist W. Brian Arthur, nearly a century later, to describe its mechanics — to ask what technology is, such that it evolves by combination at all. His answer, developed in The Nature of Technology, is that a technology is always a combination of components that are themselves technologies, arranged to harness some effect of nature toward a purpose.5 A jet engine is an assembly of compressor, combustor, and turbine; each of those is an assembly of blades, shafts, and bearings; each of those resolves into shaped metal exploiting the physics of gas flow. Because every technology is built from earlier technologies, the whole edifice bootstraps itself upward: in Arthur's striking phrase, technology creates itself out of itself. New devices become the components of the next generation of devices, which become components of the generation after, in an accreting, coral-reef structure of ever more capable combinations. Novelty is not a bolt from outside the system. It is the system folding its own outputs back in as inputs.

This is not merely a metaphor; it is a measurable regularity. When the physicist Hyejin Youn and colleagues examined the entire corpus of United States patents from 1790 to 2010 — classifying each by the technology codes it combines — they found that invention behaves, statistically, exactly like a combinatorial process.6 The overwhelming majority of patented inventions are new combinations of existing technological capabilities rather than the introduction of a genuinely new capability. More striking still, the rate at which entirely new base technologies enter the system has slowed dramatically over two centuries, even as the rate of patenting has exploded — because the number of ways to combine the existing stock grows far faster than the stock itself. The finding recasts the old anxiety that "everything has been invented already." The pool of fundamental building blocks may indeed grow slowly. The space of their possible arrangements does not.

Here the mathematics becomes almost violent. The economist Martin Weitzman built a growth model on precisely this point, and its engine is the simplest fact in combinatorics.7 If you have n distinct ideas and ask how many distinct pairs can be formed from them, the answer is n(n−1)/2 — a quantity that grows not linearly with your stock of ideas but as its square. Add a modest number of new elements and the number of possible new pairings does not creep up; it detonates. Weitzman's argument was that in such a world the binding constraint on growth is not the supply of raw ideas — combinatorics guarantees a practically bottomless well — but our finite capacity to develop, test, and refine the astronomical number of combinations the well makes available. The frontier is not out of reach for lack of doors. It is out of reach because there are more doors than we can ever walk through.

The economist Paul Romer gave this vision its formal place at the center of modern growth theory. In Romer's framework, what distinguishes economic ideas from ordinary goods is that they are nonrival — a recipe, once written, can be used by everyone at once without being used up — and that technological progress is, at bottom, the discovery of new recipes: new instructions for arranging the raw materials of the world into more valuable configurations.8 The number of possible recipes, he observed, dwarfs anything physical intuition prepares us for. The periodic table has fewer than a hundred usable elements; the number of ways to combine even a handful of them into a compound, a process, a device, exceeds the number of seconds since the Big Bang many times over. Growth, in this telling, is not the accumulation of stuff. It is the accumulation of arrangements.

1.1The anatomy of a combination

Abstractions want a body. Consider the printing press, which the writer Steven Johnson uses as the paradigm case of combinatorial invention, and rightly so.9 Johannes Gutenberg around 1440 introduced almost no component that did not already exist. Movable type had been made in China and Korea centuries earlier. Paper had travelled from China through the Islamic world into Europe. The metallurgy for casting small, uniform, durable letters came from the existing craft of the goldsmith. The oil-based ink that would cling to metal type was adapted from the pigments of Flemish painters. And the machine that pressed inked type onto paper with even force was, quite literally, a modified screw press of the kind Rhineland vintners had used for centuries to crush grapes. Gutenberg's genius was not to invent a piece. It was to see that the wine press and the punch and the ink and the paper could be made into a single system whose output was pages — a system whose whole was a revolution and whose parts were entirely ordinary.

What followed is the clearest demonstration on record that a combination can be a hinge of history. As the historian Elizabeth Eisenstein documented in exhaustive detail, the printed book did not merely make copying faster; it changed the conditions under which knowledge itself accumulated — standardizing texts, preserving them against the slow corruption of hand-copying, allowing scholars separated by distance to work from identical pages, and making it possible for an idea to reach a thousand minds before its author died.10 The Reformation, the Scientific Revolution, and the modern research tradition are unthinkable without it. And it began as a recombination of a grape press and a goldsmith's craft.

Gutenberg's genius was not to invent a piece, but to see that ordinary pieces could be made into a system whose output was a revolution.

The pattern has not aged. The device most likely to be within arm's reach as you read this is, by Arthur's account, a near-perfect specimen of combinatorial evolution.5 A smartphone is a telephone fused with a computer fused with a digital camera fused with a satellite-positioning receiver fused with a rechargeable battery fused with a pane of glass that senses the electrical field of a fingertip. Every one of those was a mature technology in its own right, the endpoint of its own long combinatorial lineage. What was new was not any component but the relationship — the decision to route them all through a single sheet of responsive glass governed by software. Here we meet, for the first time, a theme that will recur: much of what we experience as radical novelty is not new material at all. It is a new arrangement of old material, and the newness lives in the wiring.

Part II

The adjacent possible

Combinatorics promises an infinite space of possible things. But only a thin shell of that space is reachable from here — and that shell is what history actually explores.

If the space of possible combinations is effectively infinite, why does innovation feel so constrained — why can we not simply reach in and pull out the cure, the engine, the theory we lack? The answer is that the combinatorial space is not a flat plain across which we may wander freely. It is a structure with a frontier. At any given moment, only certain new things can be built, because building anything requires that its components already exist and already work. You cannot invent the microwave oven before you have harnessed electricity, and you cannot harness electricity before you understand it, and so on down a chain of prerequisites that reaches back to the first sharpened stone. The reachable frontier has a name, and it comes not from economics or engineering but from the theory of life.

The biologist Stuart Kauffman coined the phrase the adjacent possible to describe a deep feature of chemical and biological evolution.11 Consider the set of molecules that actually exist in some primordial soup. There is a further set of molecules that do not yet exist but could exist in a single reaction step from the molecules present — one bond formed or broken away. That set of one-step-away possibilities is the adjacent possible. It is not everything imaginable; it is precisely the ring of things reachable from what is currently actual. And here is the engine of open-ended growth: the moment any molecule in the adjacent possible is actually formed, it joins the set of the actual, and in doing so it opens a new adjacent possible — new reactions, new products, that were not reachable a moment before. Kauffman argued that the biosphere, and indeed the economy, relentlessly explores this expanding frontier, forever pushing into the space of the "adjacent possible" and forever enlarging it by the act of pushing.12 Every door you walk through reveals doors that were not there before you crossed the threshold.

It was Steven Johnson who carried Kauffman's biological idea into the study of human innovation and made it famous.9 His formulation is worth stating precisely, because the concept is often loosely attributed to him when the coinage is Kauffman's and the popularization is Johnson's. The adjacent possible, in Johnson's rendering, is a kind of shadow map hovering at the edge of the present — a chart of all the things the present could become in a single step, but not two. It explains why certain ideas seem to be "in the air," why the same invention surfaces in a dozen workshops at once, and why an idea that arrives too early — Charles Babbage's programmable computer in the 1830s, Leonardo's helicopter — withers for lack of the neighbouring components it needs. To be too far ahead of your time is simply to be reaching for a room to which no door yet leads.

Why does the frontier expand rather than stall? Because knowledge, unlike a physical resource, accumulates without being consumed, and each accumulated piece becomes a possible ingredient for the next combination. The economic historian Joel Mokyr has argued that sustained technological progress depends on the growth of a shared base of underlying knowledge — an understanding of why techniques work, not merely that they work — which widens the space of feasible next inventions and rescues progress from the pattern of isolated tricks that flare and are forgotten.13 As this base thickens, the adjacent possible swells. Things that were unreachable for millennia become, almost suddenly, reachable from several directions at once. Which brings us back to the uncomfortable pattern with which this essay began.

2.1Multiples: the fingerprint of the frontier

If innovation were truly the product of solitary genius, simultaneous independent invention would be a staggering coincidence, and rare. If instead innovation is the exploration of a shared adjacent possible, then simultaneity is exactly what we should expect: once the necessary components are all in place, the next combination becomes reachable for everyone standing at the frontier, and several will reach it at once. This is precisely what Ogburn, Thomas, and later Merton found.12 The multiples are not a curiosity to be explained away. They are the visible fingerprint of the adjacent possible — the observable consequence of a frontier that opens the same doors to everyone at the same time.

This reframing does not abolish genius; it relocates it. The genius is not the one who creates from nothing — no one does that — but the one who perceives, at the frontier, the combination that others standing at the same frontier do not yet see, and who reaches it first. That is a formidable and real ability. But it is an ability exercised upon a stock of available components and within a space of currently reachable possibilities, both of which are given by the age rather than by the individual. Newton himself supplied the correct metaphor, and it was not one of solitary elevation: if he had seen further, it was by standing on the shoulders of giants. The theory of the adjacent possible is the systematic statement of what Newton said in passing. We see from where the accumulated work of others has carried us.

Part III

Descent with modification

Combination explains the leaps. But novelty also arrives by a slower route — the incremental variation of a lineage, filtered by selection, in a process that looks unmistakably Darwinian.

The combinatorial account is powerful but incomplete. It explains how radically new kinds of things appear — by wiring existing components into a new system — but it says little about the long, patient process by which a crude first version becomes a refined and reliable one, or about how whole families of related artifacts arise, each a slight modification of the last. For that we need a second theory, older in its intellectual roots and drawn directly from biology: that technologies, like organisms, evolve by descent with modification.

The historian George Basalla made the case most fully. Against the popular image of invention as a series of abrupt discontinuities — heroic leaps that owe nothing to what came before — Basalla argued for radical continuity.14 Every made thing, he showed, descends from an antecedent made thing. The internal-combustion automobile descends from the horse-drawn carriage (we still measure its power in horses and once called it a horseless carriage); the carriage from the cart; the cart from the sledge. Novelty, in Basalla's account, is never the appearance of something with no ancestry. It is the modification of an existing artifact, accumulated over many small steps into forms the original ancestor could not have anticipated. The diversity of the made world — the sheer number of distinct kinds of hammer, of engine, of chair — is the technological counterpart of the diversity of life, and it arises by the same logic of variation upon inherited form.

But variation of what, guided by what? Here the psychologist Donald Campbell supplied the mechanism, in a proposal of startling economy. Creative thought, he argued, proceeds by blind variation and selective retention — the same abstract procedure that drives biological evolution.15 The mind generates variations — trial ideas, conjectures, tentative combinations — without foreknowledge of which will succeed; "blind" here does not mean random but rather not guided by advance knowledge of the solution. Most variations fail and are discarded. The few that survive the test of reality are retained, and become the basis for further variation. Campbell's claim was that this is not merely analogous to evolution but is a genuine instance of the same underlying process — an idea that grew into the research program known as evolutionary epistemology, in which knowledge itself grows by a Darwinian filtering of conjectures.

The psychologist Dean Keith Simonton has spent a career marshalling the evidence that creative production really does obey this Darwinian logic.16 The most productive creators, he has shown, are not those with an uncanny ability to generate only good ideas; they are those who generate the most ideas, good and bad alike, and let selection sort them. Quality is a probabilistic consequence of quantity. The great composers wrote more forgettable works, not fewer, than the minor ones; the celebrated scientists published more failures. Genius, on this view, is in large part a high rate of variation coupled with the ruthlessness to let most of it die. It is a numbers game played by people willing to be wrong at scale.

Quality is a probabilistic consequence of quantity. Genius is a high rate of variation coupled with the ruthlessness to let most of it die.

The same evolutionary logic reorganized the theory of the firm. In the framework of Richard Nelson and Sidney Winter, a business does not optimize in the way textbook economics imagines; it operates according to routines — inherited, semi-automatic ways of doing things that function as the firm's genes.17 Innovation is the mutation of routines, through search; the market is the selection environment that decides which mutated routines survive and propagate. Whole industries evolve as populations of competing routines, varying and being selected, with no need for any actor to foresee the outcome. The invisible hand, in this rendering, is a selection mechanism, and the economy is an evolving ecosystem rather than a solved equation.

3.1The quarrel between variation and combination

We now have two theories of where new things come from, and they do not obviously agree. The combinatorial theory says novelty comes from wiring together existing components into new systems. The evolutionary theory says novelty comes from the incremental variation of an inherited lineage. Which is right? The economist and historian Abbott Payson Usher, writing decades before either camp had hardened, offered a synthesis that still holds. Major inventions, he argued, arise by cumulative synthesis: a sequence in which a problem is perceived, the relevant elements are gradually assembled and held in mind, an act of insight suddenly grasps a new way to combine them, and then a long process of critical revision refines the crude first synthesis into a working thing.18 Note the structure: a combinatorial leap (the act of insight) is followed by evolutionary refinement (critical revision). The two theories are not rivals. They describe different phases of a single process.

Brian Arthur sharpened the point into a claim about the limits of pure variation, and it is the crux of the matter.5 Radar, he observed, is a descendant of radio — both are technologies of electromagnetic waves. But you cannot arrive at radar by varying a radio, however patiently. You can adjust the circuits of a 1930s radio set for a thousand years and never obtain a device that detects aircraft by their echoes, because radar requires a different principle — the timing of a reflected pulse — that is simply not present in the radio to be varied. Radical novelty, Arthur argued, does not come from the gradual variation of an existing device. It comes from harnessing a phenomenon the old device never used, and building a new combination around it. Variation moves you within a design; it does not, by itself, deliver you to a fundamentally new one. This is why the naive slogan — that the jet engine is merely "a variation on the piston engine" — misleads. The jet is not a modified piston engine; it is a different combination harnessing a different principle, continuous combustion driving a reaction, which no amount of tuning a piston engine would ever yield. The variation came afterward, in the decades of refinement to blades and alloys and cooling that turned the raw principle into a reliable machine.

The resolution is to see the two mechanisms as partners in sequence — what we might call recombinant variation. A combinatorial insight opens a new region of the design space by introducing a new principle or a new wiring. Then evolutionary variation explores that region exhaustively, generating and testing the myriad small modifications needed to make the raw idea actually work. The leap is combinatorial; the landing is evolutionary. Neither alone suffices. Combination without subsequent variation yields a clever prototype that never becomes a product. Variation without a prior combinatorial leap yields ever-finer versions of a thing that was already possible, and never a genuinely new kind.

No episode illustrates the partnership better than the one Weitzman himself invoked: Edison's search for a lamp filament.7 The combinatorial insight — that a thin filament, heated by current in an evacuated bulb, could give steady light — defined a new region of the possible. But which material? Edison and his team did not deduce the answer; they varied their way to it, testing thousands of candidate substances — metals, papers, threads, and famously a vast botanical survey of carbonized plant fibers — until a particular carbonized filament proved durable enough to matter. The idea was a single combinatorial leap. The working lamp was the fruit of blind variation and selective retention, industrialized. Insight set the stage; evolution found the answer.

Part IV

Stealing function from context

There is a third way to make a new thing that neither combination nor variation captures: take something that already exists, leave it unchanged, and drop it into a new context where it does a job it was never built for.

So far, novelty has come from rearranging parts or from varying a lineage. But there is a distinct and slippery third source, and once you learn to see it you find it everywhere. Sometimes the new thing is not a new arrangement and not a modified descendant. It is an old thing, physically unchanged, that has been lifted out of the setting it was made for and set down in a new one, where it suddenly performs a different and unforeseen function. The object stays the same. Its context changes, and with the context, its meaning. The theory of innovation cannot be complete without treating context itself as a place where newness lives.

Biology named this phenomenon before technology did. In a 1982 paper that has become a classic, the paleontologists Stephen Jay Gould and Elisabeth Vrba pointed out that evolutionary theory lacked a word for a crucial fact: features often come to serve functions entirely different from the ones for which they originally arose.19 They proposed the term exaptation to sit alongside adaptation. An adaptation is a trait shaped by selection for its current role. An exaptation is a trait that was shaped for one role (or for no role at all) and later co-opted for another. Their central example is now famous: feathers almost certainly evolved first for thermoregulation — insulation, keeping a small dinosaur warm — and only much later were co-opted for flight. The feather was not designed for the sky. It was a coat that turned out, by luck of its structure, to also be a wing. Flight is built on the exaptation of insulation.

The geneticist François Jacob gave this a memorable general form. Evolution, he wrote, does not work like an engineer, who designs each component to specification from raw materials chosen for the purpose. It works like a tinkerer — a bricoleur — who builds with whatever odds and ends happen to be lying around, repurposing a length of wire here and an old crate there, producing contraptions that work but that no engineer would ever have designed.20 The eye, the jaw, the metabolic pathway: all are jury-rigged from parts that were previously doing something else. Nature never starts from a blank sheet. It rummages through the drawer of the already-existing and presses old things into new service. This is exaptation as a way of life, and it is one of the most powerful generators of novelty known, precisely because it does not require inventing anything new at all — only noticing a new use for the old.

Human invention tinkers in exactly the same way, and the clearest case is one of the twentieth century's most familiar appliances. During the Second World War, the magnetron — a vacuum tube that generates high-power microwaves — was developed as the heart of radar, a device for detecting aircraft and ships by their echoes. In 1945, an engineer at Raytheon named Percy Spencer was standing beside an active magnetron when he noticed that a snack in his pocket had melted.21 The observation itself was not unique — others had noticed the warming effect of microwave equipment and thought nothing of it. What distinguished Spencer was that he investigated: he deliberately placed popcorn kernels, then an egg, in the beam, watched them cook, and grasped that a technology built to find enemy bombers could be repurposed to heat food.22 Raytheon filed for a patent on the method later that year, and the microwave oven was born — not as a new invention but as the exaptation of a radar component. Louis Pasteur's dictum applies exactly: in the field of observation, chance favours the prepared mind. The melted candy bar was chance. The prepared mind — the willingness to ask why, and to test — was what turned an accident into an appliance.

Exaptation operates on processes as readily as on objects. When Henry Ford's engineers were searching for a way to build automobiles faster, the decisive idea came not from the world of manufacturing but from its opposite. Ford credited the moving assembly line to the overhead trolley that Chicago's meatpackers used to move carcasses past a line of workers who each cut away a portion — a disassembly line.23 Ford's insight was to run the film backward: if a fixed line of workers could take an animal apart as it moved past them, a fixed line of workers could put a car together as it moved past them. The slaughterhouse became the template for the factory. Nothing about the trolley was invented anew; a technique for taking things apart was exapted into a technique for putting things together, and mass production — the defining industrial form of the twentieth century — was the result. Context, once again, was the variable. The newness lived not in any component but in the transposition of a whole method from one domain into another.

Part V

When the rules change

The deepest novelty of all is not a new arrangement of pieces on the board. It is a change in the rules of the game — and a change in the rules redraws the entire space of what can be built.

We have found newness in three places: in the arrangement of parts, in the variation of a lineage, and in the transposition of a thing to a new context. Each is real, and each operates on the objects of a domain — the components, the artifacts, the techniques. But there is a fourth and deepest source of novelty, and it operates one level up. Sometimes what is new is not a rearrangement of the pieces but a change in the rules — a shift in the concepts, the abstractions, the very framework within which pieces are defined and moves are made. This is the rarest kind of innovation and the most consequential, because a change in the rules does not merely add a new possibility to the existing space. It redraws the space, and in a single stroke makes reachable a vast territory of new combinations that did not exist as possibilities a moment before.

The supreme example is Einstein, though not in the way popular memory has it. Consider general relativity. By 1912 Einstein possessed a physical intuition — the equivalence principle, the insight that gravity and acceleration are locally indistinguishable — but he lacked the mathematics to express it, and he was, by his own account, stuck. The breakthrough came when his friend the mathematician Marcel Grossmann pointed him toward an obscure and, at the time, almost purely ornamental branch of pure mathematics: the differential geometry of curved spaces developed by Bernhard Riemann, and the tensor calculus refined by Gregorio Ricci-Curbastro and Tullio Levi-Civita.24 This mathematics had been built decades earlier for reasons internal to mathematics, with no thought of physics whatsoever. Einstein's genius, as the physicist Abraham Pais documented in his definitive scientific biography, lay in recognizing that this ready-made but unused mathematical language was exactly the framework in which gravity could be expressed as the curvature of spacetime.25 General relativity was, in this sense, an exaptation across the boundary between disciplines — a mathematical structure built for one purpose, co-opted for another — welded to a new physical principle. Notice the correction to the usual story: it was special relativity, in 1905, that reconciled the electromagnetism of Maxwell with mechanics; general relativity, a decade later, recombined the equivalence principle with Riemannian geometry. The two are often blurred, but the distinction matters, because each shows a different recombination doing the work.

What makes such episodes examples of rule-change rather than mere combination is that they alter the conceptual framework itself. After general relativity, the very meaning of "space," "time," and "gravity" was different, and an entire universe of new questions and new possible theories — cosmology, black holes, gravitational waves — became reachable, none of which existed as possibilities within the Newtonian rules. The historian and philosopher of science Thomas Kuhn gave this kind of event its enduring name.26 Normal science, in Kuhn's account, proceeds by solving puzzles within an accepted framework — a paradigm — making the incremental, combinatorial and variational progress we have already described. But anomalies accumulate that the paradigm cannot absorb, and eventually the framework itself is overthrown and replaced in a paradigm shift — a scientific revolution after which practitioners quite literally see the world differently, defining their terms in new ways and asking questions the old framework could not pose. A paradigm shift is a change in the rules of the scientific game. And like every rule-change, its effect is not to add one new move but to redraw the board.

A change in the rules does not add a possibility to the space. It redraws the space — and makes reachable a whole territory that did not exist a moment before.

5.1Bisociation, or combination in the space of ideas

If a rule-change redraws the space of the possible, what generates the rule-change itself? Here the writer Arthur Koestler offered the most penetrating answer, and it returns us, at a higher level, to combination.27 The creative act, Koestler argued, is fundamentally an act of bisociation — the sudden collision of two frames of reference, each internally consistent and self-contained, that had never before been brought into contact. The joke works by bisociating two incompatible contexts so that we perceive a situation in two self-consistent but ordinarily unrelated frames at once; the scientific discovery works the same way, fusing two previously separate matrices of thought into a new synthesis that belongs wholly to neither. Gutenberg bisociated the wine press and the seal; Einstein bisociated geometry and gravity; Darwin bisociated Malthus's economics of scarcity with the breeding of pigeons. Abstraction, in this light, is combination performed not on objects but on frameworks — the wiring-together of whole conceptual systems. It is the same combinatorial engine we met in Part I, running on the most abstract fuel available, and its output is not a new device but a new way of seeing.

The mathematician Henri Poincaré left a rare first-person account of how this feels from the inside, and it confirms the picture with unusual precision.28 Describing the moment a difficult mathematical discovery came to him — famously, as he stepped onto a bus, his mind on other things — Poincaré portrayed creation as a process in which the mind, after a period of conscious effort, generates a great many combinations of ideas below the threshold of awareness, and a selective sensibility — a feeling for mathematical elegance — filters them, allowing only the harmonious few to surface into consciousness as insight. Combination and selection again, now internalized as the mechanics of thought itself: the unconscious generates variation blindly and abundantly; an aesthetic sense performs the retention. The creative mind, it turns out, is a recombinant engine running in the dark.

Part VI

The filter that decides

Generating a novel thing is only half the story. A new thing becomes an innovation only if something in the world selects it, adopts it, and lets it spread. Most novelty does not survive — and it is not supposed to.

We have spent five parts on the generation of novelty — by combination, by variation, by exaptation, by abstraction. But generation is only the first half of any theory of innovation, and taken alone it is dangerously misleading, because the overwhelming majority of novel things generated are never heard from again. A theory that explains only how candidates arise, and not how a few are chosen while the rest vanish, explains the easy half and ignores the hard one. Novelty is cheap. Selection is where innovation is actually decided.

Schumpeter drew the line precisely, and it deserves restating as a principle: an invention is not an innovation.4 The invention is the novel thing brought into existence; the innovation is that thing successfully carried into use, adopted, made part of how the world works. The history of technology is a vast graveyard of inventions that never became innovations — brilliant combinations that no one adopted, elegant devices that solved a problem no one had, ideas that arrived before the components or the appetite that would have let them spread. The step from invention to innovation is a passage through a filter, and the filter is merciless. What determines whether a novel thing passes through it?

The sociologist Everett Rogers built the classic account of how an innovation, once it exists, actually propagates — or fails to.29 Adoption, he showed, follows a characteristic S-shaped curve over time: a slow beginning as a handful of venturesome adopters take it up, then a steep acceleration as an early majority follows, then a levelling as the laggards finally come around or the thing saturates its market. Whether an innovation ever climbs the curve depends on properties Rogers catalogued with care — its relative advantage over what exists, its compatibility with current practice, its simplicity, whether it can be tried cheaply, whether its benefits are visible to others. A novel thing is not selected because it is novel, or even because it is better in some abstract sense. It is selected because it fits — because the surrounding environment of habits, standards, and needs has a niche the new thing happens to occupy. Selection is a conversation between the novel thing and its world, and the world usually says no.

What kind of novelty does the world tend to say yes to? A remarkable study by Brian Uzzi and colleagues, examining millions of scientific papers, found a precise and counterintuitive answer.30 They measured how conventional or atypical each paper's combinations of prior work were — whether it cited sources that are usually cited together, or yoked together bodies of work that rarely meet. The highest-impact papers, the ones that went on to be most cited, were not the most conventional, and crucially they were also not the most wildly novel. They occupied a specific sweet spot: a large foundation of conventional combinations, anchoring the work in the accepted core of its field, spiked with a minority of genuinely atypical combinations that introduced something unexpected. Too conventional, and the work is safe but adds nothing the field will remember. Too strange, and the work is unmoored, unrecognizable, rejected. The winning formula is mostly familiar with a jolt of the foreign — and, tellingly, teams proved far more likely than lone authors to hit it, because assembling unusual combinations across bodies of knowledge is easier for a group whose members bring different pieces to the table.

This is a deep result, because it tells us that selection environments are not simply filters for quality; they are filters for a particular balance between the novel and the familiar. A new thing must be strange enough to matter and familiar enough to be understood. The management scholar Lee Fleming reached a complementary conclusion by studying patents: inventions that combine technologies from very different domains have, on average, lower value than more conventional combinations — but they also have a far higher variance, producing both the worst failures and the rare breakthroughs.31 Radical recombination is a high-risk search strategy. It usually fails, occasionally it changes everything, and a healthy innovation system is one that can afford to run many such searches in parallel, absorbing the many failures for the sake of the rare transformative success. Selection does not reward novelty as such. It rewards novelty that has survived contact with reality — and reality's demands are specific, contextual, and unforgiving.

The asymmetry of novelty

Generation and selection are radically asymmetric in cost and in kind. Generating a novel combination is combinatorially easy — the space is bottomless, and imagination is cheap. Determining whether a given novel thing is good — whether it works, whether it fits, whether the world will keep it — is expensive, slow, and cannot in general be known in advance. This asymmetry is why innovation is hard despite novelty being easy, why most new things fail, and why the institutions that produce sustained innovation are, above all, machines for cheaply generating many candidates and ruthlessly, rapidly selecting among them.

Part VII

The recombinant engine

The pieces now assemble into a single mechanism — a self-feeding cycle that generates novelty, tests it, keeps the fit, and in the keeping enlarges the space of what can be generated next.

We can now put the whole machine together, because the parts have been waiting to interlock. Innovation is a cycle, and the cycle turns like this. At any moment the world stands at a frontier — the adjacent possible — defined by the components, techniques, and concepts that currently exist and work. From that frontier, novelty is generated by three mechanisms working together: combination wires existing components into new systems; exaptation transposes existing things into new contexts; and abstraction occasionally changes the rules, redrawing the frontier itself. The raw candidates so generated are then refined by variation — the blind, abundant, evolutionary search that turns a crude prototype into a working thing. The refined candidates are then filtered by selection — the market, nature, the scientific community, the culture — which retains the few that fit and discards the rest. And here the cycle closes and becomes an engine: each retained novelty becomes a new component in the stock of the actual, and by joining the actual it opens a new adjacent possible, presenting the next turn of the cycle with combinations that did not exist before. The output of innovation is the raw material of the next innovation. The engine feeds itself.

One object exhibits every element of this engine at once, and it is the object that made the world you live in. In December 1947, at Bell Telephone Laboratories, John Bardeen and Walter Brattain demonstrated the first working transistor — a small solid-state device that could amplify and switch electrical signals — and within weeks William Shockley had devised the more robust junction form.32 Trace the transistor through our theory and the whole cycle is visible. It stood at an adjacent possible newly opened by wartime research: the intense study of semiconductor crystals for radar detectors during the war had produced, as a byproduct, exactly the understanding of germanium and silicon that the transistor required — the prerequisites had just become actual. It emerged from deliberate combinatorial search, in an institution built for the purpose, aimed squarely at replacing the fragile vacuum tube. It demanded years of variation — the endless refinement of materials and fabrication that carried it from a laboratory curiosity to a manufacturable component. It passed through selection, adopted first where its advantages were decisive. And then it did the thing that matters most for our story: it became a universal building block. The transistor did not merely add one device to the world. By joining the stock of the actual, it opened an adjacent possible of staggering size — the integrated circuit, the microprocessor, the computer, the network, the whole edifice of digital civilization, none of which existed as a reachable possibility before that December. A single retained novelty enlarged the frontier so profoundly that we are still, three-quarters of a century later, exploring the rooms it opened.

7.1Where "new" actually lives

Step back from the mechanism and a simple map of novelty emerges. If nothing comes from nothing — if every new thing is made of old things — then "new" is not a property of the materials but of what is done with them, and there turn out to be exactly a few things that can be done. Newness lives, first, in relationship: the same components wired into a new configuration, as the printing press and the smartphone were — this is combination. It lives, second, in degree: an existing thing pushed to a new intensity, scale, or precision until a difference of quantity becomes a difference of kind — this is the frontier that variation explores. It lives, third, in context: an unchanged thing moved to a setting where it performs an unforeseen function, as the feather was moved to the wing and the magnetron to the kitchen — this is exaptation. And it lives, at the deepest level, in the rules: a change not in the pieces but in the framework that defines the pieces and governs their moves, which redraws the entire space of the possible — this is abstraction, the meta-locus, the place from which all the other spaces are drawn. Relationship, degree, context, rules: four addresses, and every genuinely new thing in the history of the world resides at one or more of them. None of them requires creation from the void. All of them are ways of arranging, intensifying, transposing, and reframing what already exists.

This map has consequences that reach well beyond the philosophy of it. If innovation is recombination over an adjacent possible, then the conditions that accelerate it are the conditions that put more components within reach of one another and lower the cost of trying their combinations. This is why cities, dense with diverse people and skills, have always been engines of invention; why the free movement of ideas across disciplinary and organizational walls produces more novelty than any silo; why diverse teams outperform homogeneous ones at exactly the atypical combinations that selection rewards; and why tolerance of failure is not a luxury but a structural necessity, since the search is mostly failure by its nature and a system that punishes every failure forecloses the rare successes that justify the whole enterprise. The practical art of fostering innovation is, in the end, the art of enlarging and connecting the adjacent possible — assembling the components, lowering the barriers between them, and running many cheap parallel experiments in combination while selecting quickly and without sentiment.

And it has one consequence that is a caution rather than a prescription. Because each new thing reshapes the adjacent possible in ways that depend on the specific combinations it makes reachable, the far future of innovation is not merely unknown but genuinely unknowable in principle. We cannot name the transformative inventions of the next century, not because our foresight is poor but because those inventions do not yet exist even as possibilities — they live in rooms that can only be entered through doors that have not yet been built, opened by combinations that depend on components not yet made. The adjacent possible can be expanded but it cannot be leapt. This is why technological prediction so reliably fails, and why the honest posture toward the future of innovation is not confident forecasting but a kind of prepared humility: we can widen the frontier and stand ready at it, but we cannot see past it, because there is, as yet, no "past it" to see.

We began with an ancient denial: ex nihilo nihil fit, nothing comes from nothing. It has turned out to be not a limitation on innovation but the key to understanding it. Precisely because nothing comes from nothing, every new thing is a rearrangement of old things — a new relationship, a new degree, a new context, a new rule — and the seemingly boundless creativity of the world is the boundless combinatorial richness of a frontier that enlarges each time we push against it. The lone genius conjuring the unprecedented was always a myth. The truth is stranger and more hopeful: novelty is a collective, cumulative, self-accelerating process in which each of us works with the pieces our predecessors left, adds a combination or two, and hands an enlarged set of pieces to those who come after. The philosopher Alfred North Whitehead, who thought more deeply than most about how the genuinely new enters a world made of the old, caught the whole tension in a single line.33 It is where a theory of innovation ought to end, because it names the balance the recombinant engine must forever strike — enough continuity to preserve what works, enough disruption to reach what does not yet exist.

“The art of progress is to preserve order amid change, and to preserve change amid order.”

Alfred North Whitehead · Process and Reality · 1929
