01Why this is a discipline of its own

The client now lives in enemy territory

Web penetration testing rests on a quiet assumption: the application's logic stays on a server you can reason about, and the browser is a thin, broadly uniform window onto it. Mobile breaks that assumption completely. A compiled APK or IPA ships your business logic, your secrets, your cryptographic routines, and your trust decisions directly onto a device the user owns — and that user, or whoever has rooted their phone, may be precisely the adversary you are defending against.

Every artefact you hand to the device is recoverable. Bytecode can be decompiled to near-source, native libraries can be disassembled, network calls can be intercepted, and the running process can be rewritten in memory while it executes. The mobile tester's job is to assume the platform's defences will be stripped away and ask what is left standing: Does the app still protect data at rest once the sandbox is breached? Does it still verify the server's identity when an attacker controls the certificate store? Does a "premium" or "is-admin" flag mean anything once the binary is in the tester's hands? The answers live at the seam between the client and its backend, which is exactly where this work concentrates.

The field has matured around a shared body of knowledge. OWASP's Mobile Application Security (MAS) project1 consolidates it into three interlocking documents: the Mobile Application Security Verification Standard (MASVS),2 which defines what a secure app must do; the Mobile Application Security Testing Guide (MASTG),3 a deep manual of how to test it, complete with platform internals and reverse-engineering technique; and the Mobile Application Security Weakness Enumeration (MASWE),4 a catalogue of concrete weaknesses that bridges the two. The risks most often found in the wild are summarised in the OWASP Mobile Top 10,5 refreshed in 2024 after eight years and now led by hardcoded credentials and supply-chain exposure rather than the server-side controls that topped the 2016 list.

Everything described here — decompiling binaries, bypassing pinning, instrumenting a running app, intercepting traffic — is conducted only against applications you own, have written permission to assess, or run in a dedicated lab. Unauthorised interception or modification of software and traffic is a criminal offence in most jurisdictions. A signed scope of work and rules of engagement come before any tool is launched.

02Standards, frameworks & the law

The maps you test against

A mobile assessment is only as defensible as the standard behind it. Findings framed against a recognised baseline survive client pushback, map cleanly into remediation, and satisfy auditors. Four bodies of work anchor almost every engagement, supported by the regulations that make mobile data handling a legal as well as a technical concern.

OWASP MASVS — the verification standard

The MASVS is the industry reference for what "secure" means in a mobile app.2 The 2023 refactor (v2.0) and the January 2024 update (v2.1) reorganised it into eight control groups and — importantly for anyone quoting older paperwork — retired the old L1/L2/R "verification levels" in favour of testing profiles defined alongside the MASWE.4 An RFP still demanding "MASVS Level 2" is working from a stale map.

MASVS-CRYPTO
Cryptography is implemented with proven primitives and sound key management.
MASVS-AUTH
Authentication and authorisation are enforced — on the server, not just locally.
MASVS-NETWORK
Network traffic is encrypted with strong TLS and verified endpoint identity.
MASVS-PLATFORM
Platform interactions — IPC, WebViews, deep links, the UI — are used safely.
MASVS-CODE
Code quality, dependency hygiene, and safe handling of all untrusted input.
MASVS-RESILIENCE
Defences against reverse engineering and tampering for high-risk apps.
MASVS-PRIVACY
Data minimisation, consent, and transparency over what the app collects.

OWASP MASTG & MASWE — the testing manual

Where MASVS states requirements, the MASTG supplies the technique: platform overviews of Android and iOS internals, full chapters on tampering and reverse engineering, and an exhaustive set of atomic test cases (the MASTG-TEST-* identifiers) that tell you exactly what to look for, which tools to reach for, and what a pass looks like.3 The MASWE sits between standard and guide, enumerating individual weaknesses (MASWE-*) so a finding can be traced from a failed control to a concrete defect to the test that proves it.4 Together they are the single most valuable free resource in the field, and Google's Play Store assessment programme leans on them directly.

OWASP Mobile Top 10 — the awareness list

Separate from MASVS and aimed at awareness rather than verification, the Mobile Top 10's 2024 release was its first major revision since 2016.5 The reordering tells a story: hardcoded API keys and secrets baked into shipped binaries are now the single most common serious finding, and third-party SDK risk has climbed to second.

NIST and the institutional baseline

For organisations that need a government-grade reference, NIST SP 800-163 Revision 1 defines a formal process for vetting the security of mobile applications — planning, requirements, testing, and an accept/reject decision — and explicitly draws on NIAP's Protection Profile for Application Software and on OWASP for its requirement set.6 Its companion, SP 800-124 Revision 2, covers managing the security of mobile devices and the apps that run on them across an enterprise fleet.7 These pair naturally with the MASVS: NIST frames the programme, OWASP supplies the technical depth.

Regulation: data handling is a legal question

Mobile apps are data-collection engines — location, sensors, contacts, health, payment — so privacy and sectoral law sit directly inside scope. The EU's General Data Protection Regulation governs personal data of EU residents and underpins much of the MASVS-PRIVACY group.8 In US healthcare, HIPAA constrains how protected health information is stored and transmitted.9 Apps handling card payments fall under the PCI Security Standards Council's framework, including the newer Mobile Payments on COTS (MPoC) standard for accepting payments on commodity phones.10 Beyond the law, voluntary assurance schemes have grown around the MASVS: Google's App Defense Alliance runs a Mobile Application Security Assessment, and CREST operates an OWASP Verification Standard programme — both treating the MASVS as the shared yardstick.11

◆ Practical anchoring

Pick MASVS as the spine of the engagement, use the MASTG test IDs as the procedure, record each result against a MASWE weakness, and translate the headline findings into Mobile Top 10 language for executive readers. One assessment, four lenses — technical, procedural, defect-level, and board-level.

03Platform foundations

Android and iOS, viewed as attack surface

You cannot test what you do not understand structurally. Both platforms run apps inside a sandbox, but they differ in runtime, packaging, executable format, and the facilities they offer for secure storage — and those differences dictate every technique that follows.

The Android model

Android stacks a Linux kernel beneath the Android Runtime (ART), which executes Dalvik bytecode compiled from Java or Kotlin. Each installed app is assigned a distinct Linux user ID, and the kernel's process isolation is what keeps one app out of another's private data directory — the Application Sandbox.12 An app ships as an APK (or an Android App Bundle that the store compiles into APKs): a ZIP containing classes.dex bytecode, native .so libraries under lib/, resources, and the AndroidManifest.xml that declares permissions and the four component types — Activities, Services, Broadcast Receivers, and Content Providers — any of which may be unintentionally exported to other apps.

The DEX bytecode is the tester's gift: it decompiles back to readable Java with high fidelity. Sensitive logic pushed into native libraries resists this and must be disassembled instead. For secrets, Android offers the hardware-backed Keystore, which can generate and hold keys whose private material never leaves a trusted execution environment or the dedicated security chip13 — when developers actually use it. APK integrity rests on the signing scheme (v1 through v4); the absence of modern signatures, or a debuggable flag left enabled, are early findings.3

The iOS model

iOS runs on the XNU kernel with a tightly integrated hardware security story documented in Apple's Platform Security guide: a Secure Enclave coprocessor, mandatory code signing, and a per-app container sandbox.14 Apps ship as IPAs containing a Mach-O binary compiled from Swift or Objective-C. App Store binaries arrive encrypted with Apple's FairPlay DRM, so the first step on a jailbroken device is often to dump the decrypted binary from memory before any static analysis is possible — a hurdle Android does not impose.

iOS leans harder on the platform for data protection. Files are tagged with Data Protection classes tied to the device passcode and Secure Enclave, and secrets belong in the Keychain, a system-wide encrypted store with access-control attributes that can require biometric presence or device unlock.15 The recurring iOS finding is not a missing vault but the wrong one: credentials in UserDefaults or a plist instead of the Keychain, or Keychain items with an accessibility class far more permissive than the data warrants.

The cross-cutting surface: IPC, WebViews, and deep links

Beyond storage and the binary, both platforms expose an inter-app and in-app surface that the MASVS-PLATFORM controls exist to police.2 On Android, the four component types communicate through Intents, and any component marked exported — sometimes implicitly, by declaring an intent filter — can be invoked by other apps on the device; an exported Content Provider with weak path validation, or an Activity that trusts Intent extras, hands a malicious app a foothold. iOS has a smaller IPC surface but its own analogues in URL schemes and Universal Links.

Two features recur as serious findings. Deep links (custom schemes, App Links, and Universal Links) let external input reach internal screens and parameters, and a link handler that trusts its input without verification becomes an open redirect, a parameter-injection vector, or worse. WebViews are riskier still: an embedded browser that enables JavaScript and bridges it to native code through a JavaScript interface can expose native functionality to any page it loads, so a WebView pointed at attacker-influenced content — or exposed by a man-in-the-middle on a non-pinned connection — can pivot from web context into the app's privileges. The MASTG dedicates test cases to each, and they belong on every checklist.3

On both platforms the high-yield surface is the same shortlist: data written to insecure local stores, secrets compiled into the binary, transport that fails to verify the server, exported components and IPC reachable by other apps, and WebViews bridging JavaScript to native code. 04Building the test lab

The bench you work from

A productive mobile lab gives you privileged device access, a way to see and modify traffic, and the static and dynamic tooling to take an app apart. The MASTG documents the canonical setup; the essentials below are what almost every engagement needs.3

Devices and virtual devices

You need an environment where instrumentation can run unimpeded. On Android that usually means a rooted physical device or the Android Studio / Genymotion emulator with a writable system partition; on iOS it means a jailbroken device matched to a compatible exploit (checkra1n, palera1n, or Dopamine depending on hardware and version), or a virtualised iPhone on a platform such as Corellium where no physical jailbreak is needed. Keep a non-rooted device too: some apps behave differently — or refuse to run — under root or jailbreak detection, and you will want to test both states.

The interception proxy

An intercepting HTTPS proxy is the spine of dynamic testing. Burp Suite is the de facto standard: route the device's traffic through it, install Burp's CA certificate on the device so its self-signed leaf is trusted, and you can read, replay, and tamper with every request the app makes.16 The wrinkle, addressed in depth later, is certificate pinning — an app that trusts only its own embedded certificate will reject Burp's CA until you defeat the pinning at runtime.

Getting the device's traffic into the proxy is its own small craft. The common path is a manual Wi-Fi proxy pointing the device at the host running Burp, which captures any app that respects system proxy settings; apps that ignore the proxy, or use raw sockets, need transparent or VPN-based redirection instead. On a physical device this means host and device share a network the proxy can reach; on an emulator the host loopback is reachable directly. Whichever route you take, the CA certificate must land in the right trust store for the platform version — the user store suffices on older Android and on iOS once trust is enabled in settings, but Android 7 and later need it in the system store, which is the first reason rooting earns its place on the bench.16

The instrumentation layer

Two tools do most of the dynamic heavy lifting. Frida is a dynamic instrumentation toolkit that injects a scripting engine into a running process, letting you hook functions, read and write memory, and rewrite behaviour live — no source code and no app restart required.17 Objection is a command-line toolkit built on top of Frida that packages the most common tasks — bypassing pinning, dumping the keychain, exploring the filesystem, listing classes — behind a REPL, and crucially can patch a Frida gadget into an app so it runs on non-rooted, non-jailbroken devices.18

◇ Lab hygiene

Snapshot the emulator before each app so you can roll back to a clean state, keep the frida-server build version-matched to your host frida-tools (mismatches are the most common cause of silent failures), and isolate the test network so intercepted traffic never escapes the bench.

05Reverse engineering the binary

Turning a black box into source

Reverse engineering is the foundation everything else builds on. Before you can find hardcoded secrets, locate the certificate-validation routine you want to hook, or understand a custom crypto scheme, you have to read the code the developer never meant you to see. The work splits into static analysis — examining the binary without running it — and the dynamic instrumentation covered later, and the two are complementary: static analysis maps the terrain, dynamic analysis confirms what actually executes.

Android: from APK to Java

The Android workflow is forgiving because DEX decompiles so well. Pull the package off a device with adb, then run two tools side by side. jadx decompiles the DEX bytecode straight to readable Java (or Kotlin-ish) source, with a GUI for browsing and search and support for ProGuard/R8 mapping files to recover names where available.19 Apktool works one layer lower, decoding resources and the AndroidManifest.xml and disassembling bytecode to human-readable smali — and, critically, it can rebuild a modified APK, which is how you patch out a check and repackage the app.20 An older path — dex2jar to convert to a JAR, then JD-GUI to view — still has its uses when a decompiler stumbles on a particular class.

Most assessments begin with a grep. Class names, method names, strings, and resource values all survive compilation, so searching the decompiled tree for key, secret, password, http, or an API hostname routinely surfaces hardcoded credentials and backend endpoints in minutes — the M1 risk made concrete.

android-static.shdecompile + hunt
$ adb shell pm path com.example.target      # locate the installed APK
$ adb pull /data/app/.../base.apk target.apk

$ jadx -d out_java target.apk                # DEX -> Java source tree
$ apktool d target.apk -o out_res            # resources + manifest + smali

# Hunt for secrets and endpoints across the decompiled output
$ grep -rinE "api[_-]?key|secret|password|bearer|https?://" out_java/

# Inspect native libraries when logic was pushed out of DEX
$ ls out_res/lib/arm64-v8a/                   # libnative.so -> Ghidra / radare2

Reading the manifest

Before diving into code, the AndroidManifest.xml recovered by Apktool is both a map of the attack surface and a source of quick wins. Three things are worth flagging on sight: an android:debuggable flag left true in a release build (which lets a tester attach a debugger and read process memory directly), android:allowBackup left enabled (which exposes private data through adb backup), and the full permission list, which often reveals far broader access than the app's function justifies. Past those, enumerate every component declared android:exported="true" — and every component carrying an intent filter, which is exported by default — because each is a potential entry point reachable from another app. The iOS equivalent is the Info.plist and the binary's entitlements, which reveal URL schemes, associated domains, and the capabilities the app was granted. This five-minute review routinely shapes where the rest of the engagement spends its time.3

iOS: decrypt first, then disassemble

iOS demands an extra step. Because App Store binaries are FairPlay-encrypted, you must first dump the decrypted Mach-O from a jailbroken device (with tooling such as frida-ios-dump or fouldecrypt) before any static tool can read it. From there, class-dump recovers Objective-C class and method declarations, while Swift's name mangling requires demangling to make sense of symbols. There is no high-fidelity Swift/ObjC decompiler equivalent to jadx, so the work is genuinely lower-level.

Native code and the disassemblers

When logic lives in C/C++ — Android .so files or the iOS Mach-O itself — you move from decompilation to disassembly. Ghidra, the NSA's open-source reverse-engineering suite, is the workhorse here: it disassembles and decompiles native code across architectures to readable pseudo-C, handles DEX and the ELF/Mach-O containers, and resolves JNI references into a unified view of an app's executable code.21 radare2 (and its r2frida bridge to live processes) offers a scriptable, command-line alternative,22 while the commercial IDA Pro, Hopper, and JEB Decompiler remain popular where budget allows.

“

Obfuscation buys time, not silence. The app must eventually execute its real logic, and an instrumented process will tell you everything a packer tried to hide.

Obfuscation, and its limits

Resilient apps fight back. Free tooling (R8/ProGuard) renames symbols; commercial protectors (DexGuard, iXGuard, and others) add control-flow obfuscation, string encryption, and anti-tamper. Static analysis degrades against these, but it rarely stops — names are obscured, not the structure, and built-in framework symbols stay legible. More importantly, obfuscation is a static defence: once you attach a debugger or Frida and watch the code run, the de-obfuscated values pass through memory in plain view. This is why hardened apps couple obfuscation with the anti-instrumentation defences discussed under runtime manipulation — and why those, too, can be hooked away.17

06Local data storage

What the app left on the device

Insecure data storage is perennial — M9 on the Mobile Top 10 and an entire MASVS control group — because developers reach for convenient stores and forget the device may end up rooted, stolen, or backed up to a machine an attacker controls. The test is methodical: drive the app through its full workflow (log in, transact, store a card, take an action), then comb every place it could have written data and ask whether anything sensitive sits there unprotected.3

Android stores and where to look

An Android app's private data lives under /data/data/<package>/, and the usual suspects are quick to enumerate. SharedPreferences are XML key-value files that frequently hold tokens and flags in plaintext. SQLite databases under databases/ store structured data — often unencrypted unless SQLCipher is in use. Plain files, the cache directory, and anything written to external storage (historically world-readable) round out the set. The platform offers safer options — EncryptedSharedPreferences and Keystore-wrapped keys13 — and the finding is usually that they were available and not used.

Beyond the obvious stores, check the leaky peripheral surfaces: logcat output (secrets in logs), Android auto-backup when allowBackup is left true, the clipboard, the keyboard cache for sensitive fields, and the screenshot the OS captures when the app is backgrounded. The MASTG codifies these as test cases such as MASTG-TEST-0001 for local storage.

iOS stores and where to look

The iOS container lives under /var/mobile/Containers/, and the equivalent shortlist applies. UserDefaults serialises to a plist and is a classic dumping ground for data that belongs in the Keychain. Core Data and third-party stores like Realm persist structured data, often unencrypted. Files carry a Data Protection class that determines whether they are readable while the device is locked — and a sensitive file marked NSFileProtectionNone is a finding. Secrets belong in the Keychain with an appropriately strict accessibility attribute; the common defect is either the wrong store entirely or a Keychain item set to kSecAttrAccessibleAlways.15 Objection's ios keychain dump and plist readers make this surface fast to survey, with MASTG-TEST-0052 as the reference procedure.

Two nuances separate a thorough storage review from a superficial one. On Android, the backup surface is broader than allowBackup alone — cloud Auto Backup and device-to-device transfer can both carry private data off the handset unless explicitly excluded — so the test is not merely "is the flag set" but "what actually leaves the device, and is any of it sensitive". On iOS, the Data Protection class is the detail that matters most: a file written with NSFileProtectionComplete is encrypted while the device is locked, whereas NSFileProtectionNone leaves it readable to anyone who can image the filesystem, so the same file in the same directory can be either safe or exposed depending on a single attribute. Confirm the class on every sensitive file rather than assuming the default protects it.3

07Transport & certificate-pinning bypass

Seeing the traffic the app tries to hide

The richest single view into a mobile app is its conversation with the backend. To get it you place an intercepting proxy in the middle, which terminates the app's TLS connection and presents its own certificate. That only works if the app trusts that certificate — and modern apps go to some length to ensure they do not.

The default trust chain

The baseline bypass is simply installing the proxy's CA certificate on the device so its leaf certificate validates. Two platform behaviours complicate this. On Android 7 and later, apps no longer trust user-added CAs by default; they must opt in through a Network Security Configuration, so a tester often needs the CA in the system store (a rooted-device operation) or must edit that configuration and repackage the app.23 On iOS, App Transport Security mandates TLS and flags insecure exceptions — an ATS configuration that re-enables cleartext or weak protocols is itself a finding.24 Once the CA is trusted, traffic flows into Burp for inspection, replay, and tampering.16

What pinning is, and where it lives

Certificate (or public-key) pinning hardens an app by embedding the exact certificate or key it expects from its server and rejecting anything else — defeating the "trust any installed CA" assumption a proxy relies on. It is implemented in a handful of recognisable places: OkHttp's CertificatePinner and custom X509TrustManager/HostnameVerifier implementations on Android; NSURLSession delegate trust evaluation, Alamofire's ServerTrustManager, and the lower-level SecTrustEvaluate / BoringSSL routines on iOS. Bypassing it is a routine — and necessary — step in almost every assessment, because without it you simply cannot see the API.

Bypassing pinning at runtime

The fastest route is Objection, whose android sslpinning disable and ios sslpinning disable commands hook the common pinning methods across frameworks in one shot.18 When an app uses a custom or obfuscated implementation that Objection's generic hooks miss, you drop to a targeted Frida script that replaces the specific validation — installing an all-trusting TrustManager and neutralising CertificatePinner.check() on Android, or overriding SecTrustEvaluateWithError to always succeed on iOS.17 A well-maintained, broadly tested collection of these scripts ships in HTTP Toolkit's frida-interception-and-unpinning project, which covers the large majority of real-world pinning libraries.25 Where runtime hooking is undesirable, the static alternative is to edit the Network Security Configuration (or patch the validation in smali) and repackage the app.

unpin-android.jsFrida · Android
Java.perform(function () {
  // 1. Force OkHttp to accept any pinned certificate
  try {
    const Pinner = Java.use('okhttp3.CertificatePinner');
    Pinner['check'].overload('java.lang.String', 'java.util.List')
      .implementation = function (host, certs) {
        console.log('[+] OkHttp pin bypassed for ' + host);
        return;                       // no exception == trusted
      };
  } catch (e) {}

  // 2. Neutralise hostname verification
  const HV = Java.use('javax.net.ssl.HttpsURLConnection');
  HV.setDefaultHostnameVerifier.implementation = function (v) {
    console.log('[+] Default hostname verifier replaced');
  };
});

Two caveats keep this honest. A small set of apps add certificate-transparency checks or attestation on top of pinning, which need the specific logic patched after static analysis; and Burp is not the only proxy — mitmproxy offers a scriptable, terminal-driven alternative that pairs well with Frida for automated traffic manipulation.26 Once pinning is down, the entire API surface opens up, which leads directly into backend testing.

With traffic finally visible, the immediate review is what the channel itself reveals: sensitive data sent in request bodies or query strings, authentication material travelling on every call, verbose error messages exposing backend internals, and any endpoint quietly falling back to cleartext HTTP. Catalogue the API's structure here — hosts, routes, parameters, token formats — because that map is exactly what the backend testing works from next. The transport review and the API review are two passes over the same captured traffic: the first asks whether the channel is sound, the second whether the server behind it can be trusted.2

▲ Pinning is not a fix on its own

From the defender's side, pinning raises the cost of interception but never eliminates it — any tester with the binary and a rooted device can hook it away. Treat it as one layer of defence-in-depth, never as a reason to trust the client. The real protection is enforcing every security decision server-side.

08Runtime manipulation & instrumentation

Rewriting the app while it runs

Static analysis tells you what the code says; runtime instrumentation lets you change what it does. By injecting into a live process you can hook any function, inspect and alter its arguments and return value, read decrypted data straight from memory, and switch off whole defences — all without touching the binary on disk. This is the single most powerful capability in mobile testing, and one tool dominates it.

Frida, in depth

Frida is a dynamic instrumentation toolkit created by Ole André Vadla Ravnås and maintained with NowSecure, designed for developers, reverse engineers, and security researchers.27 Its core, written in C, injects a JavaScript engine (currently QuickJS, with V8 available) into a target process; your script then runs inside that process with full access to its memory and the ability to hook functions and call native code, communicating back to your host over a bidirectional channel.17 It runs on Android, iOS, and the desktop platforms, in a rooted/jailbroken mode via frida-server or, for unprivileged devices, by embedding a frida-gadget into a repackaged app.

The primitives you reach for constantly are few: Java.perform and Java.use to hook Android methods; ObjC.classes and method swizzling for iOS; Interceptor.attach and Interceptor.replace for native functions; Memory for reading and writing process memory; and Stalker for code tracing. With these you can trace every call to a crypto routine and capture keys, log the plaintext passed to an encryption function, or rewrite a licence check to always return "valid".

hooks.jsFrida · hook patterns
// Android — defeat a client-side "isPremium" gate
Java.perform(function () {
  const Account = Java.use('com.example.billing.Account');
  Account.isPremium.implementation = function () {
    console.log('[+] isPremium() forced true');
    return true;
  };
});

// Native — trace a crypto call and dump its plaintext input
const addr = Module.findExportByName('libnative.so', 'encrypt_payload');
Interceptor.attach(addr, {
  onEnter(args) { console.log(hexdump(args[0].readByteArray(64))); }
});

// iOS — make a jailbreak check return NO
const JB = ObjC.classes.SecurityManager;
Interceptor.attach(JB['- isJailbroken'].implementation, {
  onLeave(ret) { ret.replace(ptr('0x0')); }
});

Tracing behaviour and capturing secrets

Hooking is not only for switching checks on and off; it is also the fastest way to observe. Pointed at a cryptographic routine, an Interceptor can log the plaintext passed in and the key used before the data is ever encrypted, collapsing a custom obfuscation scheme into a single console line. Frida's Stalker engine takes this further with instruction-level tracing — useful for following control flow through native code that resists static analysis — while frida-trace auto-generates hooks for whole families of functions so you can watch an app's interaction with the filesystem, network, or crypto APIs in real time.17 The pattern that recovers the most secrets is simple: identify the security-relevant function in static analysis, hook it, exercise the app, and read what flows through. Because the values must be in cleartext at the moment the function uses them, no amount of at-rest encryption or string obfuscation hides them from an instrumented process — the reason runtime analysis so reliably outperforms static review against hardened targets.27

Objection — Frida without the scripting

Objection packages this power behind a command line so common tasks need no custom JavaScript.18 A single REPL gives you filesystem exploration, keychain and SharedPreferences dumps, SSL-unpinning, root/jailbreak-simulation, class and method enumeration, live argument logging, and memory dump/patch — covering the first ninety per cent of an engagement. When you need more, the raw Frida API is one command away. It is the fast path; Frida is the deep path.

Anti-instrumentation, and getting past it

High-value apps — banking, payments, streaming — deploy runtime defences the MASVS groups under resilience: root and jailbreak detection, anti-debugging, Frida detection (scanning for the default port 27042, gadget artefacts, or suspicious memory maps), file-integrity checks, and emulator detection, increasingly backstopped by platform attestation such as Google's Play Integrity API or Apple's DeviceCheck and App Attest.28 The irony is that the detection logic itself runs inside the process you control, so the standard counter is to hook the detector and force it to report a clean environment — the very isJailbroken() and isRooted() overrides shown above.17 Server-validated attestation raises the bar meaningfully, because the verdict is checked off-device; client-only checks rarely survive a determined tester.

“

Root detection, anti-debugging, and Frida detection are speed bumps for the curious and a checklist item for the prepared. None of them keep an instrumented process from doing your bidding.

09Mobile API & backend security

The server is where the real controls live

Once pinning is down and traffic is flowing into the proxy, the assessment widens to the backend — and this is where many of the most serious findings surface. A mobile app is a thin client over an HTTP API, so it inherits the entire web-API threat model. What changes is the framing: because the client is fully attacker-controlled, every authorisation and validation decision must be re-checked on the server, and the tester's job is to prove what happens when the well-behaved app is replaced by hand-crafted requests in Burp.16

Authorisation is the dominant flaw class

The OWASP API Security Top 10 catalogues the risks, and its top entries dominate mobile backends.29 Broken Object Level Authorization (BOLA, the API cousin of IDOR) is the single most common: change an object identifier in a request — an account number, an order ID, a user UUID — and see whether the server returns another user's data. Broken Function Level Authorization is its sibling: replay an administrative or privileged endpoint from an ordinary user's session and check whether the function is gated only by the client hiding a button. Mass assignment, where the API binds unexpected fields from the request body, lets an attacker set isAdmin or balance the UI never exposed.

Authentication, tokens, and replay

Mobile auth leans on bearer tokens — JWTs, OAuth access and refresh tokens — and the failures are predictable: tokens that never expire, refresh tokens with no rotation, JWTs accepting alg:none or a weak signing key, and sessions not bound to a device so a captured token replays freely from anywhere. Because the token usually sits in local storage, a storage finding and an auth finding often compound: extract the token from the device, replay it from your own machine, and you have account takeover without ever touching the password.

Hidden endpoints and server-side validation

Decompilation pays a second dividend here. The static analysis from earlier routinely reveals backend hostnames, undocumented or debug endpoints, and API routes the UI never calls — all fair game once authorised. And every input the app sends must be validated server-side: injection, oversized payloads, and malformed input that the client's own checks would have caught mean nothing when the request is forged. Systematic fuzzing of the captured endpoints — by hand in Burp, or automated — exercises this surface efficiently.18

Rate limiting, enumeration, and business logic

Two further failures round out the backend picture. The first is missing rate limiting and anti-automation: login, one-time-password, and password-reset endpoints that accept unlimited attempts invite credential stuffing and OTP brute force, while verbose or differential responses leak whether an account or resource exists and so enable user enumeration. The second is business-logic abuse, which no scanner reliably catches — race conditions in payment or transfer flows, negative-quantity or price-manipulation tricks, and multi-step workflows whose steps can be skipped or replayed out of order. These require understanding the application's intent and then deliberately violating it, and they are frequently the highest-impact findings in an otherwise well-built API.29

◆ The through-line

Local storage, transport, and API testing are one continuous chain: read the token from disk, defeat pinning to watch it travel, then replay and mutate it against a backend that may have assumed only its own app would ever call. Test the chain, not the links.

10The core toolkit

The instruments, in one place

The tools referenced throughout cluster into four jobs: take the app apart (reverse engineering), watch and bend its traffic (proxies), rewrite it as it runs (instrumentation), and tie the lot together (automated frameworks). One tool deserves a fuller introduction before the reference grid.

MobSF — the automated front door

The Mobile Security Framework, created by Ajin Abraham and maintained by OpenSecurity, is the all-in-one platform most assessments start with.30 It performs static analysis on APK, IPA, and Windows packages (and source), surfacing insecure storage, weak crypto, exported components, hardcoded secrets, and manifest misconfigurations in a single report — and it performs dynamic analysis too, driving Frida under the hood to exercise a running app. A REST API lets it slot into CI/CD so every build is scanned automatically, and its companion command-line scanner, mobsfscan, brings the same static checks (built on semgrep) into a developer's pipeline as a focused SAST step.31 MobSF rarely finds everything, but it clears the obvious ground fast and frames where manual effort should go.

11Methodology & reporting

From scope to signed-off report

Technique without process produces scattered findings and unrepeatable results. A defensible mobile assessment runs as a sequence of phases, each anchored to the MASVS so coverage is provable and nothing is tested on a hunch.2

The phases

  1. Scoping & rules of engagement. Define the platforms, build, backend environments, accounts, and — non-negotiably — written authorisation and a test window. Agree whether the backend API is in scope (it almost always should be).
  2. Information gathering. Static recon of the package: manifest, permissions, components, SDKs, and a first grep for secrets and endpoints.
  3. Lab preparation. Rooted/jailbroken and clean devices, proxy with CA installed, Frida and Objection ready, snapshots taken.
  4. Static analysis. Decompile and review for the MASVS-STORAGE, CRYPTO, and CODE controls — hardcoded secrets, weak algorithms, dangerous configuration.
  5. Dynamic analysis. Run the app under instrumentation: inspect what it writes, defeat pinning, bypass resilience controls, observe real behaviour.
  6. Network & API testing. With traffic visible, test authentication, authorisation (BOLA/BFLA), and server-side validation against forged requests.
  7. Reporting & retest. Document, rate, and deliver; then verify fixes once remediation lands.

The MASTG turns each phase into concrete procedure — its MASTG-TEST-* cases tell you exactly what to check and how — while the Mobile Application Security Checklist links those tests back to the MASVS controls so the engagement's coverage is auditable end to end.3 Each result is recorded against the weakness it proves in the MASWE,4 giving every finding a clean lineage from control to defect to evidence. For organisations running this as a formal programme, NIST SP 800-163's vetting model supplies the surrounding governance — inputs, outputs, and the accept/reject decision.6

Rating and writing it up

Rate findings by realistic impact and exploitability — CVSS where a numeric anchor helps, a qualitative critical-to-informational scale where it communicates better — and resist inflating a client-side weakness whose real risk depends on a server-side control that turned out to be sound. The report itself earns its keep through clarity: an executive summary in Mobile Top 10 terms for leadership,5 and per-finding technical detail with exact reproduction steps, evidence, affected MASVS control, and specific remediation. A finding a developer cannot reproduce is a finding that will not be fixed.

◆ Reproducibility is the deliverable

Capture the Frida script, the exact request, the device state, and the screenshot for every issue. The value of an assessment is not the list of bugs — it is the developer's ability to stand in your shoes, see the problem, and confirm the fix.

13Practice labs & vulnerable apps

Where the skill is actually built

Reading about Frida teaches you nothing a hook does. The discipline is learned by breaking deliberately vulnerable apps — purpose-built targets that are legal to attack, well-documented, and seeded with the exact flaws this manual describes. A handful are effectively the standard curriculum.

Reverse-engineering challenges

The OWASP MAS Crackmes — the "UnCrackable Apps", levels 1 through 4 on Android plus iOS variants — are the canonical reverse-engineering gym, used as worked examples throughout the MASTG itself.38 They escalate from a trivial hardcoded check to layered root detection, anti-tampering, and obfuscation, and solving them end to end is the fastest way to internalise the static-plus-dynamic loop.

◇ How to use them

Don't read the walkthrough first. Attempt each challenge cold, reach for the write-up only when truly stuck, and re-solve it later from memory. The goal is fluency with the loop — decompile, locate, hook, verify — not a checklist of solved apps.

14Learning resources & community

Where to keep learning

Mobile platforms shift yearly — new OS releases, new defences, new SDK risks — so staying current matters more here than in slower fields. The good news is that the best resources are free and actively maintained.

Reference and reading

The OWASP MASTG is both the field's manual and its best textbook; its Suggested Reading list curates the deeper books worth owning, from platform-internals titles to dedicated mobile-hacking handbooks.44 Treat the MASTG itself as the primary text3 and the wider OWASP MAS project as the source of truth for standards updates.1

Video and hands-on courses

For those who learn by watching, a few channels stand out. TCM Security publishes practical mobile-pentest walkthroughs alongside its paid course,36 NowSecure pairs its free Academy with conference talks and Frida deep-dives,37 and HTTP Toolkit's blog and writing on interception and unpinning are among the clearest practical explanations of certificate pinning anywhere.25 Vendor research blogs — SensePost, NetSPI, and the platform security teams — round out a steady stream of current technique.

Tooling at scale, and community

When physical devices become a bottleneck, Corellium provides virtualised iOS and Android hardware in the browser — full instrumentation without a jailbroken handset on the bench — and underpins the labs of several training programmes.45 The community itself congregates around the OWASP MAS GitHub and Slack, mobile-focused tracks at DEF CON, Black Hat, and Hack In The Box, and the macOS/iOS-leaning Objective by the Sea — all places where new bypasses and defences surface long before they reach textbooks.

The platforms patch, the protectors evolve, and last year's reliable bypass quietly stops working. Currency is not optional in mobile — it is the job.

15The defender's checklist

How not to fail the test

Every technique in this manual implies its own defence. For the developers and architects on the other side of the engagement, the controls below — organised by MASVS area — close the findings testers most reliably report.2 One principle outranks all of them: the client is untrusted, so every security decision that matters must be enforced on the server.

Storage
Keep secrets out of SharedPreferences, UserDefaults, plists, and logs. Use the hardware-backed Keystore13 or Keychain15, encrypt structured data, set the correct iOS Data Protection class, and disable backups for sensitive content.
Cryptography
Use proven primitives and platform crypto libraries; never hardcode keys; generate and hold key material in the hardware-backed store so it cannot be extracted from the binary or memory.
Network
Enforce strong TLS, configure the Android Network Security Configuration correctly23 and keep App Transport Security strict on iOS24. Treat certificate pinning as one defence-in-depth layer, never as a substitute for server-side checks.
Auth & sessions
Validate every authorisation server-side; issue short-lived, rotating tokens bound to the device; never gate privileged functions on the client merely hiding the UI.
Resilience
Layer root/jailbreak detection, anti-debugging, and obfuscation for high-risk apps — but verify integrity off-device with server-validated attestation such as Play Integrity or App Attest, since client-only checks are routinely hooked away.28
Code & supply chain
Vet and minimise third-party SDKs (the M2 risk), strip debug code and verbose logging from release builds, and don't export Activities, Services, or Content Providers that don't need to be reachable.
Privacy
Collect the minimum data required, obtain meaningful consent, and be transparent about what leaves the device — satisfying both MASVS-PRIVACY and the regulations behind it.

Read against the Mobile Top 10, this checklist is simply its inverse: do these things and the headline risks have nowhere to land.5 The recurring thread across storage, transport, runtime, and API is the same one the testing began with — assume the device is hostile, assume the binary is open, and put the controls that matter where the attacker cannot reach them.

◆ The whole manual in a sentence

Mobile penetration testing is the disciplined act of proving which of an app's defences survive contact with an attacker who owns the device — and the defence is to make sure the ones that matter were never on the device to begin with.
