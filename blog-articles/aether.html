The Wind That
Was Never There

For most of the nineteenth century, light was understood to be a wave, and a wave demanded a medium. Physics built an entire mechanical universe on that medium — the luminiferous aether. Then two Americans went looking for the Earth's motion through it, and found nothing at all.

In the summer of 1887, in the basement of a stone dormitory in Cleveland, an apparatus floated on a pool of mercury, turning slowly while two men watched a pattern of light. They were measuring the speed of the planet through the substance that filled the universe. The substance was not there. It had never been there. And the careful, almost obsessive demonstration of its absence would, within two decades, demolish the most intuitive picture of reality that physics had ever possessed — the picture of a world made of mechanical things moving through an absolute space.

This is the story of the most consequential failure in the history of experimental science: what the aether was, why brilliant people believed in it for a century, how its non-existence was established, and why that emptiness was so disturbing that physicists invented new properties of matter to avoid facing it.

Chapter One

The necessity of a medium

Every wave that physics had ever studied was a disturbance of something. Sound is a compression travelling through air; ripples are deformations of a water surface; a vibration runs along a string. The wave is not a thing in itself but a pattern propagating through a substrate, and its speed is fixed by the substrate's mechanical properties — its stiffness and its density. To a nineteenth-century physicist, the proposition that a wave could travel through literally nothing was not merely strange; it was close to meaningless, a verb without a subject.

So when light was finally established to be a wave, the existence of a medium for it followed as a matter of logical necessity. That medium acquired a name borrowed from antiquity — the luminiferous (light-bearing) aether — and the project of nineteenth-century optics became, in large part, the project of working out what kind of stuff it had to be.

The victory of the wave theory had been hard won. Christiaan Huygens had proposed in 1690 that light consists of pulses spreading through an all-pervading aether, each point on a wavefront acting as the source of a secondary spherical wavelet.1 But Huygens's construction was eclipsed for more than a century by the authority of Isaac Newton, whose Opticks of 1704 treated light as streams of corpuscles — tiny particles whose trajectories bent at interfaces and whose differing sizes produced the colours.2 Corpuscles travelled naturally through the void; they needed no medium, and they squared comfortably with the ballistic intuitions of Newtonian mechanics.

The corpuscular consensus broke at the very start of the nineteenth century. In a series of papers culminating in his 1804 Bakerian Lecture, Thomas Young demonstrated that two beams of light, suitably arranged, could combine to produce alternating bright and dark bands — and, crucially, that in the dark bands light added to light to yield darkness.3 No particle picture could explain this. Two streams of corpuscles can only ever deliver more particles, never fewer. But two waves can cancel: where a crest of one meets a trough of the other, the disturbance vanishes. Young's principle of interference made the wave nature of light visible, and it made the case essentially unanswerable.

Augustin-Jean Fresnel then supplied what Young's qualitative insight lacked: a complete mathematical theory. His prize memoir on diffraction (1818) showed that the precise pattern of light and shadow cast by an obstacle could be computed by summing Huygens's wavelets with their phases properly accounted for, and the agreement with experiment was extraordinary.4 The wave theory was no longer a competing hypothesis; it was a predictive instrument of great precision.

But Fresnel's work, together with experiments on polarization carried out with François Arago, forced a conclusion that would haunt aether physics for the rest of the century. The phenomena of polarization could only be explained if the oscillations of light were transverse — perpendicular to the direction of travel — rather than longitudinal compressions like sound.5 This single fact transformed the aether from a tenuous gas into something far more demanding. A fluid cannot sustain transverse waves at all; only a medium with rigidity, the capacity to resist shearing, can do so. The aether, whatever else it was, had to behave like an elastic solid.

Chapter Two

An impossible substance

Here the trouble began in earnest. The speed at which a transverse wave runs through an elastic solid is set by the ratio of the medium's rigidity to its density: stiffer media carry waves faster, denser media slower. Light travels at roughly three hundred thousand kilometres per second — vastly faster than any wave in any known material. To carry waves at that speed, the aether would need a rigidity exceeding that of the finest steel.

And yet the planets sweep through it without the faintest resistance. The Earth has been orbiting the Sun for billions of years at thirty kilometres per second; if it were ploughing through a steel-stiff solid, its motion would have decayed long ago. The aether therefore had to be simultaneously more rigid than steel and more tenuous than the thinnest gas — perfectly unyielding to the rapid shear of a light wave, yet perfectly transparent to the slow passage of a world. No ordinary substance reconciles these demands, and the contradiction was understood at the time to be the central embarrassment of the theory.6

The response of Victorian physics was not to abandon the aether but to engineer it. George Green developed a rigorous dynamical theory of an elastic-solid aether, deriving the laws of reflection and refraction from its assumed mechanical properties.7 Others proposed that the aether behaved like a substance that is rigid to fast disturbances but yields to slow ones — pitch, or cobbler's wax, or a fine jelly. William Thomson, Lord Kelvin, spent decades constructing mechanical models: aethers built of spinning vortices, of gyrostats, of nested cells, each contrived to reproduce the elastic behaviour light required while evading the contradictions.8

“I never can satisfy myself until I can make a mechanical model of a thing. If I can make a mechanical model I can understand it. As long as I cannot make a mechanical model all the way through I cannot understand.”

— Lord Kelvin, Baltimore Lectures, 1884

This was the deeper commitment beneath the aether: a conviction that the universe is, at bottom, a mechanism. Forces are transmitted by contact; influences propagate through intervening matter; to explain a phenomenon is to exhibit the gears and springs that produce it. The aether was the master gear — the material substrate that made fields, light, and action-at-a-distance intelligible by grounding them in something tangible. To give it up would not be to revise a detail. It would be to concede that the mechanical worldview itself might be wrong.

For all its contortions, the aether had one saving grace through the middle decades of the century: every attempt to catch the Earth moving through it failed in a way the theory could absorb. Understanding why those failures were reassuring rather than fatal requires a detour through the optics of moving bodies.

Chapter Three

Aberration, drag, and a first-order reprieve

The first evidence that the Earth moves relative to the light-bearing medium had arrived long before anyone worried about its consequences. In 1728 the astronomer James Bradley reported a puzzling annual wobble in the apparent positions of the stars: over the course of a year, each star traced a tiny ellipse on the sky, with a maximum displacement of about twenty seconds of arc.9 Bradley correctly identified the cause as stellar aberration. Because the Earth moves, a telescope must be tilted slightly forward — into the motion — to catch the starlight falling down its tube, exactly as an umbrella must be tilted into the rain when one walks. The size of the tilt depends on the ratio of the Earth's velocity to the speed of light, and from it Bradley obtained one of the first good measurements of that speed.

Aberration carried an important implication for the aether. The simplest reading was that the light medium stands still in absolute space while the Earth rushes through it, so that the starlight, once it enters the neighbourhood of the moving telescope, is not swept along. A stationary, undragged aether explained aberration cleanly. But this raised an obvious question: if the Earth moves through a fixed aether, there should be an "aether wind" blowing past us, and that wind ought to be detectable in terrestrial optics.

The reason it was not — at least not in any experiment of the era — was supplied by Fresnel in a remarkable letter to Arago in 1818. Fresnel proposed that a transparent body in motion partially drags the aether inside it, but only partially, by a factor that depends on the body's refractive index.10 The light inside a moving medium of refractive index n is carried forward not at the full speed of the medium but at a fraction (1 − 1/n2) of it. This Fresnel drag coefficient had a near-magical property: it was precisely the amount needed to make experiments insensitive to the Earth's motion to first order in the ratio v/c. The aether wind was real, on this account, but a conspiracy of partial dragging hid it from view in any first-order measurement.

Fresnel's coefficient was not merely a fudge. In 1851 Hippolyte Fizeau measured it directly, sending light through columns of rapidly flowing water and observing the shift in interference fringes as the water's velocity added to or subtracted from the light's.11 The measured drag matched Fresnel's prediction. A moving transparent medium really did carry light along by exactly the fractional amount the theory demanded — a result so precise and so strange that it became one of the central facts any theory of light and motion would have to explain.

An alternative picture was advanced by George Gabriel Stokes in 1845. Where Fresnel kept the aether stationary and dragged only the light inside dense bodies, Stokes proposed that the Earth drags the surrounding aether along with it completely at its surface, the entrainment falling off with distance.12 Under a special mathematical condition — that the aether's flow be irrotational — Stokes showed this too could reproduce the observed aberration. For decades the Fresnel (stationary, partially dragging) and Stokes (fully dragging) aethers competed, each saving the appearances, neither decisively confirmed.

The order-of-magnitude problem

The Earth's orbital speed is about 30 km/s; light travels at about 300,000 km/s. Their ratio is v/c ≈ 10−4. Effects proportional to v/c are small but reachable; effects proportional to (v/c)2 ≈ 10−8 are a hundred million times smaller — one part in a hundred million.

Every classical experiment that gave a null result did so at first order, where Fresnel drag guaranteed a null. The decisive test would have to reach second order — and to most physicists of the 1870s that seemed flatly impossible.

Chapter Four

Maxwell's aether and an impossible measurement

In the 1860s the aether acquired a vastly expanded job description. James Clerk Maxwell, building on Faraday's field concept, unified electricity, magnetism, and light into a single theory.13 His equations predicted that oscillating electric and magnetic fields propagate as waves, and — astonishingly — that their speed, computed purely from electrical and magnetic constants measured in the laboratory, came out equal to the measured speed of light. Light was an electromagnetic wave. The conclusion was inescapable and triumphant.

But electromagnetic waves were still waves, and for Maxwell they were still waves in the aether. In his Treatise on Electricity and Magnetism (1873) the aether is the seat of the electromagnetic field, the physical system whose stresses and strains are the electric and magnetic forces.14 Maxwell devoted real effort to mechanical models of this field-bearing medium — arrays of rotating cells and idler wheels — and although he came to treat the equations as primary, he never doubted that some material substrate underlay them. In his article "Ether" for the ninth edition of the Encyclopædia Britannica, he set out the case for the medium's reality with characteristic care, while acknowledging how little its mechanical nature was understood.15

It was Maxwell who framed the experimental challenge with brutal clarity. Shortly before his death he wrote a letter, published in Nature in 1880, considering whether the Earth's motion through the aether could be detected on Earth itself.16 His conclusion was discouraging. Any terrestrial method — one that sends light out and back along a path fixed to the moving Earth — depends on the round trip, and the first-order effects of the aether wind cancel between the outward and return legs. What survives is only the second-order effect, proportional to (v/c)2, a quantity of order one part in a hundred million. Measuring it, Maxwell judged, lay beyond the reach of experiment.

“In the terrestrial methods … the light comes back along the same path again, so that the velocity of the earth with respect to the ether would alter the time of the double passage by a quantity depending on the square of the ratio of the earth's velocity to that of light, and this is quite too small to be observed.”

— J. C. Maxwell to D. P. Todd, 1879 (published in Nature, 1880)

This was read in Berlin by a young American naval officer studying under Hermann von Helmholtz. Albert Abraham Michelson took Maxwell's "quite too small to be observed" not as a verdict but as a specification. He set out to build an instrument that could see one part in a hundred million.

Chapter Five

Michelson's interferometer, and a costly error

Michelson's insight was that interference could turn an immeasurably small time difference into a visible displacement of light and dark bands. His instrument — the interferometer that still bears his name — splits a single beam of monochromatic light into two perpendicular paths with a half-silvered mirror, sends each beam out to a distant mirror and back, and recombines them. Where the recombined waves arrive in step they reinforce; where they arrive out of step they cancel; and the eye sees a pattern of interference fringes. If the two round trips take even slightly different times, the fringes sit in a particular place. Change that time difference — by rotating the whole apparatus relative to the supposed aether wind — and the fringes shift. The shift can be read to a small fraction of a single fringe, and a fraction of a fringe corresponds to a difference in light-travel time of less than the period of a single optical oscillation.

In 1881, working in Potsdam, Michelson built the first such device and ran the experiment.17 With arms about 1.2 metres long, he expected the rotation to displace the fringes by roughly four-hundredths of a fringe — small, but he believed within his instrument's reach. He saw no displacement. The aether wind, he reported, was absent.

The 1881 result was not, however, decisive, and for an instructive reason. Hendrik Antoon Lorentz, examining the experiment in 1886, found that Michelson had miscalculated.18 Michelson had assumed that only the arm pointing along the aether wind was affected. But the transverse arm is affected too: light sent out perpendicular to the wind must travel a slightly slanted path, like a swimmer aiming straight across a river who is carried downstream and ends up swimming a longer diagonal. Accounting for this halved the expected effect, reducing the predicted shift to about two-hundredths of a fringe — uncomfortably close to the noise of Michelson's prototype. (The French physicist Alfred Potier had pointed out the same error privately.) The prototype had proved the method feasible, but it had not, strictly, proved anything about the aether.

Michelson resolved to do it properly. At what is now Case Western Reserve University in Cleveland he joined forces with the chemist Edward Williams Morley, a meticulous experimentalist of formidable patience. Before attacking the aether wind directly, the pair first turned their refined interferometric technique to a confirmation of Fresnel's drag coefficient, repeating Fizeau's moving-water experiment with far greater precision and vindicating the partial-dragging result.19 Then they built the instrument that would settle the question — an apparatus whose sensitivity and ingenuity made it one of the masterpieces of nineteenth-century experimental physics.20

Chapter Six

The experiment of 1887

The problem with a sensitive interferometer is that it is sensitive to everything. A footstep in the building, a passing carriage, a gradient of temperature across the room, the strain of rotating the apparatus — any of these could swamp a two-tenths-of-a-fringe signal. Michelson and Morley's design was an extended exercise in defeating these enemies.

The optical components were mounted on a square slab of sandstone about 1.5 metres on a side and 30 centimetres thick. The slab rested not on a foundation but on an annular wooden float, set into a circular trough of mercury. Floating on liquid metal, the massive stone could be set turning with a touch and would rotate continuously, smoothly, without vibration and without the mechanical strain that bends an apparatus clamped to the ground. The observer walked slowly around the instrument as it turned, reading the fringes through a small telescope without ever stopping the motion.

To reach the second order in v/c, the arms had to be long — and physical space was limited. The solution was to fold the light path with multiple reflections, bouncing each beam back and forth among a set of mirrors so that an effective path of about eleven metres was packed into the modest dimensions of the slab. Eleven metres is roughly twenty million wavelengths of yellow sodium light, and it is the length that sets the size of the effect.

The arithmetic of the prediction is worth stating explicitly. With both arms of length L, the difference in round-trip time between the longitudinal and transverse beams is of order (L/c)(v/c)2. Rotating the apparatus through ninety degrees swaps the two arms and doubles the observable, so the fringes should shift by a number of fringes equal to:

ΔN  =  2L · v2 / ( λ · c2 )   ≈   0.40 fringe
L ≈ 11 m  ·  v ≈ 30 km/s  ·  λ ≈ 589 nm  ·  c ≈ 3 × 10⁸ m/s

Four-tenths of a fringe is a large, unmistakable displacement — far above the instrument's resolution, which could detect a shift of about one-hundredth of a fringe. If the Earth moved through a stationary aether at its known orbital speed, Michelson and Morley would see the fringes swing by nearly half a band as the slab turned. They could hardly miss it.

Over several days in July 1887, in the basement of the Adelbert dormitory, they turned the slab and watched. They took readings at noon and again in the evening, and on different dates, so that the Earth's rotation and its orbital motion would point the apparatus in many different directions relative to any conceivable aether wind. They observed essentially nothing. The fringes did not swing by four-tenths of a band, nor by one-tenth. Whatever residual flicker they could discern corresponded to a displacement no larger than about one-hundredth of a fringe — and most likely a good deal less — implying a relative velocity through the aether of at most a few kilometres per second, far below the thirty kilometres per second of the Earth's known orbital motion.21

Michelson was bitterly disappointed. He had built an instrument of unprecedented delicacy to measure a definite quantity, and the quantity had refused to appear. Writing to Lord Rayleigh in August 1887, he called the result of the experiments on the relative motion of the Earth and the aether "decidedly negative," and for some years he regarded the whole effort as a failure — a failure to detect something he was confident must be there.22 He could not have known that the failure was the discovery, and that it was among the most important measurements ever made.

Chapter Seven

A null result, and what it could mean

The power of the 1887 experiment lay in the brutal gap between prediction and observation. This was not a marginal result hovering near the limits of sensitivity. The theory predicted a swing of nearly half a fringe; the instrument could resolve a hundredth of a fringe; and the apparatus saw, within its considerable precision, nothing at all.

What could account for it? Only a handful of escape routes existed, and each demanded a sacrifice elsewhere.

The aether could be fully dragged

One could revive Stokes's hypothesis: perhaps the Earth drags the local aether along so completely that, at the surface, there is no relative motion and hence no wind to detect. But this collided with two firmly established facts. Stellar aberration requires that the aether not be fully dragged in the space between the stars and us, and Fizeau's experiment — now confirmed to high precision by Michelson and Morley themselves — showed that transparent media drag the aether only partially, by Fresnel's fractional coefficient. A fully dragging aether was inconsistent with the very phenomena that had made the aether credible.

Light could be ballistic

Alternatively, one could abandon the wave-in-a-medium picture and suppose that light is emitted at a fixed speed relative to its source, like a bullet from a gun, so that the source's motion is simply carried along and no wind appears. Such "emission" theories did predict a null result for Michelson and Morley. But they made disastrous predictions elsewhere. As Willem de Sitter pointed out in 1913, if the speed of light depended on the source's velocity, the light from the two stars of a binary system — one approaching, one receding — would travel at different speeds, arriving out of order and grotesquely distorting the apparent orbit. Observed binary stars showed no such distortion, ruling out source-dependent light speed.23

There could be no aether at all

The remaining possibility was the one nobody wished to entertain: that there is no luminiferous aether, that light needs no medium, and that the experiment found nothing because there was nothing to find. But this seemed to dissolve the very meaning of an electromagnetic wave. A wave of what, propagating through what? To deny the aether in 1887 was to deny the mechanical substrate of physics itself, and almost no one was prepared to pay that price. Instead, the leading theorists chose a fourth path — one that kept the aether alive by attributing a strange new behaviour to matter.

Chapter Eight

Saving the aether: a contraction of matter

In 1889, the Irish physicist George Francis FitzGerald published an idea of startling economy in the American journal Science — a note of a single paragraph. Suppose, he wrote, that the length of a material body changes when it moves through the aether: that it contracts slightly along its direction of motion. If bodies shrink by exactly the right amount, the longitudinal arm of Michelson's interferometer would be shortened just enough to compensate for the slower light-travel time the aether wind imposes, and the two arms would always read equal. The expected fringe shift would be cancelled — not because there is no aether wind, but because the apparatus deforms in a way that hides it.24

FitzGerald's reasoning was not pure invention. Oliver Heaviside had shown that same year that the electric field of a moving charge is squeezed out of spherical symmetry, flattened in the direction of motion into what became known as the Heaviside ellipsoid.25 FitzGerald made the connecting leap: if the forces that hold molecules together in a solid are electromagnetic in nature — and by the 1880s this was a reasonable guess — then those forces should be distorted by motion in the same way, and a moving body should settle into a slightly contracted equilibrium shape. The contraction was small, of second order in v/c, precisely the order of the effect it was meant to cancel. (So obscure was the journal at the time that FitzGerald was never quite certain his note had appeared in print; the idea spread mainly through correspondence and through Oliver Lodge.)

Working independently in Leiden, Lorentz arrived at the same hypothesis in 1892 and, over the following years, built it into a systematic theory.26 For Lorentz this was a genuine, physical, dynamical effect. Matter moving through the stationary aether really contracts, because the equilibrium of the electric forces binding it is really altered by the motion. The required factor — now called the Lorentz–FitzGerald contraction — is:

L  =  L0 · √1 − v2/c2
a rod of rest length L₀ moving at speed v contracts to length L along its motion

In 1895 Lorentz went further, introducing a mathematical device he called local time: a time variable, different for moving frames, that made the equations of electromagnetism in a moving system look the same as in a system at rest.27 This "theorem of corresponding states" explained, in one stroke, why all the first-order experiments had given null results — the moving system's optics, expressed in local time, mimicked the stationary system exactly. Lorentz regarded local time as a useful fiction, an auxiliary quantity with no claim to being the real time, which continued to flow uniformly in the rest frame of the aether. Joseph Larmor, in Aether and Matter (1900), pushed the same programme to second order, recognizing that moving clocks would have to run slow as well — an early glimpse of time dilation, still cast as a property of motion through the aether.28

By the turn of the century, then, the aether had been saved — but at a remarkable cost. To preserve it, physics had been obliged to assert that moving rulers shrink and moving clocks slow, by amounts exactly calibrated to render the aether undetectable. Every experiment designed to reveal the aether wind would be defeated by a compensating deformation of the measuring apparatus. The medium was still there, in the rest frame of absolute space, but it had been endowed with a perfect talent for hiding.

“We had been so happy in our aether; it was a comfortable place to live in. And now we are asked to believe that it cannot be detected — that it conspires, by contracting our instruments, to escape every measurement we devise.”

— A characterization of the post-1887 predicament

This is the kind of move that makes scientists uneasy. A hypothesis introduced solely to explain away a single negative result, with no independent test of its own, is the textbook example of an ad hoc rescue. The contraction was not predicted by any prior theory and could not, by its very construction, be checked against anything other than the experiment it was designed to save. Something was clearly wrong — not necessarily false, but unsatisfying in a way that the best minds in physics could feel even when they could not yet name it.

Chapter Nine

Lorentz, Poincaré, and the edge of relativity

No one felt the unease more acutely than Henri Poincaré. As early as 1900, and with increasing force in his book Science and Hypothesis (1902), Poincaré objected to the methodology of piling one compensating hypothesis upon another.29 It seemed to him no accident that every attempt to detect absolute motion failed; the failures were too systematic, too perfectly arranged. Rather than explaining each null result with a fresh contrivance, Poincaré suspected that nature was trying to tell physicists something general: that absolute motion is simply undetectable, as a matter of principle.

Lorentz, meanwhile, perfected the mathematics. In a paper of 1904 he wrote down the full set of transformations between the coordinates of a stationary and a moving system — the equations that leave Maxwell's electromagnetism unchanged to all orders in v/c, not merely the first.30 These are, in their algebraic form, the transformations we now use in special relativity. Lorentz had them in hand a year before Einstein's paper. What he did not have was a reinterpretation of what they meant. For Lorentz, there remained a true rest frame — the aether's — in which space and time were ordinary and absolute; the transformations described how, owing to real contractions and the fiction of local time, moving observers were fooled into measuring something different.

Poincaré came closer still. In a celebrated address at St. Louis in 1904 he elevated the undetectability of absolute motion to a fundamental law, which he named the principle of relativity: the laws of physics must be the same for an observer at rest and for one carried along in uniform motion, so that we have no means of determining whether we are being carried along or not.31 In 1905, in a paper on the dynamics of the electron submitted just before Einstein's, Poincaré tidied Lorentz's transformations into their exact form, proved that they constitute a mathematical group, and named them after Lorentz.32 He even discussed gravitation propagating at the speed of light.

And yet Poincaré, too, kept the aether. For him the principle of relativity and the Lorentz group were profound truths about the symmetry of physical law, but the aether persisted as a privileged frame and a convenient mode of speech — a convention one was free to retain. He never took the final step of declaring the aether superfluous and rebuilding space and time from the ground up. That step required a different cast of mind, and it was taken, in the same year, by a twenty-six-year-old examiner in the Swiss patent office.

What Lorentz and Poincaré already had — and what they lacked

By 1905 the mathematics of special relativity existed: the contraction, an early form of time dilation, the full Lorentz transformations, the relativity principle, the group structure. The pieces were on the table.

What was missing was the interpretation: the recognition that these were not dynamical illusions imposed by a hidden medium, but the actual structure of space and time, valid with no aether and no preferred frame whatsoever. The decisive missing idea was the relativity of simultaneity — that whether two distant events happen "at the same time" has no absolute answer.

Chapter Ten

Einstein, 1905: dissolving the problem

Albert Einstein's paper "On the Electrodynamics of Moving Bodies" arrived at the Annalen der Physik in June 1905.33 It contains almost no equations for its first several pages, cites no sources, and never mentions Michelson by name. It does not try to explain the null result. It makes the null result inevitable — by changing the question.

Einstein began not from an experiment but from an asymmetry that offended him. In Maxwell's electrodynamics, the interaction between a magnet and a conductor was described by two entirely different mechanisms depending on which one was said to be "really" moving: move the magnet, and a changing field induces an electric force; move the conductor, and a magnetic force pushes its charges. The observable current is identical in both cases, yet the theory told two different stories, distinguished only by an appeal to absolute motion that no experiment could verify. Einstein took this as a clue. The asymmetry, he reasoned, was an artifact of a redundant concept. The physics depends only on the relative motion of magnet and conductor; the notion of absolute rest does no work.

From this he extracted two postulates and asked what followed if they were taken as exactly, unconditionally true:

The two postulates

The principle of relativity. The laws of physics take the same form in every inertial frame. No experiment of any kind can distinguish uniform motion from rest.

The constancy of the speed of light. Light propagates in empty space at a definite speed c that is the same for every inertial observer — independent of the motion of the source and of the observer.

The second postulate is the audacious one, because taken together with the first it appears self-contradictory. If I chase a light beam at half its speed, surely I should measure it receding at only half c? Einstein's answer was that this commonsense expectation rests on a hidden assumption: that the time and space measurements of the chaser and the chased can be straightforwardly compared. They cannot. The two postulates can both hold, with no contradiction, provided we surrender the belief that simultaneity is absolute.

This is the conceptual hinge that Lorentz and Poincaré never quite turned. Einstein showed that two events judged simultaneous by one observer are not simultaneous for another in relative motion — there is no universal "now" extending across space. Once simultaneity is relative, the rest follows by elementary algebra. Moving clocks run slow (time dilation); moving rods contract (length contraction); the transformations between frames are exactly Lorentz's. But every one of these effects is now kinematic — a consequence of the structure of space and time itself — rather than a dynamical distortion inflicted on matter by a medium.

The Lorentz–FitzGerald contraction, in Einstein's hands, ceased to be a physical shrinking caused by motion through the aether. It became a statement about how length, an inherently frame-dependent quantity, is apportioned between observers who disagree about simultaneity. There was nothing to compress and nothing doing the compressing. And the aether, the privileged frame, the absolute space — all of it could be discarded, because the theory required no "absolutely stationary space" and assigned no velocity to any point of empty space. Einstein stated the consequence plainly: the introduction of a luminiferous aether would prove superfluous.

“The introduction of a ‘luminiferous ether’ will prove to be superfluous, inasmuch as the view here to be developed will not require an ‘absolutely stationary space.’”

— A. Einstein, Zur Elektrodynamik bewegter Körper, 1905

The Michelson–Morley experiment had found no aether wind because there was no aether and no wind — but, more deeply, because the very framework in which an "aether wind" made sense had been mistaken. There is no medium at rest against which to measure the Earth's motion, because the speed of light is the same in every direction for every uniformly moving observer, full stop. The null result was not a failure to detect something small. It was a direct manifestation of the symmetry of space and time.

Three years later, Einstein's former teacher Hermann Minkowski gave the new kinematics its definitive geometric form. Space and time, he announced, were not separate stages but threads of a single four-dimensional fabric — spacetime — in which the Lorentz transformations are simply rotations.34 "Henceforth space by itself, and time by itself, are doomed to fade away into mere shadows," he declared, "and only a kind of union of the two will preserve an independent reality." The mechanical universe, with its absolute space threaded by a material aether, had been replaced by a geometry.

Chapter Eleven

Did Michelson–Morley cause relativity?

The standard textbook narrative is clean and memorable: Michelson and Morley found the null result; the null result baffled physics; Einstein explained it. It is also, as a matter of history, substantially misleading — and the corrective is one of the most illuminating episodes in the historiography of science.

The case against the legend was made decisively by the historian Gerald Holton in 1969.35 Holton pointed to the obvious but overlooked features of Einstein's 1905 paper itself. It opens with the magnet-and-conductor asymmetry, not with any experiment on the aether wind. It refers to the failure to detect the Earth's motion through the light medium only once, in a single subordinate clause, and lumps it together with "unsuccessful attempts" in general rather than singling out the famous experiment. Einstein cites no experimental paper at all. The structure of the argument is that of a theorist reasoning from principles of symmetry and simplicity, not of an experimentalist's puzzle being resolved.

Einstein's own later testimony was famously inconsistent. At various times he said the Michelson–Morley result had a "considerable influence" on him; at others he claimed he could not remember whether he had known of it when he wrote the paper, or that it played a negligible role, or that he had been more impressed by Fizeau's drag experiment and by stellar aberration. He certainly knew of the null result indirectly, since he had read Lorentz's 1895 monograph, which discusses it. But the weight of the evidence, as Holton and later scholars such as Abraham Pais and John Stachel assembled it, is that Einstein was driven primarily by the internal asymmetries and conceptual tangles of electrodynamics, and by a deep conviction that absolute motion was a fiction — not by a need to account for one specific measurement.3637

This matters beyond mere credit-keeping, because it cuts against a tidy philosophy of how science works. The myth holds that a crucial experiment delivers an anomaly, and theory dutifully follows to explain it. The reality here is messier and more interesting. The experiment and the theory were connected, but loosely and indirectly; the same empirical situation that drove Lorentz to invent a contracting aether drove Einstein to abolish the aether entirely, and the difference between them was not the data but the interpretation each was willing to impose on it. The Michelson–Morley result was part of the climate — the diffuse sense, shared across European physics, that absolute motion stubbornly refused to show itself — rather than the single falling apple.

What the experiment unambiguously did accomplish was something subtler than causing relativity. It demonstrated, with a precision impossible to wave away, that there was no aether wind to second order. It thereby foreclosed the comfortable middle positions, ruled out a partially detectable aether, and forced the choice that physics eventually had to make: either an undetectable medium hidden by a conspiracy of compensating effects, or no medium at all. Einstein chose the second. The experiment did not hand him the answer, but it had cleared the ground on which the answer could finally stand.

Chapter Twelve

The experiments that would not stop

Relativity did not end the aether-drift experiments; in some ways it intensified them. A theory this radical demanded scrutiny, and the obvious way to scrutinize it was to keep hunting for the aether wind with ever-better instruments. Each new null result tightened the noose, and a few of them tested relativity in genuinely independent ways.

In 1903, Frederick Trouton and Henry Noble suspended a charged parallel-plate capacitor and looked for the tiny turning force that motion through the aether should exert on it as the Earth carried it about. There was no torque.38 A year earlier, Lord Rayleigh — and soon afterward DeWitt Brace — had searched for a different consequence of the contraction hypothesis: if a transparent body really shrinks along its motion, it should become optically anisotropic and split light into two rays, a double refraction that ought to be detectable. None appeared.39 This null was acutely awkward for Lorentz's dynamical contraction, which seemed to predict the effect; it fell out naturally in Einstein's kinematic account, where nothing is physically strained. Morley himself, with a young collaborator named Dayton Miller, rebuilt the interferometer with longer arms and ran it again between 1902 and 1905. Again: nothing.40

The Miller anomaly

Then the consensus cracked — from inside. Dayton C. Miller did not let the question go. For more than two decades he refined and repeated the experiment, eventually hauling his apparatus to the Mount Wilson Observatory, on the theory that a solid laboratory building at sea level might be dragging the local aether and shielding the wind. In the 1920s, after tens of thousands of readings, Miller announced that he had found it: a small but persistent and systematic fringe shift, corresponding to an aether drift of roughly eight to ten kilometres per second, pointing toward a definite direction in the heavens.41

This was not a crank's claim. Miller was a respected experimentalist, a former president of the American Physical Society, and the heir to Morley's own apparatus and methods. If his result was real, special relativity was wrong. The physics community took it seriously enough that it became, for a time, a genuine crisis — and it reached Einstein, who followed Miller's reports with concern. It was in this context, on hearing that Miller's experiments might be showing a positive effect, that Einstein is said to have made his famous remark about the deviousness of nature.

“Subtle is the Lord, but malicious He is not.”

— Albert Einstein, on hearing of Miller's claimed aether drift (Princeton, 1921)

Einstein's wager was that an honest universe would not hide a true theory behind a misleading experiment — that Miller's signal, if it was not real, must have a discoverable mundane cause.36 He was right on both counts. Every contemporary who repeated the experiment with comparable or better precision — Kennedy, Illingworth, Joos, Piccard, and others, through the 1920s and 1930s — obtained a null result, often an order of magnitude tighter than Miller's claimed effect. Miller's positive signal stood alone, reproduced by no one.

The matter was finally laid to rest in 1955, the year of Einstein's death, when Robert Shankland and his collaborators undertook a painstaking reanalysis of Miller's original data sheets — work that Einstein had encouraged through correspondence in his last years.42 They showed that Miller's apparent periodic signal was an artifact: partly statistical fluctuation in data read by eye from a drifting fringe, but chiefly the product of minute, systematic differences in temperature across his apparatus. Miller's mountaintop hut was not thermally controlled, and a fraction of a degree of temperature gradient, rotating with the building's exposure to the sun, was enough to flex the long light paths and counterfeit an aether wind of just the kind he was looking for. The last empirical ghost of the aether turned out to be a draught of warm air.

Chapter Thirteen

Closing the case, to one part in 1018

The Michelson–Morley experiment tests one specific thing: whether the speed of light is the same in different directions — its isotropy. That is necessary for relativity but not sufficient, and two further experiments completed the empirical foundation.

In 1932, Roy Kennedy and Edward Thorndike performed a Michelson-type experiment with deliberately unequal arms, one much shorter than the other. An apparatus with unequal arms is sensitive not merely to the direction of any motion but to changes in the magnitude of the speed of light as the Earth's velocity changes over the day and the year. A null result here tests, in effect, time dilation — the velocity dependence that length contraction alone cannot supply. Once again the fringes held still, confirming that the speed of light is independent of the apparatus's velocity as well as its orientation.43 Six years later, Herbert Ives and G. R. Stilwell measured the transverse Doppler shift of light emitted by fast-moving hydrogen ions and found exactly the frequency change that moving-clock time dilation predicts — a direct, positive confirmation of the effect, ironically obtained by Ives, who never accepted the relativistic interpretation of his own result.44 Together, the isotropy of the Michelson–Morley experiment, the velocity-independence of Kennedy–Thorndike, and the time dilation of Ives–Stilwell pin down the full Lorentz transformation experimentally.

The hunt did not end in the 1930s; it became one of the most precise enterprises in all of physics. Modern experimenters reframe the old question in a rigorous test-theory language — the Robertson–Mansouri–Sexl framework, which parametrizes how much a hypothetical preferred frame could affect lengths, times, and light speed.45 The interferometer of mercury and sandstone has given way to laser-stabilized optical cavities and cryogenic sapphire resonators, whose resonant frequencies would shift if the speed of light were even minutely anisotropic.

The numbers are staggering. In 1979, Brillet and Hall used a laser locked to a rotating optical cavity to constrain any anisotropy to about one part in 1015.46 In 2003, a cryogenic experiment comparing two orthogonal optical resonators tightened the bound on a possible anisotropy of the speed of light to a few parts in 1015.47 These results are now routinely interpreted within the Standard-Model Extension, a comprehensive framework that catalogues every way Lorentz symmetry could conceivably be broken.48 By 2015, a rotating experiment comparing ultrastable microwave oscillators had constrained orientation-dependent variations in the speed of light to below one part in 1018 — bounding any residual aether wind some fourteen orders of magnitude more tightly than Michelson and Morley could.49

From 1887 to today

Michelson and Morley bounded the fractional anisotropy of light speed at roughly one part in 109. A century and a quarter later, the same experiment — the same idea, vastly refined — reaches below one part in 1018. The aether wind is, today, among the most thoroughly excluded effects in the whole of natural science. Lorentz invariance is one of the most stringently tested principles in physics.

Chapter Fourteen

The aether's afterlife

The word refuses to die quietly. Every few years someone asks whether modern physics has, after all, smuggled the aether back in — and the question is worth taking seriously, because the answer sharpens exactly what was, and was not, killed in 1887.

Consider the cosmic microwave background, the relic radiation of the early universe that bathes all of space. It is very nearly the same in every direction, but not exactly: it is slightly hotter in one direction and cooler in the opposite one, a dipole pattern that arises because the Solar System is moving relative to the frame in which the radiation is isotropic. From that dipole one can read off our velocity — about 370 kilometres per second in a particular cosmic direction.50 Here, surely, is a preferred frame, a cosmic standard of rest. Is this not the aether after all?

It is not — and the distinction is precise. The cosmic microwave background defines a preferred frame the way a crowd defines an average direction of motion: it is a fact about the contents of the universe, the contingent distribution of matter and radiation, not a property of the laws of physics or of space itself. You detect your motion relative to it by looking out at the sky and measuring the radiation, never by any local experiment confined to your laboratory. Special relativity never claimed that the universe lacks landmarks; it claimed that no local experiment can distinguish uniform motion from rest, because the laws governing that experiment are identical in every inertial frame. The Michelson–Morley experiment is a local experiment, and it — together with all its descendants — still returns its null. There is a cosmic rest frame in the sense of a preferred set of clocks; there is no aether in the sense of a mechanical medium that drags light and reveals itself to local optics.

The same answer applies to the quantum vacuum, which is sometimes loosely called a "new aether." The vacuum of quantum field theory is a roiling, structured thing, full of fluctuating fields and virtual processes; the Higgs field permeates all of space and gives particles their mass. But these are constructed to be — and are measured to be — Lorentz invariant. The quantum vacuum has no rest frame; it looks identical to every uniformly moving observer; it exerts no drag and supports no wind. The Higgs field is a scalar, the same in all frames, and it no more defines a state of absolute rest than the number five does. The crucial property that the nineteenth-century aether possessed — a material rest frame, detectable by local measurement — is precisely the property that every experiment from 1887 to the present has excluded, now to better than a part in 1018. Whatever fills the vacuum, it is not the thing Michelson and Morley went looking for.51

What was actually lost

It is worth being clear about the size of what changed, because it is easy, more than a century on, to underestimate it. The aether was not a peripheral hypothesis. It was the last and most fundamental gear of a mechanical universe — a universe in which every wave was a disturbance of some material substrate, every force was transmitted by contact through intervening stuff, and all of it played out on the fixed stage of an absolute space and a universal time that flowed the same for everyone, everywhere. This picture was not merely believed; it was felt to be the only intelligible way the world could be. Kelvin could not understand a thing until he had built a mechanical model of it, and in this he spoke for his entire era.

The Michelson–Morley experiment, and the long crisis it precipitated, dismantled that picture — not by discovering a new substance or a new force, but by establishing an absence with overwhelming precision and then following the logic of that absence to its end. The medium was gone; the absolute space was gone; the universal "now" was gone. In their place stood a four-dimensional geometry in which the separation of space from time depends on the observer, and in which the constancy of the speed of light is not a fact about a medium but a structural feature of reality.

There is a lesson here about how the deepest scientific revolutions actually arrive. We imagine them as discoveries — a new planet, a new particle, a new force suddenly seen. But the overthrow of the mechanical universe came from the opposite direction. It came from looking very carefully for something everyone knew was there, finding with great precision that it was not, and having the courage to believe the emptiness rather than explain it away. The contraction hypothesis was the comfortable response: keep the old world and add a conspiracy to hide its failure. Relativity was the harder one: accept that the intuition was wrong at the root. Lorentz invariance is today among the most precisely confirmed principles in all of physics50 — and it began with two men in a Cleveland basement, turning a stone on a pool of mercury, watching a pattern of light that simply, stubbornly, refused to move.
