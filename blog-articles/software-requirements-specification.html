Section 01The hardest part of building software

Every working software system is the answer to a question that was, at some point, badly understood. Before there is code, before there is architecture, before anyone argues about databases or frameworks, there is a more fundamental and far harder problem: deciding, precisely, what the thing is supposed to do. Requirements engineering is the discipline of answering that question on purpose rather than by accident, and the Software Requirements Specification is the document where the answer is written down.

In 1987 Fred Brooks, the manager who built IBM's System/360 and its operating system, distilled decades of painful experience into a single sentence. “The hardest single part of building a software system is deciding precisely what to build.”1 Brooks argued that no other part of the work cripples the resulting system so thoroughly if done wrong, and no other part is more difficult to correct after the fact. Translating syntax errors into working code is the easy part; the conceptual labor of figuring out what the program ought to be is where projects live or die.

The reason is not laziness or incompetence. It is that the people who need a system rarely know, in advance, exactly what they need. They know their problem in the impressionistic way that everyone knows their own work — full of unstated assumptions, special cases nobody mentions until they are violated, and expectations so obvious to the customer that it never occurs to them to say so out loud. The job of requirements engineering is to drag all of that into the light, write it down in a form precise enough to build from and verify against, and keep it honest as understanding deepens.

When that job is skipped or botched, the failures are not subtle. The Standish Group's CHAOS research, which has tracked software project outcomes since the mid-1990s, has consistently named requirements problems among the leading reasons projects are challenged or cancelled outright. In its early reporting, incomplete requirements and lack of user involvement sat at the very top of the list of factors that impaired projects, ahead of resources, expectations, and executive support.2 The diagnosis has barely changed across thirty years of methodological revolutions: the systems that fail tend to fail because nobody got the requirements right, not because nobody could write the code.

The hardest single part of building a software system is deciding precisely what to build.

Frederick P. Brooks, Jr., “No Silver Bullet” (1987)

This guide is about how that decision gets made well. It covers the artifacts requirements take — the formal Software Requirements Specification, the user story, the use case, the functional and non-functional requirement — and the standards that govern them, principally the international standard ISO/IEC/IEEE 29148 and its influential predecessor IEEE 830. It traces where these ideas came from, shows what a good requirement looks like next to a bad one, surveys the tools practitioners actually use, and confronts the uncomfortable evidence about what poor requirements cost. The throughline is a single transformation that sits at the heart of the discipline: taking a fuzzy human need and turning it into a precise, testable statement of what a system must do.

Section 02A short history of requirements

The idea that software needs disciplined requirements is almost as old as the idea that software is hard to build. The two were recognized together. In October 1968, a group of computing's founders gathered in Garmisch, Germany, for a NATO-sponsored conference that gave the field its enduring name: software engineering. The conference report is remembered for its candor about a discipline already in crisis — participants spoke of building systems the way the Wright brothers built aircraft, by pushing the whole thing off a cliff and starting over after it crashed.3 Much of that crisis was a requirements crisis: systems that did not do what their sponsors needed, discovered too late to fix cheaply.

The early lifecycle models tried to impose order through sequence. Winston Royce's 1970 paper, the one usually credited (somewhat unfairly) with inventing the “waterfall” model, placed a requirements phase at the head of the process and insisted that each stage be largely complete before the next began.4 Royce himself warned that a strictly sequential approach was risky and argued for iteration and prototyping — a nuance the industry spent decades forgetting. But the core insight stuck: requirements come first, and getting them stable is the precondition for everything downstream.

By the mid-1970s, researchers were studying requirements as a problem in its own right. Bell and Thayer's pointed 1976 paper asked the question directly in its title — Software requirements: are they really a problem? — and answered, emphatically, yes, with data showing that requirements errors were both common and disproportionately damaging.5 The same year, Barry Boehm's survey of software engineering laid out the economic argument that would dominate the field for a generation: errors are cheap to fix when caught during requirements and ruinously expensive when caught after delivery, because late corrections force rework through every intervening stage.6

The 1980s brought new representations. Working on telecommunications systems at Ericsson, the Swedish engineer Ivar Jacobson needed a way to describe how a system behaves from the outside, in terms of the value it delivers to its users. He treated the system as a black box and described the sequences of interaction between an external actor and the system. He called these use cases — an awkward English rendering of his Swedish användningsfall — and presented them at the OOPSLA conference in 1987 before popularizing them in his influential 1992 book on object-oriented software engineering.7 Use cases gave requirements a structure built around goals and scenarios rather than disconnected lists of features, and they remain a core technique today.

Standardization followed practice. The IEEE had been refining recommended practices for requirements documents since the 1980s, culminating in IEEE 830-1998, the Recommended Practice for Software Requirements Specifications, which codified the contents and quality attributes of a good SRS and supplied template outlines that a generation of engineers learned from.8 It became the default reference for what an SRS should contain.

Then the pendulum swung. Frustrated by heavyweight documentation that aged badly and bred false confidence, the agile movement of the late 1990s proposed a radically lighter approach. In Extreme Programming Explained (1999), Kent Beck introduced the user story: a short, plain-language description of a feature from a user's perspective, written on an index card and treated not as a finished specification but as a promise to have a conversation later.9 Stories deliberately traded completeness for adaptability, betting that face-to-face collaboration and working software would surface requirements better than any document could.

The two traditions — rigorous specification and lightweight conversation — have since been converging rather than competing. The current international standard, ISO/IEC/IEEE 29148:2018, unifies the vocabulary and processes of requirements engineering across systems and software, and is methodology-agnostic by design: it applies whether a team writes a 200-page specification or a backlog of stories.10 The history of requirements is, in the end, a long argument about how much to write down — an argument that every team still has, in its own way, on every project.

Section 03What a requirement actually is

It is worth being precise about the word itself, because casual usage blurs distinctions that matter. The international standard defines requirements engineering as an interdisciplinary function that mediates between the world of the people who want a system and the world of the people who build it — concerned with discovering, eliciting, analyzing, verifying, validating, communicating, documenting, and managing requirements throughout the life of the system.10 A single requirement, in that framing, is a statement that translates a need into something a builder can act on and a tester can check.

Karl Wiegers, whose textbook is among the most widely used in the field, draws a distinction that clears up endless confusion: requirements exist at different levels of abstraction, and statements at one level are not interchangeable with statements at another.11 A business objective (“reduce fraud losses”) is not the same kind of statement as a user goal (“an analyst can flag a suspicious transaction”), which is not the same as a system requirement (“the system shall hold a flagged transaction for manual review before settlement”). Each derives from the one above it; each is more concrete and more verifiable than the last. Conflating them is a common way for arguments to go in circles, because people think they are discussing the same thing when they are operating two or three levels apart.

This layering gives requirements their characteristic shape: a funnel that runs from the broad, ambiguous why at the top to the narrow, precise what at the bottom. At the widest point sit business or mission needs, which describe why the system is worth building at all. Below them, stakeholder requirements capture what particular users need to accomplish. Below those, system requirements state what the system itself must do to satisfy those needs — the level where the binding “shall” statements live. And at the base, software or component requirements describe how individual parts must behave. The standard organizes its processes around exactly these transitions: business or mission analysis, stakeholder needs and requirements definition, and system or software requirements definition.10

Two properties of this funnel deserve emphasis. First, traceability runs both ways. Every system requirement should trace upward to a stakeholder need that justifies it — a requirement that cannot be linked to a real need is a candidate for deletion — and every need should trace downward to the requirements that satisfy it, so that gaps become visible. A requirement floating free of any business reason is usually either gold-plating or a misunderstanding. Second, precision is earned, not assumed. The statements at the top are deliberately loose, because that is the right way to describe a goal; the statements at the bottom must be exact, because that is the only way to build and test against them. Much of the skill in requirements work is knowing which level you are operating at and writing with the precision appropriate to it — no vaguer, and no more falsely specific.

Finally, requirements are conventionally split along an orthogonal axis that cuts across all of these levels: functional requirements, which describe what the system does, and non-functional requirements, which describe how well it must do it. A login feature is functional; the demand that login complete within two seconds for ninety-nine percent of attempts is non-functional. The two are easy to state and notoriously easy to confuse, and they fail in different ways, so the next two sections take them one at a time.

Section 04Functional requirements: the “shall” statement

A functional requirement states something the system must do — a behavior, a service, a transformation of input into output. “The system shall allow a user to reset a forgotten password by email.” “The system shall calculate sales tax based on the shipping address.” “The system shall prevent a withdrawal that would overdraw an account.” These are the visible features — the things a user, an auditor, or a competitor would point to and call the product. Most people, asked what a requirement is, imagine a functional one.

The grammar of these statements is not accidental. The modal verb carries the obligation, and the international standard fixes its meaning precisely: shall expresses a binding requirement, should expresses a recommendation, may expresses permission or an allowed option, and will expresses a statement of fact or intent about something outside the system's control.10 The distinction is not pedantry. A specification that scatters “should” and “shall” interchangeably leaves a contractor genuinely unsure which clauses are obligations and which are wishes — and on a fixed-price contract, that ambiguity is litigated. The convention exists so that a single word, reliably, tells the reader whether a sentence can be ignored.

The standard goes further and describes the underlying anatomy of a well-formed requirement statement: a subject (the entity the requirement is about), a verb expressing the action, the object or result of that action, and any conditions or constraints under which it applies — often written in the order [condition] [subject] shall [action] [object] [constraint].10 “While the vehicle is in motion, the system shall disable text entry on the central console.” The condition scopes when the obligation applies; the subject names who must satisfy it; the action and object say what must happen. Strip any of these out and the requirement becomes either ambiguous or untestable.

SRS‑AUTH‑002

When a user signs in, the system shall require a second authentication factor in addition to a password.

Type: functional Priority: critical Verify: test Trace: STK‑7

Notice what the chip above does and does not say. It mandates a second factor; it does not mandate which factor — SMS code, authenticator app, hardware key. That is a deliberate choice. A functional requirement should state what must happen and leave how to the design, unless the “how” is itself a genuine constraint (a regulator may, for instance, forbid SMS as a factor, which would then become its own requirement). Over-specifying mechanism inside a requirement is one of the most common ways teams accidentally freeze a bad decision into a contract.

Taming natural language: the EARS patterns

English is a treacherous medium for requirements. The sentence “the system shall not accept invalid input” sounds rigorous and says almost nothing: invalid by what definition, rejected how, with what response? In 2009 a group of engineers at Rolls-Royce, working on control software for jet engines, published a deceptively simple remedy: the Easy Approach to Requirements Syntax, or EARS.12 Rather than inventing a formal language — which practitioners resist and managers cannot read — EARS constrains ordinary English into a small set of sentence templates, each matched to a kind of system behavior.

The authors identified that almost every well-formed requirement falls into one of a handful of patterns.12 A ubiquitous requirement always holds: “The system shall encrypt all stored credentials.” An event-driven requirement fires on a trigger, signaled by when: “When a payment is declined, the system shall notify the customer within five seconds.” A state-driven requirement holds throughout a condition, signaled by while: “While the device is in airplane mode, the system shall queue outgoing messages.” An unwanted-behavior requirement handles errors and abuse, signaled by if…then: “If three consecutive logins fail, then the system shall lock the account for fifteen minutes.” And an optional-feature requirement applies only when a capability is present, signaled by where: “Where the vehicle has adaptive cruise control, the system shall maintain a set following distance.” Complex requirements combine these building blocks into a single, still-readable sentence.

The discipline pays off precisely because it is so modest. By forcing an author to pick a pattern, EARS surfaces the questions that vague prose hides: Is this always true, or only in some state? What exactly triggers it? What is the response, and how fast? A requirement that cannot be fitted to any pattern is usually not a requirement at all — it is a goal, a design note, or a confusion. The notation has since been adopted well beyond aero-engines, in automotive, medical, and aerospace programs where ambiguous requirements carry real human cost, and it pairs naturally with the modal-verb conventions of the international standard.12

A note on “the system shall”

The relentless repetition of “the system shall” reads strangely to outsiders, and agile critics mock it. But the monotony is a feature: a uniform subject and modal verb make requirements scannable, countable, and individually addressable in a way that flowing prose is not. Each “shall” is a thing someone must build and someone must test — which is exactly why they are counted, numbered, and tracked.

Section 05Non-functional requirements: how well, not just what

If functional requirements describe what a system does, non-functional requirements — sometimes called quality attributes or, more evocatively, the -ilities — describe how well it must do it. They are the difference between a system that technically works in a demo and one that survives contact with ten thousand real users at nine in the morning. “The system shall display search results” is functional. “The system shall return search results within 200 milliseconds for 95% of queries under peak load” is non-functional, and it is very often the requirement that decides whether the product lives or dies.

Three of the most consequential examples appear again and again, and they are worth dwelling on because each conceals a trap. “The system shall support 10,000 concurrent users” sounds like a number until you ask what concurrent means — ten thousand sessions open, or ten thousand requests in flight in the same second? — and what the system is supposed to do at user 10,001: reject, queue, or degrade. “Authentication shall use MFA” reads as functional, and the mechanism is, but the real obligation lurking behind it is a security quality attribute about resistance to credential theft. And “API response times shall be under 200 ms” is meaningless without a percentile and a load: an average of 200 ms can hide a 5% tail at three full seconds, and it is the tail that users feel and remember. A non-functional requirement without a number, a condition, and a measurement method is a wish wearing a lab coat.

Unverifiable

The system shall be fast and scalable.

No metric, no load condition, no threshold. “Fast” cannot be passed or failed, so it can never be signed off — or held to.

Verifiable

Under a sustained load of 10,000 concurrent sessions, the system shall serve 95% of API requests within 200 ms and 99% within 500 ms.

A load, two percentile thresholds, and an explicit metric. A load test either meets this or does not.

The decisive property is measurability. A good non-functional requirement names a quality, attaches a quantity, fixes the conditions of measurement, and implies a test. “Reliable” becomes “no more than 4.4 hours of unplanned downtime per year” (the famous “four nines”). “Usable” becomes “a first-time user shall complete checkout in under 90 seconds without assistance in usability testing.” “Secure” — the hardest to quantify — becomes a set of concrete obligations: multi-factor authentication, encryption of data at rest, a maximum window for patching known vulnerabilities. The act of making a quality attribute measurable is often the act of discovering you did not actually know what you wanted.

A map of the qualities: ISO/IEC 25010

Because the space of “-ilities” is large and easy to sample unevenly, the field relies on a standard taxonomy. ISO/IEC 25010, first published in 2011 as part of the SQuaRE series and superseding the older ISO/IEC 9126, defines a product quality model organizing software quality into eight top-level characteristics, each decomposed into measurable sub-characteristics.13 The eight are functional suitability, performance efficiency, compatibility, usability, reliability, security, maintainability, and portability. The model's value is less as a checklist to satisfy than as a prompt to ask: a team that has written twelve performance requirements and not one maintainability or portability requirement has learned something useful about its own blind spots.

The model is not frozen. A 2023 revision updated the product quality standard, most notably promoting Safety to a top-level characteristic in its own right — a recognition that software now routinely drives vehicles, dispenses medication, and controls industrial plant, where the relevant question is not merely whether the system works but whether it can cause harm.14 The same revision broadened the scope of two existing characteristics, with usability reframed around interaction capability and portability around flexibility, reflecting how much the contexts in which software runs have multiplied since 2011.14 Karl Wiegers makes the practical point that non-functional requirements are frequently the ones omitted entirely from a specification, precisely because they are nobody's feature and everybody's assumption — until the system is slow, insecure, or unmaintainable, at which point they become everyone's emergency.11

One structural fact makes non-functional requirements especially dangerous to neglect: they are usually architecturally significant. You can add a new report or a new field to a finished system at modest cost. You generally cannot bolt on a tenfold increase in throughput, a new security posture, or a hard real-time guarantee — those properties are consequences of decisions made at the very start, in the architecture the requirements were supposed to inform. This is why a quality attribute discovered late is so much more expensive than a feature discovered late, and why the next sections on process and cost return to it repeatedly.

Section 06SRS, user stories, and use cases: three ways to write it down

The same underlying need can be captured in very different artifacts, and the choice among them is one of the more contested decisions in software practice. Three forms dominate. The Software Requirements Specification is the heavyweight: a structured, comprehensive document that aims to state, in one place, everything the system must do and how well. The use case sits in the middle: a narrative of how an actor interacts with the system to achieve a goal, including the things that go wrong along the way. And the user story is the lightweight: a single sentence on a card, deliberately incomplete, standing in for a conversation yet to be had. They are not merely different formats for the same content; they embody different bets about how much should be decided up front.

The use case: a story with a goal

Use cases, as introduced by Ivar Jacobson, model a system from the outside in: an actor (a user or another system) pursues a goal, and the use case describes the sequence of interactions that achieves it.7 Crucially, a good use case does not stop at the happy path. Alistair Cockburn, whose work made use cases a discipline rather than a doodle, emphasizes that the real value lives in the alternate flows and exceptions — what happens when the card is declined, the network drops, the item is out of stock.15 A use case for “withdraw cash” that handles only the case where everything works is worse than useless, because the difficult, expensive, defect-prone behavior is exactly the behavior it omits. Cockburn also stresses writing use cases at a clear and consistent level — a user goal such as “transfer funds” is a different altitude from a subfunction such as “authenticate” — and that mixing levels within one use case is a reliable source of confusion.15

The user story: a promise for a conversation

The user story emerged from Extreme Programming as a conscious rejection of the heavyweight specification.9 Where an SRS tries to pin everything down before construction, the story embraces the opposite premise: that requirements are best discovered through frequent collaboration and working software, so a written placeholder need only be detailed enough to schedule and resume a conversation. The widely used template — As a [type of user], I want [some goal], so that [some reason] — originated with the team at Connextra and was popularized by Mike Cohn, whose book made stories the default unit of agile requirements.16 The template's quiet genius is the final clause: forcing every story to state so that some benefit keeps the team anchored to why a feature is wanted, which is precisely the traceability-to-need that floating requirements lose.

STORY‑138

As a returning customer, I want to sign in with a second factor, so that my account stays secure even if my password leaks.

Epic: Account security Confirmation: 3 criteria Estimate: 5 pts

A story is famously incomplete by design, and Ron Jeffries captured what keeps that incompleteness from becoming chaos in his “three Cs”: the Card (the brief written token), the Conversation (the detailed discussion the card stands for), and the Confirmation (the acceptance criteria that decide when the story is done).17 The card is not the requirement; it is a marker that a requirement needs to be worked out, and the confirmation is what stops “we'll discuss it later” from meaning “we'll argue about it after release.” A story without acceptance criteria is the agile equivalent of an unverifiable “shall” — an obligation no one can confirm has been met.

None of these forms is universally correct, and mature teams mix them. A medical-device company will write a rigorous SRS because a regulator demands traceable, verifiable requirements and because a defect can kill. A consumer web startup will live in stories because its real risk is building the wrong thing fast, and heavy documentation would only slow the learning. A large enterprise system might use stories for the user-facing increments and a formal specification for the interface contracts between teams. The artifact should follow the risk: the more expensive it is to be wrong, and the harder it is to change later, the more it pays to write down precisely — up front.

Section 07The anatomy of a good requirement

It is easy to write a requirement and hard to write a good one. The international standard does not leave “good” to taste; it enumerates the characteristics a well-formed requirement should exhibit, both as an individual statement and as part of a set.10 Learning these characteristics is the closest thing the field has to learning grammar: once internalized, the defects in a bad requirement become visible at a glance.

01 Necessary

Defines an essential capability; if removed, a real need goes unmet. If it traces to nothing, delete it.

02 Unambiguous

Admits one interpretation only. “Fast,” “user-friendly,” and “etc.” are ambiguity in disguise.

03 Complete

States the obligation fully, with no missing conditions or dangling “to be determined.”

04 Singular

One requirement per statement. “And” is often two requirements hiding in one sentence.

05 Feasible

Achievable within known physical, technical, and budgetary limits. No perpetual motion.

06 Verifiable

Can be proven met by inspection, demonstration, analysis, or test. If you cannot test it, you cannot ship it.

07 Correct

Accurately reflects a genuine stakeholder need, not a misremembered or invented one.

08 Conforming

Follows the agreed template, style, and modal-verb conventions so it reads consistently with its peers.

09 Traceable

Carries a unique identifier and links upward to its source need and downward to design and tests.

Of these, verifiability is the one that quietly governs all the others, because it is the property you can actually check. The test “how would I prove this was met?” is the single most powerful instrument in requirements work. Apply it to “the system shall be user-friendly” and it collapses: there is no procedure, no instrument, no pass/fail. Apply it to “the system shall be available 99.9% of the time, measured monthly” and a clear test appears. Karl Wiegers makes the point bluntly — if you cannot define how to verify a requirement, you do not yet understand the requirement well enough to include it.11 Unverifiable requirements are not merely sloppy; they are the clauses that get quietly dropped, disputed at acceptance, or discovered missing only when a user complains.

The same need, written badly and well

Multiple defects

The system should be secure and handle errors gracefully and be quick to respond.

Three requirements in one (not singular), “should” where an obligation is meant, and three untestable adjectives. Nothing here can be passed or failed.

One clean obligation

If a request fails validation, then the system shall reject it and return an error identifying the offending field within 300 ms.

Singular, unambiguous, and verifiable; the trigger, the response, and the timing are all explicit. An EARS unwanted-behavior pattern in practice.

Characteristics also apply to the requirements set as a whole, not just to each sentence. A set should be complete (no needs left unaddressed), consistent (no two requirements contradict each other), and free of redundancy (the same obligation stated twice, in two places, drifting out of sync as one copy is edited and the other forgotten).10 Contradiction is the subtler danger: requirement 47 promises data is retained for seven years, requirement 112 promises a user can permanently erase their data on request, and nobody notices the collision until a regulator or a lawsuit forces the question. Reviewing a specification is as much about the relationships between requirements as the wording of any one.

INVEST: the same idea for stories

The agile tradition arrived at a strikingly parallel checklist for user stories. Bill Wake's INVEST mnemonic holds that a good story is Independent, Negotiable, Valuable, Estimable, Small, and Testable.18 The vocabulary differs from the standard's — “negotiable” would be heresy in a fixed contract — but the deep concerns rhyme. Testable is verifiable by another name. Small and independent serve the same end as singular: one self-contained thing you can build, check, and reason about. Valuable echoes necessary, the demand that every requirement justify its existence by reference to a real need. Two communities that spent two decades arguing about how much to write down nonetheless converged on nearly the same definition of a unit worth writing.

Section 08The standards: from IEEE 830 to ISO/IEC/IEEE 29148

For a generation, one document defined what a software requirements specification should look like. IEEE Std 830-1998, the IEEE Recommended Practice for Software Requirements Specifications, was the reference that countless teams, textbooks, and contracts pointed to when they said “write an SRS.”8 Its lasting contribution was twofold: a list of the qualities a good SRS should have — correct, unambiguous, complete, consistent, ranked for importance, verifiable, modifiable, and traceable — and a recommended table of contents that gave practitioners a place to start. That template, with sections for an overall description, specific requirements, external interfaces, performance, and design constraints, shaped the shape of specifications for two decades.

IEEE 830 has since been formally superseded. The current standard is ISO/IEC/IEEE 29148, first issued in 2011 and revised in 2018, which absorbed and replaced IEEE 830 along with several related legacy standards.10 The change was not cosmetic. Where 830 was a relatively self-contained recipe for one document, 29148 reframes requirements as a set of processes spanning the whole life cycle — eliciting, analyzing, specifying, validating, and managing requirements — and it deliberately operates at multiple levels, distinguishing stakeholder requirements, system requirements, and software requirements rather than collapsing them into a single artifact.10 An SRS, in the modern framing, is one work product emitted by a continuous process, not the process itself.

A standard does not make requirements good. It makes the difference between good and bad requirements discussable.

The enduring value of a shared definition

Two features of 29148 repay attention. The first is its precise, almost grammatical model of an individual requirement — the subject–verb–object–condition construction and the fixed meanings of shall, should, may, and will discussed earlier — which turns “write clearly” from advice into a checkable rule.10 The second is that the standard does not stand alone. It is one member of a family of life-cycle standards: it provides the requirements-engineering processes that plug into ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288, the standard for system life-cycle processes, and its software counterpart 12207.19 The intent of that architecture is coherence — that requirements engineering is not a bolt-on activity but an integrated part of how a system is conceived, built, verified, and retired, using consistent vocabulary across the whole.

It is worth being honest about what a standard can and cannot do. It cannot make a team understand its users, resolve a political disagreement about scope, or supply the domain knowledge that separates a real requirement from a plausible-sounding mistake. What it can do is give a shared vocabulary and a shared bar, so that a reviewer can say “this requirement is not verifiable” or “these two are inconsistent” and point to an agreed definition rather than a personal preference. Critics rightly note that conformance to a standard is not the same as quality, and that a thick, perfectly-formatted specification can still describe the wrong system flawlessly. The standard is a floor, not a ceiling — and a floor is still worth having.

Naming, untangled

The same standard is written “IEEE 29148” and “ISO/IEC/IEEE 29148” because it is jointly published; the long form reflects the three standards bodies that maintain it together. References to “IEEE 830” in older material point to its now-superseded predecessor. When a contract simply says “the SRS shall conform to 29148,” it means the 2018 edition unless stated otherwise.

Section 09The requirements process: elicitation to management

Requirements do not arrive pre-written. They have to be drawn out of people who often cannot articulate what they want, reconciled when those people disagree, written down precisely, checked against reality, and then governed as they inevitably change. The international standard organizes this work into activities that, while rarely strictly sequential, name the distinct kinds of thinking involved: elicitation, analysis, specification, validation, and management.10 Treating them as genuinely different activities — rather than one undifferentiated “gathering” — is what separates a deliberate process from wishful note-taking.

Elicitation is the discovery of needs, and the word is chosen carefully: requirements are elicited, not collected, because they rarely exist in finished form waiting to be picked up. Stakeholders describe solutions when they mean problems, omit the obvious because it is obvious to them, and contradict one another without realizing it. The analyst's job is to interview, observe, run workshops, study existing systems, and build prototypes that make tacit knowledge explicit. Analysis then refines the raw material: classifying requirements, resolving conflicts, negotiating priorities, detecting gaps and overlaps, and decomposing vague goals into concrete obligations. Specification is the act of writing the result down in a durable, structured form — the SRS, the backlog, the use-case model. And validation asks the question that all the rigor in the world cannot answer on its own: are these the right requirements — do they actually reflect what stakeholders need, and will satisfying them solve the real problem?11

Verification vs validation

The two words are constantly confused and mean different things. Verification asks “did we build the system right?” — does it meet its specification. Validation asks “did we build the right system?” — does that specification reflect the genuine need. A product can pass every verification test and still be a validation failure: flawlessly built to the wrong requirements.

The activity that never ends is management. Requirements change — markets move, regulations shift, stakeholders learn what they actually wanted by watching the first version fail to deliver it. Requirements management is the discipline of handling that change without losing control: assigning each requirement a stable unique identifier, recording its status and priority, versioning the set, and — above all — maintaining traceability.10 Traceability is the web of links connecting each requirement upward to the stakeholder need that justifies it and downward to the design elements, code, and test cases that satisfy it. Its payoff is the ability to answer two questions that are otherwise nearly impossible on a large system: if this need changes, what is affected? and does every requirement actually have a test?

Woven through analysis and management is a decision that teams often try to avoid and never can: prioritization. Not every requirement can be built first, and on most real projects not every requirement will be built at all, because time and money run out before the backlog does. Pretending all requirements are equally essential is not rigor — it is a refusal to choose that simply defers the choice to the worst possible moment, when the deadline makes it for you. The standard explicitly calls for requirements to be ranked for importance and stability, and the agile tradition makes prioritization the visible, continuous heart of the process by ordering the backlog.10 A widely used scheme, MoSCoW, sorts requirements into Must have (the system fails without it), Should have (important but not vital), Could have (desirable if time permits), and Won't have this time (explicitly out of scope, which is itself a requirement worth recording). The value of any such scheme is less in its categories than in the conversation it forces: asking a stakeholder to mark a requirement Could have rather than Must have surfaces, often for the first time, how much they actually need it — and reveals that the word “requirement,” for all its air of obligation, has been hiding a spectrum of wishes all along.11

The V-model in Figure 3 captures why traceability is not bureaucratic overhead but the spine of the whole endeavor. Read down the left arm, requirements grow more detailed; read up the right arm, tests grow more integrated; and the horizontal links assert that each level of requirement has a corresponding level of verification that proves it was met. Stakeholder requirements are confirmed by acceptance testing; system requirements by system testing; component requirements by unit testing. A requirement with no test on its right-hand side is an obligation no one is checking. A test that traces to no requirement is either a missing requirement or wasted effort. The model is a picture of an old discipline: decide what you need, in writing, in a form you can later prove you delivered.

None of this implies a single grand pass. On most projects the activities loop continuously — elicitation surfaces a need, analysis exposes a conflict, validation sends a requirement back to be re-elicited, and management absorbs a change that restarts the cycle for some small part of the system. Whether a team runs this loop once a year over a formal baseline or once a fortnight over a backlog, the same five kinds of work are present. The difference between methodologies is the cadence and ceremony of the loop, not the existence of its parts.

Section 10The cost of getting it wrong

The case for taking requirements seriously rests, ultimately, on an economic claim: that defects rooted in requirements are far cheaper to fix early than late, and that requirements problems are a leading cause of project failure. Both claims are substantially true and both are routinely overstated, so they deserve to be examined with some care rather than simply repeated.

The early-versus-late argument traces to Barry Boehm, who made the economic case for catching defects early in the 1970s and developed it at length in his foundational work on software engineering economics.6 The widely cited finding is that the cost of correcting a defect rises sharply the later it is discovered, with errors caught in the requirements phase costing a fraction of what the same errors cost once they have propagated into design, code, and a deployed system.20 The intuition is sound and mechanical: a misunderstood requirement caught in review is a sentence to rewrite; the same misunderstanding caught after release is a redesign, a re-implementation, a re-test, a redeployment, and very possibly a population of users who have already built workarounds around the wrong behavior.

Here honesty requires a caveat that the field too often omits. The specific numbers attached to this curve — the oft-quoted claim that a production defect costs exactly one hundred times a requirements defect — have a weaker empirical basis than their ubiquity suggests. Laurent Bossavit's careful investigation traced many famous software-engineering “facts” back to their sources and found that the precise multipliers are frequently miscited, drawn from small or dated studies, and repeated far beyond what the original data support.21 The responsible position is the one the figure's caption takes: the direction of the effect is real and well grounded, while any exact ratio should be treated as illustrative rather than measured. Requirements rigor is worth the investment because fixing things early is genuinely cheaper — not because a particular magic number says so.

How often requirements sink projects

The second claim concerns failure. The Standish Group's CHAOS research, first published in the mid-1990s, reported that a large share of software projects were challenged or cancelled outright, and — most relevant here — that the leading contributors to trouble were requirements-related: incomplete requirements, lack of user involvement, and changing requirements together dominated the list of causes.2 Whatever the precise percentages, the qualitative finding has been replicated often enough to be uncontroversial: more projects fail for getting the requirements wrong than for any failure of pure engineering.

The CHAOS figures themselves have drawn methodological criticism over the years — the definitions of “success” and “failure” are contestable, and the sampling has been questioned — which is one more reason to lean on the shape of the finding rather than its decimals. But the underlying message survives every critique: requirements are where projects are most often lost, because a requirements defect is invisible. A coding bug announces itself with a crash; a requirements defect ships quietly as a perfectly working feature that nobody needed, or a needed feature that was never written, and it is discovered only when the gap between what was built and what was wanted becomes impossible to ignore.

Case study · failure under load

The London Ambulance Service, 1992

On 26 October 1992, the London Ambulance Service switched on a new computer-aided dispatch system. Within hours it was in serious trouble. As call volume rose, the system's model of the world drifted out of step with reality — it lost track of vehicles, generated duplicate and incorrect dispatches, and could not cope as the backlog of exceptions compounded. Crews and callers were left waiting; the service reverted to manual methods, and the failure was linked in public accounts to patients who came to harm while waiting for ambulances that the system had mishandled.

The subsequent independent inquiry did not find a single catastrophic line of code. It found a requirements and process failure: an over-ambitious specification pushed in on an unrealistic timetable, inadequate consideration of how the system should behave under peak load and degrade gracefully when overwhelmed, insufficient testing against real operating conditions, and too little involvement of the ambulance crews who understood the actual work.22 An earlier attempt at the same system had already been abandoned. It remains a textbook illustration of a hard truth: the non-functional requirements — behavior under load, graceful degradation, resilience — were the ones that mattered most, and the ones least adequately specified.

The lesson of these stories is not that more documentation is always better; a project can drown in requirements just as surely as it can starve for them. The lesson is that the requirements deserve attention proportional to the cost of getting them wrong. For a throwaway internal tool, a conversation and a sticky note are enough. For a dispatch system standing between a 999 call and an ambulance, the behavior of the system under stress is not a detail to be discovered in production — it is the requirement, and the most important one.

Section 11Tools of the trade

For a small project, the right requirements tool is often a shared document or a spreadsheet, and pretending otherwise is its own kind of failure. But as systems grow — thousands of requirements, dozens of contributors, regulatory obligations to demonstrate that every requirement was implemented and tested — ad hoc documents collapse under their own weight. Two requirements drift out of sync; nobody can answer which test covers requirement 4,217; an auditor asks for a traceability matrix and the team spends three weeks rebuilding by hand what a tool would have maintained automatically. Dedicated requirements-management tools exist to make the things that matter at scale — identity, traceability, change control, and reporting — cheap and continuous rather than expensive and periodic.

The archetypal example is IBM DOORS (Dynamic Object-Oriented Requirements System), which has been the heavyweight standard in aerospace, defense, automotive, and other safety-critical industries for decades.23 DOORS treats each requirement as a uniquely identified object that can be linked, versioned, and traced, and it is built for the world where a system has tens of thousands of requirements and a regulator who will, eventually, demand evidence that each one was satisfied. Its modern incarnation extends the classic database-style approach with a web-based, collaborative platform for distributed teams, but the core proposition is unchanged: requirements are first-class objects, and the links between them are as important as the requirements themselves.

A prominent modern alternative is Jama Connect, which targets the same regulated industries — medical devices, aerospace, automotive — with particular emphasis on live traceability and review.24 Tools in this category compete on how well they keep the traceability web current as requirements change, how they support structured review and approval, how they integrate with the test-management and engineering tools where verification actually happens, and increasingly on built-in assistance that scores requirements for quality — flagging the ambiguous, the untestable, the non-conforming — against the very characteristics the standards prescribe. Other established platforms, including Siemens Polarion and Visure, occupy adjacent niches, and a vendor-neutral interchange format, ReqIF (Requirements Interchange Format), exists precisely so that requirements can move between tools and across the boundaries between a manufacturer and its suppliers without being retyped.

Choosing a tool

The tool should match the stakes. A two-person team adopting DOORS will spend more time administering it than writing software; a fifty-supplier avionics program managing requirements in a shared spreadsheet is courting disaster. The questions that actually discriminate: How many requirements? How many people? Is traceability a legal obligation or a nice-to-have? Will an auditor ever ask you to prove coverage? The honest default for most teams remains the lightest tool that still answers those questions.

It is worth resisting the most common error in this area, which is to mistake the tool for the discipline. A requirements-management tool maintains links and statuses with great fidelity; it does not, and cannot, tell you whether a requirement is correct, whether it reflects a genuine need, or whether the thing being specified is worth building. A perfectly traced, fully versioned, immaculately reported specification of the wrong system is still a specification of the wrong system. Tools make rigorous requirements practice affordable at scale — they automate the bookkeeping that would otherwise be impossible by hand — but the thinking they support is exactly the thinking the preceding sections described, and no tool performs it for you.

Section 12Agile, living specifications, and the arrival of AI

The heavyweight Software Requirements Specification was a child of its era — an era in which software was delivered on a disk after a multi-year project, changes were ruinously expensive, and a contract had to state everything because there would be no cheap second chance. The agile movement was, in large part, a revolt against the assumption that all that could or should be known in advance.9 If software can be delivered in small increments and changed cheaply, the argument runs, then exhaustively specifying it up front is not prudence but waste: you are making expensive decisions at the moment of maximum ignorance, before any working software exists to teach you what you actually need.

This is the deep reason user stories displaced the SRS in so much of the industry. A story is not a worse specification; it is a bet that the cheapest way to discover the right requirement is to build a little, show it to real users, and let their reaction write the next requirement — a bet that treats requirements as something you learn rather than something you elicit and freeze.16 Where that bet holds — evolving products, uncertain markets, cheap deployment — lightweight requirements win decisively. Where it does not — a pacemaker, an avionics controller, a contract between two companies, a system that cannot be incrementally shipped to users for feedback — the heavyweight specification remains not just defensible but necessary. The dichotomy was always false; the real question is how much it costs to be wrong and to change, and the answer dictates how much to write down.

What survives across both worlds is the irreducible core. Even the most agile team must still decide what to build, communicate that decision precisely enough to act on, and confirm that what was built is what was wanted — which is elicitation, specification, and validation under other names and at another cadence. Karl Wiegers's observation that requirements are about understanding and communication, not about documents, is the through-line: the document is a means, and a team that has stories with crisp acceptance criteria and genuine conversations is doing requirements engineering whether or not it owns a copy of any standard.11

The medium of requirements keeps changing. The act — deciding precisely what to build — does not.

Why the discipline outlives its artifacts

The newest turn is the arrival of large language models, and it cuts in two directions at once. As an aid to requirements work, these models are already useful: they can draft requirements from a rough description, flag ambiguity and untestable language, detect contradictions across a large set, suggest the missing alternate flows in a use case, and translate informally between stories, use cases, and formal “shall” statements — automating exactly the tedious, error-prone review that the quality characteristics demand. A tool that reliably catches every “the system shall be fast” and asks “how fast, under what load, measured how?” is doing real work.

But AI also raises the stakes on requirements rather than lowering them. As code generation becomes cheaper and faster — as the act of building approaches free — the binding constraint shifts even more decisively onto knowing what to build. An AI that can implement any specification flawlessly is only as good as the specification it is given; point it at vague or wrong requirements and it will produce vast quantities of precisely wrong software, faster than any human team ever could. This is why “specification-driven” approaches, in which a carefully written description of intended behavior becomes the primary artifact and the executable system is generated from it, are drawing renewed interest. The specification stops being documentation about the system and starts being the source from which the system is produced — which makes its clarity, completeness, and verifiability matter more than ever.

And so the wheel turns back to where it began. More than three decades ago, Fred Brooks identified the hardest part of building software not as construction but as deciding precisely what to construct, and warned that the most expensive errors are the ones made before a single line of code is written.1 Every shift since — from waterfall to agile, from documents to stories, from human coders to AI — has changed the form of requirements without touching that essence. The artifacts will keep evolving; the SRS will share the stage with stories, use cases, and whatever comes next. But the underlying task is permanent, because it is not really a task about software at all. It is the task of figuring out, precisely and honestly, what we actually want — and that has never been easy, and a machine that writes our code for us does not make it any easier. It only makes getting it wrong faster.
