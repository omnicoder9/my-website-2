01 — The overturning

A finished model, broken overnight

By the mid-1990s cosmology felt close to complete. The universe was expanding, decelerating under its own gravity, and the only real open question was by how much — whether it held enough matter to one day halt and recollapse, or just enough to coast outward forever. The expansion had to be slowing. Gravity attracts; nothing about it pushes. The deceleration of the cosmos was, in a sense, the one thing everyone was certain of.

In 1998 and 1999 two rival teams measured that deceleration directly, using exploding stars as cosmic mileposts — and found its opposite. The expansion is accelerating.67 Something is overpowering gravity on the largest scales and prying the universe apart faster and faster. Reconciled with everything else we know, that something must account for roughly 69% of the total energy content of the cosmos,11 yet it emits no light, clusters into no structures, and matches no particle in any theory. The community named it dark energy14 — a placeholder for an admission of ignorance about most of reality.

This article traces how a measurement meant to confirm a slowing universe instead detected a repulsive one; the physics of negative pressure that makes acceleration possible; why the most natural explanation produces the largest known mismatch between theory and observation; and why, in 2025, fresh data have begun to suggest the story is not finished after all.

02 — Background

A universe that was supposed to slow

The mathematical seed of cosmic acceleration was planted, and then disowned, by Einstein. In 1917, applying general relativity to the whole universe, he found his equations would not permit a static cosmos — gravity alone would cause it to collapse. To hold it still he inserted an extra term, the cosmological constant Λ, a uniform tension built into the fabric of spacetime itself.1

Within a few years the static picture fell apart. Alexander Friedmann showed in 1922 that Einstein's equations naturally describe a dynamic universe — one that expands or contracts.2 Georges Lemaître reached the same conclusion independently and linked it to the recession of galaxies.3 Then in 1929 Edwin Hubble found that galaxies recede at speeds proportional to their distance — the observational signature of an expanding cosmos.4 With expansion established, Λ was no longer needed to balance anything. Einstein dropped it; the term is famously remembered, in George Gamow's telling, as his "biggest blunder."17

For seventy years the cosmological constant was the embarrassing relic of a wrong idea. Its return would be more humiliating than its retirement.

— On the long exile of Λ

What replaced Λ was a clean, gravity-only picture. In an expanding universe filled with matter and radiation, every gram pulls on every other, so the expansion must decelerate. The whole question reduced to a single number — the mean density of matter, written Ωm — which fixed both the geometry of space and the cosmic fate. Measuring the deceleration would settle everything. That was the program the supernova teams set out to complete.

03 — The instrument

Standard candles in deep time

To measure deceleration you need to compare the expansion rate now with the rate long ago, which means measuring distances to objects billions of light-years away. Distance is the hardest quantity in astronomy: a faint dot could be intrinsically dim and near, or brilliant and far. You need a standard candle — a source of known luminosity, so its apparent faintness reveals its distance unambiguously.

Type Ia supernovae are nearly ideal for this. They occur when a white dwarf in a binary system reaches a critical mass and detonates, and because that threshold is roughly universal, the explosions reach comparable peak brightnesses — briefly outshining their entire host galaxy, so they can be seen across cosmological distances. They are not perfectly identical, but in 1993 Mark Phillips showed that their scatter is correctable: intrinsically brighter Type Ia supernovae fade more slowly, and calibrating brightness against the light-curve decline rate turns them into standardizable candles good to a few percent.5

Two teams, one race

Two groups raced to apply this. The Supernova Cosmology Project, led by Saul Perlmutter at Berkeley, pioneered the batch-discovery techniques that made high-redshift supernovae findable on a schedule. The High-z Supernova Search Team, led by Brian Schmidt with Adam Riess driving the analysis, formed to check the method with independent data and tighter control of dust and calibration. Both expected the same outcome: distant supernovae sitting on a decelerating Hubble curve, yielding a value of Ωm.

The measured quantity is the distance modulus — the gap between a supernova's known intrinsic brightness and its observed brightness, which encodes how far its light has travelled:

μ=m−M=5 log10(dL10 pc) μ = distance modulus · m, M = apparent and absolute magnitude · dL = luminosity distance, which depends on the full expansion history

Plot the distance modulus against redshift for objects spanning billions of years, and the shape of the curve reveals whether expansion sped up or slowed. A decelerating universe makes distant supernovae appear slightly brighter than an empty, coasting one. An accelerating universe makes them appear fainter — because the light has had to cross more expanded space than deceleration would allow.

04 — The discovery

The result nobody wanted

The distant supernovae came back too faint. Across both samples, objects at redshifts up to z ≈ 0.8 were dimmer than even an empty, freely coasting universe would predict — and far dimmer than any model with enough matter to decelerate. There was no room left for deceleration. The simplest reading of the data was that the expansion had been speeding up.

Riess and the High-z team published first, in September 1998, under a title that left nothing implicit: Observational Evidence from Supernovae for an Accelerating Universe and a Cosmological Constant.6 Perlmutter's Supernova Cosmology Project followed in 1999 with 42 high-redshift supernovae and the same conclusion, ruling out a matter-only universe at high confidence.7 Two teams, independent data, rival methods, and a result each had partly hoped to debunk: the agreement is what made it impossible to dismiss.

05 — The mechanism

The physics of a repulsive cosmos

How can anything in general relativity push outward? The answer lies in the second Friedmann equation, which governs the acceleration of the cosmic scale factor a(t). Crucially, gravity in general relativity responds not only to energy density ρ but also to pressure p:

äa = −4πG3 (ρ+3pc2) ä > 0 (acceleration) requires ρ + 3p/c² < 0 — that is, a strongly negative pressure

Ordinary matter and radiation have zero or positive pressure, so they always decelerate the expansion. But a component with sufficiently negative pressure flips the sign of ä and drives acceleration. Each component is characterized by its equation of state, the ratio of pressure to energy density:

w≡pρc2 ;   acceleration when  w<−13 matter: w = 0 · radiation: w = ⅓ · cosmological constant Λ: w = −1, a perfectly uniform negative-pressure vacuum

Einstein's exiled cosmological constant fits exactly. A constant vacuum energy has w = −1: its pressure equals the negative of its energy density, it does not dilute as space expands, and it drives an ever-accelerating expansion. The discarded blunder turned out to be the simplest description of the new data — which is why Riess's title named both acceleration and a cosmological constant in the same breath.6

The deceleration parameter changes sign

The clean way to state the result is through the deceleration parameter q, defined so that positive q means slowing and negative q means speeding up. For a flat universe of matter plus a cosmological constant, today's value is:

q0=12 Ωm−ΩΛ ≈−0.55 with Ωm ≈ 0.31, ΩΛ ≈ 0.69 · directly measured by SH0ES as q0 = −0.51 ± 0.024

The minus sign is the entire revolution. A measured value of q0 = −0.51 ± 0.02412 is not a slowing universe with a small correction — it is decisively accelerating. And because matter dilutes as the universe expands while vacuum energy does not, dark energy was negligible in the early universe and dominant now. Run the model backward and acceleration switches off: the cosmos actually decelerated for its first several billion years, until the thinning matter could no longer hold back the constant push, and q crossed zero at a redshift of roughly z ≈ 0.6, about six billion years ago.16 That crossover is the fork drawn at the top of this page.

06 — The inventory

An inventory of ignorance

Acceleration did not arrive alone. Combined with the cosmic microwave background and the large-scale clustering of galaxies, the supernova result locked in a full accounting of what the universe is made of — the ΛCDM concordance model. The Planck satellite's measurements give the modern numbers, and they are humbling.11

It is worth being precise about how little "dark energy" claims to explain. It is not a substance that has been detected, nor a field that anyone has isolated. It is a name for whatever produces the negative-pressure term in the Friedmann equation — inferred entirely through its effect on cosmic expansion, never observed directly. The cosmological constant is the leading candidate, but the label "dark energy" is deliberately agnostic: it covers Λ, a slowly evolving scalar field, or even the possibility that general relativity itself fails on cosmic scales.

07 — The reckoning

The humiliation

If the cosmological constant is simply vacuum energy, then physics already has a theory that predicts it — quantum field theory, in which empty space seethes with the zero-point energy of every field. The trouble is the number. Summed up to a natural high-energy cutoff, the predicted vacuum energy density exceeds the observed value of Λ by some 120 orders of magnitude.9

The worst theoretical prediction in the history of physics — a mismatch of 10¹²⁰ between what the vacuum should weigh and what it does.

— The cosmological constant problem9

This is the cosmological constant problem, and Steven Weinberg's 1989 review laid out just how intractable it is: the enormous contributions from different fields would have to cancel to roughly 120 decimal places to leave the tiny residue we observe, with no known symmetry to enforce the cancellation.9 For decades this was tolerable because everyone assumed the true answer was exactly zero — that some undiscovered principle set Λ to nothing. The 1998 discovery removed even that comfort. The vacuum energy is not zero; it is small, positive, and finite — the hardest of all values to explain, since it forbids any clean cancellation argument.

The coincidence problem

A second affront sits on top of the first. Matter density falls steeply as the universe expands; the cosmological constant stays fixed. For all of cosmic history these two have differed by enormous factors — yet we happen to live at the brief epoch when they are comparable, within a factor of a few of each other.15 Weinberg called this the "new" cosmological constant problem: not only is Λ unnaturally small, it is unnaturally close to today's matter density, for no reason anyone can supply.9 Either we occupy a special moment, or some deeper mechanism ties the two together that we do not understand.

So the discovery's deepest sting is not that the answer changed. It is the inversion it forced. Cosmology had believed it was measuring the last free parameter of a known universe. Instead it learned that the dominant component of reality is one for which the most natural theory is wrong by a factor with 120 zeros — and that Einstein's discarded blunder describes it better than anything built since. The reward, in 2011, was a Nobel Prize.8 The cost was the recognition that roughly 95% of the universe lies outside any settled theory.

08 — The open front

The argument isn't over

Calling dark energy "the cosmological constant" is a hope as much as a measurement. Λ is the simplest fit, but it is not the only one. If dark energy is instead a dynamical field — the class of models called quintessence — its equation of state w would differ from −1 and could change over cosmic time.10 Distinguishing a true constant (w = −1, unchanging) from a slowly rolling field is now the central observational question, and two cracks have opened in the concordance picture.

The Hubble tension

The first is a disagreement about the expansion rate itself. Measured locally with Cepheid-calibrated supernovae, the SH0ES team finds a Hubble constant of H₀ = 73.04 ± 1.04 km s⁻¹ Mpc⁻¹.12 Inferred from the early-universe physics encoded in the cosmic microwave background, Planck gives H₀ = 67.4 ± 0.5.11 These are not casually reconcilable: the discrepancy exceeds 5σ, with no identified measurement error to absorb it.12 Something connecting the early and late universe — possibly dark energy — may not be behaving as ΛCDM assumes.

DESI and the hint of evolving dark energy

The second crack is fresher and strikes at the heart of the matter. The Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument has mapped the three-dimensional positions of millions of galaxies, using baryon acoustic oscillations as a precise ruler to trace the expansion across cosmic time. Its second data release, in 2025, finds that when the supernova, CMB and galaxy-clustering data are combined, a time-varying dark energy is preferred over a fixed cosmological constant.13 The reconstructed equation of state appears to drift across w = −1 rather than sitting on it, with the combined datasets favouring dynamical dark energy over ΛCDM at the 2.8–4.2σ level depending on the supernova sample used.16

This is not yet a discovery — the significance is below the threshold the field demands, the result depends on which supernova compilation is folded in, and earlier "anomalies" of similar strength have dissolved under better data. But the implication, if it holds, would be a second overturning to rhyme with the first. The whole point of a cosmological constant is that it does not change. A dark energy that evolves would not be Λ at all; it would reopen the question the 1998 discovery seemed to have answered, and demand new physics beyond both Einstein's blunder and the model it anchors.

The universe was unfinished business in 1998, and it is unfinished business now — only the mystery has grown more specific, and harder to dismiss.

— On the second overturning

Three decades after two teams set out to measure how fast the cosmos was slowing down, the central facts are settled and the central object is not. The expansion is accelerating; roughly 69% of everything is dark energy; the best simple model is a vacuum term that theory overshoots by 120 orders of magnitude. What that dark energy actually is — a constant of nature, a dying field, or a sign that gravity itself is incomplete on the largest scales — remains the deepest open question in physics. The humiliation of 1998 has not been resolved. It has only been measured more precisely.
