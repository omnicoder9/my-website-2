The Last Line
of Defense.

Inside the chemical plant, a separate system stands between a routine process upset and a toxic cloud over a neighborhood. In 2017, attackers reached for that system on purpose. This is what it means to secure the sector where a keystroke can become a chemical release — and where one compromise can ripple into water, agriculture, healthcare, and manufacturing alike.

00Orientation

A sector defined by what its products can do

The chemical industry is, in the most literal sense, foundational: it makes the raw inputs that every other part of the economy converts into something else. That same property — the latent energy and toxicity locked inside its processes — is exactly what makes its cybersecurity a question of public safety, not merely uptime.

The United States chemical sector converts a relatively small set of feedstocks — natural gas, crude oil derivatives, minerals, air, and water — into more than 70,000 distinct products that flow into virtually every other industry.1 Several hundred thousand facilities, from sprawling Gulf Coast petrochemical complexes to small regional distributors, use, manufacture, store, transport, or deliver chemicals along a complex and globally interconnected supply chain.1 The Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA) performs the role of Sector Risk Management Agency for the Chemical Sector on behalf of the Department of Homeland Security, a designation rooted in Presidential Policy Directive 21.2

By any economic measure the sector is enormous — employing close to a million people and generating revenues well above half a trillion dollars annually — but the figure that should govern security thinking is not the dollar value of the output.3 It is the consequence profile of the materials themselves. A disruption in a financial back office costs money; a disruption to the physical containment of a chlorine railcar, an ammonia refrigeration loop, or a hydrofluoric acid alkylation unit can injure or kill people who never had any relationship with the facility at all.

This study treats the sector through four lenses, in sequence. First, the technology that runs a modern chemical process and the specific ways it can be attacked — including the uncomfortable fact that the safety systems engineered to be the last line of defense have themselves become a target. Second, the cascade problem: because chemicals are inputs to the other fifteen critical-infrastructure sectors, a compromise rarely stays contained to a single balance sheet. Third, the practices, tools, standards, and frameworks that actually reduce the risk. And fourth, the open problems — the places where current defenses are thin, where regulation has frayed, and where research and engineering still owe the field an answer.

On terminology

This document distinguishes safety (preventing accidental harm — leaks, fires, explosions) from security (preventing deliberate harm by an adversary). For most of industrial history these were separate disciplines run by separate teams. The central argument of this study is that, in the chemical sector, the cyber threat has collapsed the distance between them: an attacker who reaches a safety system is committing an act of security that produces a failure of safety. Holding the two ideas together is the whole game.

01Technology, Risk & Consequence

What runs a chemical plant — and how it breaks

A modern process facility is a layered stack of computers that sense, decide, and act on the physical world thousands of times a second. Understanding the attack surface means understanding what each layer does, why it was historically built without security in mind, and which layer you absolutely cannot allow to fail.

The control hierarchy

At the bottom of every plant are the physical processes themselves — reactors, distillation columns, heat exchangers, compressors, and the valves, pumps, sensors, and actuators that interface with them. Above that physical layer sits a hierarchy of digital control, most commonly described using the Purdue Enterprise Reference Architecture (the “Purdue model”), which divides a plant's systems and networks into numbered levels and has become the shared vocabulary for industrial network design.6 The model was originally created in the 1990s to organize data flows in computer-integrated manufacturing; it has since become the reference architecture underpinning industrial security standards, and it is explicitly the basis for both ISA/IEC 62443 and NIST guidance.6

The day-to-day operation of the process is run by the Basic Process Control System (BPCS), which in continuous and batch chemical plants is almost always a Distributed Control System (DCS) — platforms such as Emerson DeltaV, Honeywell Experion, or Yokogawa Centum that are widely deployed across chemical, refining, and life-sciences operations.7 A DCS is not a single computer but a federation of field controllers, operator workstations, an engineering workstation, and a plant-wide control network, all engineered for deterministic execution and hardware redundancy.8 The scale is substantial: a mid-sized chemical-plant DCS may manage between 2,000 and 10,000 individual input/output points, while a large refinery installation can exceed 50,000.8 Discrete machinery and packaged units are frequently driven by Programmable Logic Controllers (PLCs), and geographically dispersed assets — pipelines, tank farms, remote terminals — are monitored and controlled through SCADA (Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition) systems. A process historian records time-series data from across the plant, and increasingly bridges the operational and business networks so that production data can feed enterprise analytics.

The reason this architecture matters for security is historical. The protocols that move data between these layers — Modbus, DNP3, classic OPC, and similar — were designed for reliability and determinism in isolated networks, not for a hostile internet. They typically carry no authentication, no integrity protection, and no encryption, which means an attacker with a foothold on the control network can read every process value, inject false commands, and replay legitimate ones.9 Decades of plants were also built and configured before industrial cybersecurity was a discipline at all, leaving exposed remote-access paths and internet-reachable controllers that can be enumerated with ordinary search tools.10 The control stack, in other words, is powerful, precise, long-lived — and was largely architected on the assumption that no adversary could reach it.

The safety instrumented system, and the line that was crossed

The diagram above places the Safety Instrumented System (SIS) at Level 1 — but deliberately beside the basic control system, not beneath it. That placement is the single most important concept in chemical-sector security. An SIS is a distinct and intentionally independent system whose only job is to detect when a process is heading toward a dangerous condition and to drive it to a safe state — venting pressure, cutting feed, tripping a compressor, isolating a reactor.11 Its independence from the control system is the whole point: if the system that runs the process fails or is manipulated, the safety system is supposed to remain a trustworthy backstop. It is the last automated thing standing between a process upset and a loss of containment.

The engineering of these systems is governed by a mature international standard, IEC 61511 — “Functional safety: Safety instrumented systems for the process industry sector” — which in the United States is mirrored by ANSI/ISA 84.00.01 (commonly “ISA-84” or “S84”).11 IEC 61511 is itself a sector-specific application of the broader IEC 61508 functional-safety framework, and it introduces the concept of the Safety Integrity Level (SIL), a graded measure (SIL 1 through SIL 4) of how reliable a particular safety function must be.11 For decades, this discipline was concerned almost entirely with random hardware failures and systematic design errors — the world of probability, redundancy, and proof-testing. The threat model did not include an intelligent adversary who might reprogram the safety logic on purpose.

That assumption died in 2017. The attack the security world came to call Triton (also Trisis or HatMan) was the first malware ever discovered that was built specifically to manipulate safety instrumented systems — and therefore the first cyberattack engineered with the potential to kill.13 It is the defining event of this field, and it deserves to be examined closely.

The intruders spent close to a year inside the victim's networks before reaching their objective, moving from the corporate environment onto the operational network and ultimately onto a workstation used to program the Triconex safety controllers.12 From there they reverse-engineered the controllers' communications, exploited a previously unknown flaw in the controller firmware, and modified the in-memory programming of the safety logic — giving them remote control over the very systems meant to prevent disaster.12 In a worst-case scenario, analysts concluded, the code could have disabled the safety systems and allowed a release of toxic hydrogen sulfide gas or an explosion, endangering lives inside the plant and in the surrounding community.13

What stopped it was, ironically, a flaw in the attackers' own work: an incomplete implementation caused several Triconex controllers to detect an anomaly and trigger a safe shutdown, which is what surfaced the attack at all.13 A first such trip in June was misdiagnosed by the vendor as a mechanical fault rather than a cyber event — later investigation found that six emergency shutdown systems had actually been affected, and that the misdiagnosis bought the attackers two more months of undetected access before the August trip finally gave them away.14 Refinery engineers, having attributed the first shutdown to a mechanical error, had brought the plant back up; the intrusion was confirmed as a cyberattack only after the second event.15 Triton was also the first known incident to target the OT engineering function specifically — a domain that traditionally sat with process engineers, not security teams.14

Why it still matters: the U.S. government has warned that the same threat actor continued operations against the global energy sector, and that older, unpatched Triconex controllers remain vulnerable to similar techniques; the toolkit and tradecraft did not retire with the Saudi incident.12

The lesson of Triton is not that one Saudi plant had a bad week. It is that the safety layer — the part of the architecture whose entire justification is that it can be trusted when everything else fails — is now inside the threat model. Every assumption that flowed from treating safety and security as separate problems has to be revisited in light of an adversary who understands that the most efficient way to cause a chemical release is to first switch off the system designed to prevent one.

Safety engineering assumed failures were random. Triton proved they can be authored.

Who is actually attacking the sector

The threat to chemical operations does not come from a single kind of adversary, and the defenses that stop one rarely stop them all. It is useful to separate the landscape into three broad motivations, because each produces a different attack and a different worst case.

Nation-state actors

State-sponsored groups bring the patience, resources, and engineering depth seen in Triton. The clearest signal of where this is heading is PIPEDREAM (tracked by some researchers as Incontroller), a modular ICS-attack framework discovered in 2022 before it could be deployed — only the seventh piece of malware ever built specifically to manipulate industrial control systems.16 Unlike most malware, PIPEDREAM was not tailored to one victim: although it was demonstrated against Schneider Electric and Omron controllers, analysts warned its techniques could work across hundreds of different controller models, letting an operator scan for devices, alter their logic, and disrupt processes.17 A joint advisory from the Department of Energy, CISA, NSA, and the FBI accompanied its disclosure, and researchers compared it in significance to Stuxnet and to the Industroyer malware used against Ukraine's grid.18

The parallel concern is pre-positioning. The PRC-linked group Volt Typhoon has, according to a 2024 joint advisory, sought to embed itself on the IT networks of U.S. critical-infrastructure operators using “living off the land” techniques — using legitimate built-in tools to evade detection — in order to be in position for disruptive or destructive action in the event of a major crisis or conflict.19 Investigators found the actors had maintained access in some victim environments for at least five years, and assessed the activity as a deliberate shift from espionage toward pre-positioning that could threaten physical safety.20 While the confirmed victims clustered in communications, energy, transportation, and water, the strategic logic applies squarely to chemical operations, which overlap heavily with the energy sector and feed the rest.

Ransomware and financially motivated crime

For the typical chemical company, the most probable serious incident is not a nation-state SIS attack but ransomware — and the impact is real even when the malware never touches a controller. The benchmark case is Brenntag, the world's largest chemical distributor, attacked in 2021.

Brenntag distributes industrial and specialty chemicals across thousands of sites worldwide, making it a node that much of the sector's supply chain runs through. The DarkSide affiliate gained entry not through any exotic exploit but with valid credentials bought on the dark web, then encrypted network devices and stole a large volume of sensitive data, including personal information on employees.21 The company disconnected affected systems to contain the intrusion and ultimately paid approximately US$4.4 million to obtain a decryptor and prevent publication of the stolen files.21

Why it matters: Brenntag was not alone — the same period saw cyberattacks reported against other chemical firms including Siegfried and Symrise, underscoring that the industry had become a deliberate target for extortion crews.22 A distribution or production company does not need its DCS compromised to be paralyzed; encrypting the business systems that schedule shipments, manage orders, and run logistics is enough to choke the flow of chemicals to everyone downstream.

The trend line is steep. The industrial-security firm Dragos reported that the number of threat groups targeting industrial organizations grew from roughly 50 in 2023 to around 80 in 2024, alongside an 87% increase in ransomware activity against those organizations year over year.23 Even where ransomware is not purpose-built for control systems, it routinely causes operational disruption when it spreads from IT into the plant or forces a precautionary shutdown.24

Hacktivists and the blurring of motives

The most unsettling recent development is the convergence of cybercrime, hacktivism, and state interest into a single muddy category. Dragos's tracking of a group it calls BAUXITE — which shares substantial technical overlap with the “CyberAv3ngers” persona that the U.S. government has linked to Iran's Revolutionary Guard — is directly relevant: BAUXITE's confirmed victims span energy, water and wastewater, food and beverage, and chemical manufacturing, achieved through trivial compromises of internet-exposed devices.23 The same loose ecosystem produced the 2023 campaign against exposed Unitronics controllers at U.S. water utilities. When financially motivated crews, ideologically motivated hacktivists, and state proxies all reach for the same exposed OT devices, the line between cybercrime and cyberwarfare stops being a useful planning category — and defenders must assume the floor of the threat is higher than it used to be.24

The consequence that makes this different

It is worth stating plainly what is at the end of a successful attack on a chemical process, because it is qualitatively different from the consequence in most other sectors. The failure mode the industry fears is loss of containment: the escape of a hazardous material from the equipment meant to hold it. Depending on the chemical, that can mean a toxic vapor cloud, a flammable release that finds an ignition source, a runaway exothermic reaction, or a pressure-vessel rupture. The people harmed are frequently not employees but residents of the surrounding community, which is precisely why the now-lapsed federal security program tracked the more than 89 million Americans who live or work within two miles of a high-risk chemical facility.5

No cyberattack has yet produced a mass-casualty chemical release. But the industry already knows, from a non-cyber catastrophe, exactly what happens when the safety systems that Triton targeted are not functioning. The reference point is Bhopal.

A runaway reaction in a tank of methyl isocyanate — a highly toxic, volatile chemical used to make pesticides — sent a dense cloud of gas over densely populated neighborhoods while most residents slept.25 Investigations established that substandard operating and safety procedures at the understaffed plant had set the stage for catastrophe, and contemporary accounts record that the plant's safety systems were variously absent, undersized, or out of service at the critical moment.2526 The immediate death toll is generally placed in the thousands, with the eventual toll estimated as high as 15,000–20,000 and more than half a million people exposed to the gas.27

The connection to cyber: Bhopal was an accident, not an attack. Its relevance here is mechanical and exact. It is the empirical demonstration of the consequence that the safety layer exists to prevent — and Triton demonstrated an adversary deliberately reaching for that same layer. Bhopal answers the question “what is actually at stake if the safety systems fail,” and Triton answers “can an attacker make them fail.” Reading the two together is the reason chemical-sector cybersecurity is treated as a matter of public safety rather than business continuity.

This is the frame for everything that follows. The technology is powerful and was built trusting, the adversaries are increasingly capable and increasingly indifferent to the distinction between crime and warfare, and the consequence of getting it wrong is measured in human lives and surrounding communities. With that established, the next problem is that the damage rarely stays inside the fence line — because the chemical sector is wired into everything else.

02Cross-Sector Cascade

The sector that the other fifteen are built on

Critical-infrastructure sectors are not independent silos; they are a tightly coupled system. The chemical sector occupies a particularly load-bearing position, because its products are the physical inputs that the other sectors consume. A disruption here does not stay here.

CISA organizes U.S. critical infrastructure into 16 sectors, each with a designated risk-management agency.2 The chemical sector is distinctive within that set because so many of the others depend on it directly: its end users include critical-infrastructure sectors across the board, which is precisely why the uninterrupted production and transport of chemicals is treated as a matter of national and economic security.1 The sector has been described, with only mild exaggeration, as supporting and even driving the success of the other fifteen — and because of how internally and externally integrated it is, failures at even small chemical facilities can cascade into other chemical and non-chemical manufacturing.28

The mechanism of dependency is not only physical pipework. CISA's own infrastructure-dependency guidance stresses that dependencies can be physical, geographic, cyber, or logical, and that they extend well beyond direct connections — the disruption of a key input such as chlorine for water treatment can disrupt critical-infrastructure operations far from the chemical plant itself.29 Because these systems are so interconnected, a disruption in one can produce cascading impacts across a range of others.29

The chlorine–water nexus, in detail

The most studied cross-sector dependency is the one between the chemical sector and drinking water. Chlorine is essential for water disinfection and as a precursor for a family of water-treatment chemicals; the water sector accounts for only about 5% of total domestic chlorine consumption, which means water utilities are not the customer that drives chlorine production and have little leverage when supply tightens.30 EPA's supply-chain analysis rated the risk of a chlorine supply disruption as moderate-to-high, citing a recent history of reduced production capacity from extreme weather, equipment failures, and planned outages, compounded by competing demand.30 The same body of analysis notes that derivative disinfectants such as sodium hypochlorite depend on the chlor-alkali industry for their inputs, so disruption upstream propagates to the substitutes utilities would otherwise fall back on.30

The dependency is not only about manufacturing volume — it is also about transport. Much of the chlorine and related treatment chemistry that water systems rely on moves by rail, and the water sector has more than once come close to losing that supply to labor disputes that threatened to halt rail shipments of chlorine and other critical treatment products.31 A cyberattack does not have to strike a water utility to endanger drinking water; it can strike the chemical producer, the distributor, or the logistics layer that connects them, and the effect arrives at the tap all the same.

The point generalizes. Polymers and the monomers they are built from are used to remove dirt and debris in both drinking-water and wastewater treatment — without them, safe drinking water and safe handling of human waste both degrade.28 Water utilities themselves recognize this as a one-directional dependency: the water sector relies on the chemical supply chain, and disruptions to that chain are a standing planning concern.32

Concentration and single points of failure

What turns ordinary dependency into systemic risk is concentration. A National Academies analysis of chemical-infrastructure vulnerability observed that while the highly distributed nature of the overall supply chain makes it hard to shut the whole system down from a single point, the real question is whether an attack at a dominant node or dominant link — a chemical that is produced at relatively few facilities, or that moves through a few critical chokepoints — could halt production of something essential.33 Many commodity chemicals such as chlorine and ammonia are substitutable only at significant cost in time and money, and not always acceptably so; replacing chlorine for drinking-water disinfection, for instance, is possible but slow and expensive.33 The implication for a cyber adversary is uncomfortable: maximum downstream impact does not require attacking everything, only the right concentrated node.

The cascade logic

An attacker seeking maximum effect against U.S. infrastructure has a rational reason to target chemicals specifically: it is an upstream position. Disabling one well-chosen producer of a concentrated input can degrade water treatment, agriculture, or pharmaceutical manufacturing downstream — multiplying the impact of a single intrusion far beyond the victim's own balance sheet. This is the strategic argument for treating chemical-sector cyber risk as a national, not merely a corporate, concern.

The dependency runs both ways

Finally, the coupling is bidirectional, which compounds the fragility. Chemical production is itself dependent on the very sectors it supplies: it requires reliable electricity to run its processes, transportation to move feedstocks and products, water as both reactant and coolant, and communications and IT to coordinate operations.3 A cyberattack on the energy sector that interrupts power, or on transportation that halts rail, can shut down chemical production, which then ripples back out to everyone the chemical sector serves. Dependencies in this system are upstream and downstream, one-directional and mutual, and they routinely span regional and jurisdictional boundaries — which is exactly why CISA frames cascading failure, rather than isolated outage, as the defining risk of an interconnected infrastructure.29 Securing one plant is necessary; it is nowhere near sufficient.

03Mitigation

Frameworks, standards, and the controls that work

Reducing chemical-sector cyber risk is a layered problem with a layered answer: a regulatory floor, voluntary programs that go beyond it, engineering standards that translate intent into architecture, and technical controls implemented on the plant floor. Each layer matters; at present, one of them has a hole in it.

The regulatory floor — and the gap where one used to be

For fifteen years, the cornerstone of U.S. chemical security regulation was the Chemical Facility Anti-Terrorism Standards (CFATS) program, administered by CISA and authorized originally through the DHS Appropriations Act of 2007.34 CFATS was a risk-based regime: any facility possessing more than a threshold quantity of any of over 300 listed “chemicals of interest” had to file a Top-Screen notifying DHS, after which higher-risk facilities completed a security vulnerability assessment and were assigned to one of four risk tiers — Tiers 1 and 2 being the highest risk — each carrying performance-based security standards.3536 Critically, those standards addressed cyberattacks as well as physical attacks, theft, and sabotage, and the program let facilities vet personnel against the federal terrorist screening database.37

Status: lapsed

On 28 July 2023, Congress allowed the statutory authority for CFATS to expire, and CISA has stated it can no longer enforce the regulations: it cannot require facilities to report chemicals of interest, cannot perform inspections, and cannot compel implementation of site security plans.34 The lapse followed a House-passed reauthorization bill that was blocked in the Senate; subsequent attempts, including an amendment to the FY2025 defense authorization, failed to advance.38 As of early 2026 the program remains unauthorized and its roughly 250 dedicated staff were reassigned after the lapse — the United States is, for the first time in over fifteen years, without a mandatory chemical-security program.3940 Readers should verify the current legislative status, which may change.

The practical consequence is a visibility gap: the federal government no longer maintains a current picture of which facilities hold dangerous chemicals or who has access to them, and the proposed rulemaking that would have strengthened CFATS's physical and cybersecurity standards was halted by the lapse.40 CISA's response has been to lean on voluntary engagement (discussed below), but voluntary participation does not reconstruct a national inventory or a baseline obligation. The hole in the regulatory floor is real, and it is the single largest policy problem in the sector today.

The safety regulations that double as cyber exposure

Two long-standing federal programs govern process safety rather than security, but they are deeply relevant to cyber because a cyberattack that causes a release produces exactly the outcome they exist to prevent. The Environmental Protection Agency's Risk Management Program (RMP), under Section 112(r) of the Clean Air Act, requires facilities handling listed hazardous substances to assess worst-case release scenarios, implement accident-prevention programs, and coordinate with local responders.41 Alongside it, OSHA's Process Safety Management (PSM) standard governs the safe handling of highly hazardous chemicals in the workplace. Both regimes trace much of their modern shape to Executive Order 13650, “Improving Chemical Facility Safety and Security,” issued in 2013 after the West, Texas, ammonium-nitrate explosion and other incidents, which created a working group tri-chaired by EPA, OSHA, and DHS.4142

The RMP rule has been notably unstable, rewritten several times in just over a decade as administrations have alternated between strengthening and rolling back its provisions — a volatility that itself complicates long-term security investment.43 For waterfront chemical facilities, the Coast Guard's Maritime Transportation Security Act (MTSA) regime adds another layer, and pipeline operators carrying chemical and petrochemical products fall under Transportation Security Administration security directives. The result is a patchwork: process-safety rules that cyber can defeat, a maritime security regime for some sites, pipeline directives for some transport — and, at the center where a dedicated chemical-security mandate used to be, a gap.

Voluntary programs above the floor

With CFATS dormant, voluntary frameworks carry more weight than ever. CISA, as the sector's risk-management agency, runs ChemLock — a no-cost program open to any facility that possesses dangerous chemicals, regardless of whether it was ever tiered under CFATS.44 ChemLock provides on-site assessment and assistance visits, security planning help, training, exercises, and a library of templates and guides; it is explicitly designed to help facilities improve both their physical and their cyber security posture.44

For cyber specifically, CISA's Cross-Sector Cybersecurity Performance Goals (CPGs) — first released in 2022 and now in a 2.0 revision — define a prioritized baseline of high-impact practices spanning both IT and OT, aimed especially at smaller organizations looking for a defensible starting point.4445 Building on that baseline, in November 2024 CISA published Chemical Sector-Specific Goals (SSGs): additional voluntary practices tuned to the sector's threat landscape, highlighting measures such as full system-lifecycle management of IT and OT assets, disabling unnecessary systems and services, and managing mobile devices.46 CISA has paired these with a broader push toward secure-by-design, intended to shift the burden of security from plant owners onto the manufacturers of the technology they buy.45

The industry also polices itself. The American Chemistry Council's Responsible Care Security Code — added as the seventh code of the Responsible Care program after the September 11 attacks — requires member companies to implement a risk-based security management system covering people, property, products, processes, information, and information systems across the entire chemical value chain, and is mandatory for ACC membership.47 The code predates and helped shape CFATS, and it is recognized under the DHS SAFETY Act as a qualified anti-terrorism technology.47 Notably, the ACC updated the Security Code in 2021 to sharpen its emphasis on cybersecurity and the links between cyber, physical, and operating-system security — an acknowledgment, from the industry side, that the threat had changed.47 ACC member companies report having invested more than US$27 billion in facility security since 2001.48

The voluntary stack, layered

A facility seeking to build a program today can stack these: start with the Cross-Sector CPGs as a minimum baseline, add the Chemical SSGs for sector-tuned actions, use ChemLock for free assessments and planning support, and — if an ACC member — operate under the Responsible Care Security Code as a mandatory management system. None of this is a substitute for a binding national mandate, but together it is a coherent voluntary program.

The standards that define the architecture

Where regulation sets obligations and voluntary programs set priorities, engineering standards define how to build a defensible plant. Two are central, and they are complementary rather than competing.

The global standard for industrial-control-system security is the ISA/IEC 62443 series, jointly developed by the International Society of Automation and the International Electrotechnical Commission and organized into four groups covering general concepts, policies and procedures, system requirements, and component requirements.49 Its two foundational ideas are security levels (SL), which grade the strength of protection a zone requires, and the zone-and-conduit model. A zone groups assets that share common security requirements; a conduit is the controlled communication path between zones. The governing posture is default-deny: every path between zones must be explicitly permitted, specified as narrowly as possible by source, destination, port, and protocol — anything not expressly allowed is blocked.50

Sitting alongside it, NIST Special Publication 800-82, Revision 3 (published September 2023) is the most comprehensive public guidance on operational-technology security.50 Where 62443 is prescriptive about how to structure a security program, SP 800-82 is descriptive — it explains what to consider and how to reason about OT risk, endorses defense-in-depth built on the Purdue model, and maps to the broader NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) 2.0.50 The two are routinely used together: 62443's zones and conduits are the implementation mechanism for the architecture that SP 800-82 recommends.50 Crucially for this sector, both recognize that you cannot patch a running reactor or reboot a safety controller mid-shift, so they emphasize compensating controls — strict segmentation, application-aware firewalls, and anomaly detection — where the underlying systems cannot themselves be hardened.50

The bridge between safety and security has its own emerging standard. The functional-safety standard IEC 61511 (ISA-84) already contains clauses touching SIS cybersecurity, and the technical report ISA-TR84.00.09 provides specific guidance on applying cybersecurity across the functional-safety lifecycle — the explicit recognition, post-Triton, that the SIS must be secured as well as made reliable.10

The controls, on the plant floor

Standards become protection only when they are implemented as specific engineering choices. The following are the load-bearing controls for a chemical facility, and the reasoning behind each.

  1. Segment the network and defend the IT/OT boundary. The most consequential single control is separating the control environment from corporate IT and from the internet, with a hardened demilitarized zone at Purdue Level 3.5 brokering any required data exchange so that no direct path runs from the enterprise into the process.6 Within OT, 62443's zones and conduits subdivide further, with default-deny rules on every conduit.50 Triton's path ran enterprise → OT → engineering workstation → SIS; robust segmentation is what breaks that chain.
  2. Keep the safety system independent — and treat it as a security asset. The SIS must remain genuinely separate from the basic control system, both for functional-safety reasons and so that a compromise of the control layer does not automatically deliver the safety layer. Network access to safety controllers should be tightly restricted, the engineering workstations that program them treated as among the most sensitive machines in the plant, and the SIS brought formally into the cybersecurity program per ISA-TR84.00.09 rather than left solely to the safety team.10
  3. Build and maintain an asset inventory. You cannot defend what you cannot see. A current inventory of every OT and IT asset — including the legacy controllers that no agent can be installed on — is the precondition for every other control, and the first thing CISA's performance goals call for.45
  4. Monitor passively, never scan actively. OT visibility must come from passive network monitoring that understands industrial protocols such as Modbus and DNP3, because actively polling or scanning live control devices can itself disrupt deterministic control loops — a monitoring tool that injects traffic has no place inside a control zone.50 This is a hard constraint that distinguishes OT defense from ordinary IT security.
  5. Control remote access deliberately. Exposed remote-access paths and internet-reachable controllers are a recurring root cause — the BAUXITE intrusions succeeded through trivial compromises of exposed devices, and Brenntag fell to purchased credentials.2123 Remote access to OT should be brokered, multi-factor-authenticated, time-bounded, and logged, never a standing always-on tunnel.
  6. Manage vulnerabilities under operational reality. Because a running process cannot simply be patched or rebooted, vulnerability management in OT is a discipline of risk-based prioritization and compensating controls — segmentation, firewalling, and monitoring around an unpatched device — until a maintenance window allows remediation.50 Dragos's analysis that the majority of researched OT vulnerabilities sit deep inside the control network reinforces that perimeter defense alone is not enough.23
  7. Demand secure-by-design from vendors. The structural fix for insecure legacy protocols and default credentials is to stop buying insecure products. CISA's secure-by-design initiative aims to move that burden onto manufacturers, so that controllers and software ship hardened rather than requiring every operator to harden them after the fact.45
The minimum credible posture

If a facility did only the highest-leverage things: (1) a real IT/OT boundary with a Level 3.5 DMZ, (2) an independent, access-restricted SIS, (3) an asset inventory, (4) passive OT monitoring, and (5) brokered, MFA-protected remote access — it would have foreclosed the initial-access and lateral-movement steps common to nearly every incident in this study. None of these is exotic. The gap between knowing them and implementing them across hundreds of thousands of facilities is the real work.

04Open Problems

What is still unsolved

The frameworks above describe a defensible plant in theory. In practice, the sector faces structural problems that no individual facility can fix alone — and several genuine research questions where the field does not yet have good answers. Honesty about these gaps is more useful than a tidy conclusion.

The policy gap is unresolved

The most immediate problem is the one already named: the United States has no mandatory chemical-security program. The lapse of CFATS removed not only an enforcement mechanism but a national picture — without it, no federal entity systematically knows which facilities hold which dangerous chemicals or who can access them, and the planned upgrade to CFATS's cyber standards never took effect.40 Voluntary programs are valuable but self-selecting: the facilities most in need of a security baseline are often the least likely to opt into one. Restoring a binding floor — ideally one that explicitly incorporates the cyber-physical and safety-system threats that have emerged since CFATS was designed — is a policy question the sector cannot solve on its own.39

The install base is insecure by construction

Chemical plants run on equipment with service lives measured in decades. Much of the installed control and safety hardware was designed before cybersecurity was a consideration, speaks protocols with no authentication or encryption, and cannot host modern security agents — you cannot install endpoint protection on a programmable logic controller running a real-time operating system from years ago.6 Because these systems cannot be patched on demand and often cannot be replaced without an expensive plant turnaround, defenders are forced into compensating controls around fundamentally fragile components.10 Secure-by-design is the correct long-term answer, but it only helps the next generation of equipment; the existing base will remain a liability for many years, and bridging that gap is an unsolved engineering and economic problem.

The attack surface is expanding, not shrinking

Every efficiency trend in modern manufacturing works against OT isolation. The drive to connect plants for remote monitoring, predictive maintenance, cloud analytics, and the Industrial Internet of Things steadily erodes the air gaps that once protected control systems, and these newer architectures — cloud-connected systems, IIoT devices — do not map cleanly onto the Purdue model that segmentation guidance assumes.6 The same connectivity that delivers operational value delivers new paths inward. Dragos's finding that the share of network-exploitable, perimeter-facing OT vulnerabilities rose year over year is a direct symptom: the boundary that defenders are trying to hold is becoming more porous, not less.23 Designing security models that fit cloud-connected, sensor-dense plants — rather than retrofitting a 1990s hierarchy onto them — is an open architectural challenge.

Safety and security are still two cultures

Triton's most quietly damning detail is that it was the first incident to target the OT engineering function — a domain that traditionally belonged to process and safety engineers, not security teams.14 In many organizations those remain separate disciplines with separate tools, separate reporting lines, and separate mental models: the safety engineer thinks in probabilities of random failure, the security engineer thinks in adversaries. The June 2017 misdiagnosis of a Triton-induced trip as a mechanical fault is what an organizational seam looks like in practice — and it cost two months of undetected access.14 Integrating safety and security into a single cyber-physical risk discipline, with shared visibility into the SIS, is a cultural and methodological problem the sector has only begun to solve.

Detection inside OT remains hard

Defenders are also fighting a detection problem peculiar to control environments. The constraint against active scanning means visibility must be earned passively; legacy protocols carry little security telemetry; and adversaries have adapted accordingly. Volt Typhoon's living-off-the-land tradecraft is explicitly designed to blend into normal system activity and minimize what shows up in standard logging, which is part of why it went undetected for years.1920 Threat hunting — proactively searching for an adversary rather than waiting for an alert — is increasingly treated as a necessity rather than an option for industrial operators, but it demands scarce expertise and mature instrumentation that many facilities lack.23

The workforce does not exist in sufficient numbers

Every control above assumes people who understand both industrial processes and cybersecurity — a genuinely rare combination. The pool of professionals fluent in DCS engineering, safety-instrumented-system design, and modern security tradecraft simultaneously is small, and the smaller organizations that make up much of the sector's long tail often have limited cybersecurity capability of any kind. CISA's analysis of nation-state targeting specifically noted that some victims were smaller organizations with limited security capacity that nonetheless provide critical services — a reminder that the weakest links are frequently the least resourced.20 Closing the OT-security skills gap is a workforce-development challenge that constrains how fast any technical solution can actually be deployed.

Supply-chain and third-party risk compounds it all

Finally, the boundaries of any single facility's responsibility are blurry. Controllers, software, and services come from vendors whose own security becomes the operator's exposure; distributors and logistics providers sit between producers and downstream users; and a compromise anywhere in that chain can propagate. Brenntag's role as a distribution node, and the sector's documented dependence on concentrated producers and rail transport, all illustrate that third-party and supply-chain risk is not a side issue but a central one.2133 Securing a value chain in which a single distributor or a single upstream producer can be a dominant node is far harder than securing a perimeter.

The threat is trending toward the worst case

The trajectory of the adversary is the backdrop to every challenge above. Purpose-built ICS-attack frameworks have moved from the realm of a single Stuxnet-class event to a recurring phenomenon — PIPEDREAM was the seventh such toolkit found, was deliberately built to be controller-agnostic, and was caught before deployment rather than after.1617 Nation-states are pre-positioning for disruption rather than merely spying.19 And the motives behind OT-capable attacks are converging into a single category in which crime, hacktivism, and statecraft are no longer cleanly separable, with chemical manufacturing already appearing among the confirmed targets.23 The defender's planning assumption can no longer be that a sophisticated SIS attack is a once-in-a-decade anomaly.

Where research and innovation are most needed

Synthesizing the gaps above, the highest-value open problems for researchers, vendors, and standards bodies are:

  1. Cyber-resilient safety systems. SIS architectures and verification methods that remain trustworthy under deliberate attack, not only under random failure — extending functional-safety assurance to include an intelligent adversary.
  2. Consequence-driven, cyber-informed engineering. Methodologies that work backward from the unacceptable physical outcome (a specific release) to identify and engineer out the digital paths that could cause it, rather than treating every asset as equally important.
  3. Scalable passive OT detection. Monitoring and anomaly-detection techniques that find living-off-the-land and protocol-level manipulation inside control networks without active probing, and that work for under-resourced facilities.
  4. Securing the legacy install base. Practical, low-disruption ways to add authentication and integrity to insecure-by-design protocols and unpatchable controllers without forcing a full plant replacement.
  5. Secure-by-design at the component level. Controllers, safety logic solvers, and firmware that ship hardened by default, shifting the burden off operators — including verifiable, tamper-evident safety-controller firmware of the kind Triton exploited.
  6. Architectures for the connected plant. Security models that accommodate cloud, IIoT, and remote operations natively, rather than retrofitting a hierarchical reference model designed for an isolated era.
05Conclusion

Holding two ideas at once

The chemical sector forces a discipline that the rest of critical-infrastructure security can sometimes avoid: the simultaneous, integrated treatment of safety and security. For most of industrial history these were separate concerns — one guarding against accidents, the other against adversaries — and they could be managed by separate teams reading separate standards. The cyber threat dissolved that separation. An attacker who reaches a safety instrumented system is performing an act of security that engineers a failure of safety, and the consequence is not lost revenue but the kind of loss of containment that Bhopal made unforgettable.

The defensive picture is, in outline, well understood. Segment the network and defend the IT/OT boundary. Keep the safety system independent and bring it into the security program. Inventory the assets, monitor passively, broker remote access, manage vulnerabilities under the reality that you cannot reboot a reactor, and demand that vendors ship secure products. Wrap it in the standards — ISA/IEC 62443, NIST SP 800-82, IEC 61511 and its cyber extensions — and the voluntary programs that fill the space where a national mandate used to be. None of this is mysterious.

What is unresolved is everything between knowing and doing. A regulatory floor has lapsed and not been restored. An install base of unpatchable, insecure-by-design equipment will persist for decades. The attack surface widens with every connected sensor while the threat actors grow more numerous and less distinguishable from one another. And the workforce capable of closing the gap is too small for the size of the problem. These are not reasons for fatalism; they are the agenda. The sector that makes the inputs for everything else cannot be secured by any one facility, any one standard, or any one agency — but the path is legible, and the stakes, measured in the communities living within two miles of the fence line, could hardly be clearer.

The keystroke and the chemical release are now the same threat model. Securing the sector means never letting them meet.
