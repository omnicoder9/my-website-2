Field Guide • Cloud-Native Systems

The Orchestrated Fleet

A single container is a package. A thousand of them, spread across failing machines and shifting demand, is a coordination problem. This is a working guide to how Docker, Swarm and Kubernetes turn that problem into a control loop — and to the ecosystem that grew up around it.

00 • The premise

A container, taken by itself, is a modest thing. It is a way to package an application together with its libraries, its runtime and its configuration so that the same bundle runs identically on a laptop, a test rig and a production server — the answer to the perennial complaint that a program “worked on my machine” but nowhere else.1 When Solomon Hykes stepped onto the stage at PyCon in March 2013 and demonstrated Docker to the public for the first time, the problem he named was deliberately small: shipping code to the server is hard. Docker made it easy by standing on the shoulders of the Linux kernel, copy-on-write filesystems and developer-friendly, git-like semantics for images.2

That modesty is the point, and also the trap. Once a single container is trivial to build and run, teams build a great many of them: a web tier, an API tier, a cache, a queue, a database, a dozen background workers. Each wants to run somewhere; each can crash; the machines beneath them can crash; demand rises on Friday night and collapses by Monday morning. A person can babysit three containers on one host. Nobody can babysit four hundred containers across forty hosts, redeploying the ones that die, moving load off machines that fail, and rolling out a new version without dropping traffic. That labour — placing, watching, healing, scaling and connecting containers across a fleet of machines — is container orchestration, and it is the subject of this guide.

The central move that every orchestrator makes is the same, and it is worth stating before any of the machinery. You do not tell an orchestrator to start a container on host seven. You tell it what you want the world to look like — “five copies of this service should be running” — and hand that declaration to a controller whose only job is to make reality match. The controller observes what is actually running, compares it to what you declared, computes the difference, and acts to close the gap. Then it does the whole thing again, forever. A container dies and the running count drops to four; the loop notices the gap and schedules a fifth. A machine is drained for maintenance; the loop reschedules its work elsewhere. This is the reconciliation loop pictured above, and it is the quiet engine underneath Swarm, underneath Kubernetes, and underneath the cluster manager at Google that predates them both.

Orchestration is not automation of steps. It is automation of intent — you declare the destination, and the system steers toward it, correcting for drift.The reconciliation model

This guide follows the fleet from its origins to its operating manual. It traces the lineage from Google's Borg through Docker's arrival to the orchestration wars and the standards that ended them; it establishes the vocabulary that Swarm and Kubernetes share; it works through Docker Swarm as the simplest native orchestrator and Kubernetes as the industry-standard one; it lays the two side by side; it examines the developer toolchain bundled into Docker Desktop; it surveys the surrounding cloud-native ecosystem; and it closes with the practices that separate a demo cluster from one you would trust with real traffic. Throughout, the aim is the working engineer's aim: not to admire the diagrams, but to understand the system well enough to reason about it when it misbehaves.

01 • A short history

From Borg to Docker to the orchestration wars

The ideas arrived in a particular order. Understanding that order explains why today's tools are shaped the way they are — and why the word “Docker” now means several different things at once.

Containers did not begin with Docker, and orchestration did not begin with Kubernetes. Both rest on two primitives that the Linux kernel had accumulated over years: namespaces, which give a process its own private view of the process table, the network, mounts and users; and control groups (cgroups), which meter and cap how much CPU, memory and I/O a group of processes may consume. A container is, in the end, just an ordinary Linux process that has been placed inside a set of namespaces and cgroups so that it believes it has the machine to itself. Docker's early achievement was not inventing this isolation but making it usable without a degree in kernel administration — wrapping the primitives in an image format, a build file and a command-line tool that a working developer could learn in an afternoon.1

Google's Borg: orchestration before it had a name

Long before any of this reached the public, Google was running containers at a scale nobody else approached. Its internal cluster manager, Borg, developed from around 2003–2004, ran hundreds of thousands of jobs from thousands of distinct applications across clusters of tens of thousands of machines. It achieved high utilisation by packing tasks tightly, over-committing resources, and sharing machines while isolating workloads at the process level; it kept high-availability services alive with runtime features that minimised recovery time and scheduling policies that reduced the odds of correlated failure. Crucially, Borg let its users describe work declaratively — a job specification the system was then responsible for satisfying — and it used containers for isolation.3 Nearly every concept that Kubernetes would later make famous was already present in Borg in some form.

That heritage is not folklore; it is architecture. When three Google engineers — Craig McLuckie, Joe Beda and Brendan Burns — pitched an open-source cluster manager in 2013, they were proposing to rebuild Borg's ideas in the open, informed by a decade of running production workloads on the original. The project's name was chosen with care: Kubernetes comes from the Ancient Greek for helmsman or pilot, the person at the ship's wheel.4 Many of its earliest contributors had worked on Borg; they codenamed the effort “Project 7” after Seven of Nine, the reformed Borg character from Star Trek, and gave the logo a seven-spoked ship's wheel. Where Borg was written in C++, Kubernetes was written in Go.5

Docker's arrival and the containerisation of everything

Docker itself grew out of dotCloud, a platform-as-a-service company founded during Y Combinator's 2010 cohort; the container engine that powered its platform proved more valuable than the platform, and the company pivoted, open-sourcing the engine in 2013 and eventually renaming itself Docker, Inc.1 The dependency-hell problem it solved was universal and immediately felt, and adoption was explosive. But an explosion of single containers on single hosts only sharpened the coordination problem. As one veteran put it, if Docker's 2013 announcement was the spark that lit the fire, the container orchestration platforms were the winds that turned it into a forest fire — Mesos, Swarm, Nomad and Kubernetes were the applications that pushed containers over the edge into ubiquity.

The orchestration wars and the treaty that ended them

For a few years in the mid-2010s the field was genuinely contested. Kubernetes was announced as an open-source project in June 2014, but it took more than a year to reach a stable release: Kubernetes 1.0 arrived on 21 July 2015, and on the very same day Google and the Linux Foundation announced the Cloud Native Computing Foundation (CNCF), with Kubernetes as its seed technology. Docker's own answer, Swarm mode, did not appear until the autumn of 2015 — a few months after Kubernetes 1.0 — so it was not even on the field when Kubernetes first debuted, and it competed against alternatives such as Apache Mesos as much as against Kubernetes.6 The CNCF's backing, and its association with Google's operational credibility, is a large part of why Kubernetes pulled ahead: it lent the project legitimacy that a “random open-source orchestrator” would have lacked.6

The more important development, for the long run, was standardisation. In June 2015 Docker and a broad coalition of industry players established the Open Container Initiative (OCI), an open-governance body under the Linux Foundation whose purpose was to prevent the container world from fragmenting into incompatible formats. The OCI maintains three specifications — a Runtime Specification for how to execute a container from an unpacked “filesystem bundle” on disk, an Image Specification for how images are structured, and a Distribution Specification for how they are moved between registries.7 Docker contributed its low-level runtime, runc, as the reference implementation of the runtime spec. The effect was quietly decisive: because everyone agreed on what a container image and a running container were, tools built by different vendors could interoperate, and no single company owned the definition.

The great decomposition: Docker becomes a stack

This is where “Docker” stops being one thing. Starting around 2016, the monolithic Docker Engine was deliberately broken into discrete components. The lifecycle work — creating, starting, stopping and deleting containers, pulling images, and managing their storage — was factored out into a daemon called containerd, which sits above the low-level runc executor and manages the complete container lifecycle. containerd was later donated to the CNCF, where its deliberately small scope made it a robust, portable core that other systems could build on.8 The result is a layered stack rather than a single binary. When you type docker run, the Docker daemon calls containerd, which uses a per-container shim and ultimately runc to talk to the kernel; the docker command you know is only the top of a tower.9

Understanding this decomposition resolves the single most confusing episode in the field's recent history: the removal of Docker from Kubernetes. Kubernetes talks to container runtimes through a plug-in interface called the Container Runtime Interface (CRI). The Docker Engine predates CRI and never implemented it, so for years Kubernetes shipped an adapter called the dockershim to translate between them. Maintaining that adapter was a burden, and once CRI-native runtimes were mature, it made little sense. The removal was announced with the v1.20 release and finally carried out in Kubernetes v1.24, released in 2022; from that version, the dockershim is gone and clusters must use a CRI-conformant runtime such as containerd or CRI-O.10 This caused a brief panic that was almost entirely a misunderstanding. Because images built by docker build conform to the OCI image specification, they run unchanged on every CRI runtime; runc remains the underlying default executor for both containerd and CRI-O.10 Nothing about how developers build or run images locally changed. Only the internal plumbing of production clusters did — and even then, they simply swapped one part of the Docker stack (containerd) in for the whole of it.

02 • The shared vocabulary

The concepts every orchestrator shares

Swarm and Kubernetes differ in surface area, not in soul. Before comparing them, it pays to fix the handful of ideas they both implement — because once you see them clearly in one system, you see them everywhere.

An image is a read-only template — a stack of filesystem layers plus metadata describing how to start the program inside. A container is a running instance of an image, an ordinary process wrapped in namespaces and cgroups. Images are immutable and portable; you build one, push it to a registry, and pull it anywhere.1 Orchestration operates one level above this: it is concerned not with any single container but with keeping the right set of containers running, in the right numbers, in the right places, reachable by the right names.

Declarative desired state and the reconciliation loop

The organising idea, met already in the introduction, deserves a name and a sharp definition, because it is the difference between orchestration and mere scripting. Traditional automation is imperative: a script runs a command, then the next, then the next, and if it fails halfway you are left in an unknown state. An orchestrator is declarative: you submit a manifest that says how you want the world to look — “three replicas of this web app” — and the system inspects the actual state of the cluster, notices it has only one, and takes the steps needed to reach three.11 Kubernetes people call this a reconciliation engine, and the phrase is exact: the system runs a control loop that continuously drives actual state toward desired state. Delete a running replica by hand and, within moments, the loop schedules a replacement; you cannot “win” against it without changing the declaration itself.11 This is the origin of self-healing, and it is why an orchestrated system is resilient in a way a hand-managed one never is.

Scheduling, placement and the control plane

Someone has to decide which machine a new container lands on. That is the scheduler, and its decision balances the container's declared resource requests against what each node has free, along with constraints (run only on nodes with SSDs), affinity and anti-affinity (keep these two apart, keep those two together) and policy. Every orchestrator splits its world into two planes for this purpose. The control plane is the set of components that make global decisions — storing desired state, running the scheduler, running the reconciliation controllers. The data plane, or the worker nodes, is where application containers actually run, supervised by a small agent that reports back to the control plane and executes what it is told. This manager/worker split, and the strongly consistent store that holds the cluster's desired state, are common to Borg, to Swarm and to Kubernetes; only the names change.

Service discovery, load balancing and rolling updates

Because individual containers come and go — rescheduled, replaced, scaled up and down — you cannot address them by IP. Orchestrators therefore provide service discovery: a stable name that always resolves to the current, healthy set of container instances, usually backed by an internal DNS server and a virtual IP. Traffic to that name is spread across the instances by load balancing. And because you must deploy new versions without an outage, every orchestrator supports rolling updates: replacing old instances with new ones a few at a time, watching health as it goes, and — critically — rolling back automatically if the new version fails its checks. These four faculties — discovery, balancing, health-checked rollout, and rollback — are what make a fleet feel like a single, addressable, upgradable application rather than a pile of processes.

The runtime seam

Finally, orchestrators do not run containers themselves; they delegate to a container runtime on each node. Kubernetes formalises this boundary with the Container Runtime Interface, and requires that each node run a CRI-conformant runtime so that pods have somewhere to execute; the choice of runtime — containerd, CRI-O and others — is deliberately pluggable, and installing one is a prerequisite for joining a node to a cluster.12 This is the seam introduced in the previous section, seen now from above: the orchestrator's job ends at “make sure this pod is running here,” and the runtime's job begins at “create these containers.” Keeping that boundary clean is exactly what let Kubernetes shed the Docker shim without disturbing the images running on top.

03 • Native orchestration

Docker Swarm: orchestration in the engine

Swarm mode is the orchestrator built directly into the Docker Engine. It is the shortest path from “I have containers” to “I have a self-healing cluster,” and its small, legible model is the ideal place to see the reconciliation loop made concrete.

Swarm mode is implemented by an embedded toolkit called SwarmKit, a general library for orchestrating distributed systems that provides node discovery, Raft-based consensus and task scheduling. Machines running SwarmKit group together into a swarm; once a machine joins, it becomes a node, and every node is one of two kinds. Worker nodes run tasks using an executor — by default a Docker container executor. Manager nodes accept specifications from the operator and are responsible for reconciling the desired state with the actual cluster state. An operator can dynamically change a node's role, promoting a worker to manager or demoting a manager to worker.13

Managers, workers and Raft consensus

The managers are the brain, and they cannot simply trust one another blindly; they must agree on the single authoritative version of the cluster's state even as individual managers fail. They do this with the Raft consensus algorithm. The managers elect a single leader to conduct orchestration, and they maintain a strongly consistent, Raft-replicated, in-memory view of the cluster that makes reads extremely fast.13 Worker nodes take no part in this election; each runs an agent that executes assigned tasks and reports their status back to a manager, so the managers always know what is actually running.14

Raft has a consequence that every Swarm operator must internalise: because consensus requires a majority, the number of managers should always be odd. A swarm tolerates the loss of (N−1)/2 managers — three managers survive one failure, five survive two — but a fourth manager buys no additional fault tolerance over three, and two managers are strictly worse than one, because losing either leaves the remaining manager unable to form a majority and the cluster freezes: no scheduling, no updates, nothing.15

Advisory • quorum

Run three or five manager nodes, never two or four, and keep managers on dedicated hosts that do not also run application workloads. An even count wastes a node without improving resilience; a workload that starves a manager of CPU can cost you the whole control plane. For latency, the managers should be near one another on the network, because every state change travels through the Raft log.15

Creating a swarm

Bringing a swarm to life is a two-command affair. The Docker Engine runs with swarm mode disabled by default; docker swarm init turns the current engine into a single-node swarm and, if you do not specify otherwise, listens on port 2377.14 The command prints a join token — a secret that other machines present to enlist — and there are separate tokens for workers and managers, so that manager access, which grants control over the entire swarm, can be guarded far more tightly than worker access.14

bootstrap-swarm.shshell
# On the first manager: initialise the swarm and advertise its address
docker swarm init --advertise-addr 10.0.0.10

# Docker prints a worker join command containing a one-time token:
#   docker swarm join --token SWMTKN-1-49n... 10.0.0.10:2377

# Fetch the *manager* join token separately (guard this closely)
docker swarm join-token manager

# On each additional node, run the printed join command
docker swarm join --token SWMTKN-1-49n...xyz 10.0.0.10:2377

# Back on a manager, inspect the fleet
docker node ls
# ID          HOSTNAME   STATUS  AVAILABILITY  MANAGER STATUS
# kf9d...  *  mgr-1      Ready   Active        Leader
# p2a...      mgr-2      Ready   Active        Reachable
# 7bc...      wkr-1      Ready   Active

Services, tasks, and the two service modes

You do not run containers directly on a swarm; you declare services. A service is the definition of the work to execute: which image to run, which command, how many copies, which networks and volumes to attach. From that declaration the manager produces tasks — the unit of scheduling. A task carries exactly one container plus the instruction to run it, and once the scheduler assigns a task to a node, that task never moves; it either runs there or fails and is replaced elsewhere.14 The relationship is a hierarchy: a service is a desired state (“five replicas”), and tasks are the concrete instances the reconciler creates to satisfy it. Change the replica count and the manager adds or removes tasks to match; lose a node and the manager reschedules its lost tasks onto healthy nodes, reconciling the difference between what you asked for and what remains.13 Because tasks are organised into services, and services carry a stable identity, you can also modify a running service's configuration — its networks, its volumes, its image — without tearing it down by hand, which is the key difference between a service and a lone container.14

Services come in two modes, and choosing between them is one of the few genuinely load-bearing decisions in Swarm. A replicated service — the default — runs a specified number of tasks distributed across the swarm; this is what you want for a stateless web or API tier, where five copies behind a load balancer is exactly the goal. A global service runs precisely one task on every node in the swarm, present and future; when a new node joins, it automatically receives one. Global mode is the right tool for per-node infrastructure — a metrics exporter, a log shipper, a security agent — anything that must run once wherever there is a machine, rather than a fixed number of times somewhere.

services.shshell
# A replicated service: five copies of a web server, published on :8080
docker service create \
  --name web \
  --replicas 5 \
  --publish published=8080,target=80 \
  nginx:1.27-alpine

# Scale it by changing the desired state -- the reconciler does the rest
docker service scale web=8

# A global service: exactly one node-exporter per node, now and forever
docker service create \
  --name node-exporter \
  --mode global \
  --mount type=bind,source=/proc,target=/host/proc,readonly \
  prom/node-exporter:latest

# Inspect the tasks a service has been realised into
docker service ps web
# NAME    IMAGE              NODE    DESIRED STATE  CURRENT STATE
# web.1   nginx:1.27-alpine  wkr-1   Running        Running 2 min ago
# web.2   nginx:1.27-alpine  wkr-2   Running        Running 2 min ago  ...

The routing mesh: reaching a service from anywhere

Here Swarm does something that feels like magic until you see the mechanism. When you publish a service's port, that port is published on every node in the cluster, whether or not a given node is currently running a task for the service.16 An external client — or an upstream cloud load balancer — can connect to the published port on any node, and Swarm's routing mesh forwards the request to a running task, hopping across the cluster if necessary to reach one.17 The manager uses ingress load balancing to expose services externally, assigning a configurable published port that is answerable on every node; internally, a separate load balancer distributes requests among a service's tasks based on the service's DNS name and virtual IP.14

Mechanically, the routing mesh is built from three parts: a special overlay network named ingress, kernel netfilter rules, and IPVS — the Linux kernel's in-kernel load balancer, operating at layer 4 (transport) and routing by IP, port and protocol. Traffic arriving on the published port of any node is directed, through the netfilter rules and IPVS balancing across the service's virtual IP, over the ingress overlay to an actual task.16 The cross-node traffic itself travels inside a VXLAN tunnel on UDP port 4789, while nodes discover one another via a gossip protocol on port 7946 and the managers coordinate over port 2377 — the three ports a Swarm operator must open between every pair of nodes.15 When you need to bypass the mesh — for a latency-sensitive service, or to supply your own routing — publishing in host mode maps the task's port directly to the node's port, so requests to that node are served only by the local task.16

Declaring a whole application: stacks

Issuing docker service create commands by hand does not scale to a real application. Instead you describe the entire application declaratively in a Compose file — the same YAML format used for local multi-container development — and deploy it to the swarm as a stack. The Compose file lists each service, its image, its replica count, its update policy, its networks and its secrets; docker stack deploy reconciles the swarm to match the file, and re-running it after an edit performs a rolling update. This is the declarative model at application scale: the file is the desired state, and the swarm's job is to converge on it.

docker-compose.ymlyaml
version: "3.9"
services:
  web:
    image: registry.example.com/shop-web:1.4.2
    ports:
      - "8080:80"
    networks: [frontend]
    deploy:
      replicas: 5
      update_config:            # rolling update policy
        parallelism: 2          # two tasks at a time
        delay: 10s
        failure_action: rollback  # revert on a failed rollout
      restart_policy:
        condition: on-failure
      resources:
        limits: {cpus: "0.50", memory: 256M}
  api:
    image: registry.example.com/shop-api:1.4.2
    networks: [frontend, backend]
    secrets: [db_password]
    deploy: {replicas: 3}

networks:
  frontend: {driver: overlay}
  backend: {driver: overlay}

secrets:
  db_password: {external: true}
deploy.shshell
# Store a secret in the swarm's encrypted Raft store, then deploy the stack
printf 's3cr3t' | docker secret create db_password -
docker stack deploy --compose-file docker-compose.yml shop

# A later edit + redeploy performs a rolling, health-checked update
docker stack services shop

Two details in that file are worth dwelling on. The update_config block encodes a safe rollout directly into the desired state — two tasks at a time, a pause between batches, and an automatic rollback if the new version fails — so that a bad image cannot silently replace every healthy container at once.15 And the secret is not an environment variable but a first-class object: Swarm secrets are stored encrypted in the Raft log, mounted into containers as files rather than exposed in the environment, and are only meaningful inside a swarm.15 Between them, these two features carry much of what distinguishes a production deployment from a demo.

04 • The industry standard

Kubernetes: the reconciliation engine at scale

Kubernetes takes the same declarative, self-healing model and generalises it into a programmable platform. It is more elaborate than Swarm by design — and that elaboration is precisely why it became the substrate of the cloud-native world.

A Kubernetes cluster is, structurally, the manager/worker split of the previous section made larger and more explicit. It consists of a control plane and one or more worker nodes; the control plane makes global decisions and detects and responds to cluster events, while the nodes run the actual workloads.18 What Kubernetes adds is a richer object model, an extensible API, and a set of specialised controllers — but the beating heart is still a control loop driving actual state toward desired state.

The control plane, component by component

Five components make up the control plane, and each has a single, sharp responsibility.18 The kube-apiserver is the core component that exposes the Kubernetes HTTP API; it is the front door through which every other component, and every operator, reads and writes cluster state, and it is designed to scale horizontally by running several instances behind a load balancer.19 Behind it, etcd is a consistent and highly-available key-value store that holds all API-server data — the single source of truth for the entire cluster, and the one thing you must have a backup plan for.19 The kube-scheduler watches for newly created pods that have no node assigned and selects a node for each, weighing resource requirements, hardware and policy constraints, affinity and anti-affinity, data locality and deadlines.19 The kube-controller-manager runs the controller processes that implement Kubernetes' behaviour — logically separate control loops, compiled into one binary for simplicity — and the optional cloud-controller-manager integrates the cluster with an underlying cloud provider's APIs, isolating the code that talks to the cloud from the code that only talks to the cluster.18

The topology of these components carries a lesson. The API server is the only component that reads from and writes to etcd; everything else — scheduler, controllers, node agents — talks exclusively to the API server, often by establishing a watch and receiving a stream of change notifications. This hub-and-spoke shape is why securing the API server is the central act of cluster security, and why a healthy etcd is non-negotiable: lose consensus in etcd and the cluster can no longer agree on what it is supposed to be doing.

The node, component by component

Every worker node runs a small set of components that maintain running pods and provide the runtime environment.18 The kubelet is the node's agent: it takes the pod specifications handed to it — through the API server — and ensures that the containers they describe are running and healthy, though it pointedly does not manage containers that Kubernetes did not create.19 The kube-proxy maintains the network rules on each node that implement the Service abstraction, allowing traffic to reach pods from inside or outside the cluster, using the operating system's packet-filtering layer where one is available.19 And beneath the kubelet sits the container runtime — the CRI-conformant software, containerd or CRI-O, that actually executes the containers, exactly as established two sections ago.18

Kubernetes did not win because it was simple. It won because it was extensible — a control loop you could teach new nouns, backed by a foundation that made the platform vendor-neutral.Why the standard is the standard

The object model: pods, replica sets, deployments and services

Where Swarm has services and tasks, Kubernetes has a small tower of objects, each a declarative desired state that a controller reconciles. The Pod is the atom: one or more containers that share a network namespace and storage, scheduled together as a unit. You rarely create pods directly. Instead you create a Deployment, which declares how many replicas of a pod template you want; the Deployment manages a ReplicaSet, whose controller's entire job is to ensure that exactly that many matching pods exist, creating and deleting pods to reconcile the count. A Service gives that ever-shifting set of pods a stable virtual IP and DNS name, and load-balances traffic across the healthy members — the discovery-and-balancing faculty from the vocabulary section, realised as a first-class object. Layered on top, controllers for rollouts, autoscaling, jobs and stateful workloads extend the same pattern to new kinds of desired state.

The declarative model is not a metaphor here; it is the literal interface. You write a manifest and apply it, and Kubernetes reconciles the world to match. A Deployment for a web service, with resource requests that guide the scheduler and health probes that drive self-healing, reads like this:

web-deployment.yamlyaml
apiVersion: apps/v1
kind: Deployment
metadata:
  name: shop-web
spec:
  replicas: 5                    # desired state: five pods
  selector:
    matchLabels: {app: shop-web}
  template:
    metadata:
      labels: {app: shop-web}
    spec:
      containers:
        - name: web
          image: registry.example.com/shop-web:1.4.2
          ports: [{containerPort: 80}]
          resources:
            requests: {cpu: "100m", memory: 128Mi}
            limits:   {cpu: "500m", memory: 256Mi}
          readinessProbe:              # gate traffic on readiness
            httpGet: {path: /healthz, port: 80}
            initialDelaySeconds: 5
          livenessProbe:               # restart if it wedges
            httpGet: {path: /healthz, port: 80}
            periodSeconds: 10
---
apiVersion: v1
kind: Service
metadata:
  name: shop-web
spec:
  selector: {app: shop-web}   # targets pods with this label
  ports: [{port: 80, targetPort: 80}]
  type: ClusterIP
operate.shshell
# Apply the desired state; the controllers reconcile the cluster to match
kubectl apply -f web-deployment.yaml

# Watch the reconciler realise five pods
kubectl get pods -l app=shop-web -w

# Self-healing: delete a pod by hand and watch it come straight back
kubectl delete pod -l app=shop-web --field-selector status.phase=Running | head -1
kubectl get pods -l app=shop-web        # the ReplicaSet has already replaced it

# Scale by editing desired state, not by starting containers
kubectl scale deployment shop-web --replicas=8

# A health-checked rolling update, with a one-command rollback if it goes wrong
kubectl set image deployment/shop-web web=registry.example.com/shop-web:1.4.3
kubectl rollout status deployment/shop-web
kubectl rollout undo   deployment/shop-web    # revert to the previous ReplicaSet

The self-healing line is worth running once with your own eyes. Delete a pod and it returns within seconds, because the ReplicaSet controller's reconciliation loop observes that the actual pod count has fallen below the declared count and creates a replacement.11 There is no script that “handles” the failure; the failure is simply drift, and the loop corrects drift. That is the whole philosophy in one command.

Beyond Deployments: the other workload controllers

A Deployment is the right controller for a stateless application, where any pod is interchangeable with any other and can be replaced whenever needed.20 But not every workload is stateless, and Kubernetes supplies a small family of controllers that apply the same reconciliation pattern to different shapes of work.20

  • StatefulSet runs related pods that do track state. If a workload records data persistently, a StatefulSet can match each pod with its own PersistentVolume, and the code running in those pods can replicate data to its siblings in the same StatefulSet to improve overall resilience.20 This is the controller for databases, queues and anything with a durable identity.
  • DaemonSet defines pods that provide facilities local to a node. Every time you add a node matching the DaemonSet's specification, the control plane schedules one of its pods onto that new node; each such pod does a job much like a system daemon on a classic Unix server, and a DaemonSet may be fundamental to the cluster itself — a networking plugin, for instance.20 This is precisely Swarm's global service mode by another name: log shippers, metrics exporters and node agents, one per machine, automatically extended to machines that do not exist yet.
  • Job and CronJob define tasks that run to completion and then stop — a Job for a task that runs once, a CronJob to run that same Job repeatedly on a schedule.20 Batch processing and data migrations live here, not in a Deployment that would dutifully restart them forever.

Beyond these, the ecosystem supplies third-party workload resources: using a custom resource definition, you can add a workload controller that provides behaviour Kubernetes does not implement in its core — for instance, gang-scheduling a group of pods that must all be available before any work begins.20 This is the extensibility advantage from the comparison table, made concrete: the object model is open, and the reconciliation loop can be taught new nouns.

Exposing a service: ClusterIP, NodePort, LoadBalancer, Ingress

Swarm answers “how do I reach this?” with one mechanism — the routing mesh. Kubernetes answers it with a graded set of choices, and knowing which to reach for is a daily decision. A Service is the method for exposing a network application running as one or more pods, and a key design aim is that you should not have to modify your application to use some unfamiliar discovery mechanism: pods come and go, you may not know how many are healthy or even what they are called, and the Service makes that churn invisible.21 Four types cover the ground.21

  • ClusterIP exposes the Service on a cluster-internal IP, reachable only from within the cluster. It is the default when you do not specify a type, and it is what most services should be — internal microservice traffic, caches, databases.21
  • NodePort exposes the Service on every node's IP at a static port. Requesting one also sets up a cluster IP, exactly as though you had asked for a ClusterIP — the types build on one another rather than replacing each other.21 The “every node answers on the same port” behaviour is the closest Kubernetes analogue to Swarm's published port.
  • LoadBalancer provisions an external load balancer on cloud providers that support them; the balancer is created asynchronously and its address is published back into the Service's status. Note the division of labour: Kubernetes does not itself supply a load-balancing component — you provide one, or you integrate the cluster with a cloud provider that does.21
  • ExternalName maps the Service onto an external DNS name, configuring the cluster's DNS to return a CNAME record rather than proxying any traffic at all.21 It is an alias, not a route.

Sitting above all of these is Ingress, and the single most useful thing to know about it is that it is not a Service type; it is the cluster's entry point for HTTP traffic. An Ingress exposes HTTP and HTTPS routes from outside the cluster to Services within it, with routing governed by rules on the Ingress resource: it can give Services externally reachable URLs, load-balance traffic, terminate TLS, and offer name-based virtual hosting, letting you consolidate routing rules into a single object so that many components sit behind one listener.22 Two constraints matter in practice. An Ingress resource does nothing on its own — you must run an Ingress controller to fulfil it, typically backed by a load balancer or edge router.22 And an Ingress does not expose arbitrary ports or protocols; anything that is not HTTP or HTTPS goes out through a NodePort or LoadBalancer Service instead.22

The resulting idiom is worth committing to memory, because it is what most production clusters actually do: make nearly every Service a ClusterIP, run one Ingress controller (itself exposed via a LoadBalancer), and route all external HTTP traffic through Ingress rules. You get one external address to secure and pay for, TLS terminated in one place, and internal services that are simply unreachable from outside — which is the correct default.

Where Docker fits — and where it no longer does

Kubernetes' relationship to Docker is the source of endless confusion, and the runtime seam dissolves it. In modern clusters the default container runtime is containerd, not the Docker Engine; the direct Docker integration was removed in Kubernetes 1.24, and clusters now speak to containerd or CRI-O through the CRI.10 This changes nothing about the images developers build. Because a docker build produces an OCI-compliant image, and any OCI image looks identical to Kubernetes regardless of the tool that built it, your existing images run unchanged.12 Docker remains the tool most developers use to build and locally run containers; it is simply no longer the thing that runs them inside a production cluster. The two roles were always separable; Kubernetes 1.24 just made the separation visible.

Running Kubernetes on a laptop: kind and minikube

You do not need a cloud account to learn or develop against Kubernetes; two tools bring a real cluster to a single machine. minikube runs a single-node Kubernetes cluster inside a VM or container on your local computer, with much of the functionality of a full cluster — load balancing, node ports, persistent volumes, ingress — and it is compatible with the docker, containerd and CRI-O runtimes.23 kind — the name is short for “Kubernetes in Docker” — takes a different tack: it runs the cluster's nodes as Docker containers, spinning up containers that act as Kubernetes nodes. It was built originally to test Kubernetes itself, which makes it exceptionally fast to create and tear down, and it is well suited to local development and continuous integration.24 A common workflow ties the whole toolchain together: use Compose to run a containerised application locally during development, then deploy the same images to Kubernetes for production.23

05 • Choosing an orchestrator

Swarm or Kubernetes?

They solve the same problem with very different budgets of complexity. The honest answer to “which should I use?” is a question in return: how large is your fleet, and how much platform do you actually need?

The two orchestrators sit at opposite ends of a single trade-off. Swarm optimises for the shortest path to a working cluster and the smallest operational surface; it is orchestration you can hold entirely in your head. Kubernetes optimises for extensibility, ecosystem and scale, at the cost of a genuinely steep learning curve and more moving parts to operate. Neither choice is wrong in the abstract; the wrong choice is the one that does not match your fleet.

The scale line is the one most teams get wrong in both directions. Swarm is an excellent fit for small-to-medium deployments, and reaching for Kubernetes to run six containers on three nodes buys complexity you will pay for daily and features you will never use. But Swarm's simplicity has a ceiling; once you are running very large numbers of nodes or genuinely complex workloads, the guidance from seasoned operators is to migrate to Kubernetes, whose model and ecosystem were built for that regime.15 The deciding factors are rarely the mechanics of scheduling — both do that well — but the surrounding needs: multi-cluster and multi-cloud portability, a service mesh, fine-grained policy, a large platform team, or an ecosystem tool that only exists for Kubernetes.

There is also a non-technical reason Kubernetes so often wins by default, and it is worth naming honestly. Much of its dominance flowed from the legitimacy the CNCF and Google's operational credibility lent it early on; without that backing it might have been dismissed as just another orchestrator whose purpose was unclear given the alternatives already available.6 That legitimacy compounded into the largest ecosystem in the space, and the ecosystem is now the strongest argument for the platform — a genuinely self-reinforcing standard. The practical implication is that choosing Kubernetes is often choosing the ecosystem, not the scheduler; if you do not need the ecosystem, Swarm may serve you better for years.

06 • The developer's cockpit

Docker Desktop and the bundled toolchain

Orchestration is a production concern, but it is exercised daily from a laptop. Docker Desktop bundles the build engine, the multi-container tooling, a local Kubernetes cluster and vulnerability scanning into one workbench — the inner loop that feeds the outer one.

Docker Desktop is a bundle of Docker tools for a local machine: the Docker Engine, a command-line interface and a graphical interface for managing images, containers and containerised applications, with the ability to build, publish, run, start and stop containers. It also includes Docker Compose, the tool for defining applications made of several containers, and a feature that deploys a single-node Kubernetes cluster locally.23 On macOS and Windows it does this by running a lightweight Linux virtual machine — using Apple's Virtualization framework or WSL 2 respectively — with the Docker daemon inside it; every container operation passes through that daemon.25 Four bundled capabilities matter most to the orchestration story.

BuildKit: the modern build engine

BuildKit is the build backend that replaced Docker's legacy builder, and it has been the default for docker build since Docker Engine 23.0 — you get it without asking.26 Its central architectural idea is that a build is not a sequence of shell commands but a dependency graph. BuildKit compiles a Dockerfile into a binary intermediate format called LLB, which defines the dependency graph of the operations that make up the build; the project's own analogy is the sharpest one available — LLB is to a Dockerfile what LLVM IR is to C.26 Because the build is a graph rather than a list, independent stages can be solved concurrently and unchanged subgraphs can be skipped entirely, which is why multi-stage builds became both fast and idiomatic.

That design buys several things a production pipeline wants. BuildKit is deliberately Dockerfile-agnostic: the Dockerfile parser is merely one frontend (dockerfile.v0) that converts a build definition into LLB, and the format is vendor-neutral so that non-Dockerfile build languages can be implemented against it.26 It supports build-time mounts that never enter the final image — RUN --mount=type=cache for package-manager caches, and --mount=type=secret or type=ssh for credentials that must be used during a build but never baked into a layer.26 And because the same builder can target multiple platforms, one command produces a multi-architecture image — the mechanism by which a single tag serves both x86 servers and ARM laptops. A pinned syntax directive at the top of the Dockerfile selects the frontend version, keeping builds reproducible as BuildKit evolves.27 Builds also need not run on the laptop at all: Buildx's Kubernetes driver connects a local development or CI environment to builders running inside a Kubernetes cluster, reaching more powerful compute and, optionally, multiple native architectures — and the number of BuildKit pods can be scaled up and down to absorb build load.28

Dockerfiledockerfile
# syntax=docker/dockerfile:1        <- pin the frontend for reproducibility

# ---- build stage: heavy toolchain, never shipped ----
FROM golang:1.23-alpine AS build
WORKDIR /src
COPY go.mod go.sum ./
# A cache mount: the module cache survives between builds but is not a layer
RUN --mount=type=cache,target=/go/pkg/mod \
    go mod download
COPY . .
RUN --mount=type=cache,target=/root/.cache/go-build \
    CGO_ENABLED=0 go build -ldflags="-s -w" -o /out/shop-api ./cmd/api

# ---- runtime stage: minimal, non-root, no toolchain ----
FROM gcr.io/distroless/static-debian12:nonroot
COPY --from=build /out/shop-api /usr/local/bin/shop-api
USER nonroot:nonroot                 # never run as root by default
EXPOSE 8080
ENTRYPOINT ["/usr/local/bin/shop-api"]
build.shshell
# One command, two architectures, pushed as a single multi-arch tag
docker buildx build \
  --platform linux/amd64,linux/arm64 \
  --tag registry.example.com/shop-api:1.4.2 \
  --sbom=true --provenance=true \
  --push .

# Pass a secret to the build without ever writing it into a layer
docker buildx build \
  --secret id=npmrc,src=$HOME/.npmrc \
  --tag shop-web:dev .

Compose: the inner loop of multi-container work

Compose is the tool for applications made of several containers. It uses YAML files to configure an application's services and then creates and starts all of the containers with a single command, letting you run commands against many containers at once — building images, scaling, restarting stopped containers.1 Because commands that manipulate a single image, or that are interactive, are meaningless across a whole application, Compose deliberately omits them; its vocabulary is the application, not the container.1 The first public beta of Compose appeared in December 2013, only months after Docker itself.1

Compose's importance to orchestration is that it is the same declarative artefact in a smaller theatre. The Compose file you use to run the application on your laptop is, with a deploy block added, the file you hand to docker stack deploy to run it on a swarm — and it is the natural jumping-off point for a Kubernetes manifest. Development and production speak the same language, which is the whole promise of the container in the first place.

The bundled Kubernetes cluster

Docker Desktop can spin up a single-node Kubernetes cluster locally, enabled with a toggle in its settings; once it comes up, kubectl is configured and you can interact with the cluster immediately, with the Desktop GUI providing a graphical view of containers and cluster alike.24 For a developer who simply wants to apply a manifest and see what happens, this is the shortest possible route — nothing to install, nothing to configure.24 Its limitation is precisely its simplicity: one node, one version. When you need a multi-node topology to test scheduling, tolerations or node failure, kind's container-as-node model gives you that in seconds, which is why the two tools coexist rather than compete.

Vulnerability scanning with Docker Scout

The last bundled capability is the one that most directly serves production. Docker Scout provides a detailed view into the composition of your images and the vulnerabilities they contain.29 It works from a Software Bill of Materials (SBOM) — an inventory naming every package in the image, its version, its licence and its dependencies. Scout uses SBOMs to determine which components an image contains, either reading an SBOM already attached to the image as an attestation, or generating one on the fly by analysing the image's contents.30 Knowing exactly what is inside a piece of software is what lets you find and patch vulnerable components quickly, or notice that a dependency's licence is incompatible with your project.30

In practice this shows up as a handful of commands wired into the same workflow as the build. Scout's cves command analyses an artefact for vulnerabilities and can filter its output — for instance by EPSS score, the estimated probability that a vulnerability will be exploited in the wild in the next thirty days, so that you can triage by real-world risk rather than by raw severity count.31 It also evaluates images against policies, and the policies encode exactly the hygiene this article recommends: a default non-root user policy, for example, checks that an image is not set to run as the root superuser and is satisfied by adding a USER instruction to the Dockerfile; a complete evaluation additionally wants SBOM and provenance attestations attached to the image, which is why the build above passes --sbom and --provenance.32

scan.shshell
# A fast triage view of an image's posture
docker scout quickview registry.example.com/shop-api:1.4.2

# The full CVE report, narrowed to what is both serious and fixable
docker scout cves registry.example.com/shop-api:1.4.2 \
  --only-severity critical,high \
  --only-fixed

# Prioritise by real-world exploitation probability, not severity alone
docker scout cves --epss --epss-score 0.5 registry.example.com/shop-api:1.4.2

# Emit the SBOM for compliance, and diff two builds to prove risk went down
docker scout sbom --format spdx registry.example.com/shop-api:1.4.2 > sbom.spdx.json
docker scout compare registry.example.com/shop-api:1.4.2 \
  --to registry.example.com/shop-api:1.4.1

Practice • shift the scan left

Scanning is only useful where it can still change the outcome. Run docker scout cves in the same step that builds the image, fail the pipeline on fixable critical and high findings, and publish the SBOM as a build artefact. A vulnerability found on a developer's machine costs minutes; the same vulnerability found in a running cluster costs an incident.

07 • The wider ecosystem

What grew around the standard

An orchestrator is a platform for building platforms. The tools that accumulated around Kubernetes — runtimes, registries, packaging, delivery, observability, security — are now a larger story than the scheduler itself.

The strongest argument for Kubernetes beyond its own features is the catalogue that surrounds it: as the standard backed by the CNCF, it has an unmatched breadth of community and tooling that no other orchestration platform matches.11 Understanding that landscape means understanding which layer each tool occupies, because the layers are what the standards carved out.

Runtimes and the low level

runc is the reference implementation of the OCI runtime specification — a lightweight CLI tool for spawning and running containers in compliance with the standard — and it is the underlying default runtime for both containerd and CRI-O.10 Above it, containerd and CRI-O are the two CRI-conformant runtimes most clusters choose between; both are CNCF projects, and both can run Docker-formatted and OCI-formatted images without the Docker daemon anywhere in the picture.9 Where stronger isolation is required, sandboxed runtimes plug into the same seam: Google's gVisor implements the OCI runtime spec in a runtime called runsc that gives each container its own kernel, and Kata Containers uses lightweight VMs — both are drop-in at the OCI layer precisely because the layer is standardised.9 The same standardisation lets alternative build tools flourish: BuildKit, Podman and Buildah all produce images in the OCI format, and Buildah in particular can create OCI images without a full container engine.7

Registries and distribution

Images have to live somewhere between the build and the cluster. That is a registry — Docker Hub, or a cloud registry, or a self-hosted one — and its behaviour is governed by the third OCI specification, the Distribution Specification, which is why any registry can serve any compliant client.7 The abstraction has proven so useful that it has been extended beyond images: Helm, for instance, uses OCI-compliant registries to package and distribute applications for Kubernetes.7

Packaging and delivery

Helm is the de facto package manager for Kubernetes, bundling a set of manifests into a parameterised chart so that an application can be installed, upgraded and rolled back as a unit. Operators take the idea further: they extend Kubernetes with custom resources and a bespoke controller, teaching the reconciliation loop to manage a database or a message broker the way it manages a Deployment — the platform's extensibility used to encode operational expertise. And GitOps tools such as Argo CD and Flux close the loop on the loop: the desired state lives in a Git repository, and a controller in the cluster continuously reconciles the cluster to match the repository, making the repository the single source of truth and every change an auditable commit.

Networking, observability and security

Kubernetes deliberately does not implement pod networking itself; it delegates to plugins that implement the Container Network Interface, and clusters may not even need kube-proxy if a network plugin implements Service forwarding equivalently.19 Above that sit service meshes, which move retries, mutual TLS and traffic-splitting out of application code and into a sidecar or the node. For observability, add-ons for cluster-level DNS, a web dashboard, container metrics and centralised log storage are treated as standard extensions of the platform rather than parts of it.18 And on the security side, the boundary between static and runtime analysis is worth stating plainly: image scanners such as Docker Scout analyse static images and will not observe a running container's behaviour, so runtime security monitoring — process execution, network activity, privilege escalation — requires dedicated tools such as Falco.33 You need both; neither substitutes for the other.

Caution • the landscape is not a shopping list

The CNCF landscape's size is a feature for the industry and a hazard for a team. Every add-on is a component you must upgrade, secure and debug at three in the morning. Adopt a tool when a concrete pain justifies it — not because it appears on a diagram of the ecosystem. The most common failure mode in cloud-native adoption is not choosing the wrong orchestrator; it is assembling a platform nobody has the capacity to operate.

08 • The operating manual

Operating a fleet in production

The practices below are the ones that separate a cluster that survives a bad Tuesday from one that does not. Most are unglamorous, and all of them are cheaper to adopt before the incident than after.

Build images that deserve to be deployed

  • Use multi-stage builds. Compile in a stage with the full toolchain; copy only the artefact into a minimal runtime stage. The compiler, the package manager and the source tree are attack surface you do not need to ship.
  • Never run as root. Add a USER instruction so the container drops to an unprivileged account by default — the exact remediation Docker Scout's non-root policy asks for.32
  • Pin your tags, and pin the frontend. Avoid latest; specify a version so you know precisely which build is running and which vulnerabilities apply to it.34 Pin the Dockerfile syntax directive for the same reason.27
  • Keep secrets out of layers and environments. Use build-time secret mounts rather than build arguments, and orchestrator secrets rather than environment variables — Swarm secrets are encrypted, immutable and mounted as files.15 In Kubernetes, prefer mounting secrets into read-only volumes over exposing them in the environment.34
  • Pull only from trusted registries. Downloading and running images from unknown sources is dangerous; enforce a policy that only images built and stored in trusted registries may run.34

Declare resources and health, or the scheduler is guessing

Two fields do more for cluster stability than any add-on. Resource requests tell the scheduler how much CPU and memory a workload needs so it can place it on a node that has room; limits cap what it may consume so that one misbehaving container cannot starve its neighbours. Without requests, the scheduler is packing blind. And health probes are what convert a hung process into a self-healed one: a readiness probe keeps traffic away from an instance that is not ready to serve, and a liveness probe restarts one that has wedged. In Swarm, the equivalent discipline is a health check plus an update policy with failure_action: rollback — without automatic rollback, a broken image will happily replace every healthy container you have.15

Protect the control plane

  1. Keep quorum odd and managers dedicated. Three or five managers, never two or four, and no application workloads on them.15
  2. Back up the state store. If your Kubernetes cluster uses etcd as its backing store, you must have a backup plan for that data — it is the entire cluster.19
  3. Treat the API server as the crown jewels. Every component talks through it; anonymous access to it, to etcd or to the kubelet is effectively full administrative access to the cluster, and it should never be exposed to the open internet.34
  4. Guard the join tokens. A Swarm manager token grants control over the entire swarm, and should be protected accordingly — it is not merely a convenience string.14
  5. Encrypt what is at rest. The etcd database contains everything accessible via the Kubernetes API, so back it up with a well-reviewed encryption solution and consider full-disk encryption beneath it.34

Open the right ports, and no others

Swarm's overlay networking depends on a specific set of ports being reachable between all nodes — manager to manager, manager to worker, and worker to worker: TCP 2377 for cluster management and Raft, TCP and UDP 7946 for node discovery via gossip, and UDP 4789 for the VXLAN overlay traffic itself.15 Miss one and the cluster degrades in ways that look like application bugs. The overlay's encapsulation also adds roughly fifty bytes of overhead, so an underlay MTU of 1500 will fragment packets if you are not careful — and some cloud providers drop fragments silently.15 Test connectivity between nodes with a trivial service before declaring the cluster healthy.

Deploy from Git, not from a terminal

The final practice is procedural rather than technical. The declarative model reaches its full value only when the declaration is versioned: manifests and Compose files in a repository, changes made by pull request, and a controller reconciling the cluster to the repository. It gives you an audit trail, a review gate, a trivially reproducible environment and a rollback that is just a revert. A cluster whose real desired state lives in somebody's shell history is not really declarative at all — it only appears to be, right up until the moment you need to rebuild it.

Practice • rehearse the failure

Every claim in this section is testable in a scratch cluster in under an hour. Kill a worker node and watch the tasks reschedule. Drain a node and watch the workload move. Push a deliberately broken image and confirm the rollout halts and rolls back rather than taking the service down. The reconciliation loop is trustworthy, but your configuration of it is a hypothesis until you have watched it hold.

09 • In closing

The idea that endures

Strip away the acronyms and a decade of container history reduces to a single, durable idea. Docker made a unit of work — a standardised, portable bundle that abstracts an application away from the infrastructure beneath it, so that developers could focus on what was inside the container while operators focused on deploying any application, anywhere, in a common format.2 Orchestration took that unit and added a control loop: declare what should be true, and let a tireless reconciler make it true, and keep it true, as machines fail and demand shifts beneath it.

Everything else is consequence. The manager/worker split is what makes the loop fault-tolerant. Raft and etcd are what let the managers agree on the declaration. The scheduler is the loop's placement policy; services and routing meshes are how the loop's output stays addressable while its contents churn; rolling updates and rollbacks are the loop applied to change itself. The OCI and CRI specifications are what keep the layers swappable, which is why removing the Docker shim from Kubernetes turned out to be plumbing rather than upheaval — images built by docker build kept running exactly as before, because the standard, not the vendor, defines what a container is.10

For the practitioner, the two lessons worth carrying away are unglamorous and load-bearing. The first is that complexity is a budget, not a virtue: Swarm is an excellent orchestrator for small and medium fleets, Kubernetes is the right answer at scale and when you need the ecosystem, and choosing the larger platform than your problem requires is a cost you will pay every day thereafter. The second is that the loop only reconciles what you actually declared. It will hold your replica count, restart your wedged process and reroute around a dead node — but it cannot infer the resource requests you omitted, the health probe you did not write, or the rollback policy you left at its default. Declare well, and the fleet steers itself.
