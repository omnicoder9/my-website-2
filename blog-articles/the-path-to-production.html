The Path to Production

Between a working commit on a developer’s laptop and a page served securely to a stranger on the far side of the world lies a long, engineered pipeline. This is a field guide to every stage of it — the history, the mechanics, and the decisions that make web software ship fast and stay up.

Stage 00/Preflight

The pipeline is the product

A deploy is a small feat of coordination. A developer edits a few lines, and minutes later those lines are running on machines they will never see, answering requests from people they will never meet, reachable by a name that resolves through a global directory and protected by cryptography negotiated in a single round trip. None of that happens by accident. It is the output of a pipeline — part software, part convention, part hard-won operational discipline — and that pipeline, far more than any individual framework, is what separates teams that ship confidently from teams that ship in fear.

This guide is a tour of that pipeline, stage by stage: where code lives and how change is tracked; how environments are separated and configured; how automation moves a commit through building, testing, and release; how software is packaged into containers and scheduled across fleets; how requests are received, routed, and served; where that all runs, from rented virtual machines to functions that live at the network's edge; and finally how a human-friendly name and a padlock icon connect a stranger's browser to your code. Before walking the path, though, it is worth being precise about the word stamped on the whole endeavour.

What “DevOps” actually names

DevOps is not a job title, a tool, or a team you can buy. It is a way of organising the flow of work from idea to running software, built on the premise that the people who write a system and the people who operate it should not be separated by a wall. The term itself is almost accidental: in 2009, the Belgian consultant Patrick Debois, exasperated by the gulf between development and operations, organised a small event in Ghent he called “DevOpsDays.” The conference ended; the hashtag did not. [1] The intellectual spark, though, predates the label. At the 2009 Velocity conference, John Allspaw and Paul Hammond delivered a now-legendary talk, “10+ Deploys per Day: Dev and Ops Cooperation at Flickr,” arguing that deploying many times a day was not recklessness but its opposite — a way to make each change small, boring, and safe. [2]

The movement soon acquired its canonical texts. The Phoenix Project (2013) smuggled the theory of constraints into a business novel about an IT manager dragging a doomed initiative back from the brink, and made “flow” and “work in progress” part of the operational vocabulary. [3] Three years later, The DevOps Handbook turned the parable into a manual, codifying the practices — deployment pipelines, telemetry, blameless retrospectives — that the rest of this guide takes as given. [4]

The four numbers that matter

For years, the case for DevOps rested on anecdote and enthusiasm. That changed with Accelerate (2018), in which Nicole Forsgren, Jez Humble, and Gene Kim distilled several years of the global State of DevOps survey into a statistically defensible claim: a small set of delivery metrics predicts software-team performance, and that performance in turn predicts commercial and organisational outcomes. [5] The research program — DevOps Research and Assessment, or DORA — settled on four key measures that have since become an industry lingua franca. [6]

Deployment frequency

How often an organisation successfully ships to production. Elite teams deploy on demand, many times a day.

Lead time for changes

How long it takes to get a commit from “merged” to “running in production.” Measured in minutes to hours, not weeks.

Change failure rate

The share of deployments that cause a degradation requiring remediation — a rollback, hotfix, or patch.

Time to restore service

How quickly the team recovers when a change does break something in production.

The observation that made these metrics famous is structural. The first two measure velocity — throughput and speed. The last two measure stability — quality and resilience. Conventional wisdom held that these were in tension: go faster and you break more; stabilise and you slow down. The data said otherwise. High performers score well on both axes at once, because the same practices that make deployment fast — small batches, heavy automation, fast feedback — are the practices that make it safe. Speed without stability is merely faster breakage; stability without speed is a museum piece.

Figure 1. The DORA four keys. Two metrics track velocity, two track stability; the central finding of the research is that elite teams refuse to trade one for the other.

Keep those two axes in mind, because every stage that follows is, in the end, an attempt to move a change quickly without moving it dangerously. That balance is not achieved by heroics. It is achieved by building the change's journey as a system — one that is versioned, reproducible, automated, and observable from end to end.

An application built to be deployed

One more idea belongs in preflight, because it underpins nearly everything downstream. In 2011, Adam Wiggins and his colleagues at the platform company Heroku published The Twelve-Factor App, a compact manifesto for building web software that is meant to be deployed continuously to modern platforms. [7] Its factors read, in hindsight, like a preview of this entire guide: keep exactly one codebase tracked in version control; declare dependencies explicitly; store configuration in the environment rather than in code; treat backing services as attached resources; strictly separate the build, release, and run stages; run the app as one or more stateless processes; and keep development, staging, and production as similar as possible.

Modern deployment is the art of moving a change fast enough to learn from it and safely enough to survive it.

None of these principles is exotic today, which is precisely the point: they have been absorbed so completely into how the industry builds and ships that they now feel like common sense. The rest of this article traces a single change — a handful of edited lines on one engineer's laptop — along the path those principles define, from the moment it is committed to the moment it is served, encrypted, to someone on the other side of the planet.

Stage 01/Version control

Where the change begins

Every deployment starts as a diff. Before a change can be tested, built, or shipped, it has to be recorded — captured as a precise, attributable, reversible unit of history. That recording is the job of a version control system, and for the modern web there is effectively one answer: Git. Understanding why Git looks the way it does, and how teams organise their work inside it, is the foundation for everything downstream, because the pipeline's first trigger is almost always a commit or a merge.

A tool born from a crisis

Git exists because of a dispute over a different tool. From 2002, the Linux kernel — a project with thousands of contributors and a famously high rate of change — had been managed with BitKeeper, a proprietary distributed version control system offered to the kernel community free of charge. That arrangement collapsed in 2005 when the free licence was withdrawn amid friction over efforts to reverse-engineer the protocol. [8] Linus Torvalds faced an unappealing choice: return to trading patches as email attachments at kernel scale, or adopt an existing tool none of which met his requirements for speed, distributed operation, and strong integrity guarantees. He chose to write his own.

The pace was startling. Torvalds began work on 3 April 2005, announced the project on 6 April, and had Git hosting its own source code by 7 April — the tool became self-hosting in a matter of days. [9] By June it was managing the release of the 2.6.12 kernel. Junio Hamano, who remains the project's long-serving maintainer, took over shortly after, and version 1.0 arrived that December. [10] Two decades later, Git is the substrate beneath essentially the entire software industry — and, notably, the same 2005 rupture also gave rise to Mercurial, begun within days of Git for the very same reason.

The model: a graph of snapshots, addressed by content

Git's design is what makes it fast and trustworthy, and it is worth internalising because it explains the mechanics of everything a team does with it. At its heart Git is a small content-addressed object store. Every piece of data — the contents of a file (a blob), a directory listing (a tree), a commit — is named by a cryptographic hash of its own contents. Change a byte, and you get a different object with a different name. A commit records a full snapshot of the tree plus metadata and a pointer to its parent commit (or parents, for a merge), so the project's history forms a directed acyclic graph. [11] Because objects are addressed by content, integrity is intrinsic: the hash of a commit transitively covers everything it depends on, so a corrupted or tampered history cannot go unnoticed.

Two consequences follow directly. First, a branch is not a heavyweight construct; it is merely a movable label pointing at one commit. Creating a branch writes a few dozen bytes, which is why Git encourages branching liberally where older systems discouraged it. Second, Git is genuinely distributed: every clone contains the entire history, so committing, branching, viewing history, and diffing are all local operations that need no server. The network is involved only when you deliberately synchronise with a remote. That property — full history everywhere — is precisely what makes the hosted collaboration model that follows possible.

Why it matters for deployment

Because every commit is an immutable, content-addressed snapshot, a deployment can be pinned to an exact SHA. “What is running in production?” has a precise answer — a 40-character identifier that reproduces the exact tree of source that was built. Rollbacks, bisecting a regression, and auditing a release all rest on this guarantee.

How teams organise: branching strategies

Git imposes no workflow, which means teams must choose one. The choice is consequential: it shapes how often code integrates, how long changes live in isolation, and therefore how directly it feeds the velocity and stability metrics from preflight. Three patterns dominate.

GitFlow, described by Vincent Driessen in 2010, was the model that taught a generation how to think about branches. It maintains two long-lived branches — main for released code and develop for integration — and layers dedicated feature, release, and hotfix branches on top, each with rules about where it starts and where it merges. It is well suited to software with explicit, versioned releases shipped on a schedule. Tellingly, in 2020 Driessen appended a note of reflection to the original article, cautioning that teams delivering web applications continuously are usually better served by something simpler. [12]

Trunk-based development is that simpler thing, and it is what most high-throughput web teams actually practise. Developers integrate into a single shared trunk constantly, working in branches that live for hours rather than weeks, and hide incomplete work behind feature flags rather than in long-lived branches. The DORA research repeatedly identifies trunk-based development — few active branches, short-lived branches, and no long-lived “code freeze” branches — as a practice correlated with elite delivery performance. [13] GitHub Flow occupies a pragmatic middle ground: branch off main, open a pull request, get review and automated checks, then merge and deploy.

Figure 2. Two philosophies of branching. GitFlow isolates work on long-lived branches for scheduled releases; trunk-based development keeps a single integrated trunk and hides unfinished work behind flags. The longer a branch lives, the more painful its eventual merge.

Hosting: version control as a collaboration platform

Git is a protocol and a command-line tool, but most teams experience it through a hosting platform. GitHub, launched in 2008, made Git approachable and social and is now the default home of open-source software; GitLab followed in 2011 with an emphasis on an integrated, single-application toolchain; and Atlassian's Bitbucket rounded out the trio for teams already living in the Atlassian ecosystem. [14][15] What these platforms added on top of Git is the machinery of collaboration and governance: the pull request (or merge request) as the reviewable unit of change, protected branches that forbid direct pushes to main, required approvals, and status checks that must pass before a merge is allowed.

That last capability is the hinge between this stage and the next. Once a platform can require that automated checks pass before code integrates, version control stops being a passive archive and becomes the trigger for automation. A merge into main is no longer just a record — it is an event that sets a pipeline in motion. That pipeline is the subject of Stage 03, but it needs somewhere consistent to run, which is why we first have to talk about environments.

Stage 02/Environments & config

The same code, three realities

A change that works on a laptop has proven almost nothing. The laptop has a different database, different data, different secrets, different traffic, and a human watching it. Production has none of those comforts. Bridging that gap safely requires deliberately constructed environments — and a discipline about configuration that keeps the code identical across them while the context varies. This is where the Twelve-Factor principles stop being abstract and start being operational.

Dev, staging, production — and the ephemeral

The classic progression is three tiers. Development is where code is written and first run, optimised for fast iteration and rich debugging. Staging is a rehearsal environment meant to mirror production as closely as budget allows — the same runtime, the same kind of database, realistic (often anonymised) data — so that integration problems surface before customers meet them. Production is the real thing: real users, real data, real consequences. A change earns its way from left to right, gaining confidence at each step.

Modern platforms have added a fourth category that has quietly reshaped the workflow: the ephemeral or preview environment. Spun up automatically for each pull request and torn down on merge, it lets reviewers click through the actual running change rather than imagining it from a diff. Ephemeral environments are only feasible because of the containerisation and infrastructure-as-code techniques covered later — they are a downstream dividend of treating infrastructure as reproducible.

The cardinal rule: configuration lives in the environment

The single most important discipline here is the third Twelve-Factor principle: strict separation of configuration from code. [7] Anything that differs between deployments — database URLs, credentials, third-party API keys, feature toggles, hostnames — must live outside the codebase, injected at runtime through environment variables. The litmus test the manifesto proposes is simple and unforgiving: could you open-source the repository this instant without leaking any secret? If not, configuration has leaked into code.

In practice this means the application reads its settings from the process environment, and each environment supplies its own values. During development those values are commonly kept in a local .env file that is deliberately excluded from version control; in staging and production they are injected by the platform or a dedicated secrets manager. The code that consumes them is identical everywhere.

.envnever committed · injected per environment
# Local development values. The same KEYS exist in staging and
# production, but with different VALUES supplied by the platform.
DATABASE_URL=postgres://app:localpw@localhost:5432/app_dev
REDIS_URL=redis://localhost:6379
STRIPE_SECRET_KEY=sk_test_4eC39Hq…
FEATURE_NEW_CHECKOUT=false
LOG_LEVEL=debug
config.jsread once, fail fast
function required(name) {
  const v = process.env[name];
  if (v === undefined) {
    // Crash at boot, loudly, rather than midnight in production.
    throw new Error(`missing required env var: ${name}`);
  }
  return v;
}

export const config = {
  databaseUrl: required("DATABASE_URL"),
  redisUrl:    required("REDIS_URL"),
  stripeKey:   required("STRIPE_SECRET_KEY"),
  newCheckout: process.env.FEATURE_NEW_CHECKOUT === "true",
  logLevel:    process.env.LOG_LEVEL ?? "info",
};

Two habits distinguish a mature approach here. The first is failing fast: read and validate configuration once at startup, and refuse to boot if something required is missing, so a misconfiguration becomes an immediate, obvious crash rather than a subtle failure discovered by a user hours later. The second is treating secrets as a category apart. A raw .env file is fine for a laptop, but production credentials belong in a dedicated secrets manager — a managed vault that stores values encrypted, controls who and what can read them, rotates them, and records every access. The application still just reads an environment variable; where that value comes from is an operational concern, not a code concern.

Parity, and the build / release / run split

Two further Twelve-Factor ideas anchor this stage. Dev/prod parity holds that the gaps between environments — in time, in personnel, and above all in tooling — should be kept as small as possible, so that “works in staging” is strong evidence for “works in production.” The historically dangerous version of this gap is a substituted backing service: a lightweight database in development standing in for the production engine invites bugs that only appear once it is too late. [7] Containers, in the next stages, are largely a response to exactly this problem.

The build, release, run separation gives the deployment lifecycle its shape. The build stage turns source at a specific commit into an executable artifact. The release stage combines that immutable artifact with a specific set of environment configuration. The run stage executes the release. Because the artifact is built once and then promoted unchanged through environments — only its injected configuration differs — you get a property worth its weight in incident-free nights: the binary that passed testing in staging is byte-for-byte the binary that runs in production. This is the doorstep of immutable infrastructure, the idea that running systems are never modified in place but replaced wholesale by new, versioned artifacts. Nothing enforces that discipline as naturally as the container, which is where the path leads next.

Stage 03/Continuous integration & delivery

The automation that moves the change

Version control captures the change and environments give it somewhere to run, but something has to carry it across the gap — building it, proving it is safe, and delivering it — and to do so reliably it must do so the same way every time. That something is the CI/CD pipeline, the beating heart of modern delivery. It is what turns “a commit was merged” into “the change is live,” and it is the single largest lever on every one of the DORA metrics.

From a good idea to a named practice

The term continuous integration is older than most of the tools that implement it. Grady Booch coined the phrase in his 1991 work on object-oriented design, though he used it to describe an incremental style of development rather than the mechanical, many-times-a-day discipline the words now imply. [16] That sharper meaning arrived with Extreme Programming in the late 1990s. Working on the Chrysler payroll project that became XP's proving ground, Kent Beck and colleagues elevated integration from an occasional, dreaded event into a core practice performed constantly, each integration validated by an automated test suite. [17] The idea was counter-intuitive and correct: integrating tiny changes many times a day is far less painful than integrating large ones rarely, because small merges rarely conflict and small failures are trivial to locate.

Martin Fowler's widely read essay on continuous integration gave the practice its canonical articulation and a memorable core rule — that everyone commits to a shared mainline frequently, and that a broken build is the team's top priority to fix. [18] The tooling followed the practice. CruiseControl, released by ThoughtWorks in 2001, was the first widely used open-source CI server. [16] A few years later Kohsuke Kawaguchi built Hudson at Sun Microsystems; after Oracle's acquisition of Sun, a trademark dispute in 2011 led the community to fork the project and rename it Jenkins, which went on to dominate self-hosted automation for a decade. [19]

CI, CD, and CD: three distinct promises

The acronym conflates three ideas that are worth separating, because teams adopt them in sequence and each raises the bar.

Continuous integration

Developers merge to a shared mainline frequently; every merge triggers an automated build and test run. The goal is a mainline that is always in a known, working state.

Continuous delivery

Every change that passes the pipeline is provably releasable and can be deployed to production at the push of a button. Shipping becomes a business decision, not a technical scramble.

Continuous deployment

The final gate is removed: every change that passes the pipeline deploys to production automatically, with no human in the loop.

The distinction between the two “CD”s is the presence or absence of a human hand on the final lever, and it was crystallised by Jez Humble and David Farley in their 2010 book Continuous Delivery, which took the deployment pipeline from folklore to engineering discipline. [20] Their central argument reframed deployment itself: if releasing is rare and manual, it is inevitably risky, so the cure is to make it frequent and automatic until it becomes routine. Every stage of this guide is, in a sense, an elaboration of that thesis.

Anatomy of a pipeline

A pipeline is a fixed sequence of automated stages that every change must pass, in order, with any failure halting progress. The exact stages vary, but a representative gauntlet for a web service runs roughly as follows: check out the code; build it into an artifact; run fast unit tests; run slower integration tests against real dependencies; scan for security and licence problems; publish the resulting artifact to a registry; and deploy. Each stage is a gate. A red gate stops the line — the change does not advance, the team is notified, and the fix (or a revert) becomes the immediate priority.

Figure 3. A representative deployment pipeline. Every commit runs the identical sequence of gates; a failure at any stage stops promotion and feeds back fast, so defects are caught while the change is small and fresh in the author's mind.

Two properties make this powerful. Because the sequence is identical for every change, the pipeline is a reproducible, auditable definition of what “ready for production” means — encoded, not remembered. And because the gates run early and fast, defects are caught close to the moment they are introduced, when the author still has the context to fix them cheaply. This is the concrete mechanism behind the velocity/stability duality: fast feedback is simultaneously what makes delivery quick and what keeps it safe.

Pipelines as code

The decisive modern shift is that the pipeline itself is defined in a file that lives in the repository, versioned and reviewed alongside the application. GitHub Actions, announced in 2018 and generally available for CI/CD in late 2019, popularised this model for a vast audience by putting the workflow definition directly beside the code it builds. [15] GitLab CI had long offered the same integrated approach through its .gitlab-ci.yml file, and it remains a defining strength of that platform's single-application philosophy. [14] A minimal but realistic workflow looks like this:

.github/workflows/ci.ymlconfig as code
name: ci
on:
  push: { branches: [main] }
  pull_request:

jobs:
  test:
    runs-on: ubuntu-latest
    steps:
      - uses: actions/checkout@v4
      - uses: actions/setup-node@v4
        with: { node-version: 20 }
      - run: npm ci
      - run: npm test              # unit + integration gates

  deploy:
    needs: test                    # only if the gates passed
    if: github.ref == 'refs/heads/main'
    runs-on: ubuntu-latest
    steps:
      - uses: actions/checkout@v4
      - run: ./deploy.sh
        env:
          DEPLOY_TOKEN: ${{ secrets.DEPLOY_TOKEN }}   # injected, never stored in the repo

Notice how the earlier stages reappear here as first-class concepts: a triggering event tied to version control, jobs that depend on one another so that deployment cannot run unless tests pass, and a secret injected from the environment rather than committed. The pipeline is where the abstractions of the previous two stages become executable.

Shipping without flinching: deployment strategies

Reaching the final “deploy” gate raises a last question: how do you replace the running version without dropping requests or, if the new version is bad, taking the whole site down with it? The answer is a set of release strategies designed to bound the blast radius of a bad change — directly targeting the stability metrics of change failure rate and time to restore. [5]

  • Rolling deployment replaces instances of the old version with the new one a few at a time, so capacity is preserved and a failure surfaces before the whole fleet is converted.
  • Blue-green deployment stands up a complete parallel environment (green) running the new version alongside the live one (blue), then switches traffic over in one move — making rollback as simple as switching back.
  • Canary deployment routes a small fraction of real traffic to the new version first, watches its error and latency metrics closely, and only widens the rollout once the canary looks healthy — catching regressions with a handful of affected users rather than all of them.

Each of these depends on being able to run multiple versions of the application side by side, address them independently, and shift traffic between them at will. That, in turn, depends on how the application is packaged and scheduled — which is the domain of containers and orchestration.

Stage 04/Containers & orchestration

Packaging the change, and running it a thousand times

The oldest complaint in software is “it works on my machine.” The gap between a developer's laptop and production — different library versions, missing system packages, a subtly different runtime — has broken more deployments than any bug. Containers close that gap by packaging an application together with its entire userland environment into a single, portable, immutable artifact that runs identically wherever a compatible kernel exists. Orchestration then answers the next question: once you can run one container anywhere, how do you run thousands of them, reliably, across a fleet of machines?

The tool that made containers ordinary

The container era, for most engineers, begins in March 2013, when Solomon Hykes stepped onto a stage at PyCon and gave a five-minute lightning talk titled “The Future of Linux Containers.” [21] He was demonstrating an internal tool from his platform-as-a-service company, dotCloud — a company that would rename itself Docker and pivot entirely to the technology within the year. [22] Docker did not invent containers. What it invented was a way to make them ordinary: a simple command-line interface, a human-readable build recipe, and — most importantly — a standard image format and a registry from which images could be shared as easily as pulling a library. That packaging, sharing, and running of self-contained application images is what turned a niche kernel capability into the default unit of software delivery.

A forty-year-old idea

The primitives Docker assembled were, by 2013, decades old — each a partial answer to the question of how to confine a process. The lineage is worth tracing, because it explains exactly what a container is and is not.

Figure 4. The container was assembled, not invented. Each innovation solved a limitation of the last, from restricting a process's view of the filesystem to limiting the resources it may consume. Docker packaged the result; Kubernetes learned to run it at scale.

It starts with chroot, introduced in Unix in 1979, which could change a process's notion of the filesystem root so it could not see files outside its confined tree. [23] That was isolation of the filesystem only; a confined process could still see and interfere with others. FreeBSD jails, introduced by Poul-Henning Kamp in 2000 in a paper pointedly titled “Confining the omnipotent root,” extended the boundary to processes, users, and networking, creating genuinely separate virtual environments on one kernel. [24] Solaris Zones followed in 2004 with what its own designers described as “chroot on steroids.” The two missing pieces arrived from Linux in 2008: cgroups, contributed by engineers at Google to meter and cap a process group's use of CPU, memory, and I/O, merged into the kernel that year; and, alongside kernel namespaces that partition what a process can see, the LXC project that first combined them into a complete container manager. [23] Notably, Google had by then been running essentially all of its software in containers internally for years — experience that would prove decisive when orchestration became the problem.

So a container is not a small virtual machine. It is an ordinary process on the host, wrapped in two kinds of kernel feature: namespaces, which control what it can see (its own view of processes, network interfaces, filesystem mounts, and hostnames), and cgroups, which control what it can use (how much CPU, memory, and I/O it may consume). Everything else — crucially, the kernel — is shared with the host.

Images, layers, and registries

Docker's second contribution was the image, and its structure mirrors the content-addressed model we met in Git. An image is a stack of read-only layers, each layer the filesystem change produced by one build instruction. Because layers are content-addressed and cached, an unchanged base layer is built and downloaded once and then shared across every image that uses it, which makes builds fast and distribution cheap. A running container is simply an instance of an image with a thin writable layer on top; when it stops, that writable layer is discarded, which is what makes containers disposable and immutable by default. A registry — Docker Hub, or a private equivalent — stores images and distributes them by name and tag, and it is the registry that the CI pipeline of Stage 03 publishes to, and the orchestrator below pulls from.

Containers versus virtual machines

The distinction that most often confuses newcomers is between a container and a virtual machine, and the difference is entirely about what is being virtualised.

Figure 5. A virtual machine virtualises hardware — each guest carries a full operating system and kernel atop a hypervisor. A container virtualises the operating system — all containers share the host kernel, isolated by namespaces and cgroups. The result: megabytes instead of gigabytes, milliseconds instead of minutes.

A virtual machine virtualises the hardware: a hypervisor presents virtual CPUs, memory, and devices, and each VM runs a complete guest operating system with its own kernel. That yields very strong isolation — a compromised guest kernel does not by itself compromise its neighbours — at the cost of size and speed, since every VM carries an entire OS and boots in the tens of seconds to minutes. A container virtualises the operating system instead: it shares the host's single kernel and isolates only the userland. The trade is the mirror image — containers are measured in megabytes, start in milliseconds, and pack far more densely onto a machine, but their isolation is weaker because they share a kernel, which is why sensitive multi-tenant workloads still often reach for VMs (a tension we will meet again at the network's edge). A typical build recipe makes the packaging concrete:

Dockerfilemulti-stage · small runtime image
# --- build stage: has the toolchain, produces the artifact ---
FROM node:20-slim AS build
WORKDIR /app
COPY package*.json ./
RUN npm ci
COPY . .
RUN npm run build

# --- runtime stage: no compilers, no dev deps, smaller attack surface ---
FROM node:20-slim
WORKDIR /app
COPY --from=build /app/dist ./dist
COPY --from=build /app/node_modules ./node_modules
USER node                 # do not run as root
EXPOSE 3000
CMD ["node", "dist/server.js"]

Each instruction is a layer; the multi-stage pattern builds with a full toolchain but ships only the finished artifact and its runtime dependencies, producing a smaller, more secure final image. This single file is the immutable artifact that Stage 02's build/release/run split demanded — built once, promoted unchanged.

When one machine is not enough: orchestration

Running a single container is easy. Running a real service is not: you need many replicas for capacity and redundancy, spread across many machines; you need to restart the ones that crash, reschedule those on failed hardware, roll out new versions without downtime, give the fleet a stable network identity, and scale the whole thing up and down with load. Doing that by hand does not scale past a few machines. This is the orchestration problem, and the industry's answer is Kubernetes.

Kubernetes was conceived at Google in 2013 by Joe Beda, Brendan Burns, and Craig McLuckie, first committed to open source in June 2014, and announced that month; it reached version 1.0 in July 2015. [25] Its codename inside Google was “Project 7” — a Star Trek reference preserved in the seven spokes of its ship's-wheel logo. The project was not built in a vacuum: it was a deliberate, open-source reimagining of Borg, the cluster manager Google had used internally for over a decade, rewritten in Go and informed by the lessons (and mistakes) of Borg and its successor Omega. [26][27] To anchor its neutrality, Google donated Kubernetes as the founding project of the newly formed Cloud Native Computing Foundation in 2015; in 2018 it became the first CNCF project to “graduate,” a marker of maturity and broad adoption. [28]

The core idea: declarative desired state

You do not tell Kubernetes to do things; you tell it what you want. You declare “five replicas of this image, exposed on this port,” and a set of controllers runs continuous reconciliation loops that compare the actual state of the cluster to your declared state and take whatever action closes the gap. If a container dies, the loop notices the shortfall and starts a replacement. This control-loop model — inherited directly from Borg — is what makes the system self-healing, and it is why infrastructure can be described in a file rather than assembled by hand.

A handful of objects carry most of the model. A Pod is the smallest deployable unit — one or more tightly coupled containers that share a network identity and lifecycle. A Deployment manages a set of identical Pods, handling replication and, natively, the rolling updates that Stage 03 described — replacing Pods a few at a time and rolling back automatically if the new ones fail their health checks. A Service gives that shifting set of Pods a single stable address and load-balances across them, so the rest of the system need not track individual containers as they come and go. Blue-green and canary strategies fall out naturally from these primitives: they are just different ways of manipulating Deployments and Services to shift traffic between versions.

A decade on from that PyCon lightning talk, the combination has become the substrate of cloud-native computing: containers as the universal unit of packaging, an orchestrator as the universal way to run them. [29] But a running container still has to receive a request from the outside world — and something has to sit at the front door to accept it.

Stage 05/Web servers & reverse proxies

The front door

A request arriving from the internet does not land on your application code. It lands on a piece of software whose entire job is to accept raw network connections, make sense of the HTTP inside them, and decide what to do next — terminate encryption, serve a file, or hand the request to one of many identical application instances waiting behind it. For two decades that role has been shaped by a single engineering problem and the server written to solve it.

The problem of many connections at once

Through the late 1990s and 2000s, the Apache HTTP Server anchored the web, and it used the model that seemed obvious at the time: dedicate a process or thread to each connection. [30] When a server handled a few hundred short-lived requests, this was perfectly fine and pleasantly simple to reason about. But the web changed. Connections grew more numerous and longer-lived, and a server might need to hold tens of thousands open at once. Dan Kegel gave the challenge its enduring name in 1999: the C10k problem — how to service ten thousand simultaneous connections on a single machine. [31] Under a thread-per-connection model the arithmetic is brutal: each thread consumes memory for its stack whether busy or idle, and the operating system spends an ever-larger fraction of its time merely switching between them. The model does not scale to that regime; it collapses under it. [30]

The answer was to invert the architecture. Igor Sysoev began writing nginx in 2002 while working at the Russian portal Rambler, facing exactly this concurrency wall, and released it publicly in October 2004. [30] Rather than one worker per connection, nginx runs a small, fixed number of worker processes — typically one per CPU core — and each worker drives an event loop that juggles thousands of connections at once. Instead of blocking on a slow client, a worker asks the kernel to notify it when any of its connections is ready for work (through epoll on Linux or kqueue on the BSDs) and spends its time only on connections that actually have data to move. Memory use stays flat and low as concurrency climbs, and idle connections cost almost nothing. That event-driven design is why nginx could hold tens of thousands of connections on hardware that would buckle under a thread-per-connection server — and why, by March 2023, it had overtaken Apache to become the most used server among the world's busiest sites. [32]

From web server to reverse proxy

Here is the twist that matters for modern deployment: in a containerised architecture, nginx usually is not serving your application at all. It sits in front of it, as a reverse proxy — a server that accepts requests from clients and forwards them to one or more backend application instances, then relays the responses back. (A forward proxy sits in front of clients and represents them to the wider internet; a reverse proxy sits in front of servers and represents them to clients.) Placing a fast, event-driven proxy at the front door lets it absorb a whole category of cross-cutting concerns so the application never has to:

  • TLS termination — decrypt inbound HTTPS and encrypt outbound responses, so application code speaks plain HTTP internally and the expensive cryptography happens in one optimised place.
  • Load balancing — distribute requests across a pool of identical application instances, the exact capability that makes the rolling, blue-green, and canary deployments of Stage 03 possible.
  • Static and cached content — serve images, scripts, and stylesheets directly from disk, and cache backend responses, sparing the application from work it need not do.
  • Buffering, compression, and rate limiting — shield slow clients from the backend, compress responses, and throttle abuse at the edge.

A reverse-proxy configuration makes the division of labour concrete: static files are served locally, and everything else is forwarded to a named pool of application servers.

nginx.confTLS termination + load balancing
upstream app {
    server app1.internal:3000;      # the container instances
    server app2.internal:3000;      # from Stage 04
    keepalive 32;
}

server {
    listen 443 ssl http2;
    server_name example.com;

    # certificates issued in Stage 07
    ssl_certificate     /etc/letsencrypt/live/example.com/fullchain.pem;
    ssl_certificate_key /etc/letsencrypt/live/example.com/privkey.pem;

    location /static/ {          # serve assets directly, skip the app
        root /var/www;
        expires 30d;
    }

    location / {                 # everything else -> the app pool
        proxy_pass http://app;
        proxy_set_header Host              $host;
        proxy_set_header X-Forwarded-For   $proxy_add_x_forwarded_for;
        proxy_set_header X-Forwarded-Proto $scheme;
    }
}

With the front door in place, the full shape of the serving path comes into view — every hop a request takes from a browser to your code and back.

Figure 6. The serving path. A name is resolved (Stage 07), the request may be answered or forwarded by an edge/CDN node, a reverse proxy terminates TLS and load-balances across application instances (Stage 04), and only then does application code run — often talking to a database for state. Everything before the app is cross-cutting infrastructure.

That topology raises an obvious question this guide has so far dodged: where do all these machines — the proxy, the pool of application instances, the database — actually live? For most of the web's history the answer was “servers you rack and maintain.” Today it is something far stranger and more powerful.

Stage 06/Cloud & serverless

Where it all runs

The machines in the previous diagram are, increasingly, not machines you own or even think about. Over two decades the industry has climbed a ladder of abstraction, handing off ever more of the undifferentiated work of running infrastructure — racking hardware, patching operating systems, capacity planning — to providers who do it at planetary scale. Each rung trades control for convenience. Knowing where on that ladder to stand for a given workload is one of the defining architectural decisions of modern web engineering.

Figure 7. The abstraction ladder. Climbing upward, the shaded portion — what the provider manages for you — grows, and the surface you must operate shrinks. You give up control and gain leverage; the right rung depends on how much of that control the workload actually needs.

The bottom rungs: from bare metal to rented machines

At the bottom sits bare metal: physical servers you provision, power, cool, and maintain, kernel and all. It offers total control and no abstraction, and for most web teams it has been superseded by the rung above it. That rung is Infrastructure as a Service, and it is what people first meant by “the cloud.” Amazon Web Services opened this era in 2006, launching its S3 storage service in March and the Elastic Compute Cloud — EC2, virtual machines rentable by the hour — in beta that August. [33] The shift was economic as much as technical: capital expenditure on hardware became operating expenditure on usage, and provisioning a server collapsed from a purchasing cycle of weeks into an API call answered in minutes. With IaaS you still manage the operating system upward — patching, configuring, securing it — but the hardware beneath is someone else's problem.

Above IaaS sit the rungs this guide has already climbed. Containers as a service let you hand over a packaged image and a desired-state declaration and let a managed orchestrator run it, so you manage the image and its configuration but not the host. Platform as a Service, the model Heroku pioneered and the one that produced the Twelve-Factor App, goes a step further: you push code, and the platform builds, runs, and scales it, so you manage little more than the application and its dependencies. Each rung erases another category of operational toil.

Serverless: paying for execution, not capacity

The next rung dispenses with the idea of a running server entirely, from the developer's point of view. Serverless, in its Function-as-a-Service form, was brought to the mainstream by AWS Lambda, announced in November 2014 in a post by Amazon's Jeff Barr titled, simply, “AWS Lambda – Run Code in the Cloud.” [34] The model is genuinely different. You upload a function; the provider runs it in response to events — an HTTP request, a file upload, a queue message — provisioning an execution environment on demand, scaling from zero to thousands of concurrent invocations and back to zero automatically, and billing only for the milliseconds your code actually runs. There is no capacity to plan and nothing to pay for when idle.

The name is a misnomer, and a revealing one: there are servers, you simply no longer manage or even perceive them. That abstraction is not free. Because the platform scales instances to zero, the first request to arrive at a cold function must wait while an execution environment is created — the notorious cold start, a latency penalty that ranges from negligible to seconds depending on runtime and configuration. (Under the hood, Lambda isolates each tenant's code in a lightweight virtual machine, a design that prizes strong isolation and pays for it partly in that startup cost. [35]) The other constraints follow from the model: functions must be stateless, since any given invocation may run on a fresh instance; execution time is capped; and the deeper you integrate with a specific provider's event sources and services, the more tightly you are bound to it — the ever-present spectre of vendor lock-in.

Trade-off

Serverless is at its best for spiky, event-driven, embarrassingly parallel work where scaling to zero saves real money and cold starts do not hurt. It is a poor fit for latency-critical hot paths, long-running jobs, or workloads that need to hold state in memory. As always on the ladder, the question is not “which rung is best” but “which rung does this workload actually need.”

The top rung: computing at the edge

The newest rung pushes code not just off your servers but out of the data centre altogether, distributing it to hundreds of locations physically close to users so that requests are answered with minimal network latency. Cloudflare — founded in 2009 and operating a vast global network — brought this model to developers with Cloudflare Workers in 2017. [36][37] What makes Workers architecturally striking is how they achieve isolation, because it directly echoes the container-versus-VM tension from Stage 04. Workers use neither containers nor virtual machines. Instead they run on V8 isolates — the same lightweight sandboxing mechanism that the V8 JavaScript engine uses to separate browser tabs in Chrome. [37]

The consequences are dramatic. An isolate starts in under five milliseconds and carries so little overhead that a single operating-system process can host thousands of them, which means the edge platform can keep a tenant's code warm essentially everywhere at once and the cold-start problem all but disappears. The trade is, once again, the shape of isolation: many isolates share one process, separated by software boundaries the platform calls cordons rather than by a hardware-enforced VM per tenant, a model whose security design is documented in explicit contrast to per-tenant microvirtual-machines and hardened with dedicated mitigations against side-channel attacks such as Spectre. [35] Developers write against the same Service Worker API browsers expose, blurring the line between client and server code. [37] It is the container/VM bargain rebuilt one level up: extraordinary density and speed, in exchange for isolation enforced in software rather than silicon.

Wherever on this ladder a system finally runs — a rented VM, a managed cluster, a function, an edge isolate — it now has an address on the internet. But an IP address is not something a human can use, and a raw connection to it is not something anyone should trust. The last stage of the path makes the service reachable and trustworthy.

Stage 07/DNS, domains & TLS

Reachable, and trustworthy

Your code is running somewhere with an IP address. Two things still stand between it and a user: no human will type that address, and no browser should trust a connection to it that anyone on the path could read. The final stage of the pipeline solves both — a global directory that turns a memorable name into a location, and a cryptographic handshake that turns an open connection into a private, authenticated channel. Together they are what make the padlock appear.

The internet's directory

Humans navigate by names; machines route by numbers. The Domain Name System is the distributed database that translates between them, and it exists because the obvious first solution did not scale. In the early ARPANET, every host's name and address lived in a single file, HOSTS.TXT, maintained by hand at the Stanford Research Institute and copied to every machine on the network. [38] As the network grew, a single centrally edited file became untenable — a bottleneck, a single point of failure, and a source of constant conflict. In 1983 Paul Mockapetris, working at USC's Information Sciences Institute, designed a replacement: a hierarchical, delegated, distributed naming system, specified in a pair of documents later superseded by RFC 1034 and RFC 1035 in 1987, which remain the foundation of DNS today. [39] The reference implementation, BIND, followed from Berkeley and ran much of the early internet. [38]

DNS's genius is delegation. No single server knows every name; instead, authority is partitioned down a hierarchy, each level responsible only for delegating to the next. Resolving a name is a walk down that hierarchy, orchestrated by a recursive resolver on your behalf.

Figure 8. Resolving a name. A stub resolver on your device asks a recursive resolver, which — if it has not cached the answer — walks the hierarchy: the root directs it to the .com servers, which direct it to the domain's authoritative servers, which return the address. Every answer is cached for its TTL, so the full walk is rare.

The data returned lives in records, and a handful do most of the work. An A record maps a name to an IPv4 address, and an AAAA record to an IPv6 address; a CNAME makes one name an alias for another; MX records direct mail; TXT records hold arbitrary text, widely used to prove domain ownership; and NS records perform the delegation that makes the whole hierarchy work. Governing all of them is the TTL — the time-to-live that tells resolvers how long they may cache an answer, and therefore the lever you plan around when changing where a name points. In practice, connecting a domain to the infrastructure of the previous stages is exactly this: point an A or AAAA record at your server's address, or a CNAME at your platform's hostname, and wait for caches to expire.

example.com · zone recordsname → location
; name              TTL   type   value
example.com.        3600  A      203.0.113.10
www.example.com.    3600  CNAME  example.com.
example.com.        3600  AAAA   2001:db8::10
example.com.        3600  MX  10 mail.example.com.
example.com.        3600  TXT    "v=spf1 include:_spf.example.net ~all"

From an open pipe to a private channel

Resolving a name gets a browser to your server; it does nothing to protect what passes between them. A plain HTTP connection is plaintext, readable and modifiable by anyone on the path — the coffee-shop Wi-Fi, the ISP, any router in between. HTTPS closes that exposure by running HTTP inside Transport Layer Security, which provides three guarantees at once: confidentiality, so eavesdroppers see only ciphertext; integrity, so tampering is detected; and authentication, so the client can be sure it is talking to the real server and not an impostor.

TLS carries its history in its name. It began as SSL, developed by Netscape in the mid-1990s (SSL 2.0 shipped in 1995, SSL 3.0 in 1996); when the IETF took stewardship, the protocol was renamed, and TLS 1.0 arrived as RFC 2246 in 1999, essentially a refined SSL 3.0. [40] A long, cautious evolution followed, through TLS 1.2 in 2008 and culminating in TLS 1.3, published as RFC 8446 in August 2018 after years of design and dozens of drafts. [41][42] TLS 1.3 was less an addition than a purge: it stripped out the legacy cryptography that had caused a decade of vulnerabilities, made forward secrecy mandatory so that a key compromised tomorrow cannot decrypt traffic captured today, and streamlined the negotiation into a single network round trip. [41] By convention this all runs on port 443, the counterpart to plain HTTP's port 80. [40]

Figure 9. The TLS 1.3 handshake. In one round trip, client and server exchange key shares and agree on fresh session keys; the server proves its identity with a certificate. Everything after the server's first response is encrypted, with perfect forward secrecy by default. [42]

Certificates, authorities, and the trust problem

Encryption alone is not enough — you could negotiate a perfectly private channel with an attacker. What closes the gap is authentication, and it rests on the certificate the server presents during the handshake. A TLS certificate binds a public key to a specific domain name, and it is cryptographically signed by a Certificate Authority, an organisation the browser has been configured to trust. When your browser sees a valid certificate for example.com signed by a trusted CA, it has strong evidence that it is really talking to the operator of that domain and not an interloper. This chain of trust — browser trusts CA, CA vouches for domain — is the machinery behind the padlock.

For most of the web's history, obtaining that certificate was a manual, paid, and error-prone chore, which is precisely why so much of the web stayed on unencrypted HTTP for so long. The barrier fell in 2015. The Internet Security Research Group, a nonprofit, launched Let's Encrypt to the public that December: a certificate authority that issues certificates for free, automatically, in a fully machine-driven process. [43] The automation is the essential part. It is powered by the ACME protocol — Automatic Certificate Management Environment, standardised as RFC 8555 in 2019 — which defines how a client proves it controls a domain (typically by placing a token the CA specifies) and then requests, receives, and later renews a certificate with no human involvement. [44] A widely used client, certbot, reduces the entire ritual — the ritual that once took a system administrator an afternoon — to a single command:

shellissue, install, and auto-renew
# prove control of the domain, obtain a cert, and wire it into nginx
$ certbot --nginx -d example.com -d www.example.com

# certbot installs a timer that renews well before the 90-day expiry
$ certbot renew --dry-run

Certificates from Let's Encrypt are deliberately short-lived — ninety days — precisely because renewal is automated: short lifetimes limit the damage a compromised key can do, and automation makes the frequent renewals a non-event. The effect on the web has been profound. An organisation whose explicit mission was to encrypt the entire web has, by any reasonable measure, largely achieved it, moving HTTPS from a costly option for the few to the unremarkable default for everyone. [45] The certificate that certbot installs is the very same fullchain.pem the nginx configuration in Stage 05 pointed at — the last piece clicking into place.

Stage 08/The whole path

The system that ships the system

Step back and the whole path resolves into a single continuous motion. A change is captured as an immutable commit; it inherits its configuration from the environment rather than from code; an automated pipeline builds, tests, and delivers it; it travels as a self-contained container image scheduled across a fleet by an orchestrator; it is fronted by a reverse proxy that terminates encryption and balances load; it runs on some rung of a ladder that reaches from rented machines to functions at the edge; and it becomes reachable through a name resolved by a global directory and trustworthy through a certificate obtained in seconds. Seven stages, one journey — from a few edited lines to a page served, encrypted, to a stranger.

The throughline is that none of this is meant to be done by hand. Each stage replaces a manual, error-prone ritual with something versioned, reproducible, and automated, and the reason is the thesis we started with: the teams that deliver software best are fast and stable at once, and they achieve both not through heroics but by engineering the change's journey as a system. [5] Small batches, heavy automation, and fast feedback are simultaneously what make delivery quick and what make it safe. Every practice in this guide — short-lived branches, config in the environment, pipelines as code, immutable images, declarative orchestration — is an instance of that one idea.

The pipeline is not scaffolding around the product. Increasingly, it is a product in its own right — and it, too, is code.

That last point is where the field is heading. The infrastructure itself — the cluster definitions, the DNS records, the proxy configuration, the pipeline stages — is increasingly expressed as Infrastructure as Code: declarative files, kept in version control, applied by automation, so that an entire environment can be created, reviewed, and reproduced from a repository. Its logical conclusion is GitOps, in which the desired state of production lives in Git and a controller continuously reconciles the running world to match — the same control-loop pattern that Kubernetes brought to containers, now applied to the whole system. [28] The distinction between “the application” and “the infrastructure that runs it” is dissolving; both are becoming code in a repository, moving down the very same pipeline.

The principles that make this coherent are, remarkably, the same ones set down for a platform-era web application back in 2011: one versioned codebase, explicit dependencies, configuration in the environment, a clean separation of build from release from run, stateless processes, and parity between where you develop and where you deploy. [7] Master those, and the specific tools become interchangeable — a different CI system, a different cloud, a different edge runtime, all variations on a path whose shape does not change. Understand the path, and you can deploy anything on it.
