The Control Pyramid

Half a century after a hungover engineer sketched the first programmable logic controller on New Year's Day, the vendors he made possible still run the world's refineries, power grids, water plants, and assembly lines — quietly, proprietarily, and with an installed base no software fashion can easily displace.

I  ·  Origins

Two Revolutions, 1968–1975

The modern operational-technology stack rests on two foundational inventions, both arriving in the United States within a seven-year window and both quietly displacing the racks of relays and pneumatic loop controllers that had run twentieth-century industry up to that point.

The first was the programmable logic controller. On New Year's Day 1968, Dick Morley sketched the parameters of a rugged, memory-mapped digital controller intended to replace the wall-sized cabinets of relays then standard in automotive plants.1 Morley and his colleagues at Bedford Associates — Tom Boissevain, George Schwenk, Mike Green, and Jonas Landau — finished the device later that year and named it the Modicon 084, after the firm's eighty-fourth project. The company itself, short for “Modular Digital Controller,” was incorporated on 24 October 1968.23 General Motors took delivery of its first units in November 1969, and General Electric placed an early order worth roughly a million dollars to rebrand the controllers as its own.2

What made the Modicon different from earlier industrial computers was not raw capability but a deliberate refusal to call itself a computer at all. Morley reportedly threw away any document that used the word.2 The machine was designed to survive a foundry: convection-cooled with massive aluminum fins, sealed against outside air, programmable in a visual ladder language modeled on the relay schematics that maintenance technicians already understood. That cultural decision — making industrial control legible to electricians rather than to programmers — would shape every PLC family for the next half-century.4

The second invention solved a different problem. In 1975, two companies on opposite sides of the Pacific independently announced what came to be called the distributed control system, or DCS. Honeywell released the TDC 2000 in the United States; Yokogawa Electric released CENTUM in Japan.56 Both were aimed at the same problem — the continuous process industries (refining, petrochemicals, pulp and paper, power generation) where centralized minicomputer control had concentrated risk in a single failure point. Functional distribution dispersed both processing and consequence: a fault in one controller localized rather than propagating across the plant.6

The PLC came from the discrete world of stamping presses and conveyor belts. The DCS came from the analog world of distillation columns and steam drums. Fifty years later, the descendants of those two products are still arguing over the territory in between. — A Working Definition

Honeywell's TDC 2000 carried its flag until 1985, when it was supplanted by TDC 3000, which in turn gave way to the Experion family in 1995.7 Yokogawa's CENTUM followed a similar generational arc — CENTUM V in the early 1980s, CENTUM-XL, CENTUM CS, CENTUM CS 3000, and finally CENTUM VP, whose current release stems from 2014.8 Other entrants followed within a year of the original 1975 launches: Bristol's UCS 3000, Valmet, Bailey Network 90, Fischer & Porter, and Fisher Provox among them, before consolidation absorbed most of those names.9

The history that follows — vendors, protocols, mergers, and now the long-promised opening of the platform itself — is essentially the working-out of decisions made in those two windows.

II  ·  The Players

The Cast: Who Builds What

The OT vendor landscape resolves into perhaps a dozen names that matter at scale, organized around three concentric concerns: hardware (controllers, I/O, drives), networking (fieldbus and industrial Ethernet), and software (engineering tools, HMIs, historians, MES). Some firms span all three; a few specialize ruthlessly.

Hardware: the PLC and DCS dynasties

Two firms dominate the global PLC market by a wide margin. Siemens, headquartered in Munich, is the worldwide leader through its SIMATIC S7 family — LOGO! for micro applications, S7-1200 for machine-level work, and S7-1500 for high-end systems supporting up to 32,000 digital I/O via distributed ET 200 racks.10 Siemens dominates Europe, the Middle East, and most of Asia.11

Rockwell Automation, headquartered in Milwaukee, holds the inverse position. Its Allen-Bradley division is reported to command roughly half of the installed PLC and process-automation-controller base in the United States, with North American market share commonly estimated at 35–40 percent.1210 The Logix family — ControlLogix at the top, CompactLogix in the middle, Micro800 and the legacy MicroLogix/SLC 500 at the bottom — is programmed in Studio 5000 and networked over EtherNet/IP. Allen-Bradley's grip is strongest in U.S. automotive, food and beverage, water, and pharmaceutical plants.11

Below the two giants sits a tier of regional and specialty leaders. Schneider Electric retains the Modicon brand through the M580 ePAC and a deep installed base in process and infrastructure applications.13 Mitsubishi Electric (MELSEC iQ-R, FX5) and Omron (NJ/NX, CJ/CP) hold the Japanese discrete-manufacturing market and significant share elsewhere in Asia. Beckhoff Automation, an Austro-German firm that introduced PC-based control in 1986, runs everything on industrial PCs through its TwinCAT software runtime — effectively turning a Windows or real-time Linux machine into a PLC, motion controller, and CNC in one process.14 B&R Industrial Automation, also Austrian and similarly PC-centric, was acquired by ABB on 6 July 2017 in what ABB called the closing of its historic gap in machine and factory automation.15

The DCS market is more concentrated still. Six vendors share most of it: Honeywell (Experion PKS), Yokogawa (CENTUM VP), Emerson (DeltaV for hybrid and Ovation for power), ABB (System 800xA, the descendant of the Bailey Net 90 lineage), Siemens (SIMATIC PCS 7 and the newer PCS neo), and Rockwell (PlantPAx, a Logix-based DCS that crossed over from the PLC side in the 2000s).9

Software: the SCADA, HMI, and historian houses

The industrial-software business has been the most aggressively consolidated layer of the stack. The genealogy of one company — AVEVA — tells most of the story.

Wonderware was founded as a partnership in 1987 by Dennis Morin and Phil Huber and incorporated in California the following year. Its InTouch HMI was the first industrial visualization product designed expressly for Microsoft Windows, and it became, in time, the world's most-installed plant-floor HMI.16 Siebe plc acquired Wonderware in 1998; Siebe merged with BTR plc in 1999 to form Invensys; Schneider Electric acquired Invensys in January 2014.16

Meanwhile, AVEVA itself traced its roots to the Computer-Aided Design Centre established in Cambridge, England, in 1967 by the UK Ministry of Technology and Cambridge University — an engineering-design lineage rather than a control lineage.17 In March 2018, Schneider merged its industrial-software business into AVEVA in a reverse takeover, becoming the 60-percent majority shareholder; Wonderware InTouch, Citect, and the rest of the Schneider portfolio came with it.18 In March 2021, AVEVA closed a roughly £3.83 billion (originally announced at $5 billion enterprise value) acquisition of OSIsoft, founded in 1980 by Dr. J. Patrick Kennedy, whose PI System had become the de facto historian in process industries with deployments at more than 20,000 sites.17 Schneider Electric took AVEVA fully private on 18 January 2023.17

That single chain — Wonderware → Siebe → Invensys → Schneider → AVEVA → OSIsoft → Schneider — subsumes most of the legacy HMI and historian market under one parent.

Three notable exceptions remain. Siemens WinCC sits at the center of the SIMATIC stack and is sold both standalone and bundled with PCS 7. Iconics, founded in 1986 and acquired by Mitsubishi Electric in 2019, supplies GENESIS64 in heavy industry and building automation. And Inductive Automation, founded in California in 2003, has executed the only credible insurgency against the established SCADA stack with its Ignition platform — a Java-based, server-centric, unlimited-tag, unlimited-client system sold by single-server license rather than per-runtime, which has become the platform of choice for the unified-namespace and IIoT communities discussed later in this article.

III  ·  The Architectural Split

PLC vs DCS: A Question of Lineage

The distinction between a PLC and a DCS, once architecturally obvious, has eroded almost entirely as a hardware matter and survives mostly as a question of engineering culture and project workflow. It is still worth drawing.

A traditional PLC executes a deterministic scan cycle: read all inputs, solve the user program, write all outputs, repeat. The program is typically expressed in ladder logic, function block diagram, or one of the IEC 61131-3 textual languages, and it is engineered, downloaded, and maintained as a single discrete artifact. PLCs grew up in discrete manufacturing — stamping, packaging, conveyance, machine tools — where the work to be controlled is naturally event-driven and decomposes into independent machines.

A DCS, by contrast, was designed from the start as a system rather than a controller. The engineering workflow assumes hundreds or thousands of regulatory control loops sharing a common database, a common alarm philosophy, a common operator interface, and a redundancy story that extends from sensor wiring through the operator screen. The controller is one component among many; the value proposition is integration, configuration management, and operations engineering at the plant level. DCS systems grew up in continuous process industries — refining, petrochemicals, bulk chemicals, pulp and paper, large-scale power — where a single plant might run unchanged for two decades and a single bad alarm can lose millions of dollars.

The architectures collided in the 1990s and 2000s. As PLCs gained the memory and processing to run hundreds of regulatory loops, vendors began wrapping them in DCS-like engineering environments: Rockwell's PlantPAx is the canonical example, a process DCS built on the Logix PLC platform with FactoryTalk visualization. Coming the other direction, every modern DCS is internally a network of PLC-class controllers running real-time operating systems with function-block scheduling. What still distinguishes them is the configuration workflow, the alarm philosophy, the maturity of process libraries, and an installed base of operators trained in one or the other.

A third category — the programmable automation controller, or PAC — was promoted heavily in the early 2000s as the next step beyond the PLC. In practice the term marketed scope rather than architecture, and the industry has largely absorbed it. Allen-Bradley ControlLogix, Siemens S7-1500, B&R X20, and Beckhoff CX series are all routinely called PACs by their vendors and PLCs by their users. The label has not survived the convergence it once described.

IV  ·  The Wire

Fieldbus to Industrial Ethernet

The protocols that connect controllers to field devices have followed a more interesting evolution than the controllers themselves. The history splits into three eras: proprietary serial buses through the 1980s, an open-but-fragmented fieldbus era from roughly 1990 to 2005, and the industrial-Ethernet era that now dominates.

The first widely deployed open protocol was Modbus, released by Modicon in 1979 as a simple serial master/slave protocol for the company's own PLCs. Its lack of licensing, minimal specification, and use of plain integer register addresses turned it into the lingua franca of small-scale industrial communication. Forty-six years later Modbus TCP — the protocol's adaptation to Ethernet — remains widely deployed because it is cheap, well-understood, and free to implement.19

The fieldbus wars of the 1990s produced a long list of competing standards, each backed by an industry consortium: PROFIBUS-DP and -PA from a Siemens-led group, DeviceNet and ControlNet from Allen-Bradley, Foundation Fieldbus, HART for retrofitting smart instruments onto 4–20 mA loops, AS-Interface for bit-level wiring, and CC-Link from Mitsubishi. The eventual settlement was that no single fieldbus won outright; instead Ethernet largely supplanted them all.

The transition began around 2000 and is now most of the way complete. The leading industrial-Ethernet protocols are essentially the descendants of the fieldbuses they replaced:

Recent market reporting consistently puts PROFINET in the lead by installed nodes and revenue, with EtherNet/IP a close second and EtherCAT third. One 2025 market analysis attributed roughly 24.7 percent of the industrial-Ethernet market to PROFINET, anchored by Europe's installed base, with EtherNet/IP projected to grow fastest in North America.20 Earlier HMS Networks node-counting surveys gave PROFINET and EtherNet/IP each around 18 percent of new installations, with EtherCAT in the low teens.23 The exact figures depend on whether one counts nodes, revenue, or projects, but the ranking is stable.

The longer-term direction of travel is Time-Sensitive Networking (TSN), a set of IEEE 802.1 standards that bring deterministic scheduling to ordinary Ethernet hardware. TSN is platform-agnostic: PROFINET, EtherNet/IP, and OPC UA over TSN are all expected to converge on the same physical and data-link layer, with vendors layering their existing protocol stacks above it. Whether that convergence collapses the protocol-tribe distinctions or merely renders them invisible is one of the open questions of the next decade.

V  ·  The Operator's Glass

SCADA, Historians, and the Software Layer

Above the controller sits the software the humans actually use. The conventional taxonomy distinguishes the HMI (one-machine operator interface), SCADA (supervisory control and data acquisition across many controllers and often many sites), historian (long-term time-series storage), MES (manufacturing execution, connecting plant to ERP), and asset-management software (calibration, maintenance, instrument health).

The category boundaries are softer than the labels suggest. Wonderware InTouch began as an HMI and became, with the System Platform addition, a full SCADA suite. PI System was always a historian but is routinely used as a real-time data hub. Ignition is sold as a SCADA platform but also includes HMI, historian, MES modules, alarm notification, and reporting in a single runtime.

The vendor landscape resolves into roughly five camps:

The AVEVA stack (Schneider Electric)

AVEVA carries the deepest portfolio in the industry, accumulated through three decades of acquisition. Wonderware InTouch and System Platform remain the most-installed HMI/SCADA worldwide. Citect SCADA — an Australian-originated SCADA from the 1980s, now rebadged as AVEVA Plant SCADA — serves heavy industry, mining, and infrastructure. AVEVA PI System is the dominant historian in oil and gas, power, and pharmaceutical manufacturing.24

The Siemens stack

Siemens WinCC is sold both as a standalone SCADA and as the visualization core of SIMATIC PCS 7. WinCC Open Architecture (formerly ETM PVSS, acquired by Siemens in 2007) targets very large applications — particle physics experiments, transit systems, citywide infrastructure — where object counts exceed typical SCADA limits.

The Rockwell stack

FactoryTalk View (HMI/SCADA), FactoryTalk Historian (OEM'd from OSIsoft PI), FactoryTalk Analytics, and the broader FactoryTalk suite are tightly coupled to Logix controllers and assume an EtherNet/IP-based plant. Recent acquisitions — Plex Systems in 2021 for cloud MES, Fiix for asset management — have rounded out the cloud and SaaS portfolio.

The independents

GE Digital sells Proficy iFIX, CIMPLICITY, and Historian, products with deep installed bases in power and water. ICONICS GENESIS64, now part of Mitsubishi Electric, runs heavy in building automation and discrete industry. Trihedral VTScada and Survalent serve water and electric utilities respectively. ClearSCADA (now AVEVA) and Schneider Citect serve the gap between SCADA and small DCS.

The insurgent

Inductive Automation Ignition has, since the mid-2010s, captured significant new-build share through pricing and architecture. The licensing model — one server license, unlimited tags, unlimited clients — is genuinely disruptive in a category where competing vendors traditionally priced per tag, per runtime, per concurrent connection, or some combination of all three. Architecturally, Ignition is a Java application running on standard hardware that uses SQL databases for historians and a web-launched client (Perspective, since 2019) that runs in a browser. It has become, in particular, the platform of choice for the unified-namespace and MQTT communities discussed below.

VI  ·  Interoperability

OPC and the Hunt for a Lingua Franca

Every vendor's controllers speak the vendor's own protocol fluently and other vendors' protocols grudgingly, if at all. The recurring problem of OT for forty years has been how to get a Siemens PLC, an Allen-Bradley PLC, a Honeywell DCS, and a Wonderware HMI to share a single coherent view of the same plant.

The first widely successful answer was OPC, released in 1996 by a consortium that included Microsoft, Rockwell, Siemens, ABB, and Honeywell. The original specifications — OPC Data Access, Alarms & Events, and Historical Data Access — were built on Microsoft's Distributed Component Object Model (DCOM) and required Windows. They worked, in the sense that thousands of vendors implemented OPC servers and the protocol became the universal connector between PLCs and HMI/SCADA. They also locked the industrial-software world to Microsoft Windows for the better part of two decades, and DCOM proved consistently miserable to configure across network and firewall boundaries.25

OPC Unified Architecture (OPC UA), introduced by the OPC Foundation in 2006 and adopted as IEC 62541, replaced DCOM with a platform-independent, service-oriented architecture using its own binary or HTTPS transport and a rich information model that allows servers to publish not just values but the meaning, units, structure, and relationships of those values.2625 A 2017 extension (specification 1.04) added publish/subscribe semantics, allowing OPC UA to operate over MQTT, AMQP, or UDP multicast rather than only the original client/server model.27

OPC UA is now the closest thing the industry has to a universal language. Every major PLC and DCS vendor ships an OPC UA server on the controller itself, and dozens of so-called Companion Specifications — standardized information models for specific equipment classes such as injection molding machines, machine tools, robotics, and weighing — have been published. Where it falls short is in the high-determinism domain that real-time motion control requires; OPC UA over TSN is the consortium's answer, but adoption is still partial.

In thirty years the OT industry has converged on roughly one connector for everything — OPC UA — and is now in the middle of a second convergence on roughly one transport for everything: MQTT for loosely-coupled IIoT, TSN-class Ethernet for deterministic control. Neither convergence is complete. — On the state of play
VII  ·  The Security Reckoning

After Stuxnet

For most of OT history, security was synonymous with the air gap — the assumption that control networks were physically isolated from the corporate IT network and from the internet, and that no further hardening was needed. The assumption was already wrong by the mid-2000s, when business networks began routinely tapping plant data for reporting; it became publicly indefensible in 2010.

Stuxnet, discovered in the summer of 2010 but in circulation since at least November 2007, was the first known piece of malware purpose-built to manipulate industrial physical processes. It targeted Siemens SIMATIC WinCC and PCS 7 software and the S7 PLCs they programmed, specifically PLCs configured with PROFIBUS networks driving variable-frequency drives from two specific vendors used at Iran's Natanz uranium-enrichment facility.28 The worm carried four zero-day Windows vulnerabilities, propagated via USB drives to cross the air gap, modified the ladder logic loaded into the S7-417 and S7-315 PLCs that controlled the centrifuges, and fed falsified “normal” sensor data back to the SCADA system so operators saw nothing wrong while the centrifuges spun themselves to destruction. Iran is reported to have replaced approximately a thousand centrifuges as a result.29

Stuxnet did three things to the industry. First, it made plain that ICS-tailored malware was operationally achievable rather than theoretical, and that the path from a corporate-network compromise to a PLC payload could be walked. Second, it dramatized for regulators a class of risk they had been content to leave with plant operators. Third, it inspired a series of successors: Duqu (2011), Flame (2012), Havex (2013), BlackEnergy (used against Ukrainian utilities in 2015), Industroyer/CrashOverride (Ukraine, 2016 — the first malware ever designed specifically to attack electrical grids, leveraging the IEC 60870-5-104 protocol to operate substation circuit breakers), and Triton/Trisis (2017, targeting Schneider Triconex safety instrumented systems at a Saudi petrochemical plant).3031

The vendor response was uneven. Several PLC families have remained substantially insecure on their original communication protocols; researchers at Black Hat Europe in 2022 demonstrated that even after Stuxnet, much of the S7 protocol was protected only by obfuscation that could be bypassed, and Siemens has been pushing customers toward newer firmware revisions with proper TLS-based authentication.32 The broader industry has converged on a defense-in-depth model: the Purdue Reference Architecture and IEC 62443 standards prescribe network segmentation with industrial DMZs between IT and OT, and a small specialty industry — Dragos, Claroty, Nozomi Networks, Tenable OT, Forescout's eyeInspect (formerly SilentDefense) — has emerged to provide passive OT network monitoring and asset inventory without disrupting production.

Stuxnet is also notable for what it did not do: it did not change the underlying installed base. Fifteen years after the original attack, many of the same controller families and protocols remain in production. The lesson the industry took was less “replace the equipment” than “build a network around it that assumes the equipment cannot defend itself.”29

VIII  ·  The Unified Namespace

MQTT, Sparkplug, and the Broker

One of the more consequential architectural shifts of the past decade in OT has been the rise of the publish/subscribe message broker as the central data hub of the plant, displacing the older poll-based, point-to-point SCADA architecture.

The poll-based model has scaling problems that became severe once IIoT promised tens of thousands of edge devices per site. A traditional SCADA polls each device for each tag at each scan rate, holds an exclusive connection to that device, and acts as the single integration point for every consuming application — analytics, MES, ERP, dashboards, mobile clients. Each new consumer multiplies the load on the SCADA and, frequently, on the device.

The publish/subscribe alternative is older than OT by decades. MQTT, developed in 1999 by Andy Stanford-Clark of IBM and Arlen Nipper of what was then Arcom Control Systems, was designed for SCADA-over-satellite in oil and gas remote telemetry — bandwidth-constrained, intermittently-connected, battery-powered field sites. Devices publish messages tagged by topic to a central broker; consumers subscribe to whichever topics they care about; the broker handles fan-out. Devices and consumers do not know about each other, and adding a new consumer adds no load to the device.

The challenge with raw MQTT for industrial use is that the specification deliberately says nothing about topic structure or payload contents, leaving every implementation idiosyncratic. Sparkplug B, a specification developed by Cirrus Link Solutions and now an Eclipse Foundation standard, fills that gap. It defines a standardized topic namespace, a protobuf-based payload format with rich metadata (data types, engineering units, ranges), and birth/death certificates so that consumers always know which devices are present and reporting fresh data.33 Cirrus Link reports that more than 2,000 distinct companies were using MQTT/Sparkplug as of 2024, with the customer base shifting from its original concentration in oil and gas toward manufacturing.34

The architectural concept that has organized this shift is the Unified Namespace (UNS), an idea most clearly articulated by Walker Reynolds: a single, hierarchical, real-time data structure where every piece of plant and enterprise data lives at a well-known canonical location, and where every application both publishes its outputs and reads its inputs from that namespace rather than from other applications directly. Inductive Automation's Ignition has become the platform-of-choice for UNS architectures because its MQTT modules (Transmission, Engine, Distributor) integrate cleanly with Sparkplug B and HiveMQ-class brokers.3334

The pattern is now spreading beyond the early-adopter community: the major SCADA vendors have all shipped MQTT and Sparkplug B clients in the past few years, even where they continue to recommend their own proprietary backbones for high-determinism use.

IX  ·  The Standards Counter-Offensive

Open Process Automation

If the unified-namespace movement is the bottom-up insurgency against vendor lock-in, Open Process Automation (OPA) is its top-down counterpart, driven by the largest end-user in the process industries: ExxonMobil.

The motivating problem is straightforward. A large refinery or petrochemical complex may run dozens of DCS installations from multiple vendor generations, each requiring its own engineering tools, its own training, its own spare parts inventory, and its own migration path when end-of-life arrives. Total cost of ownership over a thirty-year plant lifecycle is dominated not by initial capital but by these compounding integration costs. ExxonMobil concluded, around 2015, that the industry needed an open, interoperable standard analogous to what server hardware had become in IT, where Dell or HPE servers running standardized operating systems could be assembled from components without re-engineering the application above them.35

The Open Process Automation Forum (OPAF), part of the Open Group, published the first version of the Open Process Automation Standard (O-PAS) in 2019 and has iterated through V2.1 and beyond. O-PAS specifies an architecture built around Distributed Control Nodes (DCNs) — small, networked controllers that can run any vendor's control application — connected by an OPA Connectivity Framework based on OPC UA. The execution standard is IEC 61499 (a function-block-based, event-driven standard developed as a successor to IEC 61131-3) running on the Universal Automation Organization's shared runtime, an Schneider-Electric-originated codebase now governed by a vendor-neutral consortium with roughly seventy member organizations as of 2024.36

ExxonMobil's commitment to the standard has been unusual. The company built a testbed lab near Houston in 2019, ran a pilot plant in New Jersey, and contracted Yokogawa as system integrator for a full field trial.37 At the end of 2024 the company deployed an O-PAS-aligned system controlling a resin-finishing plant in Baton Rouge, Louisiana — roughly 100 controllers and 1,000 I/O points — replacing an existing DCS and obsolete PLCs. Through 2025 the system reportedly ran without major issues, validating the standard at production scale for the first time.3836

Saudi Aramco, Shell, Petronas, BASF, Georgia-Pacific, and Dow Chemical are pursuing their own O-PAS testbeds. About half of respondents to a 2024 ARC Advisory Group survey reported expectations of widely deploying virtualized PLC and DCS controllers by 2030, a closely related trend that Siemens (S7-1500V virtual PLC), Schneider (EcoStruxure Automation Expert), and others have begun shipping product for.3835

Whether OPA succeeds at the scale ExxonMobil envisions remains uncertain. The standard is technically credible and now has a production reference. But the installed-base inertia in process industries is measured in decades, and the incumbent vendors have both technical reasons (real performance and reliability advantages of integrated stacks) and commercial reasons (a multi-billion-dollar maintenance business) to slow-walk the transition. The cautious read is that O-PAS will succeed first in new builds and brownfield replacements in oil and gas, then propagate slowly to other process industries over the 2030s.

X  ·  Synthesis

Regional Patterns & the Coming Decade

The vendor map of OT is, more than most software markets, a regional one. Rockwell dominates North America; Siemens dominates Europe, the Middle East, and most of Asia; Mitsubishi and Omron dominate Japan and have strong positions across the rest of Asia; Schneider holds a substantial position in process and infrastructure globally; the smaller European specialists (Beckhoff, B&R, Phoenix Contact, WAGO) have outsized influence in machine-builder OEM applications. These regional concentrations are sustained less by technical superiority than by the cost of switching trained workforces, established engineering libraries, and the partner ecosystems of integrators and distributors who actually deploy the equipment.1211

Five trends seem likely to shape the next decade.

First, IT/OT convergence is now a fait accompli at the network and data layers; the open question is how far it extends into control. MQTT brokers, Kubernetes-managed edge runtimes, time-series databases, and cloud historians have become standard plant-floor infrastructure. Whether the cyclic-scan PLC application itself migrates to virtualized containers depends on how IEC 61499 and software-defined control evolve.

Second, cybersecurity will continue to drive both regulation and consolidation. The IEC 62443 standards are increasingly written into contracts; the European NIS2 directive and the U.S. CIRCIA reporting requirements have given regulators teeth they did not previously have. Specialty OT-security vendors are likely to be absorbed by the incumbents, both for the technology and for the customer relationships.

Third, open standards — OPC UA, Sparkplug B, O-PAS, IEC 61499, TSN — are pushing further into territory previously held by proprietary stacks. The incumbents will continue to support these standards selectively and to extend them with proprietary features where they can. The contested boundary is not whether to support open standards but how much of the value-creating engineering work remains inside the vendor's tools.

Fourth, AI/ML in process control is moving from descriptive analytics (anomaly detection, predictive maintenance) toward prescriptive and closed-loop applications (model-predictive control augmented with learned models, autonomous operations). The major DCS vendors have all announced products in this space; how much of it survives contact with safety cases, regulators, and process licensors remains to be seen.

Fifth, vendor consolidation has not finished. The pattern of the last twenty years — Schneider absorbing Invensys, AVEVA absorbing OSIsoft, Siemens absorbing Mendix and others, Emerson absorbing AspenTech and National Instruments, ABB absorbing B&R, Mitsubishi absorbing Iconics — is unlikely to reverse. The customers who run on this software change their minds slowly, and the strategic value of an installed base measured in tens of thousands of plants is too large for the major industrial conglomerates to leave on the table.

None of this is fast. A refinery commissioned in 1985 may run its original DCS into the 2030s, get a partial modernization, and run another twenty years. Half a century after Dick Morley sketched a controller that could replace a wall of relays, the world he built is still being built. The names on the cabinets — Siemens, Rockwell, Honeywell, Yokogawa, Emerson, ABB, Schneider, Mitsubishi — have shuffled and consolidated, but the basic stack he and his contemporaries laid down is recognizable, durable, and very much alive.
