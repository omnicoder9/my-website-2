I
Part oneFoundations

§ 1 · The premiseWhy community is a discipline, not a hobby

Every credible technology company eventually realises that its product alone is insufficient. The choice that determines whether a database, a framework, an SDK, or an API succeeds is rarely made by procurement; it is made by an engineer who has already tried the thing, asked a question about it on a Friday night, and received a thoughtful answer from someone who was not paid to provide one. Developer Relations — DevRel — is the deliberate practice of producing that experience, at scale, with intention. At its centre is the developer community: a durable network of practitioners bound by shared problems, shared tools, and shared norms.1

The community is not a marketing channel. Jono Bacon, who built the discipline's modern vocabulary across two field-defining books, frames it as "a group of people who form relationships over time around a shared identity, purpose, or set of values", and notes that the operative word is relationships — not eyeballs, not signups, not pageviews.2,3 Communities of practice, in the sociological lineage of Lave and Wenger, are situated environments in which newcomers move from legitimate peripheral participation to full membership through doing real work alongside more experienced practitioners.6,7 The lesson for DevRel is direct: communities are not assembled like audiences; they are cultivated like ecosystems, with the long horizons and patient inputs that ecology implies.

Mary Thengvall's enumeration of the business case — adoption, retention, qualified feedback, organic content, and a hiring funnel that flows in both directions — is now widely cited evidence that developer communities are an economic asset, not a soft good.1 But the asset only materialises if the community is built well. The rest of this essay is concerned with how.

II
Part twoEstablishing the community

§ 2 · The audienceIdentifying who you are actually building for

The most common failure in community building is not poor execution; it is poor specification. Programs are launched for "developers" as if that word denoted a single population. It does not. A senior site-reliability engineer evaluating a streaming platform for a Fortune 500 has almost nothing in common — in motivations, in available time, in preferred channels — with a third-year computer science student looking for a side project to put on a résumé. Building one community for both produces a forum that serves neither.

Audience identification is therefore the first deliverable. Amy Jo Kim, whose Community Building on the Web remains the canonical text on the discipline's first decade, recommends a written purpose statement that names, with precision, the people the community exists for and the problem it helps them solve.9 The statement is then translated into segmentation that is empirically grounded — through interviews, telemetry from existing product usage, and structured listening on the platforms where the prospective audience already congregates.8

Four lenses for segmentation

A workable audience model usually combines four dimensions, none of which are sufficient alone:

Role & seniority
Backend engineer, ML researcher, platform lead, indie hacker, hobbyist, student. Each has distinct review cycles, vocabulary, and tolerance for incompleteness in tooling.
Domain & vertical
Fintech, healthcare, games, robotics, scientific computing. Domain shapes compliance posture, latency requirements, and the kinds of war stories that resonate.
Lifecycle stage
Evaluating, learning, integrating, scaling, evangelising. Useful because intervention type — a tutorial vs. a deep-dive vs. an architectural review — must match the stage.
Motivational driver
Career growth, technical curiosity, problem at work, paid contract, identity affiliation. The driver predicts which incentives — recognition, learning, money, belonging — will actually move behaviour.4

Once segments are drawn, the community team narrows aggressively. Bacon's repeated counsel is to start small and concentric: pick the segment for which you can produce disproportionate value, build deeply for that group first, and let adjacent segments be drawn in by gravity rather than recruitment.3 A community of two hundred fiercely engaged practitioners outperforms a directory of twenty thousand strangers on every metric that matters.15

§ 3 · Platform selectionChoosing the rooms in which the community lives

Platform is not a neutral container; it is a set of affordances and constraints that shapes which interactions are easy, which are awkward, and which become impossible. Choosing a platform without first defining the desired interaction patterns is the second most common failure in community building. Preece, in her foundational work on sociability and usability, observes that software design implicitly encodes social design — what the tool permits, the community will produce.8

There is no universal answer; there is, however, a serviceable taxonomy. Real-time chat (Discord, Slack) optimises for synchronous presence and fast iteration but is hostile to durable knowledge.15 Forums and Q&A platforms (Discourse, Stack Overflow, GitHub Discussions) trade immediacy for searchability and longevity. Issue trackers (GitHub Issues, GitLab) are where contributors actually do work and where governance becomes visible. Long-form platforms (mailing lists, dev blogs, RSS) carry the deep institutional memory that the most senior practitioners still rely on, particularly in infrastructure and standards communities.25

The healthiest communities are rarely on a single platform; they are multi-modal, with deliberate division of labour. A common modern stack pairs Discord for synchronous community-feel, Discourse or GitHub Discussions for durable knowledge, GitHub Issues for contribution, and a careful presence on the platforms the team does not own (Reddit, Hacker News, Stack Overflow). The discipline is to choose platforms based on interaction needs first — and only then on where the audience already is.3

§ 4 · Initial outreachThe first hundred members, and how to find them

An empty community is a dead community. Kraut and Resnick's evidence-based synthesis of online community research is unsparing on this point: the visible behaviour of the first cohort sets the norms that subsequent members will imitate.4 Outreach is therefore not a numbers exercise; it is a casting exercise.

The first hundred should be hand-picked. Practitioners who are already discussing the problem you solve — on Twitter/X, in adjacent forums, in conference Q&A — are warmer prospects than anyone on a marketing list. Bacon recommends a "one hundred conversations" opening phase: direct outreach, no automation, every message specific to its recipient, and a clear ask that is not "join our community" but "can you help me with X" — where X is the kind of problem the community is being designed around.2 The asymmetry of asking, rather than inviting, is what distinguishes outreach from spam.

01
Listen

Four to six weeks of pure observation across the platforms the audience already inhabits. Map the topics, the vocabulary, the existing leaders.

02
Identify

Build a short list of 100–300 individuals whose work or commentary intersects the community's purpose. Quality, not volume.

03
Reach

Hand-written, individually researched outreach. Reference their specific work. Ask for input on a real decision the team is making.

04
Seed

Convert response into a structured first conversation inside the community space — a question, an open RFC, a debugging session.

05
Compound

Make each early member visibly welcome. Their behaviour is the norm-setting signal for everyone who follows.4

Initial outreach is also where the community team earns its right to govern. Founders who write the first hundred posts themselves — answering, summarising, citing, thanking — are setting an example of the labour they will later expect from others. Communities that begin with founders absent become communities that members assume have no stewards, and self-organise into something that may or may not align with the original intent.9

A community without explicit norms still has norms. They are simply the ones produced by whoever speaks first, loudest, and least carefully. — Adapted from Kraut & Resnick (2012)4
III
Part threeNorms & governance

§ 5 · GuidelinesCommunity guidelines as the cornerstone

Community guidelines are the cornerstone of a thriving developer community. They are the document — short, public, and binding on staff and members alike — that articulates how participants are expected to treat each other, what the space is for, and what it is emphatically not for. Mozilla's Community Participation Guidelines, Apache's Apache Way, and the CNCF's community guidelines are the most widely-referenced exemplars in technical communities, each updated repeatedly over more than a decade of practice.11,12,25

A useful guideline document does four things, in roughly this order. First, it states the purpose of the community — what members are here to do together. Second, it states the values that govern how they do it: typical entries include respect, curiosity, generosity, candour, and the assumption of good faith. Third, it gives concrete examples of behaviour that exemplifies the values and of behaviour that violates them; abstract principles without specific examples are interpreted to taste, and the taste of the most aggressive participant tends to win.4 Fourth, it identifies who is responsible for enforcement and how members can reach them.

What separates guidelines from a code of conduct

The distinction is sometimes treated as cosmetic; it is not. Community guidelines are the broader cultural document — purpose, values, expected behaviours, how to participate well. A code of conduct is the narrower legal-cultural instrument that defines unacceptable conduct, the consequences of that conduct, and the process by which incidents are reported and adjudicated.10 A community needs both: the guidelines set the ceiling of what good participation looks like; the code sets the floor below which participation is not permitted.

§ 6 · The codeWriting a code of conduct that actually works

Writing a code of conduct for a developer community is the act of setting clear, public rules that promote a safe, inclusive, and respectful environment for all participants. The most widely adopted starting point in open source is the Contributor Covenant, now at version 2.1, which has been adopted by tens of thousands of projects including the Linux kernel, Kubernetes, and most of the CNCF graduated projects.10 Its widespread adoption is itself a feature: members arriving from other communities recognise the structure, which lowers the cost of compliance.

A workable code of conduct has, at minimum, the following components:

Scope statement
Where the code applies — official channels, official events, and conduct outside those spaces when it affects the community. Be explicit; ambiguity is exploited.
Standards
A bulleted list of behaviours that contribute to a positive environment, and a parallel list of behaviours that constitute unacceptable conduct. Both lists should be specific enough to be testable.10
Enforcement responsibilities
Who is empowered to act, and what range of actions they may take — from private warning, to temporary suspension, to permanent removal.
Reporting mechanism
A specific, monitored, low-friction channel for reports — typically a dedicated email address or web form, never a public channel. Anonymity options where safety requires.11
Confidentiality & protection
A commitment that reporters will be protected from retaliation, and that incident details are held in confidence by the enforcement team.
Attribution & versioning
Provenance of any adapted text, a version number, and the date of last revision. Public revision history demonstrates that the document is alive, not ornamental.

The hardest part of writing a code of conduct is not the document; it is the prior agreement among the founding team that they will enforce it consistently — including against high-status members, including against themselves. Communities die not from the absence of a code but from a code that exists only on paper.2 The published code should not contain a single sentence the team is unwilling to act on.

A practitioner's checklist

Before publishing a code of conduct

  • The drafting team has read the Contributor Covenant, Mozilla CPG, and at least one other reference text in full.10,11
  • Examples of unacceptable behaviour are specific to the actual community, not boilerplate.
  • The reporting channel exists, is monitored, and has a documented response-time commitment.
  • At least two enforcement officers are designated; no single point of failure.
  • An explicit escalation path exists for incidents involving the team itself.
  • The document is dated, versioned, and stored under public revision control.

§ 7 · Rules & policiesThe operational layer beneath the principles

Beneath the guidelines and the code lies a layer of specific operational rules — the policies that translate principles into reproducible practice. These rules are easier to change than the foundational documents and are typically iterated more often. Common policy areas in developer communities include:

  • Self-promotion policy. What members may post about their own work, where, and how often. Most healthy communities permit it in a designated channel or under a tagged convention, and prohibit it elsewhere.9
  • Recruiting policy. Whether and how recruiters may post. Banning recruiting outright is common; channelling it into a dedicated, opt-in forum is sometimes more honest and more useful.
  • AI-generated content policy. A live frontier: whether AI-generated answers are permitted in Q&A, whether they must be disclosed, and how moderators handle low-quality machine-generated noise. Stack Overflow's evolving policy is a useful reference.14
  • Privacy & data handling. What member data is collected, retained, and visible to whom. Particularly important for communities running their own infrastructure.
  • Channel-specific norms. What belongs in #general vs. #help vs. #showcase; how questions should be formatted; expected response times.
  • Voting, governance, and decision-making. In communities that produce work product — open source projects, standards bodies — how decisions are made, who has commit rights, how disputes are resolved. The Apache Way is the canonical reference for consensus-based decision-making in open source.25

Policies are most effective when they are visible at the moment of relevance: pinned in the channel they govern, surfaced in the new-member onboarding flow, and linked from the bot message that fires when a new question is asked. Documentation that members must hunt for is documentation that does not exist.

IV
Part fourManaging the community

Community management is the critical and ongoing work of nurturing a thriving ecosystem of developers, users, and stakeholders. It is the part of DevRel that has no dramatic launch moment and no decisive completion — only daily practice. Four sub-disciplines together constitute the bulk of the work: moderation, conflict resolution, encouraging participation, and recognition.15

§ 8 · ModerationHolding the line, with judgment

Moderation is the daily enforcement of community norms. It is exhausting, undervalued, and the single biggest predictor of whether a community feels safe enough for new members to participate. Preece's early synthesis of usability and sociability identified active, visible moderation as one of the strongest correlates of community health across dozens of studies.8

Effective moderation is structured rather than reactive. A serviceable model has three tiers. Tier one is bot-enforced and rule-based — link filtering, anti-spam, format checks, automated welcoming. Tier two is community-led — trusted regulars granted limited moderation privileges, encouraged to act on minor violations (off-topic posts, low-quality content) using their judgment, with a clear escalation path. Tier three is staff-only — anything involving the code of conduct, harassment, doxxing, or potential legal exposure. The boundary between tiers must be explicit, and tier-three matters never delegated.15

The moderation team's response-time commitment is more important than the severity of its sanctions. A community in which a flagged comment is reviewed within an hour produces dramatically different member behaviour than one in which the same comment sits for a week. Speed is a form of care.26

§ 9 · Conflict resolutionDisagreement is a feature; harm is not

Healthy technical communities argue a great deal. Disagreement over architectural decisions, language design, performance trade-offs, and the relative merits of two libraries is the substance of the work. The community manager's job is not to suppress disagreement but to keep it within bounds — substantive rather than personal, technical rather than identity-based, and resolvable rather than perpetual.

A useful diagnostic, drawn from group-process research, distinguishes task conflict (about the work) from relationship conflict (about the people). Task conflict, kept within civil bounds, improves decisions. Relationship conflict degrades them, almost without exception.4 Moderators who can identify the transition — usually a single sentence in which an argument moves from "this design is wrong" to "you are an idiot for proposing it" — can intervene before the conversation becomes unsalvageable.

A practical protocol for high-stakes conflicts has roughly the following shape:

  1. Privatise. Move the conversation out of the public channel, by inviting the principals into a thread or DM, before positions harden in front of an audience.
  2. Restate. Have each party articulate the other's position to the other's satisfaction before they restate their own. This single step resolves a surprising share of disputes that turn out to rest on misreading.2
  3. Locate the disagreement. Is it factual (resolvable by data), preferential (resolvable by choice), or values-based (sometimes resolvable only by separation)?
  4. Decide explicitly. If a decision is needed, identify who has the authority to make it and on what timeline. Open-ended deliberation is itself a source of conflict.
  5. Document. Capture the outcome — what was decided, by whom, why — in a place future members can find. Conflicts re-litigated quarterly are a sign that no decision was ever recorded.

§ 10 · ParticipationThe ladder, and how to help members climb it

Jakob Nielsen's much-cited 90-9-1 observation — that in nearly every online community, 90% of members lurk, 9% contribute intermittently, and 1% produce the overwhelming majority of content — is best read not as a complaint but as a starting condition.19 The community manager's task is to lower the activation energy at each step of the ladder, so that more members make the climb.

The participation ladder — six rungs
00

Aware

The member has heard of the community but has not joined. Reduce friction: a single-click join, no application gate for the public space, prominent links from the product and the docs.

01

Lurking

Joined, reading, not yet posting. Most members will stay here forever, and that is fine; the goal at this rung is to make the next step feel low-stakes.

02

First contribution

A first question, reply, reaction, or PR. The single highest-leverage intervention in community management is making sure the first contribution is met with a fast, warm, substantive response. The reverse is fatal.4

03

Regular

Repeated participation across multiple weeks. Cultivate by name recognition, by inviting them into smaller spaces (a weekly call, a beta channel), and by giving them small forms of authorship.

04

Leader

Answers others' questions, organises events, writes posts, holds local meetups. Formalise with a named program — Champions, Heroes, Ambassadors — that confers status and access.3

05

Steward

Trusted by both peers and staff; granted moderation or governance authority. The community's long-term resilience depends on the rate at which it produces stewards.25

Tactically, the highest-yield participation interventions are inexpensive: prompt and personal welcomes for new members; question prompts that are answerable in a sentence; weekly rituals (office hours, demo nights, paper clubs) that produce a reliable cadence; and an explicit handoff when a member's contribution outgrows the channel they posted it in — "this answer deserves a blog post, would you like to write one with us?"2

§ 11 · RecognitionPrograms that confer status without buying it

Developers are notoriously suspicious of marketing-flavoured rewards programs, and rightly so. The communities that have built durable recognition systems — Microsoft MVPs, Google Developer Experts, GitHub Stars, the Docker Captains, AWS Heroes — share a structural property: they confer status among peers rather than transactional rewards, and that status is grounded in observable contribution rather than purchase.1

A recognition program that holds up under scrutiny has, in broad outline, five properties:

1. Earned, not paid
Selection is based on visible contribution to the community, not on commercial relationship. The moment the program admits paying customers, it loses the trust of the people it was designed to attract.
2. Specific, not generic
The criteria are public, observable, and falsifiable. "Outstanding contribution" is not a criterion; "answered at least 25 questions in the last 12 months with an upvote ratio above 0.8" is.
3. Annual or termed
Membership is reviewed each year. A lifetime award becomes a long tail of inactive members whose status no longer signals anything.
4. Conferring access, not swag
The valuable benefits are early access to roadmap, direct engineer relationships, conference speaking slots, beta features — things money cannot buy. Stickers are pleasant but do not motivate sustained labour.3
5. Tied to obligations
Recognition carries expectations — code of conduct adherence, a baseline of activity, agreement to be a public face. Honour without expectations is just a list of names.

Below the formal program lies a layer of informal recognition that is, in aggregate, more important. Public thanks in a release note; a name in a changelog; a quote in a blog post; a "first PR" badge; a member-of-the-month highlight; a personal note from a maintainer after a particularly thoughtful contribution. Cheap individually, decisive collectively. Reichheld's loyalty work, though grounded in customer rather than community contexts, captures the same dynamic: recognition compounds, and the absence of it compounds the other way.20

Recognition is the most under-deployed lever in DevRel. It costs almost nothing, scales with the community's own growth, and changes the calculus by which a developer decides whether to write their next answer here, or somewhere else. — Field observation, after Bacon (2019)3
V
Part fiveGrowing the community

§ 12 · NetworkingThe community as a graph of relationships

A community's growth is not a curve; it is a graph. Each member's value to the community is partly their own contribution and partly the connections they form among other members — connections that, once formed, persist independently of the staff who first introduced them. Mark Granovetter's classic finding on the strength of weak ties applies directly: it is precisely the bridging ties between loosely-connected subgroups, more than the dense ties within tight cliques, that produce the diffusion of new information, new opportunities, and new collaborations.23

Practical interventions for networking inside developer communities tend to fall into three categories. Introductions: the community manager who deliberately connects two members working on adjacent problems performs labour the platform cannot. Convening: small, themed gatherings — a five-person video call on observability tooling, a six-person dinner during a conference — that produce the trust that turns acquaintances into collaborators. Cross-pollination: explicit programs that pair members across regions, companies, or specialities, particularly mentor-mentee pairings that move members up the participation ladder.22

§ 13 · Online communitiesThe rituals that turn a channel into a place

An online community is not the platform on which it lives; it is the set of repeated practices that give the platform meaning. Robert Putnam's work on social capital, drawn from offline contexts, identified the central role of recurring shared activity in producing the sense of belonging that distinguishes a community from a mailing list.21 The principle transfers cleanly online.

The rituals that successful developer communities reliably establish include:

  • Weekly office hours — a recurring, low-stakes video call at a predictable time, hosted by a different staff member or community leader each week.
  • Monthly demos — members showcase what they built, with a five-minute time-box and no slides required.
  • Paper-reading or code-reading groups — particularly in infrastructure, ML, and systems communities, where collective study has become a defining ritual.
  • Annual retrospectives — a public, public-document review of the year's incidents, decisions, and direction. McMillan and Chavis identified shared history as one of the four pillars of a psychological sense of community; written annual retrospectives instantiate it.22
  • Onboarding rituals — a first-question channel, a welcome bot that names new members publicly, a starter-issue label on the repo. The first 48 hours after joining are disproportionately predictive of long-term retention.15

Eghbal's analysis of modern open-source maintenance adds a critical caveat: rituals can become extractive if they impose obligations on maintainers without proportionate support. Communities that grow without expanding the body of stewards eventually consume the founders who built them. The healthiest ritual cadence is the one the team can sustain indefinitely.5

§ 14 · EventsIn-person participation, in the age of remote everything

Despite a decade of confident predictions that remote-first work would end in-person developer events, the empirical pattern is the reverse: post-pandemic, attendance at developer conferences is at or above 2019 levels at most major venues, and the events budget line is once again among the largest in DevRel programs.17 The reason is simple. The trust that produces durable community is formed disproportionately in unstructured time — hallways, dinners, the bar after the talks — and that trust then capitalises the online channels for the following year.

Event participation in a community context has three modalities, each with distinct objectives:

Sponsored / attended
Existing conferences (KubeCon, PyCon, RailsConf, etc.) where the team has a presence but does not own the agenda. Goal: presence, conversations, recruiting at the booth, talks where the criteria allow.
Co-located / satellite
An event the community runs adjacent to a larger conference — a community dinner, a half-day workshop, a hack night. Lower cost, higher intimacy, frequently higher impact per dollar than the main event itself.
Owned conference
A multi-day event the company runs for its community. Expensive, high-risk, high-reward: the canonical signal that a community has reached self-sustaining scale. Premature attempts are almost always painful.
Local meetups
Member-organised, staff-supported, geographically distributed. The most durable form of in-person community, and the layer that produces future leaders most reliably.3

The often-overlooked discipline is post-event integration: the systematic conversion of the relationships and conversations formed in person into durable presence in the online community. A conference at which the team makes hundreds of new acquaintances but does not introduce any of them to each other afterward has produced a stack of business cards and very little community.

§ 15 · CodaThe compounding case for doing this well

The case for building a developer community well, beyond the immediate business arguments, is that the work compounds in a way little else in a software business does. A landing page tested today is irrelevant in two years. A community whose norms were carefully designed in year one is producing meetings, contributions, and answers in year ten — and the team that built it is by then mostly observing, because the members are doing the work.1,3

The architecture of that compounding is not mysterious. It is the deliberate practice of identifying the right audience, choosing platforms that match the interactions, opening with care, writing guidelines and codes that are enforced rather than ornamental, moderating with judgment, resolving conflict before it metastasises, lowering the cost of climbing the participation ladder, recognising what deserves recognition, networking the graph, ritualising the online space, and showing up in person. Done well, it produces what every founder eventually realises they actually wanted: not an audience, but a community of practice that will, on a Friday night, answer a stranger's question about the thing the team made — because that is what people in the community do.
