There is a comfortable fiction that cybersecurity belongs to somebody else — the IT department, the security team, the annual compliance module you click through once a year and forget.

That fiction is now dangerous. The frontier of national security no longer runs along a military border or a corporate firewall. It runs through the smartphone in a delivery driver's pocket and the invoicing software on a local plumber's laptop. When those fall, so can a hospital, a pipeline, or a bank — and the bill lands on all of us.

This is an argument that cybersecurity is a shared civic responsibility, in the same practical sense that public health and civil defense once were; that the market, left alone, will not supply enough of it; and that treating it as universal infrastructure — funded, taught, and incentivized like infrastructure — is not idealism but arithmetic.

Part I — The diagnosis

The perimeter moved. Our habits stayed put.

For thirty years we built taller walls around a shrinking number of things worth walling. Meanwhile the valuable stuff — data, access, trust, uptime — quietly spread out across hundreds of millions of ordinary devices in ordinary hands. The wall is now in the wrong place. To see why that matters, start with the moment the internet first learned it could be turned against itself.

Section 01

The fortress that no longer exists

For most of computing history, security was a problem of walls. You drew a line around the things that mattered — the mainframe, the server room, the corporate network — and you spent your budget guarding that line. Inside was trusted; outside was not. The metaphor was medieval, and mostly it worked, because the valuable things sat in one place and the people who could reach them were few.

That world is gone. The valuable thing is no longer a machine in a room; it is data and access, and both have been scattered across billions of endpoints — the phone that approves a wire transfer, the laptop that files an invoice, the smart thermostat wired into a building's controls, the router humming in a spare bedroom. Each of these is now a piece of the frontier. The perimeter did not disappear; it multiplied until it ran through every home and every small business in the country. And a wall that runs everywhere is not a wall at all.

We have known how this ends since before the web existed. On the night of November 2, 1988, a Cornell graduate student named Robert Tappan Morris released a small self-replicating program onto the early internet, ostensibly to measure its size. A flaw in its logic made it reinfect machines it had already taken, and within twenty-four hours it had ground to a halt an estimated 6,000 of the roughly 60,000 computers then connected — about one in ten.1 Universities went dark. Government labs severed their network links. Administrators pulled all-nighters over machines that kept crashing. One security historian later wrote that November 2 was "the day computer science lost its innocence." The episode produced the first felony conviction under the 1986 Computer Fraud and Abuse Act and spurred the creation of the first coordinated incident-response body, the CERT Coordination Center at Carnegie Mellon.2

The Morris Worm did not steal anything or destroy a single file. Its entire payload was replication. And yet it demonstrated, beyond argument, that a connected society could be disrupted by software alone — that the danger was not any one villain but the structure of the network itself, in which one careless node could degrade the whole. That lesson has aged well. What has changed is the scale.

In its 2024 Internet Crime Report, the FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center tallied more than $16.6 billion in reported losses from over 859,000 complaints — a 33 percent increase from the year before — and cautioned that the true figure is almost certainly higher, because most incidents are never reported.3 Phishing and spoofing topped the complaint list; ransomware remained the most damaging threat to critical infrastructure. This is not the ledger of a niche technical nuisance. It is the ledger of a mass social phenomenon, one that touches retirees and Fortune 500 boards alike, and it is growing faster than the defenses arrayed against it.

Here is the uncomfortable implication. If the frontier now runs through every device, then national resilience is a function not of how well a few institutions defend themselves, but of how well hundreds of millions of people — most of them with no technical training and no security budget — manage the small pieces of the network they happen to hold. We have inherited a fortress mindset for a world that no longer has a fortress. The rest of this bulletin is about what replaces it.

Section 02

The myth of "too small to hack"

Ask the owner of a three-person accounting firm or a family-run machine shop why they haven't invested much in security, and you will usually hear a version of the same reassuring sentence: Why would anyone bother with us? The logic feels sound. Hackers, the reasoning goes, are cunning specialists who study a target, learn its weaknesses, and strike with intent — and no one is going to expend that effort on a business with a single storefront and a modest bank balance.

The reasoning is wrong at its foundation, because it imagines the wrong predator. Modern cybercrime is not a spear-fisher stalking a chosen fish. It is a trawler dragging an enormous automated net across the entire ocean and keeping whatever it catches. Attackers run continuous, machine-speed scans across the whole internet, probing millions of addresses for a known flaw, a default password, an exposed port. Increasingly they pair those scans with generative-AI tools that write fluent, personalized phishing messages by the thousand, stripping away the broken grammar that once gave scams away. The question is not whether you are worth targeting. It is whether you are reachable — and almost everyone is reachable.

The consequences fall hardest on exactly the organizations that assume they are safe. Industry analyses commonly attribute a large share of attacks to small enterprises — one widely cited figure holds that roughly 43 percent of cyberattacks are aimed at small businesses4 — and the structural data tells the same story from another angle. In its 2025 breach analysis, Verizon found that ransomware was present in the substantial majority of breaches at small and mid-sized organizations, far more than at large enterprises, and that stolen credentials were the single most commonly compromised asset.5 Small firms are not incidental collateral; by some measures they are the preferred prey, precisely because they hold real data behind thin defenses.

Attackers are not hand-picking victims. They are trawling with a net — and a small business behind a weak lock is not too small to catch. It is simply easier.

What makes this more than a statistic is what a breach does to a small operation. A ransomware crew does not need your company to be wealthy; it needs your company to be dependent on the systems it just encrypted. For a manufacturer whose machines sit idle, or a clinic that cannot pull up patient records, a demand for $50,000 to restore daily operations is not an abstraction — it is a choice between paying and closing. The math is brutal: business-email-compromise schemes against small firms average tens of thousands of dollars per incident, and a frequently cited industry estimate holds that around 60 percent of small businesses that suffer a serious cyberattack shut their doors within six months.6 The security firm CrowdStrike has argued that the deadliest vulnerability many small businesses carry is not technical at all but psychological — a "false sense of security" born of obscurity, which leads owners to skip the basic precautions that would have protected them.7

The people who defend the country's networks for a living have a blunt name for this population. Jen Easterly, who led the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency from 2021 to 2025, borrowed a phrase coined by the researcher Josh Corman to describe the schools, hospitals, water utilities, and small vendors that attackers love: "target rich, resource poor." They hold data and provide services that matter enormously, yet they lack the staff, money, and expertise to defend themselves the way a bank or a defense contractor can.8 That mismatch — high value, low protection — is the sweet spot of the automated attack economy. And it means the myth of being too small to hack does real damage, because it talks the most exposed people out of the cheap habits that would save them.

Section 03

The supply chain of risk

Suppose you run security for a national bank, a hospital network, or a defense contractor. You have a large team, a serious budget, and layers of technology guarding your core systems. An attacker who wants in faces a hard problem — if they come at you directly. So they rarely do. They come at your vendors instead: the small contractor with a login to your billing portal, the maintenance firm with remote access to your building, the software supplier whose update you trust and install without a second thought. Enterprises can armor themselves; they cannot armor everyone they do business with. And so the frontier moves, once again, to the least-defended node with a key to the castle.

The canonical illustration is still the breach of the retailer Target during the 2013 holiday season. The attackers did not assault Target's servers. They began with a phishing email sent to a small heating and refrigeration contractor in Sharpsburg, Pennsylvania — Fazio Mechanical Services — whose network access to Target existed for the mundane purposes of electronic billing, contract submission, and project management, not, as is sometimes claimed, for remotely controlling store thermostats.9 The phishing message carried a data-stealing trojan; the contractor was reportedly relying on a free consumer anti-malware tool with no real-time protection. With the vendor's harvested credentials, the intruders stepped through Target's vendor portal, moved laterally across a network that had not properly segmented payment systems from everything else, and eventually planted card-skimming malware on point-of-sale terminals in nearly every store.

The toll was staggering: roughly 40 million payment cards compromised and personal information for as many as 70 million customers exposed — some 110 million people affected in all. Target's own intrusion-detection system flagged the malware; the alerts were forwarded and then, fatally, not acted upon. The breach cost the company well over $200 million, and both its chief executive and chief information officer ultimately resigned.10 What began as one click by one employee at a dozen-person contractor became one of the most consequential retail breaches in history. As the security reporting at the time made clear, the phishing that snared the contractor was almost certainly not a bespoke operation but a routine, automated campaign — the trawler's net again — that happened to catch a company with a key to something much larger.11

If Target showed how vendor risk drains a corporation, the 2021 attack on Colonial Pipeline showed how a single unattended credential can seize a piece of the nation itself. Colonial operates the largest refined-fuel pipeline in the United States, carrying gasoline, diesel, and jet fuel from Texas up the Eastern Seaboard and supplying close to 45 percent of the East Coast's fuel.12 On May 7, 2021, the company discovered ransomware on its systems and shut the pipeline down. In testimony afterward, the security firm brought in to investigate explained how the attackers got in: through a single password for a disused virtual private network account that no longer needed to exist. The account was not protected by multi-factor authentication, and the password had likely been exposed in an unrelated breach because an employee had reused it elsewhere.13

One leaked, reused password, on one forgotten account, with no second factor — and half the East Coast's fuel supply went offline. The shutdown triggered panic buying, drained thousands of gas stations, spiked prices, and prompted a federal emergency declaration. The company paid the DarkSide ransomware group a ransom of roughly $4.4 million to accelerate its recovery. None of the sophistication people imagine when they picture a national-infrastructure attack was necessary. The door was simply unlocked.

The pattern is always the same shape. The most capable defender in the chain is breached through the least capable participant in the chain. This is why "our security is fine" is never a complete sentence for any organization that depends on others — which is to say, for every organization. Your risk is the sum of everyone you trust, and everyone they trust, all the way down to a clerk clicking a link.

Section 04

Your toaster is a weapon

So far the harm has flowed in one direction: an insecure device or person becomes a doorway to some victim's data. But there is a second, less intuitive danger, and it is the one that turns cyber hygiene from private prudence into public duty. Your insecurity can be used to hurt other people — strangers you will never meet, on infrastructure you do not use — while you notice nothing at all. The compromised device does not steal from you. It is conscripted.

The clearest demonstration came on October 21, 2016, when large parts of the American internet abruptly went dark. Users on the East Coast could not reach Twitter, Netflix, Reddit, Spotify, GitHub, PayPal, Airbnb, or a long list of other major sites. There was no outage at any of those companies. The target was Dyn, a firm that operated a crucial piece of the internet's addressing system, and it was buried under a flood of junk traffic — a distributed denial-of-service attack — pouring in from an army of hijacked consumer gadgets.14 The malware behind it, called Mirai, did not break into fortified servers. It scanned the internet for internet-connected cameras, home routers, and video recorders, then tried a short list of about sixty factory-default usernames and passwords — admin/admin and its cousins — against each one. That was enough to enslave hundreds of thousands of devices whose owners had never changed the credentials they shipped with.15

Researchers later documented Mirai attacks exceeding a trillion bits per second — at the time, the largest ever recorded — assembled entirely from "small, innocuous" household objects.16 The devices kept working normally for their owners. A security camera still recorded; a router still served Wi-Fi. Meanwhile, in the background, they were firing traffic at a stranger's infrastructure on command. One analyst described Mirai as "weaponized negligence at scale," and the phrase is exact: no single owner did anything malicious, yet the aggregate of their small negligences became a cannon.

A compromised baby monitor is not a private embarrassment. It is a munition in someone else's arsenal, and its owner is the last to know.

This is not a closed chapter of history. Mirai's source code was released publicly, spawning a decade of descendants; in early 2025 a Mirai-derived botnet nicknamed Eleven11bot was found to have commandeered some 86,000 devices — again, mostly cameras and recorders — and to have launched attacks reaching several trillion bits per second, among the largest ever seen from a non-state actor.17 Every insecure smart doorbell, every default-password DVR, every un-updated router is a potential recruit. The population of vulnerable devices grows by the billions each year, and the barrier to renting a botnet built from them has collapsed to a few dollars.

Now hold this next to a much older civic memory. During the Second World War, American cities practiced blackout drills: at the signal, every household drew its curtains and killed its outdoor lights, because a single lit window could help an enemy bomber find the city. No one household's lamp would have doomed anyone. The point was collective — the aggregate glow was the danger, and so switching off your own light became a small, mandatory act of shared defense. A compromised home router is the modern lit window. It endangers not you but the commons, and the only real remedy is for everyone to draw the curtains. We will return to that wartime precedent, because it turns out to be the most useful model we have. But first we have to explain why, if the fix is so cheap, people don't simply do it on their own.

Part II — Why it doesn't fix itself

Insecurity is not a mystery. It is a rational choice.

If the defenses are cheap and the stakes are enormous, why is the country so exposed? The honest answer is not that people are lazy or stupid. It is that the incentives are backwards. For most individuals and firms, under-investing in security is the economically sensible move — and that, not any technical gap, is the deepest problem we have.

Section 05

A market that won't reward locks

There is a seductive belief, common among engineers, that insecurity is fundamentally a technical failure — that with better cryptography, smarter firewalls, and tighter code, the problem would yield. In 2001 the Cambridge computer scientist Ross Anderson published a short, field-defining paper arguing the opposite. Information insecurity, he wrote, "is at least as much due to perverse incentives" as to any technical shortcoming, and the clearest way to understand it is not with the language of computer science but with the language of microeconomics: externalities, asymmetric information, moral hazard, adverse selection, and the tragedy of the commons.18

Anderson's founding example had nothing to do with hacking in the modern sense. Studying fraud against automated teller machines, he noticed that the pattern of losses depended not on the technology, which was similar across countries, but on who was legally liable when a disputed transaction occurred. In the United States, the burden fell on the bank to prove a customer wrong, so American banks had every reason to invest in getting security right. In Britain and several other countries, the burden fell on the customer to prove the bank wrong — nearly impossible — so those banks grew careless, and fraud flourished. Same machines, opposite outcomes, determined entirely by the placement of responsibility. The lesson generalized into a discipline: as Anderson and Tyler Moore later put it in Science, security failures are caused "at least as often by bad incentives as by bad design."19

The single most important of those bad incentives is the externality — a cost your choices impose on other people, which you do not have to pay. The owner of the default-password camera in the last section is the textbook case. Securing that camera costs the owner a little time and attention; the benefit of securing it flows almost entirely to strangers who won't be attacked by the botnet it would otherwise join. The owner bears the full cost and captures almost none of the benefit, so the rational owner does nothing. Anderson's comparison was to pollution: someone who connects an insecure machine to the network is like a factory venting smoke — the harm is real, but it drifts downwind onto everyone, so the polluter has little private reason to stop.

Follow the logic through a company's budget and the picture darkens. A firm will spend to protect assets it owns and losses it will personally absorb. But much of the damage from a breach lands elsewhere — on the customers whose identities are stolen, on the banks that reissue cards, on the other companies infected through a poisoned supply chain, on the public that loses a service. Because the firm does not pay those downstream costs, it rationally buys only enough security to protect itself, not enough to protect the ecosystem. Multiply that calculation across every business and household in the country and you get precisely the world we live in: not a world of villains, but a world of reasonable people each declining to pay for benefits they cannot keep.

This is why exhortation alone fails. Telling people and companies to "take security seriously" is asking them to act against their own economic interest for the good of strangers — an appeal that works, imperfectly, on the civic-minded, and not at all on everyone else. When the private incentive and the public good point in opposite directions, sermons do not close the gap. Something has to change the incentives themselves. To see what, it helps to look at two more specific ways the market breaks.

Section 06

The lemons problem

Even a buyer who genuinely wants security runs into a wall: you usually cannot tell, from the outside, whether a product is secure. Two smart doorbells sit side by side on the shelf at the same price. One receives security patches for years; the other's manufacturer abandoned it at launch and left a hardcoded password in the firmware. Nothing on the box tells you which is which. Confronted with that uncertainty, the rational shopper does the only sensible thing — refuses to pay extra for a quality they cannot verify — and buys on price alone.

The economist George Akerlof described the machinery of this failure in 1970, in a paper about used cars that won him a Nobel Prize. When buyers cannot distinguish good products from bad ones — "peaches" from "lemons" — they will only pay a price that reflects the average. That average price is too low to reward the makers of good products, who exit the market, which lowers the average quality further, which lowers the price buyers will pay, in a downward spiral. Akerlof called the mechanism a market for "lemons," and it is a precise description of the consumer market for connected devices.20 Security is invisible; invisible qualities do not command a premium; unrewarded qualities do not get built. The market does not merely under-supply security — it actively selects against the firms that provide it.

Concept / asymmetric information

The fix for a lemons market is almost never "try harder to be a smart shopper." It is to make the hidden quality visible — through a warranty, a rating, an inspection, a label. Restaurant hygiene grades, crash-test stars, and the nutrition panel all exist to solve exactly this problem: they convert a quality the buyer cannot see into a signal the buyer can act on, which restores the market's ability to reward doing the right thing.

This is the reasoning behind one of the more interesting recent experiments in American cyber policy. In January 2025 the federal government launched the U.S. Cyber Trust Mark, a voluntary labeling program administered by the Federal Communications Commission. Consumer devices that meet a baseline of cybersecurity criteria — drawn from standards developed by the National Institute of Standards and Technology — may carry a distinctive shield logo, accompanied by a scannable QR code linking to plain-language details: whether the device receives automatic security updates, and for how long it will be supported.21 The explicit model is Energy Star, the efficiency label that reshaped the appliance market not by banning inefficient products but by making efficiency legible, and therefore marketable. The Cyber Trust Mark tries to do the same for security — to give the "peaches" a way to announce themselves so that buyers can finally pay for them.22

Other governments have reached for stronger tools against the same failure. The European Union's Cyber Resilience Act, in force since December 2024, does not wait for a label to persuade the market; it imposes mandatory security requirements on virtually every connected product sold in Europe, with the loss of market access as the penalty for non-compliance. The United Kingdom's product-security law took a narrower but pointed step, simply outlawing the universal default passwords that made attacks like Mirai possible in the first place.17 These are different bets — a voluntary American signal versus a mandatory European floor — but they share a diagnosis. Left to itself, the market cannot solve an information problem it cannot see through. It needs an outside hand to make security visible, or to require it outright. Which raises the question of who that outside hand should be, and by what right.

Section 07

The tragedy of the digital commons

In 1968 the ecologist Garrett Hardin published an essay that has framed environmental thinking ever since. Imagine a pasture open to all. Each herder gains the full benefit of adding one more animal to the common ground, but the cost of overgrazing — the degraded pasture — is shared by everyone. So each herder, acting rationally, keeps adding animals, and the shared resource collapses under the weight of individually sensible decisions. Hardin called it the tragedy of the commons, and its central insight is that a resource everyone shares but no one owns tends to be ruined, not by malice, but by the ordinary arithmetic of self-interest.23

The internet is a commons in exactly this sense. Its usefulness depends on a shared substrate of trust and reliability that no single party owns: the assumption that most devices are not hostile, that most traffic is legitimate, that the addressing system will answer honestly. Every insecure device, every reused password, every unpatched server draws down that common stock of trust a little — imposing a diffuse cost on all users while sparing its owner any personal penalty. Each individual act of neglect is trivially rational. The sum is the degraded, fraud-soaked network the FBI's ledger describes. This is the same structure as the externality and the lemons market, seen from a distance: a coordination failure, in which the outcome that is best for each person is ruinous for everyone.

A network is only as trustworthy as its least-tended corner. In a commons, the neglect of the few is quietly subsidized by all.

There is a hopeful mirror image to the tragedy, and it comes from epidemiology. A population that reaches a high enough level of vaccination achieves herd immunity: the pathogen can no longer find enough susceptible hosts to spread, and even the unvaccinated are protected by the collective. Digital hygiene works along the same logic. When enough devices are patched, enough accounts are protected by a second factor, and enough people pause before clicking, an attacker's automated campaign runs out of easy hosts. Phishing that fools one household in three is a business; phishing that fools one in fifty is not. The returns to widespread basic hygiene are not linear — past a threshold, whole categories of attack become unprofitable and simply recede. The catch, and it is the same catch as vaccination, is that the benefit is collective while the effort is individual, so it does not happen on its own. It has to be organized.

That word — organized — is the hinge of this entire argument. The economics of the last three sections converge on a single, well-worn conclusion. When individually rational choices reliably produce a collectively catastrophic outcome, and when the market cannot see or price the harm, you are looking at the textbook justification for public action. This is not a novel or ideological claim; it is the same reasoning that gave us clean-air rules, food-safety inspection, seat-belt mandates, and vaccination campaigns. Cybersecurity has all the hallmarks of a public-goods problem, and public-goods problems are solved — when they are solved at all — by coordinating people who would not, and often could not, coordinate themselves. The remaining question is what that coordination should look like. And for that, it helps to remember that this country has organized shared civilian defense before, and knows how.

Part III — The civic frame

We have mobilized ordinary people for shared defense before.

The idea that everyday habits could add up to national security is not new, and it is not naive. Twice in living memory — in wartime civil defense and in modern public health — the United States turned diffuse individual behavior into collective protection. Both offer a working template. Both also reveal what it actually takes.

Section 08

Draw the blackout curtains

In May 1941, months before Pearl Harbor, President Franklin Roosevelt established the Office of Civilian Defense to prepare the home front for a war that had not yet reached American soil. What followed was one of the largest deliberate mobilizations of ordinary civilian behavior in the nation's history. By early 1943, roughly six million Americans had volunteered in public-protection roles — air-raid wardens, plane spotters, auxiliary police and firefighters — organized down to the level of individual city blocks.24 The federal government coordinated the effort; the participation was overwhelmingly local and voluntary.

The signature ritual of that mobilization was the blackout drill. At an announced signal, whole cities went dark: households drew heavy curtains, extinguished outdoor lights, and dimmed their headlights, so that the glow of the city could not guide an enemy bomber to its target. Wardens patrolled the streets to make sure no light escaped. The Office of Civilian Defense, abolished only after the threat receded in 1945, blanketed the country with posters, pamphlets, training manuals, and public-service announcements to teach people what to do and to make doing it feel expected — there was even a 1942 big-band number, "Obey Your Air Raid Warden," recorded to nudge the reluctant along.25

The transferable idea / 08

No single darkened window saved a city, just as no single patched router secures the internet. The blackout worked because the behavior was organized from the top, practiced as routine, socially reinforced by neighbors and wardens, and framed as a shared duty rather than a personal preference. The aggregate was the defense. That is precisely the recipe cyber hygiene lacks — and precisely the recipe it needs.

The parallel is almost uncomfortably exact. Practicing basic cybersecurity today is the equivalent of drawing the blackout curtains: a small, slightly inconvenient act, meaningless in isolation, decisive in the aggregate. A compromised home router really is a lit window, one that can help an attacker find and flood a hospital or a utility. And the same features that made civil defense succeed are the features cyber hygiene currently lacks. Blackout compliance was not left to whether each citizen happened to feel motivated on a given night; it was structured, rehearsed, and reinforced by a warden who might knock on your door. Cyber hygiene, by contrast, is left almost entirely to individual initiative — which, as the economics of Part II showed, is exactly the condition under which rational people do nothing.

The wartime analogy carries a warning as well as a promise. Civil defense was sustained by a shared sense of emergency; when the bombers never came, the drills faded. Cybersecurity suffers from the opposite problem — a slow, invisible, ambient threat that never produces a single galvanizing moment, and so never summons the collective will that a visible enemy would. The task, then, is harder than in 1941: to build the habits of shared defense without a Pearl Harbor to concentrate the mind. That is a design challenge, and Part IV is about meeting it. But civil defense is not the only precedent. There is a second model, less dramatic and in some ways more apt, drawn from the long war against disease.

Section 09

Cyber hygiene as public health

The phrase we already use gives the game away. We speak of cyber hygiene — of clean and dirty systems, of infection and contagion, of viruses and quarantine — because the most useful lens for network security turns out to be borrowed wholesale from public health. That borrowing is not decorative. The mathematics of how malware spreads across a population of devices genuinely resembles the mathematics of how disease spreads across a population of people, and the interventions that work rhyme accordingly.

Public health began as a discipline the moment someone realized that individual illness was a collective, environmental phenomenon. In 1854, during a cholera outbreak in London, the physician John Snow traced cases to a single contaminated water pump on Broad Street and had its handle removed — a founding act of epidemiology that reframed disease from a matter of private misfortune to one of shared infrastructure and public responsibility. The advances that followed — germ theory, routine handwashing, sanitation, mass vaccination — share a common structure: they treat the health of the population as a system, in which one person's exposure is everyone's concern, and small universal habits produce enormous collective protection.

Washing your hands protects strangers you'll never meet. So does patching your router. Both are acts of public health, performed at a sink or a settings screen.

Cyber hygiene inherits this logic directly. A patched device is a vaccinated host — it cannot be conscripted into the botnet that would attack someone else, so protecting it protects the herd. Basic practices function like sanitation: unglamorous, routine, and wildly cost-effective at the level of the whole system. And the epidemiological concept of herd immunity, introduced earlier, is the payoff — reach a high enough level of collective hygiene and the automated pathogens of the internet can no longer find enough vulnerable hosts to sustain themselves. The country has even built the beginnings of an institutional response along public-health lines: the CERT Coordination Center that emerged from the Morris Worm, and today the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency, function as something like a public-health service for networks — monitoring outbreaks, issuing advisories, and coordinating response across organizations that could never coordinate alone.

The analogy should not be pushed past its limits, and honesty requires naming them. Malware is authored by adversaries who adapt; pathogens, mostly, are not strategic. A patched system can be re-exposed by tomorrow's vulnerability in a way a vaccinated body usually cannot. And "quarantining" a device raises none of the civil-liberties stakes of quarantining a person. But the core reframe survives every one of these caveats, and it is the reframe that matters: security is not primarily a test of individual competence, to be passed or failed alone. It is the health of a shared ecosystem, in which everyone's practices affect everyone's exposure. That shift — from personal virtue to public health — is what licenses collective action, funding, and, where necessary, requirement. It is also what corrects the single most damaging misconception in the field: the habit of blaming the person at the keyboard.

Section 10

The human firewall

Almost every serious breach, when you trace it back, passes through a person. In its 2025 analysis of thousands of real incidents, Verizon found that the human element — someone clicking a link, reusing a password, misconfiguring a server, being deceived by a convincing lie — was involved in the substantial majority of breaches, on the order of four in five.5 The Target contractor who opened a phishing email, the Colonial Pipeline employee who reused a password, the camera owners who never changed the defaults: the chain of technical events almost always begins with a human decision. It is tempting to conclude that the weakest link is the user, and that the answer is simply to make users better.

That conclusion is half right and dangerously incomplete. Yes, human behavior is where most attacks land, which means human literacy is a real and irreplaceable layer of defense — the "human firewall." But "blame the user" is a moral and strategic error. It loads the entire weight of a systemic problem onto the least-resourced, least-expert participants, and it lets the parties who could have prevented the harm off the hook. When a single reused password can topple a fuel pipeline, the deepest failure is not the tired employee. It is a system designed so that one tired employee's mistake could do that much damage — a system with no second factor, no segmentation, no safety net.

This is precisely the correction the United States government made official in 2023. The National Cybersecurity Strategy opens by declaring that market forces alone have not produced enough security, and calls for a fundamental rebalancing: shifting the burden of cyber defense away from "individuals, small businesses, and local governments" and onto "the organizations that are most capable and best-positioned to reduce risks for all of us" — the large operators, cloud providers, and software makers.26 Its most consequential proposal would begin to hold software makers liable for shipping insecure products, while offering a safe harbor to those who follow sound development practices — a direct attempt to relocate responsibility to where it can actually be borne, and an echo of Anderson's ATM insight that whoever holds the liability is whoever invests in the fix.27 The companion principle, championed by CISA under the banner of "secure by design," asks manufacturers to build safety into products from the start, so that ordinary users are not the last line of defense against every threat.

The resolution, then, is not to choose between fixing systems and educating people. It is to do both, in their proper proportion. Build technology and set rules so that no single human error can cascade into catastrophe — that is the job of the capable, and it belongs to them. And at the same time, raise the baseline security literacy of the whole population, because even the best-designed systems still ask people to recognize a scam, choose a second factor, and pause before they click. The human firewall is not a substitute for good engineering and good policy; it is the irreducible last layer that good engineering and good policy make survivable. Which brings us, at last, from diagnosis to prescription — to what a serious national effort to build that last layer would actually contain.

Part IV — The blueprint

Make the right thing the easy thing.

Diagnosis is the easy part. The hard part is getting 330 million people to change small daily behaviors — and human beings rarely change without either a mandate or an incentive. What follows is a practical program: what to teach, how to pay for the teaching, where to deliver it, and where the law has to hold the whole structure up.

Section 11

A curriculum for digital survival

If a nation's digital resilience is only as strong as its least-informed citizen, then a baseline of security literacy is infrastructure, and it should be built like infrastructure — universally, and to a standard. The good news is that the curriculum is short. Universal basic training in cybersecurity requires no coding, no computer-science degree, and no aptitude for technology. It requires four practical, behavioral habits, each of which defeats a huge share of real-world attacks.

The first is social-engineering defense: the trained reflex to pause and verify whenever a message manufactures urgency and asks for money, credentials, or access. Nearly every scam runs on the same emotional script — an unexpected demand, a ticking clock, a plausible pretext — and the single most powerful countermeasure is a habit of independent verification: call the bank on its published number, confirm the wire request in person, distrust the panic. This matters more each year, because generative AI now writes flawless phishing messages and can clone a familiar voice from seconds of audio. The old tells — bad grammar, awkward phrasing — are gone. What remains reliable is not spotting the fake but refusing to act on urgency without checking.

The second is authentication: using a distinct, strong password for every important account, ideally through a password manager, and — above all — turning on multi-factor authentication wherever it is offered. The evidence here is close to decisive. Microsoft has reported that enabling MFA blocks on the order of 99.9 percent of automated account-compromise attacks, and that, viewed from the other side, more than 99 percent of the accounts its systems see compromised did not have MFA switched on.28 A second factor is the closest thing the field has to a free lunch: it would have stopped the Colonial Pipeline intrusion outright, and it defeats the credential-stuffing attacks that begin so many breaches.29

The third is data minimization: the discipline, for individuals and small businesses alike, of collecting and keeping only the data actually needed, and deleting the rest. Every record you do not hold is a record that cannot be stolen from you. Minimization shrinks the "blast radius" of any breach, and it has the additional civic virtue of reducing, rather than expanding, the amount of personal information circulating in the world. The fourth is patch management: the plain habit of installing updates promptly, on phones, computers, routers, and every smart device, because the majority of successful intrusions exploit known vulnerabilities for which a fix already exists. Patching is the digital equivalent of finishing the course of antibiotics — dull, easy to skip, and quietly decisive.

The economics of a habit / 11

These four habits cost almost nothing. The average data breach now costs a U.S. organization $10.22 million, an all-time high, and industry analyses estimate that prevention runs roughly fifty to sixty times cheaper than recovery.30 A password manager is a few dollars a month; turning on MFA is free; installing an update is a tap. Public security literacy is, quite literally, the cheapest and most effective firewall a country can build.

The delivery has to be as modern as the threat. A 1990s-style click-through slideshow, endured once a year, teaches nothing — people simply click "next" until it ends. What works is micro-learning: three-to-five-minute interactive scenarios, in the format of short-form video or a language-learning app, returned to often enough to build reflexes rather than deposit facts. And the most effective format is not a multiple-choice quiz but a simulated one — a safe sandbox with a fake inbox, where the learner has to spot the red flags in a realistic phishing attempt and feels the small, instructive sting of getting it wrong. The goal is not knowledge. It is reflex: the automatic hesitation before the click, as unthinking as glancing both ways at a curb.

Section 12

Carrots, not just sermons

Knowing what to teach is not the same as getting anyone to learn it. Part II established the central obstacle: for most people and firms, investing in security means paying a private cost for a benefit that mostly accrues to others, which is exactly the situation in which rational actors do nothing. Sermons cannot overcome that; the incentive gap has to be filled with something people actually respond to. The behavioral economists Richard Thaler and Cass Sunstein gave the toolkit its name in their 2008 book Nudge — the deliberate design of "choice architecture" so that the easy, default path is also the beneficial one, without forbidding any of the alternatives.31 If we want a nation of people who practice basic cyber hygiene, we should make doing so the path of least resistance and, where possible, pay them to take it.

Direct financial incentives are the fastest lever. Consider a modest "Digital Resilience" tax credit — on the order of $50 to $100 for any adult who completes a certified, thirty-minute interactive training module each year. For a married couple, that is up to $200, enough to motivate a working family to spend an hour of a Sunday learning to protect itself. Small businesses could claim an enhanced deduction for proving that their entire staff completed a national training standard and enabled MFA — turning a public good the firm would otherwise skip into a line item that pays. And completion could unlock tangible tools rather than cash: a voucher for a free hardware security key, or a year of a vetted password manager, delivered the moment the course is done, so that the knowledge and the means arrive together.

When the market won't reward the right behavior, the public can. A hundred-dollar credit is cheaper than a breach by five orders of magnitude.

The private sector, which has its own stake in a secure population, can be enlisted to supply incentives too. Auto insurers already discount premiums for drivers who complete a defensive-driving course; the same model maps cleanly onto cyber, with insurers offering reduced premiums on cyber, liability, or even homeowners' policies to individuals and businesses that complete certified training and adopt baseline controls. Banks, which absorb billions in losses from fraud that originates in compromised customer accounts, have every reason to reward customers who complete an anti-fraud and hygiene course — with a better savings rate, say, or waived fees — because a customer who cannot be phished is a customer who cannot be robbed on the bank's dime. In each case the structure is the same: locate a party that already bears some of the downstream cost, and let it pay to reduce the risk at the source. That is how you turn a diffuse externality back into a private incentive.

None of this is exotic. It is the ordinary machinery by which societies encourage behavior whose benefits are collective — tax credits for insulation, rebates for efficient appliances, discounts for safety courses. The novelty is only in pointing it at cyber hygiene, and in recognizing that a few hundred dollars of encouragement, spread across the population, is trivially cheap against a national cybercrime bill measured in the tens of billions. But money is only half of choice architecture. The other half is location — meeting people inside the systems they already have to use.

Section 13

Meeting people where they are

The surest way to guarantee that people never get security training is to require them to go find it. Every extra step — a separate website, a login, a scheduled hour — sheds participants, because attention is scarce and the payoff feels distant. The behavioral fix is to stop asking people to detour toward the training and instead bake it into the institutional touchpoints they already have to pass through. The government and the private sector both operate chokepoints that almost every adult encounters; each is an opportunity to deliver a few minutes of practical hygiene at exactly the moment a person is already sitting still and paying attention.

Start with the Department of Motor Vehicles. Nearly every American renews a driver's license or a vehicle registration on a predictable cycle, increasingly online. A mandatory five-minute interactive module on spotting phishing or securing a digital identity, completed before the renewal payment goes through, would reach a colossal and otherwise-unreachable share of the population, in small and regular doses, at essentially zero marginal cost. The same principle applies to the on-ramps of economic life. To qualify for a Small Business Administration loan or a federal grant, a founder could be required to complete a short "Cyber Risk for Founders" module — you do not receive the public's money to build a business until you can demonstrate that you know how to protect its digital assets, and by extension the customers and partners who will depend on it.

Choice architecture / 13

The design principle is friction reduction. Do not build a destination and hope people travel to it. Insert the lesson into a flow people are already moving through — a renewal, a loan application, a graduation requirement — so that the default path and the protective path are the same path. This is how seat belts, food-safety training, and countless other public habits actually scaled: not by persuasion alone, but by being built into the systems people could not avoid.

The most durable intervention, though, is generational. Digital hygiene belongs in public-school curricula the way physical education and health class already do — as a required module, call it "Digital Survival," that every student completes before graduating. The next generation will enter a workforce and a civic life saturated with digital risk; sending them into it without a baseline understanding of operational security is as negligent as sending them out unable to read a contract or balance a budget. Teaching a sixteen-year-old to recognize a scam, use a password manager, and think before oversharing is not a technical elective. It is basic preparation for twenty-first-century adulthood, and it compounds: a generation raised with these reflexes needs far less remediation later, and models the habits for the households around them.

Taken together — financial carrots, institutional touchpoints, modern delivery — these measures could plausibly move a large majority of the population to a baseline of competence that automated attacks would find far harder to exploit. But incentives and education have a ceiling. Some actors will not respond to a nudge or a credit no matter how well designed, and some risks are too consequential to leave to voluntary uptake. For those, the structure needs a load-bearing wall. It needs law.

Section 14

Regulation as scaffolding

Voluntary measures do real work, but they share a fatal ceiling: they reach the people already inclined to act and leave the rest — and the highest-stakes risks — untouched. This is not a controversial observation; it is the government's own conclusion. The 2023 National Cybersecurity Strategy states plainly that "market forces alone have not been enough" to produce the security the country needs, and argues that the responsibility must be rebalanced onto the parties best positioned to bear it, with incentives realigned toward long-term investment rather than the cheapest path to shipping.26 That is the case for regulation, and it rests on the same economics as everything in Part II: when private incentives and the public good diverge, only a change in the rules can close the gap.

What that regulation should look like is more contested, and it deserves an honest accounting of both sides. The interventionist case is straightforward. Where the stakes are high enough — critical infrastructure, mass-market connected devices, the software that everything else runs on — the strongest tool is liability: making the makers of insecure products bear a share of the harm those products cause. The National Cybersecurity Strategy proposes exactly this, seeking legislation that would prevent dominant software vendors from disclaiming all responsibility by contract, while granting a safe harbor to firms that follow recognized secure-development practices.27 The logic is Anderson's ATM insight scaled to a whole economy: place the liability on the party that can actually reduce the risk, and that party will invest in reducing it. Baseline requirements — the European Union's mandatory security rules for connected products, the United Kingdom's outright ban on default passwords — work the same way, setting a floor beneath which the market may not cut corners.17

The debate is not really whether to shape the market, but how firmly. Even a "voluntary" label is the government putting its thumb on the scale — on purpose.

The objections are serious and worth stating at their strongest. Poorly drawn liability could smother innovation, punishing developers for vulnerabilities that even excellent security programs cannot fully prevent, and could entrench the largest incumbents — the only firms that can afford heavy compliance — against the small challengers a healthy market needs. Mandates imposed without support could crush the very small businesses least able to absorb them, deepening the "target rich, resource poor" gap rather than closing it. And any expansion of government's role in the digital lives of citizens raises legitimate fears of surveillance and overreach; a program to improve security must not become a pretext to monitor. These are not fringe worries, and a serious proposal has to answer them rather than wave them away.

The answers, fortunately, are built into the better versions of the policy. Liability paired with a safe harbor rewards good-faith security effort instead of demanding impossible perfection, addressing the innovation concern directly. Mandates paired with the subsidies and phase-ins of Part IV — the tax credits, the free hardware keys, the training baked into existing systems — shift burden onto the capable while carrying the vulnerable along, rather than abandoning them. Standards-based rules, keyed to widely vetted baselines like those from the National Institute of Standards and Technology, keep regulators from dictating specific technologies and let the market innovate within a floor. And the surveillance concern cuts, in part, the other way: data minimization, a core hygiene practice, actively reduces the pool of personal information available to be surveilled or stolen, by anyone. The right posture is neither pure market faith nor heavy-handed control, but scaffolding — the minimum structure required to hold up behavior that will not stand on its own, paired with the freedom to build within it. The U.S. Cyber Trust Mark, a voluntary label that nonetheless represents the state deliberately reshaping a market, is a reasonable model for the light-touch end; liability for critical software is the model for the heavy end. Most of the work lies in between, and reasonable people will disagree about exactly where each risk falls on that spectrum. That disagreement is healthy. What is not defensible is the status quo, in which the rules quietly guarantee that the socially optimal level of security is never reached.

Coda

Look both ways

Every durable safety habit we now perform without thinking was once an argument, a law, and an inconvenience. Buckling a seat belt felt like a nuisance and an affront until, over decades of engineering, legislation, and relentless public campaigns, it became a reflex so automatic that its absence feels wrong. Looking both ways before crossing a street is not innate; it is taught, drilled into children until it runs beneath conscious thought. The entire project of public safety consists of taking behaviors that are individually inconvenient and collectively essential, and making them so ordinary that they stop feeling like decisions at all.

That is the destination for cybersecurity. Not a nation of experts — that was never the goal and never the requirement — but a nation of people for whom pausing before a suspicious link, turning on a second factor, and installing an update are as reflexive as glancing both ways at a curb. Getting there asks something of everyone. It asks the makers of software and devices, and the operators of critical systems, to shoulder the burden they are best positioned to bear, and it asks the law to place that burden where it belongs. It asks government to fund and organize a baseline of literacy the way it once organized civil defense and public health. And it asks each of us to treat the small piece of the network in our hands as what it now is: a stretch of the national frontier, and a lit or darkened window that helps or hinders the strangers downwind.

The fortress is gone, and it is not coming back. What replaces it is a citizenry that has learned, at last, to draw the curtains.

The perimeter of national security really has relocated — from the military border and the corporate firewall to the smartphone in a delivery driver's pocket and the invoicing software on a plumber's laptop. We can keep pretending that this makes cybersecurity somebody else's department, an IT line item or an annual compliance module, and keep paying the bill in stolen savings, shuttered small businesses, ransomed hospitals, and dark pipelines. Or we can recognize it for what it is: a matter of shared civic infrastructure, no more optional than clean water or safe food, and address it with the same seriousness and the same tools.

Cybersecurity is not a niche technical discipline. It is a fundamental life skill for the twenty-first century and a shared responsibility we owe both to ourselves and to one another. Educating the public to meet it is the cheapest, most effective firewall a country can build — and the only one that runs everywhere the frontier now runs. The perimeter is everyone. So is the defense.
