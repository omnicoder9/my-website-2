Holding the Line

Cyber and information security in the United States Defense Industrial Base — the risks, the cross-sector blast radius, the mitigation stack, and the problems still unsolved.

Section 00 — Executive brief

The supply chain is the attack surface

The Defense Industrial Base is the only one of America’s sixteen critical infrastructure sectors whose Sector Risk Management Agency is the Department of Defense itself, and the only one whose product is national military power. It is also a sector defined less by a handful of marquee primes than by the more than one hundred thousand companies and subcontractors beneath them — a depth that makes it the single richest target for state-sponsored espionage and, increasingly, for pre-positioning ahead of conflict.[2]

This reference works through four things in order: the technology and risk peculiar to the DIB; the concrete consequences when it fails; the way a single DIB compromise spreads into utilities, finance, water, and the other critical sectors; and the stack of standards, programs, and enforcement tools assembled to hold the line — followed by the problems that remain genuinely unsolved. The throughline is uncomfortable but simple: in this sector the network you must defend is not yours. It belongs to your suppliers, and to theirs.

Section 01 — The sector

What the DIB is, and why adversaries aim here first

The Defense Industrial Base is defined by CISA as the worldwide industrial complex that enables the research, development, design, production, delivery, and maintenance of military weapons systems and their subsystems, parts, and components, in order to meet U.S. military requirements.[2] It deliberately excludes the commercial power, communications, transportation, and utility services that the DoD merely consumes — those belong to other sectors and other Sector Risk Management Agencies — but it includes the primes, the long tail of subcontractors, and the suppliers of incidental materials and services.[2]

The sector’s governance lineage runs through Homeland Security Presidential Directive 7 and later Presidential Policy Directive 21, which together designate the sixteen critical infrastructure sectors and assign the DoD as the agency responsible for leading the DIB’s risk-management effort under the National Infrastructure Protection Plan.[1][3] PPD-21 frames all sixteen sectors as so vital that their incapacitation would have a debilitating effect on national security, the economy, or public health and safety — and the DIB sits at the apex of that list because its failure degrades the instrument the nation relies on to protect the rest.[1]

The structural weakness: depth, not the top of the pyramid

A defense program is a pyramid. A handful of named primes integrate systems built from thousands of lower-tier suppliers — machine shops, firmware vendors, materials houses, software libraries — many of them small businesses with a fraction of a prime’s security budget. The adversary understands this arithmetic. The cheapest path to a stealth fighter’s radar modules is rarely the prime’s hardened network; it is a third-tier supplier holding the same drawings under far weaker controls. The sector’s defining risk is therefore not a single soft target but a distribution of them, contractually bound to share the same sensitive data.

DEFINITIONS // THE DATA THAT MOVES THROUGH THE DIB
  • FCI — Federal Contract Information. Information provided by or generated for the government under contract, not intended for public release.[16]
  • CUI — Controlled Unclassified Information. Unclassified information that law or policy requires to be safeguarded; the category NIST SP 800-171 exists to protect.[4]
  • CDI / Covered Defense Information. The DFARS term for the controlled, export-controlled, or otherwise sensitive unclassified information a contractor must safeguard and whose compromise it must report.[5]
Section 02 — Risk surface

Industry-specific technology and where it breaks

The DIB’s technology stack is unusual because it spans the entire distance from a designer’s workstation to a torque wrench on a production floor, and because so much of what crosses it would be a decisive prize for a foreign military.

Two estates: information technology and operational technology

On the information-technology side sit the engineering crown jewels — computer-aided design and product-lifecycle-management systems, simulation data, source code, and the controlled and export-controlled documents that DFARS clause 252.204-7012 obligates contractors to safeguard under NIST SP 800-171 and to report on within seventy-two hours of a cyber incident.[5] On the operational-technology side sit the factories themselves: industrial control systems, CNC machine tools, robotics, and test ranges. The same convergence that lets a manufacturer pull a design straight from CAD to a machine controller also lets an intruder who reaches the business network reach the shop floor. This IT/OT bridge is precisely the path that state actors have been observed seeking, by pre-positioning on enterprise networks to enable lateral movement into operational assets.[13]

The threat actors and what they want

DIB risk is dominated by patient, well-resourced nation-state intrusion sets rather than opportunistic crime. The animating motive for two decades has been espionage: the theft of weapons-system intellectual property to compress an adversary’s own development timeline and erase U.S. technological overmatch. More recently the motive has broadened to disruption — the deliberate establishment of quiet, long-duration access that could be activated to sabotage capability during a crisis.[13][14] Both motives converge on the same weak points: a small supplier’s unmanaged credentials, an internet-facing edge device, an unpatched cloud tenant holding controlled data.

AEspionage & IP theft. Exfiltration of designs, radar and propulsion data, and source code to clone or counter U.S. systems.
BPre-positioning & sabotage. Stealthy persistence intended to disrupt production or operational technology at a moment of the adversary’s choosing.[13]
CSupply-chain implantation. Trojanized software updates or hardware that ride trusted vendor relationships into thousands of downstream networks.[15]
DRansomware & extortion. Encryption or theft-and-leak against under-resourced subcontractors, halting deliveries and exposing CUI.
Section 03 — Consequences

From stolen blueprints to eroded overmatch

The clearest illustration of what a DIB compromise costs is also the most expensive aircraft program in U.S. history. National Security Agency documents disclosed in 2015 described Chinese cyber actors exfiltrating on the order of fifty terabytes of data from U.S. defense and government networks, including stealth-radar and engine information for the Joint Strike Fighter.[6] The same body of reporting tied the campaign — tracked under the codename Byzantine Hades — to the compromise of designs for the B-2 bomber, the F-22 Raptor, and naval and missile programs, and put the damage-assessment and network-repair bill at more than one hundred million dollars, with U.S. defense systems registering hundreds of significant intrusions in a single year.[7]

INCIDENT FILE // F-35 / JOINT STRIKE FIGHTER

The principal breach is understood to have occurred at the prime, Lockheed Martin, and through a subcontractor around 2007, before international F-35 orders were placed; analysts have argued the stolen data measurably accelerated China’s J-20 and J-31 stealth programs, which bear visible resemblance to the F-35 down to sensor placement.[8] The supply chain remained the soft edge for a decade: in 2017 an Australian F-35 subcontractor was breached for further information on the aircraft, underscoring that the prime’s own hardening does not protect data once it flows downstream.[8]

INCIDENT FILE // BOEING C-17 — THE SU BIN CASE

Espionage against the DIB is not purely remote. In a long-running operation, an aerospace businessman working with hackers tied to China’s military reportedly enabled the theft of more than six hundred thousand files — on the order of sixty-five gigabytes — relating to the C-17 cargo aircraft from Boeing, alongside F-22 and F-35 material, blending insider access with cyber intrusion.[9] The case shows the sector’s human and network attack surfaces are one problem, not two.

The consequence is rarely a single dramatic outage. It is cumulative and strategic: every exfiltrated design narrows a capability gap that cost decades and billions to open, shifts risk onto the warfighters who depend on that overmatch, and strains the trust of allied governments that buy into the same programs. When the motive turns from theft to sabotage, the consequence sharpens further — the possibility that production lines or fielded systems could be degraded at the worst possible moment.[14]

Section 04 — Blast radius

How one DIB compromise reaches the other fifteen sectors

The DIB does not fail in isolation. It is woven into Information Technology, Communications, Energy, Critical Manufacturing, and Financial Services through shared vendors, shared software, and shared cloud platforms. Two campaigns make the cross-sector mechanics concrete.

SolarWinds: one vendor, every sector

In late 2020 a nation-state actor — widely attributed to Russia’s SVR and tracked as APT29 / Cozy Bear — subverted the build system of SolarWinds’ Orion network-management product and distributed trojanized updates to as many as eighteen thousand customers across government and industry.[12] Because Orion necessarily holds broad, privileged access to the networks it manages, the compromise handed the adversary a foothold of exceptional depth.[11] The FBI ultimately identified roughly nine federal agencies and fewer than a hundred private entities subjected to follow-on intrusion out of those thousands of exposed customers — a list spanning the Treasury (Financial Services), Commerce, and Homeland Security, alongside major technology and defense firms.[10][12] CISA judged the threat grave enough to issue Emergency Directive 21-01, ordering civilian agencies to disconnect the affected product.[11] The lesson for the DIB is structural: a single trusted software supplier is a shared dependency that can detonate simultaneously in the defense, financial, IT, and government sectors.

SHARED-DEPENDENCY CASCADE
Compromised vendor → Trusted update / privileged access → DIB primes & subs → IT · Comms · Energy · Financial · Water

The Colonial Pipeline and Kaseya incidents of 2021 reinforced the same pattern — that a weakness in one component of the software or service supply chain propagates to a far wider set of victims than the original target.[15]

Volt Typhoon: pre-positioning in the lifeline sectors

The second mechanism is more alarming because it is aimed not at secrets but at switches. In a 2024 joint advisory, CISA, the NSA, and the FBI assessed with high confidence that the PRC state-sponsored group Volt Typhoon had compromised IT environments across the Communications, Energy, Transportation Systems, and Water and Wastewater Systems sectors, in some cases maintaining access for at least five years.[13] The group relies on “living off the land” tradecraft — abusing valid credentials and built-in administrative tools rather than malware — specifically to evade detection, and was observed pre-positioning on IT networks to enable lateral movement onto operational technology, even testing access to OT assets using default vendor credentials.[13] U.S. agencies characterized this as a strategic shift from espionage toward staging for disruptive attacks that could jeopardize physical safety in the event of conflict.[14]

For the DIB the implication is direct. A defense contractor sits adjacent to exactly these lifeline sectors — it draws power, moves goods, depends on water and telecommunications — and a contractor network can serve as a quiet beachhead from which an adversary pivots toward the utilities that surround it, or vice versa. The sixteen sectors are not sixteen separate problems; they are one interdependent surface, and the DIB is stitched through the middle of it.

Section 05 — Mitigation

The mitigation stack: standards, programs, enforcement

The U.S. response to DIB risk is not a single control but a layered apparatus — a baseline of security requirements, a certification regime to verify them, a voluntary intelligence-sharing program, an architectural mandate, and a legal stick. Each layer addresses a different failure mode of the one below it.

Layer 1 — The baseline: NIST SP 800-171, 800-172, and the DFARS

The contractual baseline for protecting controlled unclassified information is NIST Special Publication 800-171. Revision 2 specifies 110 security requirements organized into 14 families and assessed against 320 objectives; Revision 3 consolidates these to 97 requirements across 17 families, removing redundancy and ambiguity, with the DoD publishing organization-defined parameters to tailor it.[4][19][21] For the most critical programs, NIST SP 800-172 adds enhanced requirements aimed at advanced persistent threats, and the assessment procedures live in NIST SP 800-171A.[20] The binding mechanism is DFARS 252.204-7012, which requires contractors to implement 800-171 on covered systems and to report cyber incidents within seventy-two hours; companion clauses 252.204-7019 and -7020 require contractors to post a self-assessment score to the Supplier Performance Risk System and preserve DoD’s right to conduct its own higher-level assessment.[5][21]

Layer 2 — Verification: the CMMC program

Self-attestation alone proved insufficient, so the Cybersecurity Maturity Model Certification program adds verification on top of the 800-171 baseline. CMMC 2.0 streamlined an earlier five-level model to three.[20] The program rule (32 CFR Part 170) became effective December 16, 2024, formally establishing CMMC as the mechanism by which DoD confirms that a contractor has implemented and maintains the security required to protect FCI and CUI.[16] The contractual trigger — the DFARS procurement rule that inserts clause 252.204-7021 into solicitations — was finalized and took effect November 10, 2025, partially implementing Section 1648 of the FY2020 NDAA and beginning a multi-year phased rollout.[17]

L1Foundational. Basic safeguarding of FCI; annual self-assessment.[18]
L2Advanced. The full NIST 800-171 set for CUI; most CUI handlers require a third-party assessment by a certified C3PAO — DoD estimates roughly 93% of CUI contractors land here, with about 5% eligible for self-assessment.[18][19]
L3Expert. Adds 800-172 enhanced controls for the highest-risk programs, assessed by the government’s DIBCAC — roughly 2% of contractors.[19]

Layer 3 — Collective defense: the DIB CS Program and DC3

Compliance hardens individual networks; intelligence sharing lets the sector defend as one. The DoD Defense Industrial Base Cybersecurity Program, codified at 32 CFR Part 236, is a voluntary public-private partnership through which contractors exchange cyber threat information and receive Cybersecurity-as-a-Service capabilities; its operational hub is the DoD-DIB Collaborative Information Sharing Environment (DCISE) at the DoD Cyber Crime Center (DC3), which also performs forensic analysis of malware that contractors are required to isolate and submit.[22] DCISE serves as the single focal point and data repository for DIB cyber-incident reporting; participants gain threat intelligence that is reported to be overwhelmingly unique relative to commercial feeds and is sometimes shared ahead of public disclosure.[23] A 2024 rule broadened eligibility from only cleared contractors holding a facility clearance to any defense contractor that owns or operates an unclassified system processing CUI, with access delivered through the DIBNet-U platform; the program was first established in 2013.[24] DC3 also ran a Defense Industrial Base Vulnerability Disclosure Program pilot from 2021 to extend coordinated vulnerability disclosure to volunteer DIB participants.[25]

Layer 4 — Architecture: the DoD Zero Trust mandate

Underlying all of the above is an architectural shift away from perimeter trust. The DoD Zero Trust Strategy, grounded in NIST SP 800-207, organizes implementation into seven pillars — User, Device, Network & Environment, Application & Workload, Data, Visibility & Analytics, and Automation & Orchestration.[27] Its execution roadmap defines 152 activities; 91 constitute the “target level” baseline due by the end of fiscal year 2027, and the remaining 61 “advanced” activities are due by 2032 — a mandate that reaches every DoD component and the DIB vendors that support them.[26] In late 2025 the department extended the model explicitly into operational technology, publishing 105 OT-specific zero-trust activities across the same seven pillars.[28]

Layer 5 — The stick: the DOJ Civil Cyber-Fraud Initiative

Standards without enforcement decay into paperwork, so since October 2021 the Department of Justice’s Civil Cyber-Fraud Initiative has used the False Claims Act — with its treble damages — to pursue contractors that knowingly misrepresent their compliance.[29] The pattern is now well established. Raytheon and its successor agreed to pay $8.4 million over failures to implement 800-171 on a network used for unclassified defense work.[29] MORSECORP paid $4.6 million in the first settlement premised on a contractor failing to update an inflated SPRS self-assessment score after a third-party review found it far lower.[30] Pennsylvania State University settled for $1.25 million over fifteen DoD and NASA contracts.[31] A private-equity owner and its portfolio defense contractor settled together for $11.55 million, confirming that liability can extend beyond the entity in direct privity with the government.[32] By early 2026 the government was describing a record year for cyber-related FCA recoveries, including a December 2025 settlement with a precision-machining subcontractor over inadequate protection of technical drawings — many of these cases initiated by whistleblowers under the FCA’s qui tam provisions.[33]

Section 06 — Frontier

Open challenges and where research is still needed

The apparatus above is the most comprehensive any critical sector has assembled, and it is still not enough. The hard problems that remain are structural, and several resist a purely regulatory fix.

The small-business burden

The same depth that creates the attack surface makes it hard to secure. A third-tier machine shop cannot fund a prime’s security program, yet may hold the same drawings — exactly the profile of the precision-machining subcontractor pursued by DOJ in 2025.[33] CMMC’s third-party assessment cost and the flow-down of requirements through every subcontract are a real barrier to the small innovators the DIB depends on. Research need: assurance and continuous-monitoring approaches whose cost scales down to a ten-person supplier.

The self-attestation gap

For years compliance rested on a contractor’s own word, and the enforcement record shows how often that word outran reality — the Raytheon and MORSE settlements both turned on representations that did not match the network.[29][30] CMMC’s third-party assessments narrow the gap but produce a point-in-time snapshot. The open problem is continuous assurance: machine-verifiable, always-current evidence of control state rather than an annual attestation.

Detecting living-off-the-land tradecraft

Volt Typhoon demonstrated that the most consequential intrusions may involve no malware at all, only valid credentials and native tools, evading signature-based defenses for years.[13] Detecting an adversary that looks exactly like a legitimate administrator demands far deeper behavioral visibility into authentication, command-line activity, and credential use than most DIB networks currently have — an unsolved detection-engineering and data-analytics problem.

Legacy and operational technology

Weapons platforms and the factories that build them have lifecycles measured in decades. Much OT cannot be patched without halting production, and firmware and hardware provenance are difficult to verify. The DoD’s 2025 extension of zero trust into OT acknowledges the gap, but applying identity-centric controls to a thirty-year-old controller is a research frontier, not a checklist item.[28]

N-tier supply-chain visibility

SolarWinds showed that organizations frequently cannot enumerate their own software dependencies, let alone their suppliers’ suppliers.[15] Software bills of materials, secure-by-design practices, and hardware roots of trust are the leading directions, but producing trustworthy, machine-checkable provenance at the scale and depth of the DIB remains an open engineering and economic challenge.

AI and post-quantum pressure

Artificial intelligence is a double-edged input: it lowers the cost of reconnaissance and exploit development for attackers while offering defenders automation across the zero-trust visibility and orchestration pillars.[27] In parallel, the prospect of cryptographically relevant quantum computing threatens the confidentiality of exfiltrated data over its decades-long sensitivity window, making migration to post-quantum cryptography a live research and transition problem for the sector.

Section 07 — Outlook

Holding the line on a network you don’t own

The Defense Industrial Base concentrates the highest-value secrets in the country onto the most widely distributed and unevenly defended set of networks in any critical sector. Two decades of espionage — from the F-35 to the C-17 — established the cost of getting this wrong, and the more recent shift toward pre-positioning has raised the stakes from stolen advantage to potential sabotage. The American answer has been to stop treating contractor cybersecurity as a private matter: to write a baseline into the regulation, verify it through certification, share intelligence through DCISE, re-architect toward zero trust, and enforce the whole edifice through the False Claims Act.

What remains is the part no rule can mandate — pushing real security down to the smallest supplier, detecting adversaries who carry no malware, and verifying provenance through a supply chain too deep to fully see. The sector’s defining truth has not changed: the network that must be defended belongs to someone else, and the line holds only as far down the supply chain as the defense reaches.
