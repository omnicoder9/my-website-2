Impossibility: Logic, Physics, Computation, and the Limits of Human Claims

A fair and objective taxonomy of things that are impossible, almost certainly impossible, practically impossible, or still philosophically disputed.

Prepared as a complete HTML article. Citation style: Vancouver numeric system.

Abstract

“Impossible” is a powerful word, but it is often used too loosely. A square circle, a perpetual-motion machine, a perfect antivirus program, faster-than-light communication, a risk-free high-return investment, and the nonexistence of free will are not impossible in the same sense. Some claims contradict definitions; some violate proven mathematical results; some conflict with well-tested physical laws; some are not strictly impossible but are unattainable under realistic constraints; and some remain open philosophical or scientific disputes. This article distinguishes those categories and applies them to common examples in logic, mathematics, physics, computer science, security, finance, cosmology, artificial intelligence, and philosophy.

1. Why “impossible” needs categories

To call something impossible is not merely to say that it has not yet been done. Many once-unrealized achievements—powered flight, organ transplantation, gravitational-wave detection, and practical machine translation—were difficult rather than impossible. A responsible impossibility claim needs a source of impossibility: a contradiction in the terms, a theorem, a conservation law, a causal principle, a resource limit, or a conceptual problem.

The distinction matters because evidence against one kind of impossibility does not defeat another. Better engineering can overcome practical difficulty, but it cannot make a largest integer if the number system is the ordinary integers. New physics might revise a physical impossibility claim, but it would not make a married bachelor coherent unless the meanings of the words changed. Likewise, better software can improve cybersecurity, but it does not by itself overturn undecidability results or eliminate the social and organizational attack surface of real systems.

2. A taxonomy of impossibility

Type Core reason Typical examples How strong is the verdict?
Logical The claim contains a contradiction once the terms are understood. Square circle; married bachelor. Maximally strong, assuming ordinary meanings.
Mathematical A theorem rules out the proposed object or procedure within a formal system. Largest integer; general algorithm deciding all halting cases. Very strong, relative to the stated formal assumptions.
Physical The claim conflicts with well-confirmed laws of nature. Perpetual motion; a perfect heat engine; faster-than-light signals in ordinary relativity. Extremely strong, but always indexed to current physical theory and evidence.
Computational The task asks a finite procedure to solve a class of problems known to be undecidable or unrestrictedly universal. Perfect virus detector; universal solver for every well-posed and ill-posed problem. Very strong when the problem is precisely formalized.
Practical No contradiction is known, but real-world complexity, adversaries, cost, scale, or uncertainty prevents perfection. Unhackable system; perfect security; absolute zero operational risk. Context-dependent but often decisive for policy and engineering.
Philosophical The dispute depends on contested concepts, metaphysical assumptions, or standards of explanation. Free will; non-physical consciousness; omnipotence; objective morality. Usually not settled by laboratory evidence alone.

3. Strong candidates for “almost certainly impossible”

3.1 Perpetual motion and “free energy” machines

A perpetual-motion machine, in the relevant engineering sense, is not merely a machine that keeps moving for a long time. It is a device that produces useful work indefinitely without an adequate energy source, or that extracts work from heat in a way forbidden by the second law of thermodynamics. The first law blocks energy creation from nothing; the second law blocks a cyclic device from converting all heat from a single reservoir into work. OpenStax summarizes the second law in heat-engine form as making complete conversion of heat into work in a cycle impossible, and it notes that real engines do not achieve the ideal Carnot limit because dissipative processes such as friction intervene.1

“Free energy” claims often repackage perpetual motion in marketable language. A fair assessment is not that every unconventional energy device is fraudulent; rather, a device claiming net energy without fuel, input, stored energy, or a corresponding environmental energy source must overcome conservation of energy and entropy. That burden of proof is extraordinary.

3.2 A perfect heat engine

A perfect heat engine would convert all absorbed heat into useful work with 100% efficiency in a cycle. Thermodynamics does not merely say such an engine is hard to build. It says that a cyclic heat engine operating between ordinary reservoirs must reject some heat, and any real engine suffers additional losses. Thus, a “perfect” heat engine belongs beside perpetual motion as physically impossible under standard thermodynamics.1

3.3 Faster-than-light communication

Special relativity treats the speed of light as a causal limit for matter, influence, and information. Einstein Online, an outreach project of the Max Planck Institute for Gravitational Physics, states the point plainly: in special relativity, no moving object, information, or influence can move faster than light.2 This does not mean that every apparent faster-than-light phenomenon is impossible. Shadows, interference patterns, phase velocities, or coordinate descriptions can appear superluminal without transmitting usable information faster than light. The stronger claim concerns controllable signals.

Speculative ideas such as wormholes, warp metrics, or violations of Lorentz invariance are not equivalent to working faster-than-light telephones. They require exotic assumptions, face stability and energy problems, and often threaten causality. A fair verdict is therefore: faster-than-light communication is not logically impossible, but it is physically forbidden by our best-tested relativistic framework and unsupported by reliable evidence.

3.4 A square circle and a largest integer

A square circle is logically impossible because the conditions for being a square and the conditions for being a circle exclude each other in Euclidean geometry. A largest integer is mathematically impossible in the ordinary integers because for any integer n, n + 1 is also an integer and is larger. These examples are cleaner than physical cases because they do not depend on empirical measurement. They depend on definitions and formal rules.

3.5 A universal algorithm solving every problem

There are universal computers in the sense that one machine can simulate many others. But there is no universal algorithm that solves every problem whatsoever. Turing’s 1936 analysis of computability showed that there are well-defined mathematical questions no general mechanical procedure can decide.3 The halting problem is the best-known example: no algorithm can take every possible program and input and always correctly decide whether that program eventually halts. This is not a limitation of present hardware; it is a theorem about computation.

In machine learning and optimization, “no free lunch” results add a related caution: averaged over broad classes of possible problems, no single optimizer or learner is universally superior without assumptions about the structure of the target problems.4 That does not make strong artificial intelligence impossible, but it does undermine the fantasy of a single context-free method that is best for everything.

3.6 Perfect antivirus and perfect security

The perfect antivirus problem has both theoretical and practical limits. Fred Cohen’s classic work on computer viruses argued that, in general, virus detection cannot be perfect; particular viruses can be detected by particular methods, but unrestricted detection is impossible in the general case.5 Later expositions connect this result to the same computability limits that underlie the halting problem.6

An unhackable system is even broader. It includes software defects, hardware faults, supply chains, administrators, users, physical access, cryptographic assumptions, social engineering, and future discoveries. NIST’s cybersecurity work frames security as risk management rather than perfection, and a NIST report quoted in its guidance states: “No system is 100 percent secure: every system has vulnerabilities.”7 The objective standard is therefore resilience, defense in depth, monitoring, recovery, and acceptable risk—not invulnerability.

3.7 Risk-free high-return investment

In finance, “risk-free high return” is usually not a theorem-level contradiction, because “high” and “risk” can be defined in many ways. But in practical markets the phrase is a major red flag. The U.S. Federal Trade Commission warns that investment scams often promise profitable, low-effort, “risk free” opportunities.8 The SEC and CFTC similarly warn that claims of high guaranteed returns with little or no risk are red flags of fraud, especially in digital-asset schemes.9 The fair formulation is: high return without corresponding risk, hidden subsidy, information advantage, insurance, or fraud is economically implausible; as a retail offer, it should be presumed suspicious.

4. Controversial cases: debated, not dismissed

Some topics are often placed in the “impossible” basket because they are strange, unresolved, or hard to test. That is a mistake. The following disputes remain live partly because the concepts are contested and partly because evidence underdetermines the conclusion.

4.1 Free will

Free will is not a single hypothesis. It may mean uncaused choice, agent causation, rational self-control, responsiveness to reasons, absence of coercion, or moral responsibility. The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy describes free will as a significant kind of control over action and surveys major compatibilist and incompatibilist positions.10 Neuroscience can constrain theories of agency, but it does not by itself settle what kind of control is required for responsibility.

4.2 Consciousness as non-physical

Physicalism says, roughly, that everything is physical; non-physicalist views deny this or add irreducible mental properties. The debate remains difficult because consciousness is both intimately familiar and resistant to third-person explanation. The Stanford Encyclopedia describes physicalism as the thesis that everything is physical,11 while its entry on consciousness emphasizes how puzzling conscious experience remains.12 It would be premature to call non-physical consciousness logically impossible, but it also lacks the evidential support normally demanded of scientific claims.

4.3 Time travel to the past

Past-directed time travel is not as simple as “relativity allows it” or “grandfather paradoxes refute it.” General relativity contains models with closed timelike curves, yet whether such structures can exist, be created, remain stable, or avoid contradiction is unresolved. The Stanford Encyclopedia’s article on time travel treats the subject as a serious philosophical problem, while its article on time machines notes that physicists have studied whether devices producing closed timelike curves would be admissible in relativity and quantum theory.13,14 The prudent classification is physically speculative and philosophically contested, not established.

4.4 An infinite universe

The observable universe is finite because light has had only a finite time to reach us. The whole universe may be finite or infinite depending on its global geometry and topology, which cannot be read directly from the observable patch alone. ESA’s discussion of finite and infinite cosmological models illustrates that flatness, curvature, and finitude are distinct questions.15 Therefore, “an infinite universe is impossible” is too strong; current cosmology leaves room for multiple global possibilities.

4.5 Simulation hypothesis

The simulation hypothesis is neither established science nor simple nonsense. Nick Bostrom’s influential argument is a trilemma: at least one of several propositions is true, including that civilizations like ours rarely reach a posthuman stage, rarely run many ancestor simulations, or that we are almost certainly simulated.16 The argument depends on assumptions about consciousness, computation, future civilizations, and reference classes. Its weakness is not a known contradiction; its weakness is underdetermination and limited testability.

4.6 Omnipotence

Omnipotence is often challenged with paradoxes: Can an omnipotent being make a stone it cannot lift, or create a square circle? The Stanford Encyclopedia frames omnipotence as maximal power and asks whether a consistent analysis can handle such puzzles.17 Many philosophers and theologians respond by defining omnipotence as the power to do all logically possible things, not contradictions disguised as tasks. Thus, unrestricted omnipotence may be incoherent, while qualified omnipotence remains a debated concept.

4.7 Objective morality

Moral realism holds that moral claims purport to report facts and that at least some such claims are true; moral anti-realism denies objective moral facts or treats moral truth as non-objective in some relevant sense.18,19 This dispute is not settled by pointing to cultural disagreement, nor by pointing to moral confidence. The key questions concern what kind of facts moral facts would be, how we could know them, and whether moral language commits us to them.

4.8 Strong artificial general intelligence limits

Claims that strong artificial general intelligence is impossible are usually weaker than they appear. Some argue that machines lack consciousness, embodiment, intentionality, common sense, or genuine understanding; others argue that these are engineering challenges rather than principled barriers. Computability theory rules out algorithms that solve all possible problems, and no-free-lunch theorems caution against context-free universal superiority.3,4 But those results do not by themselves prove that machines cannot match or exceed human performance across many domains. The stronger conclusion is that AGI, if possible, will still face formal, physical, social, and epistemic limits.

4.9 Infinite economic growth

Infinite economic growth is ambiguous. If it means unbounded physical throughput on a finite planet, it conflicts with resource, ecological, and thermodynamic constraints. If it means unbounded value, knowledge, services, or welfare with declining material intensity, the claim becomes more abstract and harder to rule out. Climate and sustainability assessments such as those of the IPCC examine the interaction of development, energy use, emissions, risks, and mitigation pathways rather than endorsing a simple “growth is impossible” slogan.20 The fair conclusion is that infinite material expansion in a finite system is physically implausible, while the future of measured economic value depends on definitions, institutions, technology, and environmental limits.

6. How to evaluate a new impossibility claim

A useful test is to ask six questions. First, does the claim contradict itself? Second, has a theorem ruled it out under clear assumptions? Third, does it violate a well-tested physical law? Fourth, is the proposed exception testable, or merely insulated from failure? Fifth, are the words being used consistently? Sixth, would success require perfection in an adversarial or open-ended environment?

This approach avoids two opposite errors. The first error is scientism by slogan: dismissing all strange ideas as impossible before defining the claim. The second is possibility inflation: treating any conceptual loophole as if it were a plausible technology, investment, or worldview. Fairness requires both openness and proportional skepticism.

Conclusion

The strongest impossibility claims are those grounded in contradiction, proof, or deeply confirmed physical law. Square circles, largest integers, unrestricted halting solvers, perpetual-motion machines, perfect heat engines, perfect virus detectors, and truly unhackable systems are strong candidates for “almost certainly impossible,” though each for a different reason. By contrast, free will, non-physical consciousness, past time travel, an infinite universe, simulation, omnipotence, objective morality, AGI limits, and infinite economic growth are not all settled in the same way. They require careful definitions, domain-specific evidence, and humility about what has actually been proved.
