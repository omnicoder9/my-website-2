Deep Time: How Geology Broke the Biblical Clock

For most of Western history, the Earth was young enough to fit inside a genealogy. Then two Scotsmen, a Victorian physicist, and a handful of radioactive atoms forced humanity to reckon with a planet 750,000 times older than anyone had imagined — and the argument has never fully ended.

On an autumn evening in 1788, a sixty-two-year-old Scottish farmer-turned-natural-philosopher stepped off a boat onto a wave-battered promontory on the Berwickshire coast and looked, for the first time, at the true age of the world. What James Hutton saw at Siccar Point — and what it did to the confident, comfortable, six-thousand-year-old Earth of his contemporaries — is one of the most consequential and least appreciated ruptures in the history of human thought. It compressed all of recorded civilization into a rounding error. It made Genesis, read literally, scientifically untenable. And it opened a controversy that, remarkably, is still being fought out in American courtrooms and school boards more than two centuries later.

This is the story of deep time: how it was discovered, how violently it was resisted, how it was nearly strangled in its crib by a brilliant physicist who got the physics wrong, how a fortuitous accident of nuclear physics rescued it, and why — unlike almost every other settled question in the physical sciences — it has never stopped being a fight.

4004 BC • 165001

The World Was Six Thousand Years Old

To understand why deep time was scandalous, you first have to understand how confident, how rigorous, and how mainstream the young-Earth consensus was before geology existed as a discipline. The best-known version of that consensus belongs to James Ussher, Archbishop of Armagh and Primate of All Ireland, whose Annales Veteris Testamenti (1650) computed that the first day of Creation began at nightfall preceding 23 October 4004 BC.1

Ussher's date is routinely caricatured as the work of a credulous cleric counting begats on his fingers. The reality is less flattering to modern smugness. Ussher was one of the most accomplished scholars of his generation, and the great majority of his chronology — by most estimates five-sixths of it — was reconstructed not from Genesis but from secular history: Ptolemy's canon of Babylonian kings, Greek and Roman historiography, astronomical tables, and the fixed, externally verifiable death date of Nebuchadnezzar II in 562 BC, from which he worked backward through the unbroken genealogies of Genesis 5 and 11.2 The work ran to more than 1,600 pages and cited roughly 12,000 sources.2 Whatever else it was, it was not lazy.

Nor was it eccentric. This is the detail modern readers most often miss: independent Christian and Jewish scholars working from comparable methods and comparable texts kept landing within a few centuries of the same number. Bede proposed 3952 BC; the Rabbinic scholar Jose ben Halafta, 3761 BC; Joseph Scaliger, 3949 BC; Johannes Kepler, 3992 BC; Isaac Newton, roughly 4000 BC.1 This was not one man's idiosyncrasy. It was a broad scholarly convergence, reinforced by a specific theological expectation: a reading of 2 Peter 3:8 (“one day is with the Lord as a thousand years”) that mapped the six days of Creation onto a six-thousand-year lifespan for the world, with Christ arriving at its midpoint.1 A young Earth was not a fringe belief in 1650. It was the considered, cross-checked position of European learning.

It mattered institutionally, too. Starting in 1701 the Church of England printed Ussher’s dates in the margins of official editions of the King James Bible, and from there they migrated into the enormously influential Scofield Reference Bible and countless family Bibles.3 For roughly two centuries, opening almost any English-language Bible to Genesis 1 meant reading, in print, right beside the text: 4004 BC. The young Earth was not merely a doctrine. It was typeset.

This is the world that geology was about to demolish — not by attacking Genesis directly, but by doing something more subversive: taking rocks seriously as physical evidence and following that evidence wherever the arithmetic led.

1669 • 177802

Cracks in the Chronology

The tools that would eventually break Ussher’s chronology were forged more than a century before anyone dared use them for that purpose. In 1669, the Danish anatomist-turned-geologist Nicolas Steno published a slim treatise, De solido intra solidum naturaliter contento dissertationis prodromus, based on his study of rock exposures in Tuscany.4 In it he laid down what are still taught today as the founding principles of stratigraphy: that sedimentary layers are deposited horizontally (original horizontality), that they extend continuously until they thin out or hit a barrier (lateral continuity), and — most consequentially — that in an undisturbed sequence, the layer on the bottom was deposited first and is therefore the oldest, with each successive layer younger than the one beneath it (superposition).4

Steno's laws sound almost too obvious to need stating. That is precisely their power: they gave naturalists a rigorous, repeatable method for reading relative chronology directly out of the rock itself, independent of any text. Steno did not use them to challenge Scripture — he was a religious convert who ended his life as a Catholic bishop — but the method he built would eventually make a young Earth impossible to sustain once enough rock had been read.5

The first person to put a number to that unease, rather than just a method, was Georges-Louis Leclerc, Comte de Buffon. In his 1778 Les époques de la nature, Buffon heated iron spheres of varying sizes, measured how long they took to cool to the touch, and extrapolated the result to a planet-sized ball of molten rock — arriving at an estimated age for the Earth of roughly 75,000 years.5 Seventy-five thousand is a modest number by modern standards, but at the time it was a seismic multiple of the accepted figure, and Buffon knew exactly how radioactive — in the reputational sense — his claim was. He reportedly suspected the true figure was far larger still, and softened his published estimate accordingly; the Sorbonne's theology faculty forced him to sign a retraction regardless.5

By the late eighteenth century, then, two things were true simultaneously: the intellectual tools for reading deep time out of rock strata already existed, and the first person to use even a crude physical method to estimate the Earth’s age had been forced into public retreat. What geology needed was not a new technique. It needed someone willing to follow Steno’s logic all the way to its unbounded conclusion, in public, and defend it.

1785 • 179703

Hutton at Siccar Point

James Hutton was, on paper, an unlikely revolutionary. Trained in medicine at Edinburgh, Paris, and Leiden, he never practiced as a physician; instead he ran a farm in Berwickshire for over a decade, improving its drainage and soil, before retiring to Edinburgh to devote himself to chemistry, mineralogy, and the circle of the Scottish Enlightenment — Adam Smith, the chemist Joseph Black, and the mathematician John Playfair were among his closest friends. It was farming, by Hutton’s own account, that first forced him to think seriously about how slowly soil forms and how patiently rock erodes.

Hutton read his theory to the Royal Society of Edinburgh in two sessions, on 7 March and 4 April 1785, under the title Theory of the Earth; or an Investigation of the Laws Observable in the Composition, Dissolution, and Restoration of Land upon the Globe.6 The published version appeared in the Society’s Transactions in 1788 and was expanded into a two-volume work in 1795.7 Its argument, stripped to its skeleton, was this: the continents are continuously worn down by rain, rivers, and frost into sediment; that sediment is carried to the sea and deposited in horizontal layers, exactly as Steno had described; heat and pressure inside the Earth consolidate those layers into rock and periodically thrust them back upward into new land, tilting and folding them in the process; and the new continents are then, in turn, worn down again. Hutton called the engine behind this cycle subterranean heat, pointing to volcanoes and hot springs as its visible expression — a strikingly modern intuition, arrived at two centuries before plate tectonics supplied the actual mechanism.8

This put Hutton at odds with the dominant theory of the age, Abraham Werner’s Neptunism, which held that virtually all rock — including basalt and granite — had crystallized out of a single primordial global ocean, a framework far more congenial to a biblical flood narrative. Hutton’s rival system, which came to be called Plutonism for its emphasis on subterranean heat, required something Neptunism did not: an effectively unlimited amount of time for repeated cycles of erosion, deposition, consolidation, and uplift to run their course.6 A theory is only as credible as the evidence behind it, and Hutton needed a single, unambiguous outcrop that could not be explained any other way.

He found it in 1788, at Siccar Point, a spray-lashed headland on the Berwickshire coast, which he reached by boat along with the chemist James Hall and his friend John Playfair.7 What the three men saw was a junction between two utterly different rock formations: below, near-vertical beds of grey Silurian greywacke, laid down in deep ocean roughly 435 million years ago, later uplifted, tilted onto their sides, and eroded flat; and resting directly on top of that eroded surface, nearly horizontal beds of younger red Devonian sandstone.9 Modern dating puts the gap in time represented by that single surface — the interval during which the tilted greywacke was uplifted, planed flat by erosion, and then submerged again to receive new sediment — at roughly sixty-five million years.10 Hutton, of course, had no radiometric ages to put on the two layers. He did not need them. The geometry alone — one set of rocks tilted to the vertical, worn flat, and then buried under a second, undisturbed set — was proof that an entire cycle of Hutton’s rock machine, deposition, consolidation, uplift, tilting, and renewed erosion, had run to completion before the second layer had even begun to form. And that was only one such junction among the countless others visible in the geological record. If one required sixty-five million years of missing time, how many such gaps might the whole of the Earth’s rock record contain?

Playfair, recalling the visit decades later, put the psychological effect of that afternoon into a sentence that has become one of the most quoted lines in the history of science:

“The mind seemed to grow giddy by looking so far into the abyss of time.”John Playfair, on the excursion to Siccar Point, 17887

Hutton’s own conclusion, stated in the dry, careful prose of Theory of the Earth, was more restrained in tone but even more radical in substance. Having laid out his case that the same processes of erosion, deposition, and uplift visible today must have operated, essentially unchanged, for cycle after cycle stretching back through the rock record, he closed with a sentence that would become the founding motto of geological deep time:

“The result, therefore, of this physical enquiry is that we find no vestige of a beginning, no prospect of an end.”James Hutton, Theory of the Earth, 178811

It is worth being precise about what Hutton was and was not claiming. He was not asserting that the Earth was literally eternal in a theological sense, and he was careful elsewhere to frame his system as evidence of a wise, providential design rather than an attack on it. What he was asserting, as a strictly empirical matter, was that geological processes furnished no observable marker — no visible starting line and no visible finish line — within the rock record itself. Whatever the Earth’s true age was, it was long enough that the ordinary, observable rates of erosion and deposition could not resolve a beginning or an end. That is a claim about the limits of geological evidence, and it turned out to be exactly right: Hutton could not date the Earth numerically, and neither, as later chapters of this story show, could anyone else for another century and a half. But he had already shown that whatever number eventually emerged, it would not be measured in thousands of years. It would be measured in a currency nobody in 1788 had a name for yet: deep time.

Hutton died in 1797, his prose famously dense and his book little read outside a small circle.9 His ideas might well have stalled there had John Playfair not, in 1802, published Illustrations of the Huttonian Theory of the Earth — a lucid, readable restatement that did for Hutton what Hutton could never do for himself. But the popularization that actually carried deep time into the Victorian mainstream, and into direct collision with Genesis, belonged to a different geologist entirely, working a generation later. Before reaching him, though, it is worth pausing on exactly how hard the establishment pushed back.

1795 • 183004

The Establishment Strikes Back

Hutton’s theory did not conquer geology on contact. For the next four decades the dominant framework, especially in Britain, was something closer to its opposite: catastrophism, the view that the Earth’s surface and its fossil record had been shaped primarily by sudden, violent, short-lived events rather than by slow, continuous processes. Its most rigorous proponent was not a theologian but one of the sharpest comparative anatomists in Europe.

Working in the gypsum quarries of the Paris Basin with the mineralogist Alexandre Brongniart, Georges Cuvier established, over a series of publications culminating in his 1813 Essay on the Theory of the Earth, something that had been merely suspected before him: that entire species — not just individual populations, but whole categories of large animal — had genuinely ceased to exist.12 This was not a small claim in 1813. Many naturalists still assumed that any bones belonging to an unfamiliar creature simply meant the living species had not yet been found in some unexplored corner of the globe. Cuvier’s meticulous comparative anatomy of mammoth, mastodon, and giant ground-sloth remains closed off that possibility and made extinction a scientific fact rather than a speculative embarrassment.12

To explain why the fossil record showed one distinct assemblage of species abruptly replaced by another, again and again, moving up through the strata, Cuvier proposed a sequence of sudden regional “revolutions” — catastrophic floods and upheavals that exterminated local faunas, after which new species migrated in from unaffected regions.13 Crucially, Cuvier himself was cautious: his revolutions were regional in scope and he never explicitly equated his most recent one with the biblical Deluge. That equation was made for him. His English editor and translator, Robert Jameson, and Jameson’s Oxford ally William Buckland took catastrophism, broadened it into a single global event, and welded it directly onto Genesis.13

Buckland’s 1823 Reliquiae Diluvianae — “Relics of the Flood” — presented cave deposits at Kirkdale in Yorkshire, containing the jumbled bones of hyenas, elephants, and rhinoceroses, as physical proof of a single universal deluge that had swept the animals of an antediluvian world into their present resting places.14 The approach, later labeled diluvialism, was for a time the semi-official geological orthodoxy at Oxford, where Buckland held the university’s first readership in geology. It was not crude apologetics dressed up as fieldwork; Buckland was a genuine, careful field geologist, and diluvialism briefly commanded real scientific respect as well as religious comfort.

On Terminology Neither “catastrophism” nor its rival “uniformitarianism” were the coinages of the geologists who practiced them. Both terms were minted in 1832 by the historian and philosopher of science William Whewell, in an anonymous review of Charles Lyell’s Principles of Geology for the Quarterly Review1514 — a reminder that the tidy two-camp framing of this debate is itself a piece of nineteenth-century rhetoric, sharper and more binary than the messier reality of how working geologists actually moved between positions as evidence accumulated.

That messier reality mattered for those trying to hold Genesis and geology together without abandoning either. Two harmonizing strategies emerged among geologically literate believers who had already accepted, in some form, that the Earth was older than 4004 BC. The day-age theory read each of the six “days” of Genesis 1 as standing for a vast geological era rather than a literal twenty-four hours. The gap theory instead inserted an unspecified, geologically enormous interval between Genesis 1:1 (“In the beginning God created the heaven and the earth”) and 1:2, preserving a literal six-day re-creation while quietly relocating almost the entirety of deep time into a wordless gap between two verses.14 Buckland himself toyed with the gap theory before committing more fully to flood geology.14 Both strategies are worth noting for what they concede: by the 1820s, a serious Earth scientist who also wished to remain a serious biblical literalist already needed some mechanism for smuggling millions of years into the text. Ussher’s clean six thousand years no longer fit the rocks, even for many of geology’s most committed religious defenders.

Buckland himself eventually abandoned flood geology altogether, persuaded not by Hutton’s heirs but by a visit to the Swiss Alps, where Louis Agassiz demonstrated the scouring, boulder-transporting power of glacial ice firsthand — convincing Buckland that many of the deposits he had once read as flood debris were in fact the leavings of ancient glaciers.13 Catastrophism’s most influential British champion had, within his own lifetime, watched one of his own signature arguments dissolve under new field evidence — a small, instructive preview of how this entire controversy would keep resolving itself: not by decree, but by whichever side kept its explanations tethered most tightly to what the rocks actually showed.

Lyell, Darwin, and the Triumph of Gradualism

The geologist who finally tipped the balance decisively toward Hutton’s side had, ironically, studied under Buckland at Oxford. Charles Lyell published Principles of Geology: Being an Attempt to Explain the Former Changes of the Earth’s Surface, by Reference to Causes Now in Operation in three volumes between January 1830 and May 1833.16 The subtitle was, in effect, Lyell’s entire argument compressed into a single clause: everything visible in the rock record, however dramatic, could be explained by causes still observable and still operating today, at rates no greater than those we can presently measure. Where Hutton had sketched the theory in dense, difficult prose that almost no one outside a small Edinburgh circle actually read, Lyell built it into a rigorous, exhaustively evidenced, and genuinely readable synthesis — effectively relaunching Huttonian uniformitarianism for an entire generation of scientists who had never encountered the original.16

Lyell was also, pointedly, arguing against his own teacher. Much of the Principles functions as a systematic rebuttal of Buckland’s diluvial interpretations, which Lyell dismissed as “non-actualistic” — invoking forces and rates not observable in the present-day world, and therefore, in his view, outside the bounds of legitimate science.17 By 1830, Lyell judged, very few working geologists still accepted Buckland’s single-flood interpretation of the diluvium in any case; the Principles delivered the closing argument.

The book’s most consequential reader turned out not to be a geologist at all. When the twenty-two-year-old Charles Darwin sailed from Plymouth aboard HMS Beagle in December 1831, Captain Robert FitzRoy gave him a copy of Lyell’s first volume; Darwin had the second mailed to him at a port of call during the voyage.16 Darwin later credited the book with fundamentally reorganizing how he saw landscape and time — he described reading it as learning to see the world “through Lyell’s eyes.” Within weeks of landfall, at the Cape Verde island of Santiago, Darwin found a band of fossil-bearing marine limestone lifted high into a sea cliff, and interpreted it, exactly as Lyell would have, as evidence of slow, ongoing uplift rather than a single catastrophic event.17 Uniformitarian deep time was no longer just a geological doctrine Darwin had read about. It was now the lens through which he read every rock face he encountered for the next five years.

That lens turned out to be a precondition for the theory Darwin would spend the next two decades building. Natural selection, as Darwin conceived it, works by the gradual accumulation of minute, individually almost imperceptible variations across an enormous number of generations. A young Earth of six thousand years offers nowhere near enough generations for that process to produce anything like the diversity of life on Earth. An old, uniformitarian Earth — Hutton and Lyell’s Earth, with no vestige of a beginning and no prospect of an end — offered, for the first time, a canvas large enough for the theory to actually work. Deep time did not just happen to coincide with the rise of evolutionary biology. It was the enabling condition for it.

By the time Darwin published On the Origin of Species in 1859, uniformitarian geology and evolutionary biology had become mutually reinforcing pillars of the same intellectual revolution — each one demanding, and each one supplying evidence for, an Earth immeasurably older than 4004 BC. It would take exactly one more decade for the most serious scientific challenge to that alliance to arrive, and it would come from neither a geologist nor a bishop, but from the most respected physicist in the English-speaking world.

1862 • 189706

Kelvin’s Challenge

Darwin, to his later regret, was the one who first put a specific number on geological deep time and handed his critics a target. In the first edition of On the Origin of Species, hoping to convey some sense of the timescales his theory required, he calculated that the erosion of the Weald — a chalk anticline exposed across Kent, Surrey, and Sussex — must have taken roughly 300 million years.18 The estimate was little more than an illustrative guess, built on shaky assumptions about erosion rates, and Darwin knew it; under sustained criticism he progressively softened the claim in later editions before removing the calculation altogether.18 But the number had already done its damage by existing at all. It gave the emerging discipline of thermodynamic physics, then at the height of its Victorian prestige, a concrete figure to aim at.

The man who aimed at it was William Thomson, the Glasgow physicist later ennobled as Lord Kelvin, and by the 1860s arguably the single most authoritative physical scientist in Britain. Kelvin’s attack was elegant, quantitative, and devastating in a way that fossil-based geological reasoning simply could not match on its own terms. Modeling the Earth as a sphere that had begun in a molten state and had been losing heat by conduction ever since, and using the observed present-day rate at which temperature increases with depth in mines and boreholes, Kelvin calculated in 1862 how long the planet must have taken to cool to its current state. His answer: somewhere between 20 million and 400 million years, with a preferred figure near 100 million.19

Even the top of that range fell an order of magnitude short of what Darwin’s Weald calculation had implied, and Kelvin did not relent. Working through further refinements in the following decades, and reinforced by an independent estimate from Darwin’s own son, the astronomer George Darwin, who calculated that tidal friction between the Earth and Moon implied a minimum age of about 56 million years, Kelvin steadily tightened his range.20 By 1897 his final published verdict had narrowed to “more than 20 and less than 40 million years, and probably much nearer 20 than 40.”20 Twenty million years is not enough time for gradual, Darwinian natural selection to plausibly generate the diversity of life on Earth, and both Kelvin and Darwin understood the collision perfectly well. Kelvin used his authority in physics to declare the matter essentially settled against the geologists and biologists; the physicist Thomas Huxley, Darwin’s most combative public defender, retorted that Kelvin’s mathematics might be unimpeachable while his premises remained entirely open to question — a distinction that would prove exactly correct, though not for another seven years.

Not everyone in physics agreed with him. In 1895 the engineer John Perry, a former assistant of Kelvin’s, published a sharp technical challenge arguing that Kelvin’s assumption of a rigid, uniformly conductive Earth was almost certainly wrong — that convection within a partially fluid interior could move heat far more efficiently than simple conduction, which would allow for a much older planet without contradicting the surface heat-flow measurements.20 Perry was, in the broad outline, correct, though the full mechanism he was groping toward — mantle convection driven partly by an internal heat source Kelvin did not know existed — would not be confirmed for decades. Kelvin’s physics was rigorous. His premises, as Huxley had guessed, were not simply wrong; they were catastrophically incomplete. He had built an exact, defensible model of the wrong Earth — one with no internal source of heat beyond its primordial formation. Nature, as it happened, had hidden a second furnace inside the planet, one that no thermometer of Kelvin’s era could have detected, because it had not yet been discovered at all.

1896 • 195607

The Second Furnace

The resolution to Kelvin’s challenge arrived from a direction almost nobody in geology or biology was watching: a French physics laboratory studying uranium salts. Henri Becquerel discovered radioactivity by accident in the spring of 1896, when photographic plates stored near a uranium compound fogged despite being wrapped in opaque paper.21 For several years the discovery remained a laboratory curiosity, of interest mainly to physicists. Then, on 16 March 1903, Pierre Curie and his assistant Albert Laborde announced something that changed everything for Kelvin’s cooling-Earth model: radium salts continuously release heat, on their own, indefinitely, with no external energy input at all.21 Kelvin’s entire calculation rested on the assumption that the Earth had no internal heat source beyond the leftover warmth of its formation. Curie and Laborde had just found, sitting inside ordinary rock, a source that had been running the whole time.

The physicist who connected the two facts in public, in the most theatrically satisfying way possible, was Ernest Rutherford. In 1904, Rutherford was invited to lecture at the Royal Institution in London on the new physics of radioactivity — and Lord Kelvin himself, by then approaching eighty and still the most formidable authority in British physics, was seated in the audience. Rutherford later described the moment with evident relish:

“I came into the room, which was half dark, and presently spotted Lord Kelvin in the audience, and realized that I was in for trouble at the last part of my speech dealing with the age of the earth, where my views conflicted with his. To my relief he fell fast asleep, but as I came to the important point, I saw the old bird sit up, open an eye and cock a baleful glance at me! Then sudden inspiration came, and I said Lord Kelvin had limited the age of the earth, provided no new source of heat was discovered. That prophetic utterance refers to what we are now considering tonight, radium! Behold! The old boy beamed upon me.”Ernest Rutherford, recalling his 1904 Royal Institution lecture22

The line was diplomatic theater, but the underlying physics was real: Kelvin’s model had been rigorously correct about a planet with no internal heat source, and radioactive decay in the Earth’s crust and mantle was exactly such a source, hiding in plain sight the entire time. Rutherford saw immediately that the same phenomenon undermining Kelvin’s argument also handed geology something it had never had before: an actual clock. Radioactive elements decay into stable “daughter” products at fixed, measurable rates, entirely unaffected by heat, pressure, or chemical environment. Measure the ratio of parent atoms remaining to daughter atoms accumulated in a mineral, and — given the decay rate — the age of that mineral falls directly out of the arithmetic.

The chemist who first turned that insight into a working method was Bertram Boltwood at Yale. Boltwood focused on the decay of uranium into lead, reasoning that the accumulated lead-to-uranium ratio in a mineral should climb steadily and measurably with age. In a 1907 paper in the American Journal of Science, he applied the method to more than forty mineral samples gathered from around the world, publishing ages that ranged from about 410 million to a startling 2.2 billion years for a sample of thorianite from Ceylon — a figure roughly an order of magnitude beyond even Kelvin’s original, most generous upper bound.23 Boltwood’s decay-rate constants were still crude — the true half-life of uranium-238, at roughly 4.5 billion years, would not be nailed down for decades — but the method itself, and the order of magnitude it revealed, was sound.

Switched from physics to geology after reading Boltwood, and spent the next five decades refining uranium–lead dating into a working geological timescale — from 370 million years for a single Norwegian rock in 1911 to a lifetime spent pushing the whole Earth’s age steadily older.24

Boltwood’s convert was a young physics-trained student named Arthur Holmes, who switched fields to geology specifically to pursue the implications of uranium–lead dating.24 Working with nepheline syenite samples from Norway, Holmes published, in 1911, a uranium–lead age of 370 million years for rock of known Devonian age — recalculating and correcting several of Boltwood’s earlier figures in the process — and in 1913 assembled the first genuinely quantitative geological timescale in his book The Age of the Earth.24 Holmes would spend the rest of a five-decade career refining the method and steadily revising his own estimate of the Earth’s age upward, from roughly 1.6 billion years in 1913 to about 3.4 billion years by the late 1940s, as decay constants improved and measurement techniques grew more precise.

The figure that finally held was published in 1956 by Clair Patterson, a geochemist at Caltech who had spent years wrestling with a maddening practical problem: airborne industrial lead contamination was seeping into every laboratory sample he tried to measure, anywhere on Earth, corrupting the very isotope ratios he needed to read cleanly.25 Patterson’s solution was to build one of the first ultra-clean laboratories in the history of science — acid-washing every instrument, distilling every reagent, sealing the room against outside air — effectively inventing the modern cleanroom in order to get a trustworthy reading.25 His key move was to stop trying to date Earth rocks directly, since the oldest surviving terrestrial rock is always younger than the planet itself, and instead to date meteorites — primordial material assumed to have formed at the same time as the Earth and the rest of the solar system.26 Using lead isotope ratios measured in the Canyon Diablo iron meteorite, the object that carved Arizona’s Meteor Crater, together with several stony meteorites, Patterson calculated an age of 4.55 billion years, with an uncertainty of only about 70 million years.27

That number has not been meaningfully overturned since. Seventy years of subsequent measurement, using entirely different instruments, different laboratories, and samples returned from the Moon and other meteorites, have narrowed Patterson’s uncertainty from ±70 million years down to roughly ±20 million years without shifting the central figure at all.26 The modern, formally adopted age of the Earth — 4.54 billion years — is, in effect, Clair Patterson’s number.

1956 • today08

Deep Time Enshrined

With Patterson’s figure corroborated by decades of subsequent measurement, geologists formalized the modern geologic timescale as a nested hierarchy — eons, divided into eras, divided into periods, divided into epochs — each boundary pinned, wherever possible, to a radiometrically dated horizon in the rock. At the coarsest level, Earth’s history is divided into four eons. The Hadean, from the planet’s formation roughly 4.6 billion years ago until about 4 billion years ago, was a world of frequent asteroid bombardment and a largely molten surface, predating any reliable fossil record. The Archean, from 4 billion to 2.5 billion years ago, saw the first stable continental crust and the earliest evidence of life. The Proterozoic, from 2.5 billion to 541 million years ago, saw the Great Oxidation Event transform the atmosphere and the rise of complex, eukaryotic, eventually multicellular life. And the Phanerozoic — Greek for “visible life” — runs from 541 million years ago to the present, and is the only eon with an abundant, easily read fossil record.28

That last fact is worth sitting with. The three eons that precede the Phanerozoic — collectively called the Precambrian — account for roughly eighty-eight percent of the Earth’s entire history.29 Nearly everything most people picture when they hear the phrase “geologic time” — trilobites, coal forests, dinosaurs, saber-toothed cats — belongs to the remaining twelve percent. Even the discipline’s own popular imagination undersells how much of deep time there actually is.

What matters historically is not merely that geology got a bigger number. It is that deep time turned out to be load-bearing infrastructure for nearly every other historical science that followed it. Evolutionary biology needed it first and most obviously, as the previous chapters have shown. But plate tectonics, formalized only in the 1960s, needed hundreds of millions of years for continents measured drifting at a few centimeters per year to arrive at their present configuration from a single supercontinent. Astrophysics needed a Sun whose age, once nuclear fusion was understood in the 1920s, turned out to be consistent with — and give independent support to — the same multi-billion-year figure geology had already converged on. Once radiometric dating supplied a reliable clock, deep time stopped being a controversial hypothesis and became a shared unit of measurement, silently underwriting fields that had nothing to do with rocks at all.

The Controversy That Never Ended

Inside professional science, the story more or less stops there: deep time has not been a live scientific controversy since the mid-twentieth century. Outside it, the argument that began with Hutton’s unconformity has never actually closed.

Its most theatrical early skirmish came in 1925, at the Scopes trial in Dayton, Tennessee, where the defense attorney Clarence Darrow put the three-time presidential candidate and biblical literalist William Jennings Bryan on the witness stand and pressed him, in front of a packed courthouse lawn, on whether he accepted Ussher’s specific chronology for the age of the Earth. Bryan hedged: “That has been the estimate of a man that is accepted today,” he allowed, before adding, “I would not say that it is accurate.”30 The exchange was widely seen as damaging to the anti-evolution cause, and for the following three decades, biblical literalism’s retreat from a young Earth looked, if anything, to be accelerating. By the 1950s most evangelical scientists themselves had grown openly skeptical of flood geology; the American Scientific Affiliation, an evangelical scientific society founded in 1941, drifted steadily toward accepting an ancient Earth through progressive creationism and theistic evolution.31

Then, in February 1961, the retreat reversed. John C. Whitcomb, an Old Testament scholar, and Henry M. Morris, a hydraulic engineer, published The Genesis Flood: The Biblical Record and Its Scientific Implications, reviving and systematizing flood geology into a comprehensive alternative framework: essentially the entire sedimentary rock record, they argued, had been deposited in a single globe-spanning catastrophe during Noah’s year-long flood, with the appearance of great geological age built into the world from the start.32 The historian of science Ronald Numbers, who was not sympathetic to the book’s claims, nonetheless credited it with elevating young-Earth creationism “to a position of fundamentalist orthodoxy” it had not held in decades.31 Around 1970, the movement Whitcomb and Morris had reignited began describing itself with a deliberately scientific-sounding label: “scientific creationism,” later “creation science.”33

That relabeling turned out to matter enormously, because it set up the movement’s next confrontation: a fight not over pulpits or textbooks, but over the legal definition of science itself. In October 2004 the Dover Area School District in Pennsylvania directed its ninth-grade biology teachers to read students a statement introducing “intelligent design” as an alternative to evolution. Eleven parents, led by Tammy Kitzmiller, sued. The resulting case, tried before Judge John E. Jones III — a Republican, appointed by President George W. Bush — ran from late September through early November 2005.34 On 20 December 2005, Jones issued a 139-page ruling finding that intelligent design was not a scientific theory but, in his words, “a religious argument” and “nothing less than the progeny of creationism,” and permanently barred the district from teaching it as science.35 By the time the ruling came down, voters in Dover had already removed every school board member who supported the policy in the preceding month’s election.35

What the Court Actually Decided Kitzmiller v. Dover did not rule on whether intelligent design is true. It ruled, as a matter of the U.S. Constitution’s Establishment Clause, that intelligent design is a religious rather than scientific proposition, and that presenting it as a scientific alternative to evolution in a public school science class therefore constitutes an unconstitutional endorsement of religion.35 The distinction matters: the case settled a legal question about public education, not the underlying scientific one — which, inside geology and biology, had already been settled for the better part of a century.

And in the general public, unlike in the peer-reviewed literature, the argument has simply never resolved. Gallup has tracked American beliefs about human origins since 1982, using a three-way question that asks whether God created humans in their present form within roughly the last ten thousand years; whether humans developed over millions of years with God guiding the process; or whether humans developed over millions of years with no involvement from God at all. In Gallup’s most recent reading, from May 2024, 37 percent of American adults chose strict creationism — the lowest share in the poll’s four-decade history, but still the single largest of the three groups — while 34 percent chose God-guided evolution and 24 percent, a new high, chose evolution without any divine involvement.36

The split tracks religious observance closely: among Americans who attend religious services weekly, 61 percent hold the strict creationist view, versus 3 percent who accept evolution with no role for God; among those with a college degree, the numbers move substantially the other way, though creationism still draws more support (26 percent) than fully naturalistic evolution (30 percent) among that group only by a narrow margin.36 Four centuries after Ussher, and seven decades after Clair Patterson closed the scientific question to a working tolerance of about twenty million years on either side of 4.54 billion, more than one in three American adults still place the origin of humanity within the same order of magnitude Ussher did.

Why It Still Upsets Us

The phrase this article has been using throughout — “deep time” itself — is startlingly recent. It was not coined by Hutton, or Lyell, or Patterson, but by the writer John McPhee, in his 1981 book Basin and Range, the first installment of what would become his Pulitzer Prize–winning geological travelogue Annals of the Former World.37 McPhee had put his finger on something that two centuries of geologists had struggled with as a communication problem even after they had solved it as a scientific one:

“Numbers do not seem to work well with regard to deep time. Any number above a couple of thousand years — fifty thousand, fifty million — will with nearly equal effect awe the imagination to the point of paralysis.”John McPhee, Basin and Range, 198137

McPhee’s own solution was not another number but a gesture: stretch your arms out to your sides, he suggested, and let the entire history of the Earth run from the tip of one middle finger to the tip of the other. On that scale, a single stroke of a medium-grained nail file across the nail of one middle finger erases the whole of human history.37 This is, in essence, the same maneuver behind this article’s own Figure 1 and Figure 4: not a better calculation, but a better trick for smuggling a genuinely inhuman quantity past a nervous system that evolved to track the passage of days, seasons, and at most a few generations. McPhee did not discover deep time. He gave the rest of us, for the first time, functional bodily furniture to think about it, and geologists adopted his phrase so completely that it now returns tens of thousands of results in the scientific literature.38

That difficulty is not merely rhetorical. The paleontologist and historian of science Stephen Jay Gould, in his 1987 study Time’s Arrow, Time’s Cycle, situated the discovery of geological deep time within a lineage Sigmund Freud had proposed decades earlier: a short list of scientific revolutions that each forced humanity to surrender a cherished form of self-importance — Copernicus displacing Earth from the center of the cosmos, Darwin displacing humanity from a separate act of creation, and Freud himself displacing the conscious mind from full command of its own behavior.39 Deep time, on Gould’s reading, delivers a related but distinct demotion, one more geological than cosmological or psychological: not simply that humans are not physically central, and not simply that humans share ancestry with other animals, but that the entire span of human existence — every dynasty, every war, every book, Ussher’s entire chronology and everything before and after it — occupies a fraction of Earth history too small for this article’s own Figure 1 to render visibly until the fourth successive zoom.

That, in the end, is the real and durable source of the controversy this article has traced from a Berwickshire cliff to a Pennsylvania courtroom. Deep time was never really a dispute about rock strata, or decay constants, or the precise wording of Genesis 1. It was, and for thirty-seven percent of Americans still is, a dispute about how small a claim on the universe’s attention human history is entitled to make. James Hutton looked into that abyss in 1788 and, in a single restrained sentence, refused to flinch from what he saw: no vestige of a beginning, no prospect of an end. Two hundred and thirty-eight years later, most of the species he wrote it for still has not entirely made its peace with the view.
