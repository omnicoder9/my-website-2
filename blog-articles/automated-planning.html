Introduction

Automated planning is the branch of artificial intelligence concerned with constructing sequences of actions that transform an initial world state into one satisfying a set of goal conditions.[1] Unlike reactive systems that respond to immediate percepts, a planner reasons explicitly about future consequences, searching through a space of possible action sequences to identify one that achieves its objectives. This capacity for deliberative foresight distinguishes planning systems from stimulus-response architectures and positions them at the centre of autonomous agent design.

The distinction between planning and scheduling is frequently blurred in practice but admits a principled separation. Planning determines what actions to perform and in what logical order, while scheduling assigns those actions to resources and time intervals subject to capacity, duration, and precedence constraints.[2] In industrial deployments — manufacturing, logistics, space mission operations — both processes are deeply coupled, but the algorithmic foundations of each field developed substantially independently before converging in integrated systems such as NASA's Remote Agent Experiment.[3]

This article provides a formal treatment of classical planning algorithms, covering the theoretical properties, pseudocode formulations, and practical implementations of forward chaining state-space search, backward chaining (goal regression), and partial-order planning. The Sussman Anomaly is examined as a canonical case study illustrating the limitations of total-order planners. Contemporary tools, libraries, and research directions are surveyed throughout.

Historical Development

The intellectual lineage of automated planning reaches back to early cybernetics and the first symbolic AI programs of the late 1950s. The General Problem Solver (GPS), developed by Newell, Shaw, and Simon in 1959, introduced means-ends analysis as a method for reducing the difference between a current state and a goal state by selecting operators applicable to that difference.[4] Although GPS was not a planner in the modern sense, it established the idea of goal-directed symbolic search that would underpin subsequent systems.

The theoretical complexity of classical planning was placed on firm ground by Bylander, who proved that determining plan existence is PSPACE-complete in the general case and NP-complete when plans are bounded in length.[13] This complexity-theoretic understanding motivated the heuristic approaches that dominate modern practice.

Formal Representations

The STRIPS Formalism

The Stanford Research Institute Problem Solver (STRIPS), introduced by Fikes and Nilsson in 1971, established the representational foundation upon which classical planning algorithms operate.[5] A STRIPS planning problem is a tuple ⟨F, O, I, G⟩ where:

STRIPS Tuple Components

F — a finite set of ground atomic formulae (fluents) constituting the fact language.
O — a finite set of operators (actions), each with preconditions, add list, and delete list drawn from F.
I ⊆ F — the initial state, the set of facts true at time zero; the closed-world assumption holds: facts not in I are false.
G ⊆ F — the goal specification, a set of facts all of which must be true in the final state.

For an operator o ∈ O, applying o to state s is possible only when Pre(o) ⊆ s. The resulting successor state is s' = (s \ Del(o)) ∪ Add(o).[1] A plan is a finite sequence of operators π = ⟨o₁, o₂, …, oₙ⟩ whose sequential application to I produces a state containing G.

PDDL — The Planning Domain Definition Language

PDDL was introduced by McDermott and colleagues in 1998 to provide a standard interchange language for the AIPS Planning Competition.[11] It separates the problem specification into a domain file describing action schemas with lifted (variablised) preconditions and effects, and a problem file specifying concrete objects, initial facts, and goals.

    :precondition (and (clear ?x) (on-table ?x) (arm-empty))

                       (not (clear ?x))
                       (not (on-table ?x))
                       (not (arm-empty))))

  (:action put-down
    :parameters  (?x - block)
    :precondition (holding ?x)
    :effect       (and (clear ?x) (on-table ?x) (arm-empty)
                       (not (holding ?x))))

  ;; Stack block x onto block y
  (:action stack
    :parameters  (?x - block ?y - block)
    :precondition (and (holding ?x) (clear ?y))
    :effect       (and (on ?x ?y) (clear ?x) (arm-empty)

  (:action unstack
    :parameters  (?x - block ?y - block)
    :precondition (and (on ?x ?y) (clear ?x) (arm-empty))
    :effect       (and (holding ?x) (clear ?y)
                       (not (on ?x ?y)) (not (clear ?x))
                       (not (arm-empty)))))

PDDL has been extended through successive versions: PDDL2.1 introduced numeric fluents and plan metrics; PDDL2.2 added timed initial literals; PDDL3.0 added trajectory constraints and preferences; and PDDL+ extended the language with continuous change and processes modelled by differential equations.[14]

Forward Chaining State-Space Search

Forward chaining state-space search, also termed progression planning, explores the space of world states reachable from the initial state by progressive application of applicable operators, terminating when a state satisfying the goal is reached or the state space is exhausted.[1] The algorithm is conceptually equivalent to classical graph search — indeed, BFS or A* applied to the state-transition graph — with the planning-specific details confined to the branching factor (which actions are applicable in a given state) and the goal test.

"The forward search approach is simple, complete, and can be made efficient through powerful domain-independent heuristics derived from relaxed problem formulations."

Bonet & Geffner, 2001[15]

Algorithm Specification

Algorithm 1 — Forward State-Space Search (Best-First)
Input: Planning problem P = ⟨F, O, I, G⟩, heuristic function h
Output: A plan π or FAILURE

1. open ← priority queue ordered by f(n) = g(n) + h(n)
2. closed ← ∅
3. Enqueue( open, node(state=I, path=⟨⟩, g=0) )
4. while open ≠ ∅ do
5. n ← Dequeue(open)
6. if n.state ⊇ G then return n.path // goal test
7. if n.state ∈ closed then continue
8. closed ← closed ∪ { n.state }
9. for each o ∈ O such that Pre(o) ⊆ n.state do
10. s' ← (n.state \ Del(o)) ∪ Add(o)
11. g' ← n.g + cost(o)
12. Enqueue( open, node(s', n.path⊕⟨o⟩, g') )
13. return FAILURE

Completeness and Optimality

When h is the zero function (uniform-cost search), forward state-space search is complete — if a solution exists, it will be found — assuming the state space is finite and all action costs are positive.[1] When h is admissible (never overestimates the true cost to the goal), the A* variant is also optimal: the first solution found has minimum cost.[16]

The Delete-Relaxation Heuristic (h⁺)

The most influential family of heuristics for forward planning derives from the delete-relaxation: a version of the problem in which delete lists of all actions are emptied, yielding a monotone approximation in which facts, once established, persist forever.[17] The optimal cost of the relaxed problem, denoted h⁺, is admissible and provides an effective search guide, though computing h⁺ exactly is NP-hard.[17] Practical planners use polynomial approximations: the additive heuristic hadd sums the costs of achieving each goal fact independently (inadmissible but informative), while hmax takes the maximum (admissible but often weak).[15]

Python 3 — Minimal Forward Chaining Planner (BFS)from collections import deque
from dataclasses import dataclass, field
from typing    import FrozenSet, List, Optional, Tuple

State = FrozenSet[str]   # a frozenset of ground fact strings

@dataclass
class Action:
    name:   str
    pre:    FrozenSet[str]
    add:    FrozenSet[str]
    delete: FrozenSet[str]

    def applicable(self, state: State) -> bool:
        return self.pre.issubset(state)

    def apply(self, state: State) -> State:
        return frozenset((state - self.delete) | self.add)

def forward_bfs(
    initial: State,
    goal:    FrozenSet[str],
    actions: List[Action],
) -> Optional[List[str]]:
    """
    Breadth-first forward search.
    Returns a list of action names forming a plan, or None if unsolvable.
    """
    # Each queue entry: (current_state, action_sequence)
    queue:  deque[Tuple[State, List[str]]] = deque([(initial, [])])
    closed: set[State] = set()

    while queue:
        state, path = queue.popleft()

        if goal.issubset(state):
            return path         # goal reached

        if state in closed:
            continue
        closed.add(state)

        for action in actions:
            if action.applicable(state):
                successor = action.apply(state)
                queue.append((successor, path + [action.name]))

    return None   # no plan found

The FF Planner and Enforced Hill-Climbing

The Fast Forward (FF) planner, introduced by Hoffmann and Nebel in 2001, became the dominant forward planner of its era by combining two innovations: an efficient relaxed-plan heuristic computed via the planning graph, and an enforced hill-climbing (EHC) procedure that aggressively pursues states with lower heuristic values, falling back to BFS only when local minima are encountered.[12] FF won first place in the satisficing track of the AIPS 2000 planning competition and established the template for subsequent heuristic planners including Fast Downward and LAMA.[18]

Backward Chaining Search

Backward chaining, also termed regression planning or goal regression, reverses the direction of search: starting from the goal specification, the algorithm identifies actions whose application could have produced the goal, computes the set of states that would necessitate each such action (the regression), and treats these as subgoals to be further regressed until the initial state is reached.[1] The key advantage over forward search is that backward search operates on the goal structure, which is typically far smaller than the reachable state space, yielding a lower effective branching factor when goals are highly constraining.

Regression Operator

For an action o with preconditions Pre(o), add list Add(o), and delete list Del(o), the regression of a goal G through o is defined as:

Regression Definition

Regress(G, o) is defined when:
   (i) G ∩ Del(o) = ∅   — the action does not undo any goal fact
   (ii) G ∩ Add(o) ≠ ∅   — the action achieves at least one goal fact

When applicable: Regress(G, o) = (G \ Add(o)) ∪ Pre(o)

The regressed set represents all facts that must be true before executing action o in order for G to hold afterwards. If the initial state I satisfies some regressed goal, a valid plan has been found by reading the chain of chosen actions in reverse order.[1]

Algorithm 2 — Backward Goal-Regression Search
Input: Planning problem P = ⟨F, O, I, G⟩
Output: A plan π or FAILURE

1. open ← {(G, ⟨⟩)} // (subgoal set, reversed plan so far)
2. closed ← ∅
3. while open ≠ ∅ do
4. (goals, path) ← pop(open)
5. if goals ⊆ I then return reverse(path) // reached initial
6. if goals ∈ closed then continue
7. closed ← closed ∪ { goals }
8. for each o ∈ O do
9. if goals ∩ Add(o) ≠ ∅ and goals ∩ Del(o) = ∅ then
10.     g' ← (goals \ Add(o)) ∪ Pre(o)
11.     push( open, (g', path ⊕ ⟨o⟩) )
12. return FAILURE

Completeness and the Regression Graph

Backward search is complete under the same conditions as forward search: a finite state space with positive action costs. However, the search space for regression is a graph over goal sets (subsets of F), not individual states, and this space can be exponentially smaller than the forward state space when goal conditions are few.[1] Conversely, when the initial state is highly constraining or goals are broad, forward search may be preferable.

Python Implementation — Goal Regression

Python 3 — Backward Goal Regression Plannerfrom typing import FrozenSet, List, Optional, Tuple
from collections import deque

GoalSet = FrozenSet[str]

def regress(goals: GoalSet, action: Action) -> Optional[GoalSet]:
    """
    Compute the regression of goals through action.
    Returns None if the action is not relevant or destroys a goal.
    """
    if not goals.intersection(action.add):
        return None   # action achieves nothing we need
    if goals.intersection(action.delete):
        return None   # action destroys something we need
    return frozenset((goals - action.add) | action.pre)

def backward_bfs(
    initial: GoalSet,
    goal:    GoalSet,
    actions: List[Action],
) -> Optional[List[str]]:
    """Breadth-first backward regression search."""
    # Each entry: (current goal set, plan so far in REVERSE order)
    queue:  deque[Tuple[GoalSet, List[str]]] = deque([(goal, [])])
    closed: set[GoalSet] = set()

    while queue:
        goals, rev_path = queue.popleft()

        # Success: all remaining goals are true in the initial state
        if goals.issubset(initial):
            return list(reversed(rev_path))

        if goals in closed:
            continue
        closed.add(goals)

        for action in actions:
            regressed = regress(goals, action)
            if regressed is not None:
                queue.append((regressed, rev_path + [action.name]))

    return None

Partial-Order Planning

Total-order planners — whether forward or backward — commit at each step to a specific sequencing of all actions. Partial-order planning (POP) adopts a least-commitment strategy: a partial plan is a set of actions connected by ordering constraints and causal links, with no more ordering imposed than is strictly necessary to avoid conflicts.[19] The resulting plan is a partial order that can be executed in any linear extension, allowing parallel or interleaved execution on distributed systems.

Plan Representation

A partial-order plan is a tuple π = ⟨A, ≺, B, L⟩ where:

Partial-Order Plan Components

A — a set of plan steps (action instances), always including designated dummy steps Ainit (adds all initial facts, no preconditions) and Agoal (requires all goal facts, no effects).

≺ — a set of ordering constraints of the form aᵢ ≺ aⱼ ("aᵢ must execute before aⱼ"), forming a strict partial order.

B — a set of variable binding constraints (for lifted planning with variables).

L — a set of causal links, each of the form aᵢ →c aⱼ meaning "aᵢ achieves condition c for aⱼ". A causal link implicitly imposes aᵢ ≺ aⱼ.

Flaws and Flaw Resolution

The POP algorithm maintains a set of flaws — conditions that prevent the partial plan from being a correct solution. Two types of flaws exist:[1]

Open preconditions: An action step aⱼ has a precondition c not yet established by any causal link. The flaw is resolved by either (i) inserting a new action aᵢ that achieves c and adding the causal link aᵢ →c aⱼ, or (ii) extending an existing causal link from an action already in the plan.

Threats: An existing causal link aᵢ →c aⱼ is threatened when a third action aₖ — which could be ordered between aᵢ and aⱼ — has c in its delete list. The flaw is resolved by either promotion (adding aⱼ ≺ aₖ) or demotion (adding aₖ ≺ aᵢ), as first described by Chapman in the TWEAK analysis.[8]

Algorithm 3 — Partial-Order Planner (SNLP-style)
Input: Planning problem P = ⟨F, O, I, G⟩
Output: A partial-order plan or FAILURE

1. π ← initial plan: {A_init, A_goal}, ≺={A_init ≺ A_goal}, L=∅
2. flaws ← open preconditions of A_goal
3. while flaws ≠ ∅ do
4. flaw ← select(flaws) // agenda selection strategy
5. if flaw is an open precondition (aⱼ, c) then
6. Resolvers ← { (aᵢ, c) | aᵢ ∈ A, c ∈ Add(aᵢ), consistent with ≺ and B }
7. ∪ { (new aᵢ, c) | oᵢ ∈ O, c ∈ Add(oᵢ) }
8. if Resolvers = ∅ then backtrack
9. Choose resolver (aᵢ, c); add aᵢ to A if new; add link aᵢ →c aⱼ to L
10. else if flaw is a threat (aₖ threatens aᵢ →c aⱼ) then
11. Choose: promote (aⱼ ≺ aₖ) or demote (aₖ ≺ aᵢ)
12. if chosen ordering introduces a cycle then backtrack
13. Update flaws from new open preconditions and threats
14. return π // no flaws remain; π is a valid partial-order plan

Python 3 — Partial-Order Plan Data Structuresfrom dataclasses import dataclass, field
from typing import Dict, FrozenSet, List, Optional, Set, Tuple
import itertools

@dataclass
class CausalLink:
    producer:  str      # step name/id that adds the condition
    condition: str      # the ground fact being established
    consumer:  str      # step name/id that needs the condition

@dataclass
class PartialPlan:
    steps:    List[Action]                   # action instances
    orderings: Set[Tuple[str, str]]           # (before, after) pairs
    links:    List[CausalLink]
    flaws:    List                            # open preconditions + threats

    def is_consistent(self) -> bool:
        """Check that ordering constraints form a DAG (no cycles)."""
        # Build adjacency list and run DFS cycle detection
        step_names = [s.name for s in self.steps]
        adj: Dict[str, List[str]] = {n: [] for n in step_names}
        for (a, b) in self.orderings:
            adj[a].append(b)
        visited, rec_stack = set(), set()
        def dfs(node):
            visited.add(node); rec_stack.add(node)
            for nb in adj.get(node, []):
                if nb not in visited:
                    if dfs(nb): return True
                elif nb in rec_stack: return True
            rec_stack.discard(node); return False
        return not any(dfs(n) for n in step_names if n not in visited)

    def find_threats(self) -> List[Tuple]:
        """
        Identify causal links threatened by actions in the plan.
        Returns list of (threatening_step, causal_link) tuples.
        """
        threats = []
        for link in self.links:
            for step in self.steps:
                if (link.condition in step.delete
                        and step.name != link.producer
                        and step.name != link.consumer):
                    # Could step interpose between producer and consumer?
                    not_after_consumer  = (link.consumer, step.name) \
                                         not in self.orderings
                    not_before_producer = (step.name, link.producer) \
                                         not in self.orderings
                    if not_after_consumer and not_before_producer:
                        threats.append((step.name, link))
        return threats

    def linearise(self) -> List[str]:
        """Return one valid topological ordering of steps."""
        from functools import reduce
        import copy
        in_deg = {s.name: 0 for s in self.steps}
        adj    = {s.name: [] for s in self.steps}
        for (a, b) in self.orderings:
            adj[a].append(b); in_deg[b] += 1
        queue  = [n for n, d in in_deg.items() if d == 0]
        result = []
        while queue:
            n = queue.pop(0); result.append(n)
            for nb in adj[n]:
                in_deg[nb] -= 1
                if in_deg[nb] == 0: queue.append(nb)
        return result

Theoretical Properties

McAllester and Rosenblitt proved that the SNLP algorithm is both sound — every plan it returns is executable and achieves the goal — and complete — if a solution exists, SNLP will find one given sufficient backtracking.[9] The algorithm maintains soundness by only adding ordering constraints and causal links that are logically justified, and completeness by systematically enumerating all possible resolvers for each flaw.

"Partial-order planning achieves a representational elegance that total-order planners cannot match: the plan itself encodes exactly the necessary temporal commitments and no more."

Russell & Norvig, 2020[1]

Despite these attractive properties, partial-order planners fell out of practical favour following empirical evidence that forward search with heuristic guidance significantly outperforms POP on most benchmark domains.[20] Nevertheless, POP remains highly relevant for domains requiring explicit reasoning about concurrency, for temporal planning, and in task-and-motion planning (TAMP) where partial ordering of geometric and logical steps is essential.[21]

The Sussman Anomaly

The Sussman Anomaly, named for Gerald Jay Sussman who documented it in his 1975 doctoral thesis on the HACKER program, is a canonical example demonstrating that linear planners — those that solve subgoals in strict sequence and commit to partial solutions before considering all interactions — can fail to find optimal or even correct plans when subgoals interfere with each other.[6] Though the anomaly concerns a trivial three-block configuration, it encapsulates the fundamental challenge of subgoal interaction that confronts all planning algorithms.

The Problem Configuration

Sussman Anomaly — Initial and Goal States

Initial State: C is on A; A and B are on the table; B and C are clear; arm is empty.
on(C,A) ∧ on-table(A) ∧ on-table(B) ∧ clear(C) ∧ clear(B) ∧ arm-empty

Goal State: A is on B, and B is on C.
on(A,B) ∧ on(B,C)

Why Linear Planners Fail

A linear planner that processes each subgoal independently, protecting earlier achievements against later interference, encounters an unavoidable conflict on this problem. Consider two natural orderings of the two subgoals:

Attempt 1: Achieve on(A,B) first, then on(B,C).

To achieve on(A,B): unstack C from A, put C down, pick up A, stack A on B. Now A is on B. But to achieve on(B,C), we need B clear — yet B has A on top of it. Achieving the second subgoal requires undoing the first.

Attempt 2: Achieve on(B,C) first, then on(A,B).

To achieve on(B,C): unstack C from A, put C down, pick up B, stack B on C. Now B is on C. But to achieve on(A,B), we need A clear and B clear — A still has C's ghost and B has nothing on it (C is below B). Actually A is clear now, but B is not clear because nothing is on it. Wait — B is the top of the B-on-C stack, so B is clear. We can pick up A (on the table) and stack A on B. But we first had to unstack C from A back in step 1, so A is on the table. This sequence does work: unstack(C,A), put-down(C), pick-up(B), stack(B,C), pick-up(A), stack(A,B).

The Correct 6-Step Plan

1. unstack(C, A)   → holding(C), clear(A)
2. put-down(C)     → on-table(C), clear(C), arm-empty
3. pick-up(B)      → holding(B)
4. stack(B, C)     → on(B,C), clear(B), arm-empty
5. pick-up(A)      → holding(A)
6. stack(A, B)     → on(A,B) ✓ on(B,C) ✓ — goal satisfied

The anomaly arises specifically for linear planners that commit to a subgoal ordering and protect established conditions using "protection intervals." Such a planner achieving on(A,B) first will protect the condition on(A,B) against being deleted; but the only way to then achieve on(B,C) is to temporarily violate this protection by unstacking A. A planner without protection commitment — including the forward BFS above — finds the optimal plan trivially.

Implications for Planning Architecture

The Sussman Anomaly was historically significant because it demonstrated the inadequacy of the early STRIPS-style planner's commitment to a total order of subgoal achievement. The subsequent development of partial-order planning was directly motivated by the desire to avoid premature commitment to subgoal ordering, thereby sidestepping the interference problem by deferring ordering decisions until they are logically necessitated.[22]

The anomaly also motivated the theoretical study of serialisable subgoal sets — cases where subgoals can be achieved in some sequential order without mutual interference — and non-serialisable problems where any fixed ordering fails.[23] Korf demonstrated that the blocks world possesses non-serialisable subgoal structures in general, though the Sussman Anomaly is the simplest instance.[23]

        print(f"  State: {sorted(state)}\n")

    print("Goal achieved:", goal.issubset(state))

Heuristics and Optimisation

Landmark-Based Heuristics

A landmark is a fact or action that must occur in every solution to a planning problem.[24] Hoffmann, Porteous, and Sebastia formalised landmarks and developed efficient algorithms for their extraction.[24] The LAMA planner, winner of the 2008 International Planning Competition, used landmark counting heuristics combined with preferred operators to achieve state-of-the-art satisficing planning performance.[25]

LM-cut Heuristic

The LM-cut heuristic, introduced by Helmert and Domshlak in 2009, computes admissible cost estimates by iteratively identifying and cutting "justification sequences" in the relaxed planning graph, yielding substantially tighter bounds than hmax while maintaining admissibility.[26] LM-cut underpins the optimal configurations of the Fast Downward planning system.

Pattern Database Heuristics

Pattern Database (PDB) heuristics, originating in combinatorial puzzle solving and extended to planning by Edelkamp, abstract the problem by projecting the state space onto subsets of state variables (patterns) and precomputing exact costs within each abstraction.[27] Combining multiple PDBs via their maximum or additive composition produces admissible heuristics that capture complex goal dependencies.

Merge-and-Shrink Abstractions

Helmert, Haslum, and Hoffmann introduced the merge-and-shrink framework as a generalisation of PDB heuristics, allowing the systematic construction of composite abstractions by iteratively merging variable groups and shrinking state spaces via bisimulation-based reduction.[28] The framework subsumes both PDB and atomic projections as special cases and is the basis for the optimal track performance of Fast Downward Stonehenge.

Tools, Libraries, and Frameworks

Fast Downward

Fast Downward, developed by Malte Helmert at the University of Freiburg and subsequently at the University of Basel, is the most widely studied general-purpose planning system in the research community.[18] It accepts PDDL 2.2 input, implements a rich suite of heuristics (LM-cut, merge-and-shrink, potential heuristics, landmarks), and supports both satisficing and optimal search configurations. The planner's modular architecture allows straightforward integration of new heuristics and search algorithms.

PyperPlan

PyperPlan is a lightweight, pure-Python STRIPS planner primarily intended for educational and research prototyping purposes.[29] It implements BFS, A* forward search, a partial-order planner, and Graphplan, accepting PDDL input. Its clean codebase makes it suitable for algorithmic experimentation.

Unified Planning Framework (UPF)

The Unified Planning Framework (UPF), developed under the AIPlan4EU project, provides a Python API for formulating and solving planning problems across multiple backends — including Fast Downward, ENHSP, Tamer, and others — through a unified interface.[30] UPF supports classical planning, numeric planning, temporal planning, and hierarchical task network (HTN) planning, making it a versatile tool for both research and deployment.

Contemporary Research

Learning-Augmented Planning

A major research thrust couples learned heuristics with classical search. Deep learning models trained on planning problem instances can produce informative heuristic estimates that outperform hand-crafted relaxations on specific domains, while maintaining the soundness guarantees of the underlying search algorithm.[36] The STRIPS-HGN architecture, for example, uses graph neural networks to learn generalised heuristic functions from small problem instances and applies them to unseen instances of the same domain.[36]

Lifted Planning and First-Order Representations

Most competitive planners ground lifted (universally quantified) action schemas into propositional instances before searching. The Powerlifted planner demonstrated that lifted successor generation — operating directly on first-order representations without grounding — can be substantially more efficient when problems have many objects, avoiding the combinatorial blowup of grounding.[35]

Probabilistic and Conformant Planning

Classical planning assumes deterministic action outcomes and complete observability. Probabilistic planning, as addressed by the PPDDL language and MDPs, extends classical planning to stochastic domains where actions may have multiple possible outcomes with associated probabilities.[37] Conformant planning addresses incomplete initial knowledge, seeking plans that achieve the goal regardless of the true initial state.[38]

Hierarchical Task Network (HTN) Planning

HTN planning decomposes high-level tasks into subtasks via decomposition methods, enabling the incorporation of domain knowledge that is difficult to express in STRIPS.[31] HTN planners such as SHOP2 have been deployed in military logistics, video game AI, and robotic assembly. The Totally-Ordered HTN planning problem is decidable if the decomposition methods are acyclic; the partially-ordered case becomes undecidable in general.[39]

Task-and-Motion Planning (TAMP)

TAMP integrates classical task planning with geometric motion planning, recognising that in physical robot systems, the feasibility of a logical action (e.g., pick-up(A)) depends on continuous configuration parameters that classical planners cannot represent.[21] Systems such as PDDLStream (Garrett et al., 2020) extend PDDL with streams — external processes that certify the existence of geometric parameters — and interleave planning and geometric sampling.[40]

LLM-Augmented Planning

Large language models (LLMs) exhibit limited but non-trivial planning capabilities on simple benchmarks, leading to research on their use as heuristic generators, action proposers, or constraint translators for classical planners.[41] LLM-Modulo frameworks proposed by Kambhampati and colleagues treat LLMs as "generators" whose outputs are verified by classical planners acting as "critics," combining language fluency with formal correctness guarantees.[41]

"The combination of learned world models, symbolic planning, and neural perception marks the most promising convergence in autonomous systems research since the advent of probabilistic robotics."

Garrett et al., 2021[40]

International Planning Competition

The International Planning Competition (IPC), held biennially since 1998, provides standardised benchmarks and comparative evaluation of planning systems across satisficing, optimal, agile, and learning tracks.[42] IPC benchmark domains — including Logistics, Depots, Elevators, Transport, Woodworking, and Barman — have driven algorithmic innovation for over two decades. The 2023 IPC introduced a Learning Track requiring planners to generalise from training problems to unseen instances, reflecting growing interest in sample-efficient planning.[42]
