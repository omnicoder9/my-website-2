Information Wants to Be Free… Or Should It?

A deep dive into the history, ethics, and economics of the global battle over knowledge access — and the projects trying to fix it.

"On the one hand, information wants to be expensive, because it's so valuable... On the other hand, information wants to be free, because the cost of getting it out is getting lower and lower. So you have these two fighting against each other." — Stewart Brand, 1984 Hackers Conference[1]

The question of whether most information should be freely accessible is far from settled. Today, millions of students and researchers bypass paywalls every day using shadow libraries like Sci-Hub and Z-Library — not because they want to break the law, but because the current system makes legal access impossible for much of the world. These platforms are merely a symptom of a deeper, structural problem. This article explores the history, the competing schools of thought, and — most importantly — the innovative projects working to build a better knowledge commons.

1. A Brief History of the Tension

Although the phrase "Information wants to be free" is often used as a battle cry, its origin is more nuanced. Stewart Brand first uttered the words at the 1984 Hackers Conference in a conversation with Apple co-founder Steve Wozniak. His full remark described a permanent tension: information's high value pulls it toward being expensive, while the plummeting cost of digital distribution pulls it toward being free[1].

This tension predates Brand. Historian Adrian Johns traces the idea back to mid‑20th‑century thinkers like Norbert Wiener, Michael Polanyi, and Arnold Plant, who advocated for the free communication of scientific knowledge and criticized the patent system[9].

The modern Open Access movement was born in the 1990s with the rise of the internet and the creation of arXiv.org in 1991 — a free repository of scientific preprints founded by physicist Paul Ginsparg[17]. The movement gained formal definition in the early 2000s through three landmark declarations:

  • 2002: The Budapest Open Access Initiative (BOAI) first coined the term "open access" and declared that "an old tradition and a new technology have converged to make possible an unprecedented public good."[2]
  • 2003: The Bethesda Statement on Open Access Publishing and the Berlin Declaration on Open Access to Knowledge further solidified the principles of the movement.
  • 2008: Aaron Swartz published the Guerilla Open Access Manifesto, calling for civil disobedience to liberate the world's scientific heritage from private corporations. "Information is power," he wrote. "But like all power, there are those who want to keep it for themselves."[3]

Meanwhile, the economics of scholarly publishing were becoming increasingly unsustainable. Between 1986 and 1997, research library expenditures on journals rose by 142%, while spending on monographs grew only 30%[23]. This became known as the "serials crisis" — a structural problem that persists today, with subscription costs rising 5–9% annually even as library budgets remain flat[22].

2. The Schools of Thought

2.1 The Case for Free and Accessible Information

Proponents of maximal free access rest their arguments on several pillars:

  • Ethics and Human Rights: Access to knowledge is fundamental for education, scientific progress, and democratic participation. As Swartz argued, "Sharing isn't immoral — it's a moral imperative."[3]
  • Public Investment, Public Benefit: A significant portion of research is publicly funded, yet the results are locked behind paywalls controlled by private corporations. Citizens effectively pay twice — first to fund the research, then to read it[9].
  • Acceleration of Innovation: Removing paywalls speeds up global research and problem‑solving by allowing knowledge to flow freely across borders and disciplines.
  • Global Equity: Paywalls create deep inequalities between wealthy institutions and those in the Global South, where researchers often cannot afford access to the very literature they need to contribute to global science[14].

2.2 The Case Against Unfettered Free Information

Those who argue that information should not be universally free raise equally important concerns:

  • Creator Incentives and Livelihoods: Copyright and intellectual property laws provide the financial incentive for authors, artists, and inventors to create. Without the ability to monetize their work, the economic model for producing high‑quality content would collapse.
  • Quality Control and Curation: Paywalls fund editorial oversight, fact‑checking, and rigorous peer review. The rise of "predatory" open‑access journals that exploit author‑pays models demonstrates what can happen when quality controls are stripped away.
  • The Economics of "Free": "Free" access does not mean production is costless. The costs of publishing are simply shifted — often to authors, who must pay article processing charges (APCs) that can reach $11,000 per article in top‑tier journals like Nature[8].
  • Data Exploitation: In the broader digital economy, "free" content is often funded by harvesting user attention and data, creating problematic incentives for content creation.

3. The Broken System: Symptoms and Shadow Libraries

The scholarly publishing market is dominated by a handful of for‑profit corporations — Elsevier, Springer Nature, Wiley, Taylor & Francis, and SAGE — that control over 50% of the market[9]. These publishers enjoy extraordinary profit margins: Elsevier's parent company, RELX, reported a 33.1% adjusted operating margin in 2023[24], and across the industry, typical profit margins exceed 30%[24]. By one estimate, the academic publishing industry generates approximately $19 billion in annual revenue, with roughly $7.5 billion of that representing pure profit[24].

These profits are built on a remarkable model: academics produce research (often publicly funded), peer‑review it for free, and then hand over the copyright to publishers, who sell it back to the same academic institutions at escalating prices. A 2023 analysis estimated that only 15% of a journal's subscription fee goes toward actual publication costs; the rest covers non‑publication expenses (55%) and profit (30%)[9].

It is within this broken system that shadow libraries emerged:

  • Library Genesis (LibGen) was created around 2008 by Russian scientists, rooted in the underground samizdat culture of the Soviet Union[7]. It has since grown into one of the world's largest repositories of free scholarly books and articles.
  • Sci-Hub was founded in 2011 by Alexandra Elbakyan, a neuroscientist from Kazakhstan frustrated by paywalls blocking her research[4]. It now hosts over 88 million papers and has been ruled illegal in multiple jurisdictions, with publishers winning millions in damages[10].
  • Z-Library claimed to offer over 11 million e‑books for free download. In November 2022, its operators were arrested in Argentina and charged with criminal copyright infringement, wire fraud, and money laundering by the U.S. Department of Justice[5].

These platforms are not just acts of piracy; they are a direct, market‑driven response to a system that many see as fundamentally unjust. As the Wikipedia entry for Library Genesis notes, supporters argue that "academic publishers unfairly benefit from government‑funded research, written by researchers... and that LibGen is helping to disseminate research that should be freely available in the first place."[7]

4. Current Projects: Weaving a New Knowledge Commons

While shadow libraries represent a guerrilla response, a rich ecosystem of legal, sustainable projects is emerging to address the root causes of the access crisis. These initiatives span global policy, innovative publishing models, and library‑led transformations.

4.1 Global Policy and Funder‑Led Initiatives

  • Plan S and cOAlition S: Launched in September 2018, Plan S is a mandate requiring that all scholarly publications resulting from publicly funded research be published in open access journals or made immediately available through open access repositories without embargo. Backed by a coalition of national research funders and the European Commission, it represents the most ambitious policy intervention to date[6].
  • UNESCO Recommendation on Open Science (2021): This global framework encourages member states to promote open access to scientific knowledge and data as a public good.

4.2 Transformative Agreements: The DEAL Model

In Germany, the DEAL agreements between German scientific organizations and major publishers (Wiley, Springer Nature, Elsevier) represent a pioneering approach. These "publish and read" contracts bundle access to journal portfolios with discounted open access publishing fees for German researchers. While they have increased Germany's open access rate significantly, critics note that the transformative effect — converting subscription journals to full open access — has not yet materialized as hoped[11]. Similar transformative agreements are now being negotiated by library consortia worldwide.

4.3 Diamond Open Access: Free for Authors, Free for Readers

Diamond OA journals charge neither readers nor authors, relying instead on institutional subsidies, volunteer labor, and low‑cost open‑source infrastructure. This model is gaining significant momentum:

  • Open Research Europe (ORE): Launched by the European Commission in 2021 and now hosted by CERN, ORE is a no‑fee, diamond OA publishing platform for EU‑funded research. It uses an innovative publish‑review‑curate model where peer‑review reports are made public alongside articles. As of 2026, ORE has published over 1,200 articles from more than 6,300 authors worldwide[16].
  • Planetary Research: A new community‑governed diamond OA journal in planetary science, scheduled to launch in January 2026. It will charge no fees to authors or readers, leveraging volunteer editorial processes and open‑source platforms. Geoscience diamond OA journals typically report annual operational costs of approximately USD 1,000, demonstrating the economic feasibility of this model[12].
  • Diamond Discovery Hub (DDH): Launched in October 2025, the DDH is a registry of diamond OA journals that meet six quality criteria (persistent identification, scholarly journal status, open licenses, no author fees, open to all authors, and community ownership). At launch, it already listed more than 2,600 diamond journals across 44 countries[15].
  • Open Journals Collective (OJC): Announced in January 2026, the OJC invites libraries to invest collectively in a scalable, community‑led alternative to commercial big deals. It covers 284 DOAJ‑indexed diamond OA journals across arts, humanities, social sciences, and STEM[13].

4.4 Preprint Repositories and Green Open Access

Green OA — where authors deposit their accepted manuscripts in free repositories — remains a powerful complement to formal publishing. arXiv.org, founded in 1991, now receives about 24,000 submissions per month and holds over two million articles[17]. PubMed Central (PMC), launched in 2000, archives over 10.8 million full‑text biomedical articles freely accessible to anyone[18]. These repositories have fundamentally changed how research is disseminated in physics, mathematics, computer science, and biomedicine.

4.5 Library‑Led and Community‑Driven Models

  • MIT Press Direct to Open: Instead of libraries purchasing individual digital copies of monographs, they collectively fund the open access release of entire book collections. As of March 2025, this program has funded the open release of more than 320 monographs, with data showing that OA titles enjoy greater usage and higher citation rates than their paywalled counterparts[20].
  • University of Michigan Press Fund to Mission: This program aims to publish 75% of its scholarly monographs as open access each year. As of July 2025, more than 250 titles in the humanities and social sciences have been published through this model[20].
  • Opening the Future: A collective funding model where library subscriptions to curated backlist books generate revenue that funds new OA frontlist titles. It has been adopted by several university presses, including CEU Press and Boydell & Brewer[20].

4.6 Equity‑Focused Initiatives for the Global South

Diamond OA is particularly critical for scholars in developing countries. African nations invest on average only 0.5% of GDP in research and development, compared to 2–3% in high‑income countries, making APC‑based OA models prohibitive[14]. Journals like Dhaxalreeb, published by the Somaliland Centre for African Studies, aim to ensure community ownership of knowledge, increase the visibility of Global South scholarship, and promote epistemic justice through diamond OA[14]. The Make Inclusive Data the Norm (MIDN) initiative, a South‑South collaboration between Colombia, Ghana, and Kenya, is similarly working to advance inclusive data systems that reflect the lived realities of underrepresented communities[21].

5. Conclusion: The Permanent Tension

As Stewart Brand presciently noted decades ago, the tension between information wanting to be expensive and information wanting to be free "will not go away."[1] The rise of shadow libraries is not a problem to be solved by stricter enforcement alone, but a symptom of a deeply imbalanced system. The path forward lies in building sustainable alternatives — diamond OA journals, community‑governed platforms, transformative agreements, and library‑led collective action — that align the incentives of creators, publishers, and the global public.

The question is no longer whether information should be free, but how we can build a knowledge commons that is both open and sustainable, both accessible and trustworthy. The projects described here offer a glimpse of what that future might look like.
