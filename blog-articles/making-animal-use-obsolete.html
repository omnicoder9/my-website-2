Technology & Scientific Innovation

Making Animal Use
Obsolete

The most powerful argument for veganism may not be moral at all — it may be technological. A quiet revolution in food science and materials innovation is on the verge of making cruelty the expensive, inefficient option.

by Nicholas Knapp · May 2, 2026
Introduction

For decades, the case for veganism has been made on moral grounds — and it has been the right case to make. But moral conversion is slow, contested, and never complete. What if the most transformational lever available to the movement is not persuasion at all, but obsolescence? History suggests that the most durable social changes happen not when people are talked out of a behaviour, but when a superior alternative makes the old behaviour simply impractical. The internal combustion engine did not fall to environmental argument — it is being displaced by a battery that does the same job without the emissions. The same dynamic is now underway in animal agriculture and materials production, and it may ultimately achieve what decades of advocacy could not.

This article maps the five major technological frontiers — plant-based meat, cultivated (lab-grown) meat, precision fermentation, alternative proteins from mycelium, and next-generation vegan materials — and surveys the evidence that these innovations are on trajectories capable of rendering large-scale animal use economically and culturally obsolete. The goal is not to replace moral argument, but to supplement it with a hard-nosed assessment of where the leverage is greatest.

Part One

Plant-Based Meat: Bridging the Taste Gap

The plant-based meat sector is the most mature of the alternative-protein technologies, and it has already demonstrated the core thesis: when products are good enough, consumers switch without requiring moral conversion. Impossible Foods achieved this by reproducing heme — the iron-containing molecule that gives meat its characteristic "bleed" and flavour profile — through a genetically engineered yeast. Beyond Meat approached the same problem through meticulous mechanical and biochemical mimicry of muscle-fibre structure. Both proved that the sensory experience of meat can be uncoupled from the killing of animals.

The market data underlines the scale of the opportunity. The global plant-based meat market was valued at approximately USD 16.69 billion in 2024 and is projected to reach USD 100.31 billion by 2033, growing at a compound annual rate of 21.92%.3 North America currently holds a 36.2% share, but Asia-Pacific is the fastest-growing region.3 In the United States alone, the market was valued at USD 3.21 billion in 2024, with a projected CAGR of 18.1% through 2030.4

The sector has not been without turbulence. Beyond Meat has faced significant financial difficulties, and consumer enthusiasm cooled after an initial surge. This reflects a persistent challenge: price parity and taste equivalence must both be achieved simultaneously. Progress on both fronts is real. In May 2025, Beyond Meat launched a reformulated burger earning certifications from both the American Heart Association and the American Diabetes Association.5 The European market is also accelerating: some 32% of European households purchased plant-based meat at least once in 2024, and the European Commission's Farm to Fork Strategy explicitly positions reduced meat consumption as integral to its 2050 climate-neutrality goal.6

The Strategic Significance of Flexitarians

The target audience for plant-based meat is not vegans — they are already converted. It is the large and growing population of flexitarians: people who are not ideologically committed to animal consumption, whose purchasing decisions are governed by convenience, taste, and price. This is where the market and the movement align.

Part Two

Cultivated Meat: Real Flesh, No Slaughter

Cultivated meat — produced by culturing animal cells in bioreactors rather than raising and killing whole animals — represents perhaps the most philosophically significant technology in this article. It produces genuine animal tissue. It is not a substitute for meat; it is meat, produced without factory farming, without slaughter, and with a radically lower environmental footprint. Its potential to convert the most committed meat-eaters, who regard taste simulations as inferior, is correspondingly higher.

The cultivated meat market is projected to grow from USD 800 million in 2025 to USD 4.19 billion by 2035, at an 18% compound annual growth rate, with poultry leading at a 48% segment share.7 Regulatory milestones are accumulating. The US FDA and USDA have approved lab-grown poultry cuts, which has de-risked capital commitments from major investors.7 The US market is forecast to expand at a 13.5% CAGR through 2035, while Japan, France, and Germany are each tracking around 13% growth, supported by government subsidies and EU-funded bioreactor scale-up programmes.7

The primary technical challenge remains cost. Current production depends on expensive growth factors and growth media, and serum-free formulations — essential for both cost reduction and the ethical claim that no animals are harmed in production — are still being refined. Researchers and companies are tackling this from multiple angles, including the use of precision fermentation (discussed below) to produce the growth factors themselves. A key projection from Ontario Genomics estimates that cultivated meat and seafood will reach price parity with conventional equivalents by 2032.8

A phaseout of animal agriculture has the potential to stabilise greenhouse gas levels for 30 years and offset 68 per cent of CO₂ emissions this century — even in the absence of any other emission reductions.

Part Three

Precision Fermentation: Rewiring the Dairy System

Of all the technologies surveyed here, precision fermentation may be the most immediately impactful — and the least understood by the public. The process uses programmed microorganisms (yeasts, bacteria, or fungi) to produce specific proteins identical to those found in animal products, without involving any animal in the production chain. The outputs include whey and casein (the key proteins in dairy), egg whites, heme protein, collagen, and numerous enzymes — the molecular building blocks of virtually every animal-derived food.

Companies such as Perfect Day (dairy proteins), The Every Company (egg proteins), and Remilk (animal-free whey) have already launched commercial products. Nestlé launched its first precision fermentation-based dairy protein powder — an animal-free, lactose-free whey isolate — in February 2024.9 In the same month, Vivici, formed from a collaboration between Fonterra and DSM-Firmenich, announced plans for industrial-scale animal-free whey production.9

The market growth projections are striking. The global precision fermentation market is valued at USD 5.93 billion in 2025 and is projected to reach USD 65.07 billion by 2032, at a CAGR of 40.8%.10 Another analysis projects the market reaching USD 75.76 billion by 2035, growing at 31.47% annually.11 The dairy alternatives segment leads, holding a 38.2% revenue share in 2025, driven by Perfect Day and Remilk scaling manufacturing facilities across the US, Europe, and Asia.12 The meat and seafood segment is growing fastest, at a projected CAGR of 46.4%, driven by the use of precision-fermented proteins, fats, and heme in plant-based and cultivated meat products.12

The environmental case is equally compelling. Life cycle assessments show that dairy proteins produced via precision fermentation require 81% less water, 99% less arable land, and produce 72% fewer greenhouse gas emissions than conventional dairy production.8 Another projection suggests precision fermentation will ultimately require between 74–99% less water and 77–91% less land while emitting 37–87% fewer greenhouse gases across application categories.8

Part Four

Mycoprotein and Biomass Fermentation: The Protein of the Future

Alongside precision fermentation, a parallel track of biomass fermentation — producing high quantities of whole microbial biomass rich in protein — is generating a category of foods with significant potential. Mycoprotein, produced from the fungal species Fusarium venenatum by Quorn, has been on shelves since the 1980s, but a new generation of mycoprotein companies is producing whole-cut products with superior texture and nutritional profiles. Meati Foods uses mycelium to produce whole-cut "steaks" and "fillets" that have attracted major retail partnerships.

The fermentation sector more broadly — encompassing precision fermentation, biomass fermentation, and traditional fermentation — has raised USD 4.8 billion in disclosed private investment since 2013, with at least 21 partnerships formed in 2024 alone across dairy, seafood, and process optimisation.13 The EFSA (European Food Safety Authority) approved the use of mycoprotein and precision-fermented proteins in novel food applications in 2023, expanding the regulatory runway for commercial scale-up across the EU.6

Part Five

Materials Innovation: Ending the Leather Economy

Animal use for food is the central concern of most vegan activism, but the leather, wool, down, and silk industries collectively implicate billions of additional animals each year. Here too, a technological revolution is underway — and it presents an analogous strategic opportunity.

Mycelium Leather

The most advanced next-generation leather alternative uses mycelium — the root-like network of fungi — fed on agricultural waste such as corn stalks or sawdust, and grown into flexible, durable sheets in 7–14 days. The resulting material looks and handles almost identically to premium calfskin, is 100% vegan, and has a dramatically lower environmental footprint than bovine leather. MycoWorks (whose Reishi material is partnered with Hermès), Ecovative (Forager, partnered with Calvin Klein parent PVH Corp), and the now-paused Bolt Threads (Mylo) are the leading developers.14,15

The mycelium leather market reached USD 12 million in 2024 and is projected to reach USD 336 million by 2033.15 The broader vegan leather market — encompassing mycelium, cactus, pineapple (Piñatex), and synthetic alternatives — is expected to reach USD 27.80 billion by 2034, growing at a CAGR of 10.8%.16 MycoWorks opened a large-format production facility in South Carolina in 2023, with a capacity of millions of square feet per year, providing a critical test of whether mycelium materials can achieve the scale needed to penetrate mass markets.14

Significant challenges remain. Bolt Threads suspended its Mylo operations in 2023 due to the high costs of scaling.14 Current mycelium leather pricing runs at 2–4 times conventional leather, though costs are projected to drop towards parity by 2028–2030 as production scales.17 Consumer sentiment is shifting: a Vegan Society survey found that 55% of UK consumers were keen to purchase or already owned something made from plant-based leather, and 42% believed it to be more sustainable.18 A survey of Chinese consumers found that 90% preferred next-generation alternatives to animal leather, citing environmental protection (72%) and harm reduction to animals (68%) as primary motivations.18

Traditional leather prices are rising fast — droughts and feed costs — while mycelium costs are dropping 30–40% per year as production scales. The crossover is not a question of if, but when.

— Materials Industry Analysis, 202517

Wool and Textile Alternatives

Beyond leather, the wool and silk alternatives space is advancing rapidly. Bolt Threads' other major material — Microsilk, produced by yeast programmed with spider-silk genes — has demonstrated the viability of biosynthetic luxury fibres. Organisations such as the Material Innovation Initiative are tracking over 90 companies developing next-generation alternatives to wool, silk, down, and leather. While regulatory and consumer adoption challenges remain, the trajectory mirrors the earlier arc of plant-based food: initial niche appeal, performance parity achieved, price reduction through scale, mainstream adoption.

Strategic Synthesis

The Obsolescence Model: What It Means for Activists

The technologies surveyed above share a critical characteristic: they do not require moral conversion. A consumer choosing a Perfect Day protein shake is not necessarily making an ethical statement about dairy cows; they may simply prefer a product that is lactose-free, or that came up first in search results, or that their gym recommends. The behaviour change — the removal of animal products from the supply chain — occurs regardless of the motivation. This is the strategic power of the obsolescence model.

Ontario Genomics projects that by 2035, products produced by precision fermentation, cultivated meat, and plant-based alternatives could collectively capture 22% of the protein market.8 Even capturing 22% of the animal protein market represents a reduction in the number of animals raised and killed that dwarfs what decades of advocacy alone could achieve.

This does not argue for abandoning moral suasion. Advocacy plays an essential role in accelerating regulatory approvals, in building the cultural climate that makes these technologies desirable, and in maintaining pressure on the remaining 78% of the market. But it does suggest that strategic investment — of activist energy, philanthropy, political lobbying, and public communication — in the acceleration of these technologies may offer the highest expected return on effort of any approach available to the movement today.

The animals in factory farms today will not be saved by a lab-grown steak. But the animals that would otherwise be born into those farms in 2035 may be. The arc of this technological transformation is long, but it bends — rapidly — toward a world where the killing of animals for food and materials is not a moral question that societies are still debating, but an expensive anachronism that the market has already resolved.

Where Advocates Should Focus

Push for government investment in alternative protein research and infrastructure. Advocate for regulatory modernisation that enables — rather than delays — the approval of cultivated meat and precision fermentation products. Support institutions such as the Good Food Institute. Engage media to shift coverage from novelty to inevitability. And choose — in your own purchasing and institutional relationships — the products that accelerate the transition. Every dollar shifted from animal to alternative is a market signal.
