The Splitting of Reality

Take the Schrödinger equation literally, delete the collapse, and the mathematics describes a universe that branches at every measurement — every outcome real, in its own world. This is the many-worlds interpretation, and the story of why it was almost buried.

Section 01 · The inheritance

A theory with two laws that contradict each other

By the early 1950s quantum mechanics could predict the outcome of almost any experiment to staggering precision, yet at its foundations it carried a contradiction that no amount of accuracy could paper over. The textbook formulation Everett inherited — the von Neumann–Dirac scheme — ran on two incompatible rules of change.1,9

The first rule is the Schrödinger equation itself: a smooth, deterministic, perfectly reversible evolution of the wavefunction. Left alone, a quantum system glides through Hilbert space according to this linear dynamics, and it is this rule that lets systems enter superpositions and become entangled.1 The second rule is the collapse postulate: the moment a measurement occurs, the wavefunction is said to jump, discontinuously and at random, onto a single definite outcome, with probabilities fixed by the squared amplitude.1,9

The trouble is the word measurement. The theory never defines it. It enters as an undefined primitive, a magic moment at which the first rule is suspended and the second takes over — but nothing in the mathematics says when that moment is, or what physically distinguishes a "measurement" from any other interaction.1 Worse: if observers and their instruments are themselves physical systems made of atoms, then they too must obey the Schrödinger equation, and the abrupt random jump of rule two can never actually happen. Stipulating a collapse on measurement then produces a flat contradiction. This is the quantum measurement problem.1

Everett regarded the orthodox Copenhagen reading — which placed the observer and the apparatus outside the quantum description by fiat — as not even attempting a consistent account of the act of observation, and therefore as the less satisfactory of the two.1 He wanted a single, uniform set of physical laws that applied to everything, observers included. There was an obvious way to get one.

Section 02 · The move

Take the equation literally; delete the collapse

Everett's proposal was as economical as it was radical. Drop rule two. Keep only the deterministic Schrödinger evolution, apply it without exception to every system in the universe — electrons, cats, laboratories, physicists — and treat the resulting theory as a complete and literal description of reality. He called it pure wave mechanics: the standard theory minus the ad-hoc jump.1

He developed the idea as a graduate student at Princeton under John Archibald Wheeler. The ambitious early draft, handed to Wheeler in January 1956 under the title Wave Mechanics Without Probability, grew into the long manuscript later published as The Theory of the Universal Wave Function. A heavily compressed version was accepted as his doctoral thesis in March 1957, and an even shorter paper appeared that July in Reviews of Modern Physics.1,2

The phrase "universal wave function" is the heart of it. If there is no collapse and the dynamics is universal, then the entire cosmos — every particle and field, including you reading this — is described by one enormous, never-collapsing wavefunction evolving deterministically for all time. The cost of this clean, observer-free, deterministic picture is what comes out the other side.

Section 03 · Relative states

The branches were there all along

State E looks like a disaster. Read through the orthodox lens it describes an observer with no definite measurement result — not "up", not "down", not even a state she can call her own, because the joint state of observer-plus-system no longer factorises. Everett's escape was a reinterpretation he called the fundamental relativity of states: it is meaningless, he argued, to ask for the absolute state of a subsystem; one can only ask for its state relative to a given state of the rest.1,2

Seen that way, E is not one confused observer but a superposition of branches, and within each branch the correlation is perfectly sharp. Relative to the system being spin-up, the observer definitely recorded "up"; relative to the system being spin-down, she definitely recorded "down".1 Each term of the superposition contains a complete, internally consistent observer with a definite memory who perceives a single, ordinary outcome and is wholly unaware of the others. Nothing was added to the mathematics to produce these branches — they are simply what the universal wavefunction already contained once you stop deleting all but one of them.

Everett's reason for taking every branch as equally real was physical, not mystical. Because one could in principle perform an interference experiment that recombines the components of E — the kind of test dramatised in his own version of the Wigner's-friend story, published four years before Wigner's own telling1,10 — no single element of a superposition can be granted more reality than any other. All of them, he insisted, exist simultaneously.1

The picture in one line

There is one wavefunction, it never collapses, and every physically possible outcome of every quantum event is realised — each in its own branch, each containing a version of the observer who sees only that result.

Section 04 · The probability puzzle

If everything happens, what does “probability” mean?

Here lies the deepest technical wound in Everett's programme, and it was visible from the start. The orthodox theory is irreducibly stochastic: outcomes have genuine chances given by the squared amplitude — the Born rule. But pure wave mechanics is deterministic and every outcome occurs. In what sense, then, is a spin-up result "probability one-half" if both spin-up and spin-down branches are equally actual?1

Everett's answer was a typicality measure over branches. He showed that if one demands a measure satisfying a short list of natural formal constraints — the usual probability axioms, dependence only on the branch amplitudes, and additivity across sub-branches — then the measure is forced to be the squared amplitude, the very weighting the Born rule uses.1 He then argued that the sequence of recorded outcomes within a typical branch would display the standard quantum statistics, so a typical observer would see exactly the frequencies orthodox theory predicts.1

Critics have never been satisfied that this fully closes the gap, because a measure of "typicality" across branches is not obviously the same thing as a probability for an observer who knows that all branches certainly occur. The probability problem would become the central battleground of the modern revival — and a small industry of attempted derivations and rebuttals, treated below.

Section 05 · The heresy

Copenhagen’s long shadow, and a thesis cut in half

The reception was brutal in the quiet way that academic receptions can be. In the spring of 1956 Wheeler carried the draft dissertation to Copenhagen, hoping to win Niels Bohr's blessing and frame Everett's work as an extension of the official line rather than an attack on it.7,11 It did not go well. Bohr and his circle treated the idea of a literally splitting reality as heresy; one member of the Copenhagen camp dismissed it as theology, another reportedly derided Everett himself.8 Wheeler wrote back to his student that the formalism was beautiful and unshaken, but that the words attached to it were the problem.7

What followed was an editorial amputation. Wheeler pressed Everett to cut the manuscript to roughly half its length, to soften the language, and in particular to remove the talk of observers and objects "splitting" — a word Wheeler found indigestible.7,8 The chapter on information and probability, which many later readers consider the strongest material in the work, was stripped out; the full long version would not see print until DeWitt and Graham published it in 1973.4,8 Even the journal paper was paired with an accompanying assessment by Wheeler that gently held the idea at arm's length.6

The peer review compounded the obscurity. The referee at Reviews of Modern Physics was Bryce DeWitt, who initially rejected the reality of branching outright. In a May 1957 letter to Wheeler he conceded the scheme was beautifully consistent but objected that the full state vector was simply too rich — he did not, he wrote, feel himself split into copies.1,11 Everett's famous riposte was that Copernicus's critics had likewise not felt the Earth move.

When the paper finally appeared in 1957 it slipped almost immediately into obscurity, and Wheeler gradually distanced himself from the association over the following years, in his heart remaining loyal to Bohr's view that the universe does not branch.7,8 Everett, for his part, had already left. He took a defence-analysis post at the Pentagon's Weapons Systems Evaluation Group in 1956, never returned to academic physics, and watched from the outside as his idea was ignored.1,7

Section 06 · The naming of worlds

DeWitt revives it — and gives it its dangerous name

The thaw came in 1970. The same Bryce DeWitt who had refereed the paper a decade earlier now championed it, publishing an exposition in the widely read Physics Today that pulled Everett's work out of thirteen years of neglect.3,5,11 It was DeWitt who supplied the charismatic label that stuck: the many-worlds interpretation — a phrase that appears nowhere in Everett's own papers, which spoke only of relative states.3,4 Three years later, the DeWitt–Graham anthology collected Everett's long thesis alongside commentary and fixed the new name in the literature.4

DeWitt also dramatised the metaphysics in a way Everett's cautious prose never had. He pressed the point that every quantum transition taking place anywhere — on every star, in every galaxy, in every remote corner of the cosmos — is continually splitting the local world on Earth into countless copies of itself.5 Where Everett had arguably described a single observer with branching relative records, DeWitt read the formalism as the literal fission of the universe into ever-multiplying parallel realities, each containing its own copy of every observer.5,18 This vivid, uncompromising version is the one that entered the popular imagination — and the one most people mean by "many worlds" today.

Section 07 · What makes a branch

Decoherence: why the worlds don’t bleed together

An obvious objection dogged the early theory: which branches? A wavefunction can be written in infinitely many bases, so what privileges the decomposition into the tidy, classical-looking worlds — cat-alive and cat-dead, spin-up and spin-down — that we actually experience, rather than some bizarre superposed basis? This is the preferred-basis problem.3

The answer that matured through the 1970s and 1980s was the theory of quantum decoherence, developed in large part by Wojciech Zurek and others.15,18 A macroscopic system is never isolated; it is relentlessly entangled with a vast environment of photons, air molecules and internal degrees of freedom. That coupling rapidly destroys the interference between macroscopically distinct alternatives and dynamically singles out a robust, approximately classical "pointer" basis — the basis in which stable structures like tables, instruments and people can persist.13,15 On the modern Everettian reading, decoherence is what turns the abstract universal wavefunction into a branching structure of quasi-classical, effectively non-communicating worlds, and it explains why the splitting feels like a sharp, irreversible event even though the underlying evolution is smooth.3,13

Decoherence rescued the interpretation from vagueness and is the reason the preferred-basis worry is no longer regarded as a fatal objection.3 It did not, however, settle the harder question of probability — and it arguably sharpened it, since the branches it defines are only approximate and their count depends on how finely one chooses to grain them.13

Section 08 · The Born-rule wars

Deriving chance in a world without chances

The modern defence of many-worlds is most ambitious where the theory is weakest: it tries to derive the Born rule rather than postulate it. In 1999 David Deutsch argued that an Everettian agent who is rational — in the precise sense of classical decision theory — is compelled to set her betting preferences over branch outcomes exactly as if the squared-amplitude weights were probabilities.12 David Wallace then turned this into an explicit theorem, resting on the symmetry structure of unitary quantum mechanics together with consistency assumptions about an agent's preferences and indifference to the fine-grained details of how the world branches.3,13 A related route, pursued by Charles Sebens and Sean Carroll, grounds the rule in an observer's self-locating uncertainty: in the brief interval after a branching but before she reads the result, an observer rationally does not know which branch she is in, and that ignorance can be shown to obey the Born weights.14 Zurek has offered yet another derivation built on the symmetry of entangled states.3,15

None of this has won consensus. The most persistent complaint is circularity: the decoherence that defines the branches is itself usually formulated using the reduced density matrix, which already presupposes the Born rule, so a "derivation" of the rule may quietly assume it.3,13 Other critics challenge the decision-theoretic axioms directly, arguing that they smuggle in substantive assumptions about how a rational agent should treat a future in which all of her successors exist.3 Defenders respond that no rival interpretation does demonstrably better at explaining where the Born probabilities come from, so the difficulty, even if real, is not uniquely a strike against many-worlds.3,13 The argument continues.

Section 09 · The upset

The price of determinism: infinite copies of you

Strip away the equations and the source of the discomfort is plain. Many-worlds restores everything physicists say they want — a single set of laws, exact determinism, no privileged observer, no mysterious collapse — and charges for it in a currency many find intolerable: a literally unbounded number of branches, in each of which a nearly identical copy of you lives out a different version of your life. Every choice you did not make, every accident that did not befall you, every coin that landed the other way is, on this view, fully real in some branch, witnessed by someone who is, in every sense that matters, also you.18

The implications turn quickly from abstract to vertiginous. The thought experiment of quantum suicide, developed by Max Tegmark in 1998 (and anticipated independently by Hans Moravec and Bruno Marchal in the late 1980s), pushes the logic to its edge: an experimenter rigged to a quantum trigger should, the argument goes, only ever find himself in the branches where the device failed to fire, since the branches in which he dies contain no one to have an experience.3,16 Extended over a lifetime, this yields the unsettling notion of quantum immortality — the claim that your stream of subjective experience always continues into some surviving branch. Most physicists, including many sympathetic to many-worlds, treat this as a cautionary reductio resting on contrived, idealised premises rather than a real prediction.3,16 But that it can be stated at all captures why the interpretation unsettles people at a level no equation reaches.

Why it disturbs

The bargain is exact. To remove the observer and the collapse and recover a clean deterministic physics, you must accept that you are one of countless branching copies — and that "the result of the measurement" is not a single fact about the world but a fact only about which copy you happen to be.

Section 10 · Where it stands

A respectable minority, and the man who paid for it

Once fringe, many-worlds is now a mainstream contender. It is routinely ranked the leading challenger to the Copenhagen orthodoxy and draws prominent advocates in quantum cosmology and quantum computation, fields where the absence of any external "observer" to collapse the wavefunction of the whole universe makes Everett's observer-free picture especially attractive.3,16 Yet it remains a minority view. An informal 2013 poll of specialists at a foundations meeting found roughly 42% favouring Copenhagen against about 18% for many-worlds;17 a much larger survey reported in 2025, marking a century of quantum mechanics, again put Copenhagen ahead near 36% with many-worlds around 15%, while underscoring how little consensus exists and how few physicists feel confident in any interpretation at all.19 Because the leading interpretations make the same experimental predictions, no measurement has yet decided between them.18

The human coda is as strange as the theory. Hugh Everett III, born in 1930, never went back to physics after Princeton; he built a successful defence and software career, drank and smoked heavily, and died of heart failure in 1982 at the age of fifty-one.7,8 A committed atheist who reportedly spoke of his own immortality across branches, he asked that his ashes be discarded with the household trash — and, after some years, they were.8 In 1996 his daughter Elizabeth took her own life, leaving a note asking that her remains be treated the same way so that, as she put it, she might find the right parallel universe and rejoin her father.8 His son, the musician Mark Oliver Everett of the band Eels, later traced his father's life and idea in a 2007 BBC documentary, Parallel Worlds, Parallel Lives.20

It is a fitting epilogue to an idea that asks us to hold tragedy and possibility in the same breath. Whether or not the universe truly branches, Everett's central provocation has outlasted its inventor and every attempt to bury it: that if you simply believe the Schrödinger equation, all the way down and without exception, the mathematics has already told you that you are not alone — and never were.
