Who Suffers More?

A scientific and philosophical inquiry into the nature of pain, consciousness, and emotional depth across species

~3,200 words by Nicholas Knapp

In the opening pages of Dostoyevsky's The Brothers Karamazov, the skeptic Ivan asks his brother a question that has haunted philosophy ever since: is any human joy, any harmony of the cosmos, worth the tear of a single tortured child? The question assumes, silently, that the child's suffering is the most exquisite and morally weighty kind. But what if a pig weeping in a slaughterhouse has a comparable claim on our conscience? Modern neuroscience has begun, cautiously and somewhat uncomfortably, to force that question out of metaphor and into the laboratory.

The Problem of Measuring Pain Across Species

Before we can meaningfully ask whether humans suffer more than other animals, we need to be precise about terms. Pain, suffering, and nociception are related but distinct phenomena. Nociception is the purely physiological detection of damaging stimuli—a reflex wired into even simple invertebrates. Pain is the conscious, affective experience of that detection. Suffering is broader still: the aversive mental state that can arise not only from pain, but also from fear, boredom, grief, and longing.[1]

This distinction has real consequences. A sea anemone recoils from a needle; we would not say it suffers. A dog whose limb is burned yelps, guards the wound, and displays cortisol spikes; the claim that it does not suffer requires increasingly implausible special pleading. The philosophical challenge—what philosophers call "the problem of other minds"—is that we cannot directly access another being's subjective experience, human or otherwise. We infer suffering from behavior, physiology, and structural analogy. The same method we use to confidently assert that another person is in pain.

Key Distinction: Nociception vs. Pain vs. Suffering Pain, suffering, and nociception are conceptually different and should not be conflated. Suffering can result from a variety of states including pain, anxiety, fear, and boredom. Nociception can explain the behavior of insects and perhaps other invertebrates, but a behavioral inhibition system associated with anxiety in humans appears to be present in mammals and most vertebrates. — Bateson, Mendl & Feaver, Theoretical Medicine and Bioethics, 1990 [1]

The Neurological Case for Animal Suffering

The modern turning point arrived publicly on July 7, 2012, when a group of leading neuroscientists signed the Cambridge Declaration on Consciousness at Churchill College, in the presence of Stephen Hawking. Their conclusion was blunt: convergent evidence indicates that non-human animals have the neuroanatomical, neurochemical, and neurophysiological substrates of conscious states, and the weight of evidence indicates that humans are not unique in possessing the neurological substrates that generate consciousness.[2] The declaration explicitly named all mammals, birds, and many other creatures, including octopuses.

In 2024, a further milestone arrived: the New York Declaration on Animal Consciousness, signed by over 500 scientists and academics, extended the reasonable possibility of conscious experience to all vertebrates—including reptiles, fish, and amphibians—and a range of invertebrates, including cephalopod mollusks, decapod crustaceans, and insects.[3]

The anatomical argument underlying both declarations is that the limbic system—the ancient, subcortical architecture responsible for emotional processing—is conserved across all vertebrate species.[4] Cortical networks involved in pain are likewise highly conserved across mammalian evolution.[4] PET and fMRI imaging have shown that the separation-distress system in animal brains is strikingly similar to human sadness circuits.[5] Using electrical and chemical stimulation of homologous subcortical regions, neuroscientist Jaak Panksepp identified seven primary emotional systems—SEEKING, RAGE, FEAR, LUST, CARE, PANIC/GRIEF, and PLAY—that appear to operate similarly across all mammals.[5]

There is, however, an important caveat in the neurological data. While subcortical pain processing appears widely shared, the affective dimension of pain—the "it hurts and I know it hurts"—is thought to depend more heavily on the prefrontal cortex and anterior cingulate cortex (ACC), which are more elaborated in humans and great apes. Some researchers have argued that even if animals possess the necessary pain-detection machinery, they may lack the higher cortical architecture to process that signal into the full register of suffering as humans experience it.[6] This remains a contested claim. What is not contested is that common laboratory mammals possess nervous systems that receive and process noxious stimuli in a manner similar to humans, at least at subcortical levels.[7]

We have no magic human 'pain' centers that other species do not have—it is increasingly absurd to believe we are unique in our ability to suffer pain.

Paraphrased from neuroscience literature on vertebrate nociception [4]

Emotions Across Species: Dogs, Pigs, Elephants, and More

Beyond raw pain, what about the richer emotional life—grief, empathy, social loss, fear of separation? Here, too, the evidence has become difficult to dismiss.

Dogs

Service dogs display clear anxiety responses when separated from their companions and show empathic attunement to human emotional states—responding to distress, offering comfort, and apparently reading emotional cues others in the room missed. Behavioral and neuroimaging research confirms dogs process social information and emotional signals in ways functionally analogous to humans.[8]

Pigs

A 2013 study in Physiology and Behavior found that pigs in negative emotional states (social isolation) influenced the cortisol levels and behavior of nearby pen-mates, demonstrating a form of emotional contagion. Pigs displaying distress caused measurable stress responses in surrounding animals—a functional signature of empathy.[9]

Elephants

Elephants display a wide emotional spectrum including grief, joy, and apparent empathy. They have been observed gently touching and carrying the bones of dead conspecifics. Neurologically, elephants—along with humpback and killer whales—possess spindle neurons (Von Economo neurons), specialized cells found in the human anterior cingulate cortex and frontoinsular cortex, regions that regulate empathy and social emotion.[10]

Rodents

A landmark study by Langford et al. found that mice experiencing pain together exhibited greater pain responses than when suffering alone; moreover, observing a companion in pain induced a pain response in the observer comparable to direct stimulation. The authors concluded this is evidence of emotional contagion—a foundational element of empathy—in rodents.[10]

Even simpler organisms show behavioral signatures worth noting. In 2014, research on longfin squid found that injured animals began their defensive behaviors significantly sooner than uninjured ones, and that administering an anesthetic prior to injury eliminated this sensitization—suggesting the behavioral change was mediated by something analogous to a pain state, not mere reflex.[11]

The cross-species case for primal empathy is perhaps strongest in the neuroscience of social emotion. Research in rodents has shown that fear can be shared across individuals via emotional contagion, and that the neural circuits mediating this are homologous to those underlying human empathy.[12] In higher mammals—cetaceans, great apes, elephants—this primal empathy appears to interact with higher cognitive functions in ways that begin to approach the sophistication of human compassion.

What Is Uniquely Human About Suffering?

None of the above means that human and animal suffering are equivalent in every dimension. There are compelling reasons to think that the human experience of pain and distress is qualitatively different in specific ways—not less real for other species, but differently structured.

The Ruminating Mind

Human beings are, so far as we know, the only animals who suffer extensively from anticipatory and retrospective pain. Research comparing human and animal responses to trauma finds that animals typically exhibit adaptive behaviors—avoidance, desensitization—after traumatic events. Humans, by contrast, tend to revisit their traumas cognitively, reactivating distress through memory and reflection.[13] Clinical psychology has documented this extensively: rumination—repetitive, recursive negative thought—is a defining feature of human depression and anxiety that appears to have no precise parallel in the animal world.

Anxiety itself exhibits a phylogenetically layered structure. Some rudimentary form of anxiety-like state—an anticipatory fear of uncertain threat—appears to exist from invertebrates to humans. But the sophistication of this experience, and particularly the self-conscious awareness of it, varies enormously by cognitive capacity.[14] A dog may be afraid of the vet. A human can spend weeks in anticipatory dread of a single appointment, constructing elaborate mental scenarios, and then relive the event for months afterward.

Mortality Awareness

Almost 200 years ago, Kierkegaard argued that human self-consciousness produces two fundamental emotions: awe and dread. It is awesome to be alive; it is dreadful to know that death is inevitable and can arrive at any moment. This "struggle of being against non-being" is, according to existential psychology, a constant undercurrent in human life.[15]

Do animals know they will die? Some philosophers argue that without a conceptual and reflexive understanding of mortality, what animals experience when a companion disappears is better classified as separation anxiety rather than grief in the morally weighty human sense.[16] Others push back: research on corvids and non-human primates suggests they can engage in mental "time travel"—planning for future needs not currently experienced, and anticipating outcomes over hours or even days.[16] Whether any non-human animal grasps its own mortality in the way a terminal cancer patient does remains genuinely uncertain.

Ernest Becker, in his 1973 Pulitzer Prize-winning work The Denial of Death, argued that human culture itself is largely a massive system for managing death anxiety—our religions, our art, our monuments and legacies, all built to shield us from the terror of annihilation.[15] No animal builds cathedrals to outlive its body. This dimension of suffering—the existential weight of finitude—appears to be distinctly, perhaps uniquely, human.

Language as Amplifier

Human language does not merely describe suffering—it structures it, extends it, and can tragically intensify it. The capacity to narrate one's own pain to oneself, to situate it in a story with past and future, gives human suffering a temporal depth that appears absent in other animals. A dog biting its injured paw is responding to present-tense pain. A human with chronic pain lives in a narrative of decline, defeat, and feared futures that can be as disabling as the physical sensation itself.

Cognitive-behavioral research has shown that threat and anxiety powerfully affect attention allocation in humans in ways that appear far more pronounced than in non-human animals—an attentional bias that, in people suffering from chronic anxiety, becomes self-reinforcing and extraordinarily difficult to break.[14]

No ape, elephant, dog or cat would or could suffer in the way that a human with severe phobia has suffered—propelled through a labyrinth of excruciating worries and imagined horrors. That has to be uniquely human.

Paraphrased from King, B. J. in NPR — 13.7: Cosmos and Culture, 2014 [17]

The Scale Problem: Sheer Quantity of Animal Suffering

Even if we accept that individual human suffering may, in its most extreme cognitive-existential forms, exceed what any individual non-human animal can experience, the question of suffering across populations forces a different and deeply uncomfortable arithmetic.

As of 2023, approximately 74% of all land livestock on Earth are factory farmed. In the United States, the estimate for 2017 was 99% of all livestock.[11] These billions of sentient creatures, whose capacity for pain and fear and social grief we have documented above, endure confinement, separation, and slaughter on a scale that staggers moral imagination. Even if each unit of bovine or porcine suffering is "smaller" than an equivalent unit of human existential anguish, the sheer numbers represent an aggregate of misery that may well dwarf all human suffering combined.

The philosopher Peter Singer, drawing on Jeremy Bentham's foundational principle—"The question is not, Can they reason? nor, Can they talk? but, Can they suffer?"—argued in Animal Liberation (1975) that excluding animals from moral consideration simply on the basis of species is a form of arbitrary discrimination comparable to racism or sexism. The neuroscience reviewed here lends that argument empirical weight it lacked in Bentham's era.

The New York Declaration on Animal Consciousness (2024) Signed by over 500 scientists, the declaration states: "There is strong scientific support for attributions of conscious experience to other mammals and birds. The empirical evidence indicates at least a realistic possibility of conscious experience in all vertebrates and many invertebrates." It further asserts that when there is a realistic possibility of conscious experience, it is irresponsible to ignore that possibility in decisions affecting those animals.[3]

What the Evidence Cannot Resolve

An honest accounting demands acknowledging what we simply do not know. The "hard problem" of consciousness—why any physical process gives rise to subjective experience at all—remains unsolved. We can map neural correlates of pain in rats with precision; we cannot determine, from the outside, what that experience is like to be a rat. The argument from analogy that undergirds virtually all comparative suffering research (if the brain looks similar and the behavior looks similar, the experience is probably similar) is powerful but not conclusive.

We also face the taxonomic problem. "Animals" is not a single category. The evidence for rich emotional experience in a chimpanzee is vastly stronger than for a sea urchin. The capacity for suffering appears to scale—roughly, imperfectly—with neurological complexity. Fish occupy a genuinely contested middle territory: they possess nociceptors physiologically similar to mammalian equivalents, but, as some researchers argue, may lack the forebrain architecture that in mammals transforms nociceptive signals into conscious pain.[18] Others disagree strongly, citing behavioral evidence and the presence of opioid receptors in fish that respond to analgesics in ways consistent with pain relief.

The philosopher Thomas Nagel captured the core difficulty in his famous 1974 essay: What is it like to be a bat? The question may be permanently unanswerable from the outside. What neuroscience tells us is that there almost certainly is something it is like to be a bat—and a dog, and a pig, and perhaps even an octopus. Whether that something is more or less than what it is like to be a grieving human widow, we may never know with precision.

A Tentative Synthesis

What can we say, objectively, from the evidence assembled here?

First: the assumption that suffering is a uniquely or predominantly human experience is scientifically indefensible. The neurological substrates for pain, fear, grief, and social distress are shared across mammals and broader vertebrate lineages. The Cambridge and New York Declarations represent the scientific consensus position, not a fringe view.

Second: human suffering has distinctive features—notably rumination, anticipatory dread, mortality awareness, and the narrative amplification provided by language—that likely make the architecture of human suffering more complex and, in certain registers, more intense than anything achievable by a non-linguistically-structured mind.

Third: even if individual human suffering is more cognitively elaborate, the scale of animal suffering—particularly under industrial farming conditions—may represent an aggregate moral burden that equals or exceeds all human misery combined. The ethics of this are left to the reader; the empirical facts are not in serious dispute.

Fourth: we must resist the temptation to use the difference in cognitive sophistication as a reason to discount animal suffering entirely. A less elaborate form of suffering is still suffering. A child who lacks the language to narrate their pain does not thereby hurt less. The inability to reflect existentially on one's misery may actually, in some respects, be a mercy—the animal hurt by a trap is in acute pain, but it does not spend years afterward reliving it in therapy.

Perhaps the most honest conclusion is also the most disquieting: the question "who suffers more?" may be the wrong question. The better question, forced upon us by a century of converging neuroscience, might be simpler and more urgent: given everything we now know, what are we obligated to do?
