No object of human inquiry is at once so near and so resistant as the one doing the inquiring. The self is the thing that reads this sentence, yet it is also among the hardest things in the world to find. Turn attention inward, as philosophers from Avicenna to Hume have urged, and you encounter sensations, memories, moods, and the chatter of inner speech—but the owner of those states, the bare I that supposedly has them, keeps slipping out of view. Every culture has felt the pull of this puzzle, and almost every era has proposed a different answer to the same blunt question: what, or who, is a human person?

This essay surveys those answers as a connected argument rather than a museum of curiosities. The models recur and mutate; a doctrine refined in a monastery in northern India in the fifth century BCE reappears, transformed, in a cognitive-science laboratory twenty-five hundred years later. To keep the through-line visible, it helps to name the major fault lines that any model of the self must fall on one side of. Is the self a substance—a thing that exists in its own right and endures—or a process, a pattern that the world keeps weaving and unravelling? Is it simple and unified, or a bundle of parts held together loosely or not at all? Is it given—discovered by looking—or constructed, assembled by memory, language, culture, and brain? And finally, the question that lends the others their urgency: does the self's persistence matter, ethically and existentially, in the way we instinctively assume it does?

What follows traces these questions through four great phases—antiquity, the medieval synthesis, the early-modern turn inward, and the long scientific dismantling that runs from the nineteenth century to the present. The disciplines change—theology gives way to metaphysics, metaphysics to empirical psychology and then to neuroscience—but the question is continuous, and so, surprisingly often, are the answers.

The person is, or contains, an immaterial soul or mind distinct from the body.Plato, Descartes

There is no enduring self; only a stream of impermanent, causally linked events.Buddha, Hume

Psychological continuity

You persist as a person through overlapping chains of memory and mental connection.Locke, Parfit

Narrative self

IAntiquityc. 800 BCE – 200 CE

The breath that becomes a soul

In the oldest layer of Greek thought there is no self in our sense at all. For Homer, a person is a loose federation of organs and impulses—the thumos that drives anger and courage, the noos that perceives, the psuchē that is little more than the breath of life, a faint wisp that departs at death to a shadowy half-existence in Hades. The Homeric hero does not deliberate with a unified will so much as get visited by gods and gusts of feeling. The very word that would come to mean “soul” and then “mind,” psuchē, began as the thing you lose when you stop breathing.1

The transformation of this faint breath-soul into the seat of thought, character, and moral worth is one of the pivotal events in the history of the self, and it gathers force in the Pythagorean and Orphic movements and then in Socrates. Pythagoreans treated the soul as something that could transmigrate between bodies, which already implies that the real person is separable from any particular body. By the time of Socrates, as Plato presents him, caring for one's psuchē—making it as good and wise as possible—has become the whole point of a human life. The soul is no longer merely the breath one exhales but the self one is.1

Plato: the divided soul and the self that outlives the body

Plato gave the West its first fully articulated model of an inner self, and two of its features have proven nearly impossible to shake. The first is that the soul is genuinely distinct from the body and survives its death. In the Phaedo, set on the day of Socrates' execution, Plato has him argue that the soul is more akin to the eternal, unchanging Forms than to the perishable body, and that the philosopher's life is a kind of rehearsal for the soul's release from its bodily prison.2 Here the person is essentially an immaterial, immortal thing temporarily lodged in flesh—the template for every later substance dualism.

The second feature is subtler and arguably more influential: the soul is internally divided. In the Republic, Plato analyses it into three parts—a reasoning part (logistikon) that loves truth, a spirited part (thumoeides) that loves honour and feels indignation, and an appetitive part (epithumētikon) that hungers for food, drink, and sex.3 The argument for this partition is psychological rather than metaphysical: because a person can be thirsty and yet refuse to drink, something within is pulling against the appetite, and one thing cannot be both pushing and pulling in the same respect at the same time.3 Justice in the soul, on this picture, is the right ordering of the parts, with reason ruling, spirit enforcing, and appetite obeying—a structure Plato deliberately mirrors in the just city. The idea that the self is a hierarchy of competing drives, to be governed rather than simply expressed, runs straight from here to Freud's tripartite psyche two and a half millennia later.

A person can be thirsty and yet refuse to drink—so something within is pulling against the appetite. From this small observation Plato built an entire architecture of the divided soul.

Aristotle: the soul as the life of a body

Aristotle, Plato's student for twenty years, rejected the master's dualism in favour of a view so different that it still defines one of the live options. In De Anima he argues that the soul is not a thing inside the body but the form of a living body—its organisation, its capacities, the way its matter is arranged so as to be alive.4 His analogy is deliberately deflationary: if the eye were an animal, sight would be its soul; the soul stands to the body as cutting stands to an axe, or as vision stands to an eye.4 On this hylomorphic view (from hulē, matter, and morphē, form), asking whether soul and body are one is as confused as asking whether the shape of a wax seal and the wax are one. They are distinguishable in thought but not separable in fact.5

Aristotle further ranks souls by their powers. Plants have a nutritive soul (growth, reproduction); animals add perception, appetite, and locomotion; humans add reason (nous). Each higher capacity presupposes the lower, so the rational human soul is not a separate spiritual item bolted onto an animal but the highest organising principle of a single, integrated living thing.4 This is why Aristotle's view is the natural ancestor of modern functionalist and biological accounts: the self is what a living human body does, not a substance that haunts it. Aristotle did flirt with one exception—the active intellect, which he hints might be separable and deathless—and that ambiguous passage gave medieval commentators centuries of work, as we will see.5

Hellenistic selves: atoms and the commanding faculty

After Aristotle, the great Hellenistic schools naturalised the soul in starkly different ways. The Epicureans, following Democritus, held that the soul is simply a configuration of especially fine, mobile atoms dispersed through the body; at death those atoms scatter, the person ceases utterly, and—Epicurus' famous therapeutic conclusion—“death is nothing to us,” since where death is, we are not.6 This is perhaps the first thoroughgoing materialist self in the Western canon, and its moral payoff is the dissolution of the fear of death.

The Stoics, by contrast, identified the governing self with the hēgemonikon, the “commanding faculty” located (they thought) in the heart, through which a unified rational soul perceives, assents, desires, and acts.6 Stoic ethics turned on a sharp distinction between what is “up to us”—our judgements and assents, the inner citadel of the commanding faculty—and the externals of fortune that are not. The self that matters is the locus of rational agency, and freedom is the right governance of that inner faculty regardless of circumstance. Both schools, for all their differences, agree against Plato that there is no immortal personal soul, and both make the management of one's own mind the core of the good life.

Ātman: the self behind the self

While Greek philosophy was being born, the Indian tradition was pursuing the question of the self with at least equal sophistication and reaching conclusions both more radical and more various. The Upaniṣads, composed across several centuries in the first millennium BCE, articulate the doctrine of ātman—a true self that is not the body, not the senses, not even the stream of thought, but a deep, unchanging witness underlying all experience.7 The tradition's most celebrated formula, tat tvam asi (“that thou art”), identifies this innermost self with brahman, the ultimate ground of reality: the deepest layer of the subject turns out to be identical with the deepest layer of the cosmos.7 Liberation (mokṣa) consists in realising this identity and so escaping the cycle of rebirth driven by ignorance of one's true nature.

The Ātman doctrine is, in structure, the most ambitious version of the “given, simple, substantial” self imaginable—more radical even than Plato's, because the true self here is not merely separable from the body but is pure consciousness without qualities, the silent ground that makes experience possible while never itself becoming an object of experience. It set the terms for two millennia of Indian debate, and it is precisely the doctrine that the next great Indian movement was founded to deny.

The Buddhist challenge: there is no self to find

The Buddha, traditionally dated to the fifth or sixth century BCE, taught anattā (Sanskrit anātman), the doctrine of not-self, which directly contradicts the Upaniṣadic Ātman. The claim is not the nihilistic one that persons do not exist or that nothing matters. It is the more precise and more unsettling claim that there is no permanent, unchanging, unitary self underlying experience—no Ātman, no soul, no metaphysical owner of one's states.8 When you search for the self, the Buddhist analysis runs, you find only the five aggregates (skandhas): material form, feeling, perception, mental formations, and consciousness—five interdependent, constantly changing processes.8 The self is not any one of these, nor their sum; it is a useful convention, like the word “chariot” for an assemblage of parts, with no further thing behind the name.9

What replaces the substantial self is a causal stream. Successive mental and physical events arise in dependence on prior ones, lawfully but without any abiding subject persisting through them—a position startlingly close to the bundle theory David Hume would propose in Edinburgh in 1739, and one that later Buddhist schools defended with great logical rigour against opponents who pressed the obvious objection: if there is no self, who is reborn, and who reaps the fruits of action?8 The Buddhist answer—that karmic continuity needs only causal connectedness, not a persisting identity—anticipates almost exactly the move Derek Parfit would make in twentieth-century Oxford when he argued that what matters in survival is connectedness and continuity, not identity as such.9 Few facts in intellectual history are as striking as this convergence across twenty-four centuries and half a planet.

There is no permanent self underlying experience—only five interdependent, changing processes, and a convenient name. The Buddha reached in the fifth century BCE a conclusion Hume would rediscover in 1739.

The relational self of the Confucian world

A third ancient settlement deserves mention because it reframes the question itself. In the classical Chinese tradition, especially the Confucian, the person is conceived less as a bounded inner substance than as a node in a web of relationships and roles—child, parent, ruler, subject, friend. One becomes a person, on this view, by cultivating oneself within these relationships and fulfilling their attendant duties; the self is something achieved through ritual, learning, and right relation rather than a given interior possessed from birth.10 This relational, role-constituted conception is not a failure to discover the “real” inner self so much as a different and coherent answer to what a person fundamentally is—and, as we will see, it anticipates by millennia the cross-cultural psychology that distinguishes independent from interdependent construals of the self.

IIThe Medieval Synthesisc. 200 – 1400 CE

Augustine and the discovery of inwardness

If Plato gave the West the immortal soul and Aristotle the soul-as-form, it was Augustine of Hippo, writing around 400 CE, who gave it the inner self—the sense of a private interior depth to be explored, and a first-person voice in which to explore it. Charles Taylor argues that Augustine effected a decisive turn: where Plato directs the soul outward and upward toward the eternal Forms, Augustine directs it inward, teaching that the road to God and to truth runs through the examination of one's own mind.11 The Confessions, often called the first autobiography, is the literary invention of this interior—a sustained, agonised, first-person inquiry into memory, desire, time, and the will, addressed to God but overheard by every reader since.12

Two Augustinian discoveries proved especially durable. The first is the depth of memory. In Book X of the Confessions, Augustine marvels at the “vast palaces of memory,” an inner space so capacious it astonishes its own owner, in which the self stores and re-encounters itself.12 The second is the divided will. Against any simple picture of rational self-governance, Augustine reports the experience of commanding himself and not being obeyed—of willing and not-willing the same thing at once—and locates in this internal conflict the wound of the human condition.12 He also anticipates the cogito: confronted with the sceptical worry that he might be deceived about everything, he replies that the very fact of his doubting proves his existence, for “if I am deceived, I exist” (si fallor, sum)—an argument Descartes would make world-historical twelve centuries later.13 In Augustine's De Trinitate the mind's self-knowledge, self-love, and self-memory even become an image of the triune God, making the structure of selfhood theologically central.13

Plotinus and the ladder of soul

Standing behind Augustine is the Neoplatonism of Plotinus, whose third-century Enneads reconceived the soul as occupying a graded position in a hierarchy of reality descending from the ineffable One through Intellect to Soul and finally to the material world.14 For Plotinus the human self is layered and mobile: at its lowest it is absorbed in the body and sensation, but it can ascend—through contemplation—toward Intellect and ultimately toward mystical union with the One. Crucially, Plotinus held that a part of the soul never fully descends into the body but remains always in the intelligible realm, so that the true self is something we are mostly unaware of and must turn inward to recover.14 This image of the self as a vertical structure, with a divine summit ordinarily hidden from everyday awareness, shaped both Christian mysticism and the broader intuition—still with us—that the self has depths beneath the surface of consciousness.

Avicenna's floating man

The richest medieval contribution to the bare phenomenology of selfhood comes from the Persian philosopher Ibn Sīnā (Avicenna, c. 980–1037), in a thought experiment of startling modernity. Imagine, Avicenna proposes, a person created in an instant, fully formed but suspended in empty air, in the dark, limbs splayed so as to touch nothing—deprived of every sensation, every memory, every perception of body or world. Such a being, Avicenna insists, would still affirm one thing: that it is, that it exists. It would be aware of itself without being aware of anything bodily.15 From this he concludes that self-awareness is primitive and direct—not inferred from anything, not dependent on the senses or the body—and that the self thus immediately known must be something other than the body: an immaterial soul.15

The flying-man argument is at once a precursor to Descartes' cogito and importantly different from it. Avicenna does not argue from systematic doubt but from a direct intuition of pure self-presence; the self is given in a wordless, immediate first-personal awareness that no amount of sensory deprivation can remove.15 Modern phenomenology would later call something very like this the “minimal self” or “pre-reflective self-awareness,” and Avicenna's experiment shows that pre-modern, non-European philosophy had already isolated the phenomenon with great precision—a point that should chasten any account treating inwardness as a uniquely modern Western invention.15

Aquinas: the soul as the form of this body

The towering medieval synthesis is that of Thomas Aquinas, who in the thirteenth century fused Aristotle's hylomorphism with Christian doctrine—a marriage that looks, on its face, almost impossible. Aristotle's view that the soul is simply the form of the body seems to leave no room for personal immortality; Christian doctrine demands it. Aquinas' solution was to argue that the rational soul is the substantial form of the human body—that which makes a parcel of matter into a living, sensing, thinking human being—and yet is also subsistent, able to exist on its own because its highest activity, intellectual thought, does not depend on any bodily organ.16 The soul is therefore not a complete substance in its own right (against Plato) but the form of the body (with Aristotle), and yet it can survive the body's death (with Christianity).17

This elegant compromise carries a famous strain. If I just am a human being—a body informed by a rational soul—then my disembodied soul, persisting after death and awaiting the resurrection, is precisely not me; it is a fragment of me, an incomplete substance longing for its body.17 Aquinas embraces this: the separated soul is not the person, which is why the bodily resurrection is doctrinally indispensable; only when soul and body are reunited does the person exist again in full.16 The result is a deeply embodied account of the person—far closer than Plato's or Descartes' to what contemporary philosophers call the “animalist” view that we are fundamentally living organisms—held in tension with the requirements of immortality. Aquinas thus stands at the hinge between the ancient and modern selves, and the unresolved question he bequeathed—whether the human person is essentially a soul, essentially a body, or essentially the union—remains a live one.17

IIIThe Early-Modern Turn Inwardc. 1600 – 1800

Descartes: I think, therefore I am a thinking thing

With René Descartes the modern self arrives in its most influential and most contested form. Seeking an indubitable foundation for knowledge, Descartes in the 1641 Meditations subjects everything to radical doubt: the senses deceive, the external world might be a dream, and a malicious demon might be falsifying even mathematics. But one thing survives: that I am doubting, and therefore thinking, and therefore exist. Cogito, ergo sum—I think, therefore I am—is the one proposition that cannot be doubted, because the act of doubting it confirms it.18

From this fixed point Descartes draws a momentous conclusion about what he is. He can doubt that he has a body; he cannot doubt that he thinks; therefore his essence is to be a thinking thing (res cogitans), a mind whose whole nature is consciousness and which is, in principle, entirely distinct from the body (res extensa, extended thing).18 The person is essentially an immaterial mind that has a body but is not identical to it. This substance dualism—two radically different kinds of stuff, somehow interacting—became the default modern picture of the self and the explicit target of nearly everything that followed. Its great difficulty, pressed immediately by Descartes' contemporaries and never solved on his terms, is the problem of interaction: how can an unextended, non-physical mind move an extended, physical body, or be affected by it?18 The mind–body problem, in the sharp form that still organises philosophy of mind, is Descartes' bequest.

He can doubt that he has a body; he cannot doubt that he thinks. From this asymmetry Descartes concluded that the self is essentially a mind—and handed modern philosophy a problem it has never put down.

Locke: consciousness, memory, and the invention of personal identity

John Locke performed a quiet revolution by changing the question. Earlier thinkers asked what kind of substance the self is—material or immaterial, mortal or eternal. In the 1694 chapter “Of Identity and Diversity,” added to the second edition of his Essay Concerning Human Understanding, Locke argued that the question of personal identity is independent of the question of substance altogether.19 A “person,” he proposed, is a forensic and psychological concept—a thinking, intelligent being that has reason and reflection and can consider itself as itself, the same thinking thing, in different times and places.19 And what makes a person at one time identical with a person at another is neither the same soul nor the same body but the same consciousness, extended backward through memory.19

The consequences are radical, and Locke pursues them fearlessly. If the same consciousness could in principle be transferred to a different soul or a different body, personal identity would follow the consciousness, not the substance.20 His thought experiments—the prince whose consciousness enters the cobbler's body, the man who cannot remember his youthful deeds—are the direct ancestors of every brain-swap and teleporter puzzle in contemporary philosophy and science fiction.19 The view that personal identity consists in psychological continuity, with memory at its core, is often simply called the “memory criterion,” though Locke's actual position is more careful than the slogan suggests and his interpreters still debate exactly what he held.20 His critics pounced at once: Thomas Reid's objection that a brave officer might remember, as a young soldier, an early flogging, and later, as an old general, remember the gallant soldier but no longer the flogged boy—making him both identical to and not identical to that child—exposed that bare memory is not transitive and so cannot by itself constitute identity.20 The standard repair to Locke's theory—replacing direct memory with overlapping chains of psychological connection—defines the dominant modern approach.21

Leibniz and the windowless self

A very different early-modern self appears in the metaphysics of Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz, for whom reality is composed of monads—simple, immaterial, indivisible centres of perception, each a complete world unto itself, “windowless” in that nothing enters or leaves but each unfolds its whole future from within according to its own nature.22 The human soul is an especially developed monad, distinguished by self-consciousness and reason. What is striking for our purposes is Leibniz's insistence that each self contains, in some sense, its entire history and destiny folded within it, and his recognition—remarkable for its date—of petites perceptions, minute unconscious perceptions below the threshold of awareness that nonetheless shape the conscious mind.22 The idea that the self has an unconscious dimension, so often credited to the nineteenth century, has this seventeenth-century anticipation.

Hume: looking for the self and finding only the stream

David Hume brought the empiricist method to bear on the self with devastating directness. If every genuine idea must derive from an impression—some sensory or inner experience—then we should be able to point to the impression from which our idea of the self derives. But when Hume looks, in the 1739 Treatise of Human Nature, he cannot find it. For his part, he reports, whenever he enters most intimately into what he calls himself, he always stumbles on some particular perception—heat or cold, light or shade, love or hatred—and never catches himself at any time without a perception, nor observes anything but the perceptions.23 There is no impression of a constant, unchanging self, and therefore no legitimate idea of one.

Hume's positive proposal is the bundle theory: the mind is nothing but a bundle or collection of different perceptions, which succeed one another with inconceivable rapidity and are in perpetual flux and movement.23 There is no theatre in which the perceptions appear—only the perceptions, which are the mind. The apparent unity and identity of the self is something we fabricate: the imagination, noticing the resemblance and causal connection among successive perceptions, glides easily from one to the next and mistakes this smooth transition for the observation of an identical object, much as we mistakenly attribute identity to a ship whose planks have all been gradually replaced.23 Personal identity, in short, is a kind of fiction the mind cannot help producing. The convergence with Buddhist anattā—a stream of impermanent, causally linked mental events mistaken for an abiding self—is so close that scholars have debated whether Hume might have absorbed Buddhist ideas through Jesuit reports, though the case for direct influence remains unproven.9 Tellingly, Hume himself confessed in an appendix that he could not fully reconcile his own account—a rare and honest admission that the bundle, lacking any principle to bind it, leaves something unexplained.23

Kant: the “I think” that must accompany every thought

Immanuel Kant accepted much of Hume's negative point—we have no experience of the self as an object, no inner intuition of a soul-substance—but drew a profoundly different conclusion. In the 1781 Critique of Pure Reason, Kant argues that although the self is never given as an object of experience, a unified self is nonetheless a necessary condition of experience. For my scattered perceptions to be experiences of mine, to be bound into a single coherent world, it must be possible for the “I think” to accompany all of them—the famous “transcendental unity of apperception.”24 This unifying “I” is not a thing we observe but a formal requirement of any possible experience: a logical subject presupposed by the very having of a unified mental life.

Kant therefore distinguishes the self as it appears (the empirical self, an object of inner sense, available to psychology) from the self as it is in itself (the transcendental subject, which we can never know as it is). The rationalists' error, he argues in the “Paralogisms,” was to mistake this formal, necessary “I think” for knowledge of a simple, immortal soul-substance—to treat a condition of thought as if it were a thing thought about.24 With Kant the self splits in two for good: there is the self that science can study and the self that does the studying, and the second forever eludes the first. Much of the subsequent history of the self—phenomenology's first-person subject against cognitive science's third-person mechanism—is a working-out of this Kantian division.

IVSelf as History, Will, and Streamc. 1800 – 1900

Hegel and the self made through others

The nineteenth century socialised and historicised the self. For G. W. F. Hegel, self-consciousness is not a solitary given but an achievement reached only through encounter with other self-consciousnesses. In the celebrated dialectic of recognition in the Phenomenology of Spirit (1807)—the master–slave dialectic—Hegel argues that I become fully a self-conscious self only by being recognised as one by another, and that the struggle for such recognition is constitutive of selfhood and, ultimately, of history.25 The self is not a fixed inner substance but a process that unfolds dialectically and socially over time. This relational, developmental conception—that we are made selves by and for others—feeds directly into later social psychology, into theories of identity as socially constituted, and into the entire tradition that treats recognition as a basic human need.

Kierkegaard: the self as a relation that relates to itself

Søren Kierkegaard offered, in 1849, one of the most compressed and demanding definitions of the self ever written: the self is a relation that relates itself to itself.26 For Kierkegaard the human being is a synthesis of opposites—the infinite and the finite, the temporal and the eternal, freedom and necessity—and the self is not any of these elements but the active relating of them, the reflexive activity by which a person takes up and shapes the tensions of their own existence.26 The self, on this view, is a task rather than a fact: something one must become through choice and commitment, and which one can fail to become. Despair, in Kierkegaard's diagnosis, is precisely the misrelation of the self to itself—not wanting to be oneself, or wanting in defiance to be a self of one's own invention apart from the power that established it.26 This dynamic, self-constituting, never-finished self is the seed of twentieth-century existentialism's insistence that existence precedes essence—that we are not given a nature but must make one.

Nietzsche: the subject as fiction and multiplicity

Friedrich Nietzsche pressed the deflationary line further than anyone before Freud. The unified, sovereign “I” presupposed by grammar and by Descartes is, for Nietzsche, a fiction—a misreading of the structure of language into the structure of reality. When we say “I think,” he argues, we illegitimately posit a doer behind the deed, a stable agent behind the activity, when in truth there is only the thinking, which comes when it wishes and not when “I” wish.27 The self is better understood as a shifting plurality of drives and forces—a “social structure of the drives and affects,” a political community of competing impulses under a temporarily dominant ruling drive—than as a single unified ego.27 The apparent unity of the self is the momentary victory of one drive-configuration, not the expression of an underlying substance. This picture of the self as multiplicity, with the conscious unified “I” as a kind of surface effect or after-the-fact construction, anticipates both psychoanalysis and the modern neuroscience of the divided, modular brain.

William James and the two faces of the self

With William James the self becomes a subject of empirical psychology while losing none of its philosophical depth. In the landmark 1890 Principles of Psychology, James draws a distinction that organises self-research to this day: the self as knower (the “I,” the self-as-subject, the ongoing stream of consciousness doing the experiencing) and the self as known (the “Me,” the self-as-object, everything a person can call their own).28 The “Me” James further divides into the material self (the body, possessions, family), the social self (the recognition one receives from others—and, he notes, a person has as many social selves as there are individuals who recognise him), and the spiritual self (one's inner psychological faculties and dispositions, the most intimate and prized layer).28

For the “I,” James proposes a strikingly modern, deflationary mechanism. Rather than positing a soul-substance to provide the unity and continuity of consciousness, he suggests that each passing moment of thought—each “pulse” of the stream—appropriates the thoughts that came just before; the present “Thought” inherits and owns the prior ones, like the latest in a succession of herdsmen each of whom takes possession of the herd from the one before.28 The continuity of the self, on this account, is achieved by an ongoing act of appropriation within the stream of consciousness itself, with no need for a separate substantial owner—a proposal that prefigures Damasio's nested selves and Dennett's narrative self by a full century. James thus hands the twentieth century both a research framework (the empirical Me, eventually the self-concept) and a theory of subjective unity that requires no soul.

VTwentieth-Century Philosophyc. 1900 – 2000

Phenomenology: the embodied, worldly self

The phenomenological tradition returned to first-person experience with new rigour and, in the process, recovered the body. Edmund Husserl, founding the movement in the early twentieth century, sought to describe the structures of consciousness as they are lived, and identified a fundamental feature he called the “pure” or transcendental ego—the standpoint of the first person, the “I” for whom there is a world at all.29 But where Husserl risked a residual Cartesianism, his successors radically embodied and situated the self.

Martin Heidegger, in Being and Time (1927), refused even to begin with a subject confronting an external world. He renamed the human being Dasein—literally “being-there”—to mark that we are always already thrown into a world, absorbed in practical projects and dealings, “being-in-the-world” before we are ever detached observers of it.30 The Cartesian self, isolated and worldless, is for Heidegger a derivative abstraction from this primordial engaged existence. Maurice Merleau-Ponty, in the 1945 Phenomenology of Perception, completed the recovery of the body: the self is fundamentally an embodied subject, a “body-subject,” and perception, motility, and the sense of one's own body (the “body schema”) are the ground of all higher cognition.31 I do not have a body as I have a possession; I am my body as my very opening onto the world—a claim that would be vindicated, decades later, by the cognitive science of embodiment and bodily self-consciousness.31

Jean-Paul Sartre pushed the existential consequences to their limit. In Being and Nothingness (1943) he argued that human consciousness (the “for-itself”) has no fixed essence or nature; it is a kind of nothingness, a perpetual self-transcendence, condemned to be free and to define itself through its choices.32 There is no pre-given self that we discover; there is only the self we make, moment by moment, in the anguish of radical freedom—and the “bad faith” by which we flee that freedom, pretending to be a fixed thing (a waiter, a coward, a settled self) in order to escape the vertigo of our own indeterminacy.32 Contemporary phenomenologists such as Dan Zahavi have distilled from this tradition the notion of a “minimal” or “core” self: not a substance but the basic first-personal character of experience itself—the fact that experience is always given for someone, from a point of view, with a built-in sense of mineness that no analysis into contents can capture.33,34 This minimal self will reappear, recast, at the foundation of the cognitive-science debates.

I do not have a body as I have a possession. I am my body, as my opening onto the world. Merleau-Ponty’s claim would be vindicated, decades later, in the laboratory.

Parfit: when identity is not what matters

In analytic philosophy, the Lockean tradition reached its most sophisticated and unsettling development in Derek Parfit's 1984 Reasons and Persons. Parfit defends a broadly psychological-continuity view—you persist through overlapping chains of memory, intention, belief, and character, what he calls relation R—but he draws from it a conclusion most people find deeply counterintuitive.35 Through a battery of thought experiments—teletransporters that scan and rebuild you, surgeries that divide your brain between two bodies, gradual replacements of your cells with another's—he argues that in the cases that matter there is often simply no determinate answer to whether the resulting person would be you. And the deeper moral is that this indeterminacy does not matter the way we assume.35

Parfit's central claim is that personal identity is “not what matters” in survival.35 Consider being told that tomorrow your brain will be divided and transplanted into two bodies, each psychologically continuous with you. Since both cannot be you (they will lead separate lives), strict identity seems to fail—yet your relation to each is everything that ordinarily matters about survival. So what matters is not identity but psychological connectedness and continuity, which can hold to varying degrees and can even branch.35 Parfit takes this to be liberating rather than dispiriting: if there is no deep, further fact of a persisting self over and above the continuities—if the existence of a person just consists in the existence of a brain and body and the occurrence of a series of interrelated mental and physical events—then the boundaries between lives matter less, the prospect of one's own death becomes less terrifying, and one's concern can extend more impartially to others.35 The resonance with Buddhist not-self is one Parfit himself drew explicitly, and it marks one of the clearest points of contact between the analytic and contemplative traditions.9 Not everyone follows him: Bernard Williams had argued, in a famous 1970 paper, that vividly imagining the prospect of torture reawakens the conviction that bodily continuity carries the self regardless of psychological change—showing how unstable our intuitions about these cases really are.36

The narrative self: a life is a story told

A third twentieth-century current holds that the self is neither a substance nor a bundle but a narrative—a story a person constructs to render a life coherent across time. Paul Ricœur developed the most careful version, distinguishing idem-identity (sameness of a thing through time, like the persistence of an object) from ipse-identity (selfhood, the identity of a someone), and arguing that the latter is constituted narratively: I know who I am by emplotting the events of my life into a story with me as its protagonist.37 Alasdair MacIntyre made the ethical stakes explicit—a human life has the unity of a narrative quest, and I can answer the question “what ought I to do?” only by first answering “of what story do I find myself a part?”38 Marya Schechtman has since argued, on related grounds, that having an articulated self-narrative is partly constitutive of being a person, not merely a description laid over one.39

Daniel Dennett gave the view its sharpest—and most deflationary—formulation from the side of cognitive science. The self, he proposes, is a “center of narrative gravity”: a useful abstraction, like a physicist's centre of mass, that is not itself a thing you could find by dissecting the brain but is posited to make sense of the stream of behaviour and speech.40 Our brains spin a continuous web of words and deeds, and the self is the fictional-but-indispensable protagonist that the web implies—an author we invent in the very act of telling our story, rather than a pre-existing author doing the telling.40 On this account the question “but where is the real self?” is as confused as asking where, precisely, within an object its centre of gravity is located: it is real as an abstraction, indispensable for prediction, and nowhere to be found as a thing.

The case against narrative

The narrative consensus has a forceful dissenter in Galen Strawson, whose 2004 essay “Against Narrativity” denies both that human lives are in fact experienced as narratives and that they ought to be.41 Strawson distinguishes “Diachronic” people, who naturally experience themselves as a single self persisting from the remembered past into the imagined future, from “Episodic” people—among whom he counts himself—who have little sense of being a self that was there in the past or will be there in the future, and who live richly without storifying their lives at all.41 The strong claim that narrative self-construction is necessary for a good or even a fully human life, he argues, is not only false but potentially harmful, valorising one psychological style as if it were a universal requirement.41 In other work Strawson defends a vivid sense of a “thin” subject of experience—a self that is real and present in each conscious moment but does not persist as a single thing across a whole life, a position he locates between the substantial soul and the no-self view.42 The dispute remains unresolved, and it bears directly on the empirical psychology of identity to which we now turn.

VIPsychology’s Selvesc. 1900 – present

Freud and the divided psyche

Sigmund Freud returned the Platonic insight—that the self is internally divided and far from fully governed by reason—to the centre of Western self-understanding, now in a clinical and developmental key. In his mature 1923 model, the psyche comprises three agencies: the id, the reservoir of unconscious instinctual drives operating on the pleasure principle; the ego, which mediates among the id, reality, and conscience, operating on the reality principle; and the superego, the internalised voice of parental and social prohibition, the conscience and ego-ideal.43 The conscious, rational “I” of which we are aware is, on this picture, merely the surface of the ego, itself buffeted by forces it does not control and cannot directly perceive—not even master in its own house.43

Whatever the scientific fate of Freud's specific mechanisms, the structural claim—that the self is a dynamic system of partly unconscious, partly conflicting subsystems, and that the felt unity of the conscious ego conceals this division—has been broadly vindicated by later cognitive science, which finds the mind to be massively parallel, modular, and pervaded by processing inaccessible to awareness.43 Freud is the bridge between Nietzsche's drive-multiplicity and the modern neuroscience of the divided brain.

Self-concept and self-schema

Scientific psychology operationalised James's empirical “Me” as the self-concept: the organised set of beliefs and knowledge a person holds about themselves. Hazel Markus's influential 1977 work reconceived this as a system of self-schemas—cognitive structures, built from past experience, that organise and guide the processing of self-relevant information.44 People who are “schematic” on a given dimension (say, independence) process information about that trait faster, remember it better, and resist counter-evidence more strongly, demonstrating that the self functions not as a passive repository but as an active cognitive apparatus that shapes perception, memory, and inference.44 The psychologist Ulric Neisser later argued that there are in fact several distinct kinds of self-knowledge—the ecological self directly perceived in the environment, the interpersonal self engaged with others, the extended self of memory and anticipation, the private self of inner experience, and the conceptual self or self-narrative—cautioning against treating “the self” as a single unified object of study.45

Identity and the narrative of a life

Erik Erikson placed identity at the centre of human development. In his 1950 framework of psychosocial stages, the central task of adolescence is the achievement of identity as against role confusion—forging a stable, coherent sense of who one is, integrating one's roles, values, and history into a continuous self that links childhood to an anticipated adulthood.46 Identity, for Erikson, is both deeply personal and irreducibly social, located at the meeting point of the individual and the community.46

Erikson's legacy fused with the narrative tradition in the research programme of Dan McAdams, who argues that, beginning in late adolescence, people construct an internalised and evolving life story—a “narrative identity”—that selectively reconstructs the past and imagines the future so as to provide a life with unity, purpose, and meaning.47 On this layered model, a full account of personality requires three levels: dispositional traits, characteristic adaptations (goals, values, coping strategies), and the integrative life story that sits atop them.47 Empirical work in this tradition links the themes of people's self-narratives—for instance, “redemption” sequences in which suffering is reframed as leading to growth—to measurable differences in well-being and generativity, giving the philosophers' narrative self a testable empirical edge.47

The cultural self: where the self ends

Perhaps the most consequential empirical discovery about the self is that its very boundaries are drawn differently across cultures. In a landmark 1991 synthesis, Hazel Markus and Shinobu Kitayama distinguished an independent construal of the self—dominant in many Western, especially North American, contexts—in which the self is bounded, autonomous, and defined by its internal attributes, from an interdependent construal—common in many East Asian and other contexts—in which the self is fundamentally connected to others and defined by its relationships, roles, and social context.48 On the interdependent model, the boundary of the self includes close others, and fitting in, maintaining harmony, and reading relationships are more central to selfhood than expressing a unique inner essence.48

The argument is not merely that people in different cultures hold different beliefs about themselves but that these divergent construals shape cognition, emotion, and motivation at a basic level—what one attends to, what counts as a positive emotion, what motivates achievement.48 This finding gives empirical teeth to a point made independently by the Confucian tradition and by Hegel: the bounded, sovereign individual self that Descartes and Locke took as the universal starting point is, to a significant degree, a culturally specific construction rather than a fact of human nature.48 Much psychology built on Western samples, the cross-cultural work implies, may have mistaken a local self for the human self.

The dialogical self

A further development, the “dialogical self” theory of Hubert Hermans, integrates the narrative and cultural threads. The self, on this account, is not a single unified narrator but a dynamic multiplicity of relatively autonomous “I-positions,” each able to speak in its own voice, arranged in an inner landscape across which the “I” moves and between which it holds internal dialogues.49 I can take the position of the ambitious professional, the dutiful child, the self-critic, and the dreamer, and these positions converse, negotiate, and contest one another much as different people would.49 This explicitly revives, in empirical psychological dress, both Nietzsche's society-of-drives and the Bakhtinian idea of the polyphonic self, modelling the self as a kind of internal society—decentralised, voiced, and dialogical—rather than a single command post.49

VIICognitive Science & Neurosciencec. 1990 – present

Two selves: the minimal and the narrative

Contemporary cognitive science has found it indispensable to distinguish two very different things that the word “self” names. Shaun Gallagher's influential formulation separates a minimal self from a narrative self.50 The minimal self is the immediate, pre-reflective sense of being the subject of one's current experience and the agent of one's current action—a self that is short-lived, bodily, and present in the moment, requiring no memory or story. The narrative self is the extended, temporally stretched self constituted by memory and self-interpretation, the self of one's autobiography.50 The two come apart empirically: a patient with profound amnesia may lose the narrative self almost entirely—unable to recount their life or anticipate their future—while the minimal self, the bare sense of being the one who is now seeing and acting, remains intact.50 This distinction—essentially Gallagher's redrawing of James's “I”/“Me” and Avicenna's bare self-presence in cognitive-scientific terms—structures nearly all current empirical work, and it lets us ask which neural and bodily mechanisms underlie which self.

Owning a body: the rubber hand and the bodily self

The minimal self turns out to be surprisingly manipulable, and the experiments that reveal this are among the most striking in the science of selfhood. In the rubber-hand illusion, first reported by Botvinick and Cohen in 1998, a participant's real hand is hidden while a visible rubber hand is stroked in synchrony with it; within minutes most people come to feel the touch as occurring in the rubber hand and to experience the fake hand as part of their own body.51 The sense of ownership of one's body—which feels like the most basic and incorrigible fact about the self—is shown to be a construction the brain assembles from the statistical correlation of visual, tactile, and proprioceptive signals, and it can be redirected onto a piece of rubber by feeding the brain the right correlations.51 Neuroimaging localises the emergence of this illusory ownership to activity in multisensory regions including premotor cortex, suggesting that bodily self-attribution is the brain's solution to a multisensory binding problem rather than a primitive given.52

The same logic scales up to the whole body. Using virtual reality and head-mounted displays, researchers have induced full-body illusions in which people feel located in, and identified with, a virtual or mannequin body seen from outside—experimentally inducing something like an out-of-body experience and demonstrating that even the sense of where one is, the spatial self-location at the core of bodily selfhood, is a revisable construction of multisensory integration.53 These results give Merleau-Ponty's body-subject a precise mechanism and show that the most basic layer of the self—feeling that this body is mine and that I am here—is built, not given.53

Within minutes, most people feel a rubber hand become their own. The sense of owning a body—the most incorrigible-seeming fact about the self—is something the brain assembles, and can be fooled into reassigning.

The brain’s self-network

Where in the brain is the self—or at least, self-related processing? Functional imaging consistently implicates a set of midline structures: the medial prefrontal cortex and the posterior cingulate cortex / precuneus are reliably engaged when people make judgements about their own traits, preferences, and experiences, as a large meta-analysis of self-referential processing established.54 These “cortical midline structures” overlap substantially with the default mode network—the network identified by Marcus Raichle and colleagues that is most active precisely when the brain is not engaged in any external task, during rest, mind-wandering, and self-referential thought.55 The brain's baseline activity, in other words, when left to its own devices, is heavily self-related: remembering one's past, imagining one's future, ruminating on oneself.54

This is suggestive but must be read with care. The midline regions are not a “self spot” or a soul-organ; they support certain kinds of self-referential processing, and self-relevant cognition is distributed across many systems. What the imaging does establish is that the self is the product of ongoing neural activity—activity that has a characteristic signature, that runs by default, and that can be modulated, for instance by meditative training that quiets self-referential processing.54 The self is something the brain continuously does, not a thing it contains.

Damasio’s nested selves

Antonio Damasio has proposed the most influential layered, biological model of the self, grounding it in the body and its regulation. In The Feeling of What Happens (1999) he distinguishes three nested levels, each built upon the one below.56 The proto-self is a non-conscious, moment-to-moment neural map of the internal state of the organism—the brain's continuous representation of the body it is keeping alive. The core self arises when the organism registers how an object or event changes that bodily state, generating a transient, here-and-now sense of being a subject to whom things are happening; it is recreated pulse by pulse and has no memory.56 The autobiographical self emerges when this core self is enriched by memory and anticipation—the accumulated record of who one has been and projects of who one will be—yielding the full, temporally extended self we ordinarily identify with.56

The deep claim is that consciousness and selfhood are rooted not in abstract cognition but in the body's homeostatic self-regulation—that the self begins as the brain's feeling of its own living body, and that feeling is the foundation on which all higher selfhood is constructed.57 This makes Damasio's a thoroughly embodied and biological account—closer to Aristotle's soul-as-the-life-of-a-body than to Descartes' disembodied mind—in which the self is the activity of an organism maintaining itself, scaled up through memory into a person.57 It also maps remarkably onto the minimal/narrative distinction: the core self is the minimal self given a mechanism, and the autobiographical self is the narrative self given a substrate.

The interpreter: how the divided brain feels unified

If the brain is in fact a parallel, modular system—as Nietzsche and Freud suspected and neuroscience confirms—why does the self feel so seamlessly unified? The most illuminating evidence comes from split-brain patients, whose corpus callosum has been surgically severed to control epilepsy, disconnecting the two cerebral hemispheres. Michael Gazzaniga's decades of work with these patients revealed that the disconnected hemispheres can be made to perceive and even act on different information—and, crucially, that the left hemisphere contains what he termed an interpreter: a system that constantly constructs explanations and narratives to make sense of behaviour and feelings, even when it has no access to their true causes.58

In the classic demonstration, the right hemisphere is shown a command (say, “walk”) that the verbal left hemisphere cannot see; the patient gets up and walks, and when asked why, the left-hemisphere interpreter instantly confabulates a plausible reason (“I'm going to get a drink”)—sincerely, with no awareness that it is making the story up.59 The unified, rational self that narrates and explains our actions is, on this evidence, partly a post-hoc storyteller, weaving a coherent autobiography over processes it did not initiate and cannot observe—a striking neuroscientific vindication of Nietzsche's “doer behind the deed” critique and of Dennett's centre of narrative gravity.58 The felt unity of the self is, at least in part, a narrative achievement of a single brain region papering over a divided system.58

The self-model: the self as the brain’s simulation

The most philosophically radical contemporary view synthesises these findings into a single thesis: the self is not a thing but a model—a representation the brain builds and runs. Thomas Metzinger argues, in Being No One (2003), that no such thing as a self exists in the world; what exists is a “phenomenal self-model,” a dynamic representation of the organism-as-a-whole that the brain constructs and that, crucially, is transparent—the system cannot recognise the model as a model.60 Because we cannot experience the representational vehicle as a representation, we look straight through it and take ourselves to be in direct contact with a real self and a real world. The self, on this view, is the content of a model the brain cannot see as such—hence the deliberately provocative claim that “no one” is ever born or dies, because there was never a self-thing there, only an ongoing self-modelling process.60 In his more accessible The Ego Tunnel (2009), Metzinger frames conscious experience as a kind of tunnel—a low-dimensional, brain-constructed projection of reality, with the self as the model at its centre.61

This self-model view has converged with the broader “predictive processing” framework, on which the brain is fundamentally a prediction engine that constructs a model of the world—and of the body and self within it—by continually generating and updating hypotheses to minimise prediction error.62 Anil Seth has developed this into a striking account on which perception itself is a “controlled hallucination,” the brain's best guess about the causes of its sensory signals, and the experienced self—including the feeling of being a unified subject and of owning a body—is likewise a controlled hallucination, a prediction the brain makes about the causes of its own internal, especially bodily, signals.63 On this view the self is real as an experience and indispensable as a control mechanism for keeping the organism alive, while being, in the end, a construction—a model the brain runs, not a passenger it carries.63 The convergence with Buddhist anattā, Hume's bundle, and Dennett's narrative gravity is, once again, hard to miss.60

VIIIConvergences & Open QuestionsThe present

Is the self an illusion?

A recurrent contemporary claim is that the self is an “illusion.” Bruce Hood, surveying the social and cognitive neuroscience, argues that the unified self is something the brain conjures—a construction generated largely by our immersion in a social world, with no single, enduring entity behind it.64 But “illusion” must be handled with care, and the disputes here are partly verbal. To say the self is an illusion can mean one of several quite different things: that there is no immaterial soul-substance (almost universally accepted in the sciences); that there is no enduring, unchanging entity persisting unaltered through a life (the Humean and Buddhist claim); that the brain's self-model misrepresents its own nature by presenting a construction as a given (Metzinger's transparency thesis); or—the strongest and least defensible—that there is no self in any sense at all.60 Most careful theorists reject the last. The self may be a process rather than a thing, a construction rather than a given, a model rather than a soul—and still be perfectly real as a process, construction, or model, in the way that a hurricane or a melody or a centre of gravity is real without being a substance.64 What is illusory is a particular metaphysical interpretation of the self, not the self as such. The American cognitive scientist Douglas Hofstadter captures the residue that survives the deflation: the “I” is a real but strange pattern, a self-referential loop by which a sufficiently complex system comes to represent and refer to itself—genuine, causally potent, and yet not a further thing over and above the loop.65

The extended and enactive self

A different frontier asks not whether the self is real but where it ends. The “extended mind” thesis of Andy Clark and David Chalmers argues that the machinery of the mind need not stop at the skull: when a notebook, a smartphone, or another person reliably plays the functional role that internal memory would play, it can count as a genuine part of one's cognitive system—and, by extension, of one's self.66 The self, on the most ambitious reading, leaks into its tools, its environment, and its relationships; the boundary of the self at the skin is a convenient default, not a metaphysical given.66

The deepest synthesis here is the enactive tradition launched by Francisco Varela, Evan Thompson, and Eleanor Rosch in The Embodied Mind (1991), which argues that cognition and selfhood are enacted—brought forth—through the dynamic coupling of a living body with its environment, rather than computed inside a brain that passively represents a pre-given world.67 On this view the self is not located anywhere in particular; it is an ongoing activity of a body-in-its-world, a self-producing process more like a flame or a whirlpool than a stone.67 The position recovers, with new precision, the ancient Aristotelian intuition that the self is the life of a living thing rather than a substance lodged within it—and joins it to the phenomenologists' embodied subject and the neuroscientists' self-as-process.

The contemplative turn

Strikingly, that same 1991 book was also the founding text of the modern dialogue between cognitive science and Buddhist philosophy. Varela, Thompson, and Rosch observed that Western science had arrived, by the third-person route of experiment, at a picture of the self—selfless, processual, constructed—that the Buddhist contemplative traditions had reached two and a half millennia earlier by the first-person route of disciplined introspection, and they argued that the two could correct and enrich each other.67 The convergence is not coincidental window-dressing: when the cognitive scientist concludes that there is no unified controller in the brain, only distributed processes that the system models as a self, and the Buddhist contemplative reports that sustained attention to experience reveals no abiding self but only impermanent, interdependent events, they are describing recognisably the same terrain from opposite directions.9 This dialogue—extended in recent decades into the empirical study of meditation and its effects on self-referential processing—is perhaps the clearest sign that the long argument about the self is genuinely cumulative rather than merely cyclical: traditions that never met are converging on an answer.54

Western science arrived, by the third-person route of experiment, at a picture of the self that the contemplative traditions had reached, by the first-person route of introspection, two and a half millennia before.

Why the answer matters

It would be a mistake to treat all this as an abstract metaphysical parlour game, because the model of the self one holds reaches into the most practical corners of a life. The criterion of personal identity decides who may be punished for a past act, who is bound by a past promise, and who inherits a past debt—Locke saw clearly that “person” is at bottom a forensic term, tracking accountability across time.19 It bears on the ethics of dementia and severe brain injury: if the narrative self can erode while the minimal self persists, what do we owe the person who no longer remembers being one?50 It frames the prospect of one's own death, which Epicurus and Parfit alike tried to defang by arguing that there is, in the end, less of a persisting self to lose than we fear.35 And it now confronts genuinely novel cases the ancients could not have imagined—the moral status of a brain emulation that is psychologically continuous with a person, the boundaries of a self extended into devices and networks, the question of whether an artificial system that models itself could be a someone rather than a something.60 On each of these, what one is prepared to count as the same self, or as a self at all, does real work.

What remains unresolved is not whether the self is constructed—on that, an unusually broad coalition of phenomenologists, neuroscientists, analytic philosophers, and contemplatives now agrees—but what follows from its being constructed. Does the manifest unity of consciousness reduce without remainder to the integration of distributed processes, or does the first-person character of experience—the bare fact that there is something it is like to be this self—mark a limit that the third-person sciences cannot dissolve?33 Here the Kantian division reasserts itself: the self that science studies and the self that does the studying have still not been made fully to coincide, and the explanatory gap between the mechanism and the experienced subject remains the discipline's hardest open problem.60

The question that will not resolve

Surveyed across twenty-five centuries, the models of the self do not form a simple march from error to truth. They form something more like a long conversation in which a handful of basic options—substance and process, unity and multiplicity, given and constructed—are taken up, refined, abandoned, and recovered in new vocabularies. The immortal soul of Plato gives way to the soul-as-form of Aristotle, which the Christian Aquinas fuses with immortality, which Descartes sunders back into a thinking substance, which Locke recasts as a continuity of consciousness, which Hume dissolves into a bundle, which Kant reconstitutes as a formal condition, which the phenomenologists re-embody, which the neuroscientists rebuild as a model the brain runs—and which the Buddhists, all along, had described as a process with no owner. The trajectory is real, and it has a direction: away from the self as a thing and toward the self as an activity, away from a given soul and toward a construction, away from a substance you could in principle find and toward a pattern that is nowhere and yet everywhere in what a person does.

Yet the deflation never quite completes itself, because the question is asked from inside the very thing it questions. Each of us remains the one for whom there is a world, the one who reads this last sentence and recognises—against every argument that no such recogniser exists—that someone is doing the reading. That recognition is not refuted by the discovery that the self is built rather than given; it is, if anything, deepened by it. The self may be the most elaborate thing the universe builds out of itself, a model so vivid it cannot see that it is a model, a story so continuous it forgets that it is being told. To understand it as such is not to lose it but to meet it—which is, perhaps, what the philosophers and contemplatives meant all along by knowing oneself.
