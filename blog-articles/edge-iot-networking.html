Edge & IoT Networking Models:
The Architecture of the Always-On World

From oil-pipeline telemetry to planetary-scale sensor networks — how the Device→Gateway→Cloud stack and MQTT's publish-subscribe model are redefining distributed computing at the edge.

April 25, 2026 by Nicholas Knapp ~3,200 words Networking · IoT · Edge Computing · MQTT

Somewhere in the Bakken Formation, beneath the winter plains of North Dakota, a pressure transducer on a crude oil pipeline fires a 47-byte packet skyward to a satellite. That signal — low-bandwidth, battery-constrained, intermittently connected — is the founding problem of an entire networking paradigm. In 1999, two engineers designed a protocol to solve it. A quarter-century later, their creation underpins billions of connections across factories, farms, hospitals, and cities. This is the story of Edge and IoT networking: its origins, architecture, mechanics, tradeoffs, and the open challenges still being worked through today.

§ 01 — Origins & History

Before the "Internet of Things" Had a Name

The phrase "Internet of Things" was coined by Kevin Ashton, co-founder of MIT's Auto-ID Center, in 1999 — but the impulse to network physical devices predates that framing by decades.[1] SCADA (Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition) systems had been connecting industrial sensors to control computers since the 1960s. What those systems lacked was a lightweight, interoperable protocol designed for constrained devices on unreliable links.

The Oil Pipeline Problem

The proximate ancestor of today's IoT communication stack was born out of a very specific industrial pain point. In 1998–1999, Arlen Nipper (then of Arcom Control Systems) and Andy Stanford-Clark of IBM were confronted with the challenge of monitoring pipelines that traversed hundreds of miles of remote terrain.[2] Sensors existed. Satellite links existed. But the cost of those satellite connections was enormous — measured per kilobit — and the link quality was notoriously unreliable.

"The goal was a protocol that is bandwidth-efficient, lightweight, and uses little battery power — because the devices were connected via satellite link, which was extremely expensive at that time."

— Wikipedia on MQTT's origins, citing Stanford-Clark & Nipper's design intent [3]

Their initial draft, internally called "Argo Lightweight On The Wire Protocol," was explicitly designed to move away from the "legacy of literally hundreds of proprietary poll/response protocols" that dominated industrial communication at the time.[4] By the end of 1999, the protocol had been renamed "MQ Integrator Pervasive Device Protocol" (MQIpdp) and included will messages, keepalive processing, and the ability to unsubscribe — core features still in use today.[4]

1999
MQTT Version 1 & 2 Drafted

Stanford-Clark (IBM) and Nipper (Arcom) author the first version for oil pipeline SCADA monitoring. Version 2 follows within months, adding will messages and keepalive.[4]

2000
Version 3 — Stable for a Decade

Minor clarifications produce MQTT 3, which remains stable for the next ten years as IBM uses it internally across industrial deployments.[4]

2010
IBM Opens MQTT 3.1 Royalty-Free

IBM releases the protocol specification as a royalty-free open standard, shifting focus from proprietary embedded systems to open IoT use cases.[5]

2011
Eclipse Paho Project Founded

IBM contributes MQTT client implementations to the Eclipse Foundation's new Paho project, creating a supportive open-source ecosystem that dramatically accelerates adoption.[5]

2014
OASIS Standard — MQTT 3.1.1

MQTT v3.1.1 becomes an OASIS open standard and is subsequently ratified as ISO/IEC 20922. Reserved TCP port: 1883 (plaintext), 8883 (TLS).[3][6]

2019
MQTT 5.0 Released

Major upgrade adds reason codes, session/message expiry, shared subscriptions, and request/response patterns — addressing scalability limits of 3.1.1 in large deployments.[6]

2024
56% IIoT Protocol Share

The Eclipse Foundation's 2024 IoT & Embedded Developer Survey finds MQTT leads as the preferred IIoT communication protocol with 56% adoption — a 7% increase from 2023.[7]

Sending all data generated at industrial edges to a central cloud is no longer feasible — financially or technically. As one analysis notes, data volumes from energy consumption tracking, manufacturing process telemetry, and on-site computer vision inference results are growing steadily and unavoidably: transmitting everything centrally is impractical both from bandwidth and cost perspectives.[10] This fundamental constraint drives the three-tier architecture that now dominates the field.

§ 03 — Core Architecture

The Device → Gateway → Cloud Stack

Tier 1: The Device Layer

The device layer encompasses the physical sensors, actuators, and embedded systems that interface with the physical world. These devices are characterized by severe resource constraints: limited CPU cycles (often 8- or 32-bit microcontrollers), limited RAM (frequently kilobytes, not megabytes), constrained power budgets (battery or energy-harvesting), and heterogeneous connectivity options — Bluetooth Low Energy, Zigbee, LoRaWAN, Modbus, RS-485, or simple serial UART.[1][11]

A key design constraint at this layer is that devices rarely speak IP natively. Many older industrial protocols use polling-based models — a controller regularly queries each device for its current state. This approach was designed to avoid packet collisions when multiple devices share a network, but it creates latency and scales poorly.[4] Protocol translation is therefore a fundamental gateway function.

Tier 2: The Edge Gateway

The edge gateway is the most architecturally complex tier in the stack. It performs several critical functions simultaneously: protocol translation (converting Modbus or BLE data to MQTT), local computation and filtering (running lightweight analytics to reduce upstream bandwidth), buffering (storing messages during cloud connectivity outages), and security enforcement (terminating TLS, authenticating devices, enforcing ACLs).[12]

Modern edge gateways support 80 or more industrial protocols — including Modbus, OPC-UA, Ethernet/IP, and BACnet — converting their output to MQTT for standardized upstream communication.[12] The gateway is also where edge AI increasingly runs: inference results from computer vision models, anomaly detection algorithms, and predictive maintenance models may never need to reach the cloud, reducing latency and bandwidth costs simultaneously.[10]

Tier 3: The Cloud Platform

The cloud tier receives the filtered, pre-processed data forwarded by gateways. Its responsibilities are long-horizon: historical storage in time-series databases, batch and streaming analytics, machine learning model training, cross-site aggregation, and exposing APIs to business applications and dashboards. The key distinction from pre-edge architectures is that the cloud no longer sees raw sensor telemetry — it sees curated, meaningful events and aggregated metrics that the edge has already processed.[10][13]

§ 04 — The Protocol Layer

MQTT: Publish-Subscribe for a Constrained World

MQTT is a lightweight, publish-subscribe, machine-to-machine network protocol designed for connections with remote locations that have devices with resource constraints or limited network bandwidth.[3] It is an open OASIS standard (ISO/IEC 20922) and the de facto protocol binding for modern IoT systems.

The Publish-Subscribe Model

Unlike traditional request-response protocols (HTTP, CoAP in its basic form), MQTT uses a broker-mediated publish-subscribe pattern. Producers — sensors, devices, gateways — publish messages to named topics on the broker without needing to know who, if anyone, is listening. Consumers subscribe to topic patterns and receive messages asynchronously. The broker acts as the intermediary, routing messages and decoupling data producers from subscribers in both space (no need to know each other's addresses) and time (no need to be online simultaneously).[14]

# Canonical MQTT topic hierarchy — pipeline telemetry plant/bakken/well-7/pressure ← specific sensor plant/bakken/well-7/+ ← all sensors on well-7 (single-level wildcard) plant/bakken/# ← all data from Bakken plant (multi-level wildcard) plant/+/+/temperature ← all temperature sensors across all plants/wells # Minimum overhead: 2-byte fixed header + variable header # A typical PUBLISH packet: ~20–40 bytes total (vs HTTP: 600–800 bytes) QoS: 0 → fire and forget (0 bytes ACK overhead) QoS: 1 → at least once (PUBACK) QoS: 2 → exactly once (PUBREC → PUBREL → PUBCOMP)

Quality of Service Levels

One of MQTT's most important design decisions is its tiered Quality of Service (QoS) system. The three levels offer deliberate tradeoffs between message delivery guarantees, network bandwidth consumption, and processing overhead.[15]

Level Guarantee Handshake Overhead Typical Use Case
QoS 0 At most once ("fire and forget") None Minimal High-frequency sensor readings; losing one value is acceptable (next will arrive shortly)[15]
QoS 1 At least once (acknowledged) PUBACK Low Important alerts or state changes where duplicates can be handled; most common general choice[15]
QoS 2 Exactly once (assured) 4-step (PUBREC/PUBREL/PUBCOMP) Highest Financial transactions, actuation commands where duplication causes physical harm[16]

Retained Messages and Last Will & Testament

Two less-discussed MQTT features are critical to real-world deployments. Retained messages allow the broker to store the last published value for a topic so that newly subscribing clients immediately receive current state without waiting for the next publication.[3] This is essential for bootstrapping dashboards and newly connected consumers. Last Will and Testament (LWT) messages are registered during connection setup; if a client disconnects ungracefully, the broker publishes the will message on the client's behalf — effectively providing a decentralized "device offline" notification system without requiring external heartbeat polling.[17]

§ 05 — Resilience Patterns

Designing for Disconnection: Intermittent Connectivity Handling

Industrial edge systems must be designed with an offline-first mindset. Connectivity outages are not exceptional conditions — they are an expected operating state that systems must accommodate gracefully.[10] This reality shapes every layer of the architecture.

"Edge workloads should continue to operate and make local decisions, even when connectivity to the cloud is degraded or entirely unavailable. This is not uncommon; it is an expected operating condition."

— Avassa, "MQTT at the Edge: Deploying Lightweight Messaging at Scale" (2026) [10]

MQTT's Built-In Resilience Mechanisms

Persistent Sessions: When a client connects with cleanSession=false, the broker stores the client's subscription information and any undelivered QoS 1 or 2 messages. When the client reconnects — even after an extended outage — it receives all buffered messages in order, resuming exactly where it left off. The session is keyed to a unique client ID and can survive across complete broker restarts when properly configured.[18]

Keep-Alive and Reconnect Logic: MQTT clients specify a keep-alive interval during connection. If no messages flow in that window, the client sends a PINGREQ; the broker responds with PINGRESP. If the broker detects the connection has died, it triggers the client's LWT message. Well-implemented clients use exponential backoff reconnect strategies to avoid thundering-herd reconnection storms when connectivity is restored after an outage.[17]

Gateway-Side Store-and-Forward: For extended outages — where even a QoS 2 broker-side buffer might be insufficient — the standard pattern involves the gateway maintaining a local durable queue (typically backed by an embedded database like SQLite or RocksDB). Incoming device messages are written to local storage immediately. A separate upstream agent reads from this queue and publishes to the broker, removing messages only after receiving acknowledgment. Upon reconnection, the agent drains the queue in order, optionally deduplicating using embedded monotonic sequence numbers in message payloads.[19]

Message written to local queue BEFORE ack to device # 2. Message removed from queue ONLY after broker PUBACK # 3. Each message carries: timestamp + sequence_num + device_id

Edge-Side MQTT Brokers

An increasingly common pattern deploys an MQTT broker directly at the edge gateway — not just a forwarding client. This architecture allows local device-to-device communication (e.g., a temperature sensor triggering a cooling relay) to function entirely without cloud connectivity. The edge broker then bridges upstream to a cloud broker, forwarding only a subset of topics. This hierarchical broker topology is explicitly supported by major implementations including Mosquitto (via bridge configuration) and EMQX.[10][20]

The tradeoff is operational complexity: rather than managing one central broker, teams must lifecycle-manage multiple brokers per site — handling upgrades, certificate rotation, and failure modes across a distributed fleet of potentially ARM-based industrial gateways with limited compute headroom.[10]

§ 06 — Design Tradeoffs

Key Architectural Decisions and Their Consequences

Every edge/IoT architecture involves a set of recurring tradeoffs. Understanding them explicitly — rather than discovering them at 2 AM during a production incident — is the hallmark of mature systems design.

Decision Option A Option B Key Consideration
QoS Level QoS 0 Fast, minimal overhead QoS 2 Exact-once, high overhead Message loss vs. latency + bandwidth. QoS 1 is the practical default for most telemetry.[15]
Processing Location Edge Low latency, offline-capable Cloud Elastic compute, richer analytics Latency-critical and offline-must-work → edge. Cross-site aggregation and ML training → cloud.[13]
Session Persistence Persistent Survives reconnects Clean Always fresh state Long-lived subscribers (dashboards, historians) need persistence. Short-lived command publishers may prefer clean.[18]
Broker Topology Centralized Simpler ops Hierarchical Offline-resilient Each edge broker adds operational burden but enables local autonomy during WAN outages.[10]
Data Granularity Raw Full fidelity, high bandwidth Aggregated Reduced bandwidth, data loss Push aggregation to the edge; forward only anomalies, statistics, or events exceeding thresholds.[10]
Security Model Username/Password + TLS Simple Mutual TLS + JWT/OAuth Strong Device certificates scale better than passwords but require PKI infrastructure. mTLS adds per-connection overhead on constrained devices.[19]

§ 07 — Open Challenges

What the Industry Is Still Working Through

Security at Scale

Each device represents a potential attack surface. With billions of endpoints, managing certificates, rotating credentials, and enforcing least-privilege topic ACLs across heterogeneous hardware fleets is an unsolved operations problem. Italian researchers demonstrated slow DoS attacks against MQTT in 2020.[3]

Standardization Gaps

Despite MQTT's dominance, the broader IoT landscape remains fragmented across dozens of device protocols (Modbus, BACnet, OPC-UA, LoRaWAN, Zigbee, Z-Wave). Protocol translation at the gateway layer is still largely custom, bespoke work. No universal standard exists.[11]

Edge Orchestration

Standard Kubernetes assumes stable, continuous connectivity — the opposite of edge realities. Its control plane introduces CPU and memory overhead that often exceeds what ARM-based industrial gateways can support. Edge-native orchestration (K3s, MicroShift, Akri) is still maturing.[10]

Event Ordering Inversions

In pub/sub systems with intermittent connectivity, disconnect notifications can arrive after reconnect notifications — causing monitoring systems applying last-write-wins to record a device as offline when it has already reconnected. This is a known failure mode in asynchronous message passing, documented in distributed systems literature.[3]

Data Volume Growth

Edge AI (computer vision, LLMs on embedded hardware) is dramatically increasing data generation at edge sites. As inference results, model update traffic, and telemetry volumes grow, the bandwidth assumptions baked into current gateway and broker designs will require revision.[10]

Regulatory Complexity

Data processed at the edge may still be subject to GDPR, HIPAA, or sector-specific regulations — even if it never reaches the cloud. Determining where data residency obligations attach in a multi-tier architecture is an active legal and compliance challenge.[9]

§ 08 — Evolution & Outlook

Where the Architecture Is Heading

The edge/IoT networking stack is not static. Several converging trends are reshaping what the reference architecture looks like in 2025 and beyond.

5G and Network Slicing

The rollout of 5G networks is a powerful catalyst: sub-millisecond latencies, network slicing, and mobile edge computing allow compute to be pushed closer to devices without requiring on-premises hardware. This partially relaxes the "must work offline" constraint for some applications — though it introduces new dependencies on carrier infrastructure reliability.[21]

Edge AI Integration

MQTT is increasingly used to transport not raw sensor readings but inference results from on-device ML models. A camera on a factory floor runs a computer vision model locally; only the detection result ("anomaly detected at conveyor 3, confidence 0.94") traverses the WAN to the cloud broker — reducing upstream bandwidth by orders of magnitude while enabling real-time local response.[10] The advent of models like IBM's Granite 3.0, optimized specifically for edge inference, signals that this pattern will become standard.[22]

Unified Namespace (UNS)

The Unified Namespace pattern, popularized in industrial contexts through Sparkplug B (an MQTT payload specification), treats the MQTT broker as a "single source of truth" for all operational data across a facility. Rather than point-to-point integrations between systems, every producer publishes to a common namespace and every consumer subscribes as needed. This dramatically reduces integration complexity in large manufacturing environments and is gaining rapid adoption in the IIoT space.[23]

MQTT 5.0 and Beyond

MQTT 5.0's addition of session expiry intervals, shared subscriptions, and reason codes has addressed most of the scalability limitations of 3.1.1. The protocol's co-inventors and the HiveMQ technical community have indicated that MQTT 5.0 is expected to remain stable for the foreseeable future — with no new major version planned — as the focus shifts to ecosystem maturation over protocol evolution.[24]

"MQTT is no longer limited to simple sensor telemetry. Increasingly, AI and machine learning workloads at the edge use MQTT to transport inference results. MQTT's simplicity and flexibility make it well suited to these evolving use cases."

— Avassa, "MQTT at the Edge: Deploying Lightweight Messaging at Scale" (Feb 2026) [10]
