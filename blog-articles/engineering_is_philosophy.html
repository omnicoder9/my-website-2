Field Notes · Design & Epistemology No. 07  /  July 2026
An essay on assumptions, models, and the buried claims inside every system

Engineering Is
Philosophy

Every design is a bet on a theory of reality. The only question is whether the philosophy has been examined — or left to compile silently into the walls.

◆ ◆ ◆

Ask most people to place engineering and philosophy on a map of human activity and they will set them at opposite poles. Philosophy is the discipline of unanswerable questions, pursued from an armchair. Engineering is the discipline of answerable ones, pursued with steel and silicon. One trades in speculation; the other in results. The engineer, on this view, is precisely the person who has stopped asking what reality is and started getting something to work within it.

This is a category error, and an expensive one. Every engineered system is a philosophical argument rendered in matter. It makes claims about what exists, what can be known, what may be safely ignored, and what human beings are like — and then it stakes real consequences on those claims being true. The engineer who sizes a beam is doing metaphysics with a load table. Herbert Simon named the strangeness of it: the natural sciences study how things are, but engineering is a science of the artificial — of how things ought to be, of the contingent world we design rather than the given one we find.1 To design is to take a position.

The trouble is that the positions are almost always implicit. A bridge does not announce its theory of wind. A database does not print its metaphysics on the login screen. The philosophy is compiled away — folded into defaults, tolerances, and schemas — until one day the world tests a claim the designers never knew they were making, and the system fails in a way that cannot be explained from inside its own assumptions. What follows is a tour of those buried claims: where they hide, why they matter, and what changes when engineers treat them as philosophy rather than detail.

The map is not the territory

The foundational move in all of engineering is also the foundational move in a certain kind of philosophy: build a model. Alfred Korzybski compressed the whole hazard of it into a sentence — the map is not the territory.2 A representation is not the thing represented; it is a smaller, flatter, more tractable stand-in, and its usefulness comes precisely from what it leaves out. You cannot design a bridge by simulating every atom in the river beneath it. You choose what to include and what to discard, and that choice is the design.

But discarding is a philosophical act. The statistician George Box put the engineer's version of it plainly: every model is wrong, because no finite representation can exactly capture a real system; the only serious question is whether it is wrong in a way that stops it being useful.3 Later he added the line that has since become a discipline's motto — essentially, all models are approximations, and the good ones are useful anyway.4 Being wrong is not a defect to be engineered out; it is the price of having a model at all. What separates a great designer from a mediocre one, Box argued, is knowing which wrongness matters — worrying about the tigers rather than the mice.

Fig. 1 A model earns its power by throwing almost everything away. The judgment of what to discard — and the residual it leaves behind — is where engineering becomes philosophy.

Every ordinary design decision is one of these judgments in disguise. When a structural engineer models a distributed load as perfectly uniform, they assert that the real, lumpy distribution is close enough that the difference will not bite. When a software architect treats a database transaction as atomic, they take a position on how spinning disks and memory caches behave beneath layers of abstraction. When a traffic planner sizes a road to average daily volume, they make a claim about the relationship between averages and the extremes that actually break things. None of these is merely technical. Each is an epistemological commitment: a bet about what reality is like and which of its features are allowed to matter.

Ontology: what a system rules to exist

Before a system can compute anything, it must decide what kinds of things there are. This is ontology — the branch of metaphysics concerned with what exists — and every schema, data type, and dropdown menu is a ruling on it. A form that offers two options for gender has legislated a metaphysics of gender. A date field with two digits has declared what a year is permitted to be. A "name" split into first and last has defined what a person's name fundamentally is. Whatever the schema cannot represent does not, for that system, exist.

Geoffrey Bowker and Susan Leigh Star showed that classification is never innocent: to sort the world into categories is to make some things visible and legible while rendering others invisible, and those choices carry moral and political weight long after their authors are gone.5 The categories feel like neutral description; they are in fact decisions about what counts. And the decisions are not even consistent within a single project. Louis Bucciarelli, studying how engineers actually work, found that a designer, a thermal analyst, and a manufacturing engineer looking at the "same" device in fact inhabit different object worlds — each modeling the artifact in terms their discipline makes real, often with no shared representation at all.6 Much of engineering is the labor of reconciling these private ontologies into one that a machine can execute.

Abstraction, the technique that makes all of this manageable, is itself a metaphysical promise — and a leaky one. Joel Spolsky's law of leaky abstractions names the recurring betrayal: an abstraction offers a clean, simple ontology (a network is a reliable pipe; a file is a contiguous thing), and reality seeps through it at exactly the moment you were relying on the simplicity.7 The abstraction was a small map. The territory was always underneath, waiting.

Epistemology: how a system claims to know

If a system's schema is its ontology, its sensors are its epistemology — its theory of how it comes to know anything about the world. And like any theory of knowledge, it can be naive. The deepest engineering question a sensing system faces is the oldest question in epistemology dressed in new clothes: how do you know your instrument is not lying to you?

The Boeing 737 MAX answered that question badly, and 346 people died. Its Maneuvering Characteristics Augmentation System, or MCAS, was designed to push the aircraft's nose down when the angle of attack grew dangerously high — and it took that angle-of-attack reading from a single sensor, trusting one vane on the fuselage as ground truth.8 When that sensor fed false high readings, the system did not doubt them. It treated a lie as reality and acted on it with lethal confidence, trimming the nose down against pilots who could not overpower it. Lion Air Flight 610 fell into the Java Sea in October 2018, killing all 189 aboard; Ethiopian Airlines Flight 302 crashed in near-identical fashion five months later, killing 157; the worldwide fleet was grounded for roughly twenty months.8 A correctly designed system would have cross-checked a second sensor before acting — would have built in the elementary epistemic humility of not believing a single witness on a matter of life and death.

Redundancy, voting, plausibility checks, sensor fusion, error bars on a measurement — these are not merely engineering techniques. They are applied epistemology: formal admissions that instruments are fallible, that a single observation underdetermines the truth, and that a system's confidence should be calibrated to how it knows, not merely to what it reads.

Assumptions as invisible load-bearing walls

In an old building, the dangerous wall is the one no one labeled. It looks like every other wall until you remove it and the ceiling comes down. Assumptions in engineered systems have exactly this character: they hold enormous weight while remaining, to everyone including their authors, apparently ordinary.

Fig. 2 Every layer of a system rests on an assumption made at the interface below it. Any of those assumptions can be the hidden load-bearing wall; the fracture propagates upward as an unexplained failure.

Consider Ariane 5. On its maiden flight in June 1996, the European rocket veered off course and self-destructed about forty seconds after liftoff, destroying its payload of four satellites — a loss of some 370 million dollars. The cause was a single unprotected conversion of a 64-bit floating-point value to a 16-bit integer, which overflowed because the rocket's horizontal velocity was larger than the software had ever been built to expect.9 That software had been inherited, unchanged, from Ariane 4, whose slower flight profile never produced such a value; the code was performing an alignment calculation that served no purpose at all once the rocket had left the pad. A dead routine, carrying a true-for-Ariane-4 assumption into a world where it was false, took down the live mission — and the backup unit, running the identical code under identical conditions, failed in the identical instant. The board's recommendation was, at bottom, an epistemological correction: stop assuming software is correct until shown faulty, and start assuming it is faulty until shown correct.9

That backup failure is worth pausing on, because it exposes a limit of redundancy that Charles Perrow made central to his theory of catastrophe. In tightly coupled, complex systems, Perrow argued, failures do not stay isolated; they interact and cascade in ways no designer anticipated, and duplicating a component does nothing if both copies share the same false premise.10 Two identical channels executing one flawed assumption are not redundant. They are one point of failure wearing a disguise.

Sometimes the buried assumption is an entire system of measurement. When NASA's Mars Climate Orbiter reached Mars in September 1999, it entered the atmosphere far lower than intended — roughly 57 kilometers up instead of the planned 226 — and was destroyed. The investigation found that one team's ground software had produced thruster impulse figures in pound-force seconds while the navigation software expected newton-seconds, a discrepancy of a factor of 4.45 that silently corrupted the trajectory for the whole nine-month cruise.11 The 327.6-million-dollar mission was lost not to a mathematical error but to a failure of shared ontology: two groups modeling the same physical quantity in incompatible units, each correct within its own world, with no meeting point between them.

Or take the Therac-25, a radiation-therapy machine whose control software carried a race condition that could deliver massive overdoses; between 1985 and 1987 it gravely injured or killed at least six patients.12 The buried assumption there was a design philosophy: that software interlocks could safely replace the hardware safety mechanisms of earlier models. That was a claim about how much trust software had earned — and it was wrong. The Year 2000 problem was the same species of buried claim at civilizational scale: representing a year in two digits was an entirely reasonable economy in an era of costly memory, but it quietly encoded a belief about time — that the year 2000 lay safely beyond the horizon of any system then being written. The belief expired. The wall had to be found and reinforced, at a cost eventually reckoned in the tens of billions of dollars, precisely because no one had ever labeled it.

Every interface is a theory of the user

The philosophy embedded in a design is not only physics and probability. It is also anthropology. Every interface encodes a theory of the human being who will meet it — and, more quietly, a verdict on which humans count.

The humble name field is the classic illustration. A form that splits a name into "first" and "last," each of similar length and drawn from a Latin alphabet, asserts a whole theory of human identity — one that fails a large fraction of the species. Patrick McKenzie's much-cited catalogue of the falsehoods programmers believe about names runs to dozens of confident assumptions that are each false somewhere: that people have exactly one canonical name, that names have a first and last part, that they fit in ASCII, that they do not change, that they are not a single word.13 Mononyms are ordinary across Indonesia; family names come first across much of East Asia; some names are longer than any database designer imagined. The design did not merely make a technical choice. It quietly defined what a person is, and excluded the people who did not fit.

Scale that up and the verdict becomes political. Langdon Winner's essay asking whether artifacts have politics argued that technologies can, as he put it, embody specific forms of power and authority — that the arrangement of a bridge or a machine can settle social questions as firmly as any law.14 His most famous example, drawn from Robert Caro's biography of Robert Moses, was a set of extraordinarily low overpasses on Long Island's parkways, some with as little as nine feet of clearance, which by keeping out the tall public buses effectively kept poor and Black New Yorkers — the people who rode them — away from Jones Beach. Whether Moses's intent was quite so tidy is debated by historians, but Winner's structural claim has proven robust: a design is never only a means to a function. It distributes access, cost, and dignity, and those distributions are choices even when no one chooses them deliberately.

This is why value-sensitive design emerged as a formal discipline — an attempt, in the work of Batya Friedman and colleagues, to drag the values embedded in a system into the open and treat them as first-class design requirements rather than accidents of implementation.15 And it is why Cathy O'Neil's account of algorithmic scoring systems reads as a warning about buried anthropology: a credit or recidivism or hiring model encodes assumptions about what human behavior predicts and which populations it was built to represent, then hardens those assumptions into consequential judgments and feeds the results back into the world, entrenching the very patterns it assumed.16 The theory of the user does not stay a theory. It becomes the user's reality.

The philosophy of the safety margin

The most explicitly philosophical object in all of engineering is the safety factor: the deliberate practice of building a system to withstand far more than it is ever expected to face. A factor of two means a structure is designed to carry twice the load it will legally be asked to bear. Why deliberately overbuild? Because the engineer does not fully trust the model. The margin is institutionalized doubt — a standing admission that the mathematics is a map, that the map omits something, and that the omitted thing has a habit of arriving at the worst possible moment.

Henry Petroski built an entire philosophy of engineering on this insight: failure, he argued, is not the opposite of successful design but its engine, because every new design is really a hypothesis about the limits of the last one, and only failure reveals where those limits actually lie.17 The safety factor is what you carry when you know your hypothesis is incomplete but cannot yet say how.

The de Havilland Comet made the point in metal. The world's first commercial jet airliner, it began breaking apart in flight in 1954, and investigators eventually traced the disasters to metal fatigue — cracks initiating at points of stress concentration near the aircraft's window openings and propagating under the repeated pressurization cycles of jet-altitude flight.18 The failure did not lie in a miscalculation within the engineers' model. It lay in a phenomenon their model did not yet contain: fatigue behavior of that alloy under those cyclic loads was, at the time, a genuine unknown. The margin exists for exactly this — for the phenomena not yet named. And different engineering cultures hold radically different philosophies about how large that margin should be. Aviation's obsession with redundancy and forensic failure analysis is one worldview; the early software industry's slogan of moving fast and breaking things was another — a wager that in its domain, failure was cheap information rather than catastrophe. Neither is simply a preference. Each is a theory of where the costs of being wrong will fall, and on whom.

Tacoma Narrows: the omitted phenomenon

No case shows the stakes of an incomplete model more cleanly than the Tacoma Narrows Bridge, which tore itself apart in a moderate wind in November 1940, four months after it opened. The story survives in physics textbooks as a parable of simple resonance — wind gusting at just the frequency of the bridge's natural sway. That story, K. Yusuf Billah and Robert Scanlan demonstrated, is wrong.19 The collapse was not forced resonance at all but aeroelastic self-excitation: an interaction in which the wind's force was coupled to the bridge's own twisting motion, so that the structure fed energy into its own oscillation — a condition of negative aerodynamic damping in which the movement, once begun, amplified itself without any external rhythm to match. The periodic vortex shedding that the resonance story blames occurred near one cycle per second, nowhere near the roughly one-fifth-of-a-cycle-per-second torsional mode that actually destroyed the bridge; the numbers rule the simple explanation out.

Fig. 3 The two are different phenomena, not different intensities. Forced resonance is bounded by its driving force; self-excitation feeds on the structure's own motion and grows without limit. The designers' model had a variable for the first and none for the second.

Here is the philosophically decisive part. The bridge's designers were not careless, and their model was not miscalculated. It was complete for every force then in their vocabulary — static wind pressure, dead and live loads, the aerodynamics understood by suspension-bridge engineers of the day. The failure lived entirely in a phenomenon for which the model contained no variable at all. This is the most dangerous kind of wrong assumption, because it is not a value set incorrectly but a dimension of reality the model does not know exists. You cannot put a safety factor on a force you have never conceived. The map was not merely too small; it was drawn in the wrong number of dimensions.

How designs meet reality

Put a system into the world and you have, whether you say so or not, advanced a hypothesis: that its assumptions hold. Karl Popper argued that what makes a claim scientific is that it can be falsified — that it forbids something, and so can be broken by observation.20 A deployed system is falsifiable in exactly this sense. Every bridge that stands, every release that survives contact with users, is a hypothesis that has not yet been refuted. Every failure is a refutation.

But a refutation of what? Here engineering runs headlong into a classical problem in the philosophy of science. Pierre Duhem and W.V.O. Quine observed that hypotheses are never tested one at a time: any prediction depends on a whole web of assumptions, so when the prediction fails, logic alone cannot tell you which strand of the web is the false one.21 A crashed system embeds hundreds of premises — about hardware, inputs, timing, users, the correctness of every layer beneath the one you wrote. The failure indicts all of them jointly and none of them individually. This is precisely why post-mortems are hard and why blame is so slippery: the world tells you that something in your web of assumptions was wrong, and leaves the far harder question — which one — to you.

And engineering has paradigms in Thomas Kuhn's sense: shared models, standard tolerances, canonical designs, and rules of thumb that a whole profession takes for granted and that work reliably — until anomalies accumulate.22 Tacoma Narrows was such an anomaly; so, in their fields, were the Comet and the financial models of 2008. Each forced a discipline to revise assumptions it had not known it was making. Engineering knowledge, like scientific knowledge, does not only accumulate smoothly. It also lurches, in the aftermath of the failures that expose the limits of a reigning paradigm.

When the philosophy is catastrophically wrong

The largest failures come when a model is deployed as though its domain of validity were unlimited. Every model is accurate only within some envelope of conditions — the region where its simplifying assumptions approximately hold. Push the operating point outside that envelope and the model does not merely lose accuracy; it can lose all contact with reality, because the very relationships it encodes no longer obtain.

Fig. 4 A model tracks reality inside the conditions it was validated against and can diverge without warning outside them. Catastrophe is rarely bad arithmetic; it is a good model used where its assumptions have quietly stopped holding.

The financial crisis of 2008 is the textbook instance. A formula built on the Gaussian copula gave the market a tractable way to price the risk of correlated defaults — and it rested on an assumption that the correlations between regional housing markets were stable and modest, so that a collapse in one place would not drag down the others.23 That assumption held comfortably within the historical envelope the model had been fit to. It did not hold when national housing prices fell together, and at that moment the model had no vocabulary for what was happening; the quantity it treated as a stable parameter was the very thing coming apart. Donald MacKenzie's post-crisis analysis adds a deeper and stranger turn: the model was not a passive measurement of the market but an active ingredient in it — embedded in the practices, prices, and instruments it purported to describe, so that its assumptions became self-referential and shaped the reality they were supposed to track.24 The map had been mistaken for the territory so thoroughly that it began to reshape the territory in its own image — right up to the point where the territory refused.

None of the engineers or quants in these stories was foolish. Each built on a philosophy — an implicit theory of what reality was like — that turned out to be false in exactly the way that mattered, and that had never been written down where anyone could challenge it.

The discipline this demands

To say engineering is philosophy is not to make it vaguer or more speculative. It is the opposite: it is to demand that the philosophy be made explicit, examined, and argued, rather than left to compile silently into the artifact. Walter Vincenti's history of aeronautical engineering makes the case that engineering knowledge is a distinct and rigorous thing, generated by the field's own methods — and that a great deal of it is, specifically, hard-won knowledge of where models break and how much to distrust them.25 Maturity in the discipline looks like knowing your assumptions well enough to state their limits.

The best engineering cultures have built rituals for exactly this. Gary Klein's pre-mortem asks a team to imagine that the project has already failed and to work backward to explain why — a technique whose whole purpose is to surface the assumptions that optimism keeps invisible.26 Failure mode and effects analysis does the same thing more formally, forcing each assumption's failure to be assigned a probability and a severity. Architectural decision records make the reasoning legible, documenting not just what was decided but why, and which alternatives were rejected and on what grounds. Red-teaming and adversarial testing try to falsify a design before reality gets the chance. Value-sensitive design brings the buried theory of the user, and the values riding on it, into the room as explicit requirements.15 Every one of these practices is a method for turning an implicit philosophical claim into an explicit one that can be inspected and, if necessary, refuted in advance.

The shift is in the question the engineer asks. Not merely does it work? — which invites the answer yes, so far, under the conditions I happened to try — but under what conditions does it work, what am I assuming to be true, and what happens at the edges of those assumptions? That second question is a philosophical one. Asking it does not make an engineer less practical. It makes them the kind of engineer whose bridge is still standing when the wind does something the textbook never mentioned.

All engineering is applied philosophy. A system is a theory of its slice of reality, cast in a form that reality can test to destruction. The only choice an engineer really has is whether that philosophy is examined or not — whether the assumptions are dragged into daylight and interrogated, or left buried in the walls to be discovered, at ruinous cost, on the day the world declines to behave as assumed. The examined system is the one that can tell you where it will break. It is also, not coincidentally, the one that keeps standing when it does.
