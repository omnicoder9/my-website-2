An AI agent is only as capable as the systems it can reach. Strip away the demos and the model benchmarks, and an enterprise agent is a piece of software that has to log into your CRM, read your HRIS, write to your payroll system, and do it safely, in real time, on someone’s behalf. That is an integration problem before it is an intelligence problem — and it is where most deployments quietly fail.

CH 01The bottleneck

The Hard Part Was Never the Model

A large enterprise today runs an average of 291 software-as-a-service applications, up from 254 in 2023 and roughly 110 in 2020; the largest organizations operate more than 470.1 Each one holds a slice of the data and a fragment of the workflow that an agent would need to actually finish a job. None of them was designed with an autonomous software actor in mind. This is the terrain any agent strategy has to cross, and it explains a gap that has become the defining feature of the agentic era: the distance between what agents can demonstrate and what they can durably do inside a company.

That gap is now well documented. McKinsey calls it the “gen AI paradox”: nearly eight in ten companies have deployed generative AI in some form, yet roughly the same proportion report no material impact on earnings.2 The reason is structural. Horizontal tools — enterprise copilots and chatbots — scaled quickly because they sit lightly on top of existing work and ask little of the surrounding systems. The higher-value vertical use cases, the ones that re-plumb a specific function like claims or collections or onboarding, mostly stall; by McKinsey’s count, around nine in ten of them never leave the pilot.2 The thing that separates the two is not model quality. It is depth of integration.

The split between the two has a clean explanation. Horizontal copilots succeed because they ask almost nothing of the systems around them: they draft, summarize, and answer inside a chat window, and a human carries the output the last mile into whatever application it belongs in. The vertical use cases that actually move a number — resolving a claim end to end, collecting a debt, onboarding an employee — require the software to reach into systems of record and complete the transaction without a human courier. That is precisely the work that does not generalize from a demo, and it is why the firms making progress are the ones that stopped treating integration as a downstream implementation detail and started treating it as the strategy itself.2

The numbers around agents specifically are starker. Nearly two-thirds of enterprises have experimented with agents, but fewer than one in ten have scaled them to deliver tangible value, and eight in ten companies name data limitations as the roadblock to doing so.3 A separate global survey found that while 88% of organizations now use AI in at least one function and 62% are at least experimenting with agents, only 39% report any enterprise-level impact on operating profit.4 Adoption is nearly universal; consequence is rare.

Analysts have priced in the fallout. Gartner projects that more than 40% of agentic AI projects will be canceled by the end of 2027, attributing the wave of cancellations not to the agents underperforming but to escalating costs, unclear business value, and inadequate risk controls — the predictable symptoms of projects that underestimated what it takes to wire an agent into production.5 Integrating agents into legacy systems, the firm notes, is technically complex, often disrupts established workflows, and frequently demands costly modifications before any value appears.5

To see why integration is the binding constraint and not a footnote, it helps to be precise about what changed when the industry moved from copilots to agents. A copilot generates an output for a human to review and act on: a draft email, a summarized ticket, a suggested query. An agent closes that loop. It takes the action itself — issues the refund, updates the record, provisions the access, schedules the interview — which means it must hold real, authenticated, bidirectional connections to the systems where those actions land.6 The copilot can be wrong and waste a minute of someone’s time. The agent can be wrong and move money. The moment you ask software to do rather than suggest, the connection to your systems of record stops being plumbing and becomes the product.

The strategic question is no longer whether agents are capable. It is whether your systems are reachable, your data is legible, and your permissions are governable. Everything else is downstream of that.

// The integration thesis

This brief takes that thesis seriously and follows it through. It examines why agents break precisely where software has always been hardest to connect; the three concrete problems — authentication, data schema, and permission structure — that any integration strategy has to solve; the emerging protocol layer that is starting to standardize the connection; the toolkit of unified APIs, integration platforms, and orchestration frameworks now available; and the deployments, both triumphant and cautionary, that show what each choice costs. It ends with the architectural shift that separates the companies capturing value from the ones generating press releases: treating agents as first-class citizens of the enterprise stack rather than bolt-ons to it.

CH 02Where it breaks

Agents Break Where Software Connects

Connecting one piece of software to another has been the unglamorous, expensive heart of enterprise IT for decades. Agents inherit every part of that difficulty and add a multiplier. Before any shared standard existed, linking an AI system to a data source or a tool required a bespoke integration for each pairing — what Anthropic described as a fragmented landscape in which every new data source needs its own custom implementation, making genuinely connected systems hard to scale.7 Engineers gave the shape of the problem a name: the N×M problem. With M agents and N systems, the naive path is to build and maintain a connector for every combination, and the connector count grows as a product rather than a sum.8

The figure makes the trap visible. Four agents and four systems is sixteen integrations to build, secure, version, and monitor; route every side through a shared bus instead and the same coverage costs eight. Real enterprises do not have four of anything — they have hundreds of systems and a growing fleet of agents — so the difference between a product and a sum is the difference between a maintainable program and a permanent tax. This is the first reason integration, not intelligence, governs the outcome: the work does not scale linearly with ambition unless the architecture is chosen deliberately.

The second reason is that the systems on the other end of those connectors are old, opinionated, and load-bearing. For large organizations built over decades, the idea of ripping out the technology foundations is a fantasy: the data lives in mainframes, the business logic is written in legacy code, and the processes have been tuned through years of iteration.9 The pragmatic path, as McKinsey frames it, mirrors the way microservices let teams modernize without dismantling the core: the first wave of agents sits atop existing systems as a layer of augmentation rather than replacement.9 An insurer can stand up an underwriting agent that queries a legacy risk engine through an API and translates its output into plain-language explanations for underwriters and regulators — new value extracted from an old asset, with the old asset left intact.9 That is the realistic ambition. But “query through an API” hides an enormous amount of labor, because most of those systems were never built to be queried this way.

The cost is not only the initial build; it is the carry. Every bespoke connector is a living dependency that has to be re-tested when either side changes a field, re-authenticated when a token policy shifts, and re-monitored when an API is deprecated, so a portfolio of point-to-point integrations behaves like deferred maintenance that compounds quietly until a routine vendor update breaks a workflow no one remembered owning.8 This is the hidden arithmetic behind the cancellation forecasts: projects priced against the cost of the first integration rather than the cost of operating dozens of them through years of change, then surprised when the bill arrives as escalating cost and disruption instead of a single line item.5

The third reason is the nature of agentic access itself. The brief for an enterprise agent is rarely “read this one field.” It is secure, real-time, bidirectional access to systems of record — an agent that can read an employee’s status from the HRIS, cross-reference an offer in the ATS, and write an update back to payroll, all within a single task and all while respecting who is allowed to do what. Each of those verbs carries hidden cost. Real-time means the agent cannot rely on a nightly export; it needs live reads and event-driven updates, which forces a choice between brittle polling and webhook plumbing. Bidirectional means write-backs, which raise consistency and idempotency questions that read-only analytics never had to answer — what happens when the agent retries, or when two agents act on the same record. And every one of those reads and writes has to clear an authorization check, a schema translation, and a rate limit. The agent’s reasoning may take milliseconds; the integration around it is where the engineering lives.

Put plainly: an agent program is an integration program wearing a more exciting name. The companies that internalize this early stop asking “which model” and start asking “through what fabric, under whose identity, against what schema, with which permissions.” Those four questions are the real surface area of the work, and the next section takes the three hardest of them in turn.

CH 03Three hard problems

Authentication, Schema, and Permission

Strip an integration strategy down to its load-bearing walls and three problems remain. An agent has to prove who it is and on whose behalf it acts (authentication and identity). It has to understand the data it touches across systems that describe the same things in incompatible ways (schema and semantics). And it has to be constrained to exactly what its task requires, no more, with every action traceable (permission structure). These are not new problems in enterprise computing. What is new is that agents make all three sharper at once, because an agent is autonomous, fast, and connected to many systems simultaneously — so a weakness in any one of the three is amplified rather than contained.

03.1Authentication and identity

The first problem is the one most enterprises are least prepared for, because it inverts a thirty-year assumption. Identity and access management was built to govern human users: a person logs in, a directory knows who they are, and their permissions are relatively static. Agents do not fit that model. They are non-human identities — ephemeral, autonomous, and frequently acting on a human’s behalf while switching, mid-task, to acting on their own.10 And there are now vastly more of them than there are people. Across enterprise environments, machine and agent identities already outnumber human identities by somewhere between 45-to-1 and 100-to-1, and roughly 80% of IT leaders report agents behaving outside their expected boundaries.10

This population is not just large; it is structurally misconfigured. The OWASP Non-Human Identities Top 10, published in 2025, catalogues the failure modes, and the supporting data is sobering: some 24 million non-human identity credentials were discovered leaked on public repositories in a single year, and around 70% of those dating from 2022 were still valid.11 Surveys of deployed environments find that the overwhelming majority of these identities carry far more privilege than they use — on the order of 97% over-privileged by one measure — and that roughly 90% of deployed agents are over-permissioned relative to the scope of their assigned tasks.12 Each over-scoped agent is a master key left in a lock.

The protocols meant to help were not designed for this. OAuth, the workhorse of delegated access, assumes relatively static permissions granted to a human user or a well-defined application; its token model does not adapt gracefully to an actor whose permissions may need to change with risk level or task in real time.13 SAML, with its XML assertions and session-based authentication, is even less suited to actors that may need continuous re-authentication and dynamic privilege adjustment.13 The hardest case is the dual-mode problem: an agent that begins by acting for a specific user, then switches to a non-human identity to perform a database query or call an API, and no current system reliably tracks which mode was active for a given action.12 Standards bodies have begun to respond — the OpenID Foundation published guidance on identity management for agentic AI in October 2025, concluding that OAuth and OIDC can secure many agent use cases within well-defined boundaries but that real gaps remain — and infrastructure like SPIFFE and SPIRE is being adapted to give each agent a cryptographic workload identity and certificate so that mutual-TLS authentication can replace shared secrets.12 The work is early. Microsoft’s Copilot Studio users alone have collectively created more than a million agents, and the identity infrastructure to govern a population that size does not yet exist at most organizations.14

03.2Data schema and semantics

The second problem is quieter and, at scale, just as fatal. An agent that can authenticate into ten systems still has to make sense of what it finds there, and those systems describe the same realities in mutually unintelligible ways. One platform’s “employee” is another’s “worker” is another’s “resource”; a termination date, a cost center, an employment status, a customer record — each is modeled differently, named differently, and validated differently across the HRIS, the payroll system, and the ERP. This is why data limitations, not model limitations, are the most-cited barrier to scaling agents.3

The remedy is not merely cleaner data; it is shared meaning. McKinsey’s data-architecture guidance for the agentic era is explicit on the point: organizations should share meaning, not just data, ensuring that information arrives with clear, common definitions so that analytics, models, and agents all interpret it the same way.3 In practice this pushes toward treating data ingestion like a product — making it easy and consistent for all data, batch or real-time, structured or unstructured, to enter the company once and be usable everywhere — and toward domain models and ontologies that give agents a stable semantic layer to reason over rather than a tangle of raw, conflicting schemas.3 Because agentic systems coordinate multiple models and sources continuously, often without a human in the loop, they place far more operational pressure on these foundations than a dashboard ever did.3 An agent that misreads a schema does not produce a wrong chart a human can catch; it takes a wrong action no one sees until later.

The concrete shape of the problem is mundane and unforgiving. Ask an agent to confirm that a newly hired employee is ready to be paid, and it has to reconcile an “employee” record in the HRIS, a “worker” in the time system, a “payee” in payroll, and a cost-center code in the ERP — four representations of one person, each with its own identifiers, status enumerations, and validation rules. A human resolves the mismatch with tacit knowledge; an agent has only the schemas it is given, and if “active” means one thing in the HRIS and another in payroll, the agent will either stall or act on a false equivalence. Multiply that across every entity an enterprise tracks and the semantic gap, not the model, becomes the ceiling on what an agent can safely do.3

The discipline that addresses this treats meaning as infrastructure. Rather than cleaning data reactively per project, the durable approach builds a semantic layer — domain models and ontologies that define, once and centrally, what an employee or an order or an account is — and exposes data through that layer as a product with clear ownership, documentation, and quality guarantees.3 The payoff is leverage: every new agent inherits a consistent vocabulary instead of re-deriving it, and the organization stops paying the translation cost once per integration. It is the same logic that let large enterprises modernize through service layers rather than rewrites — expose a stable interface over a messy interior, and let new capability build against the interface.9 The semantic layer is that interface for meaning, and an agent program without one is a translation project that never finishes.

03.3Permission structure

The third problem braids the first two together and adds the question of restraint. Once an agent can authenticate and can understand the data, the governing concern becomes what it is allowed to do, under what conditions, and whether anyone can reconstruct why it did what it did. The instinct carried over from service accounts — provision broad, long-lived credentials and forget about them — is precisely the pattern that produced the 90%-over-permissioned figure, and it is exactly wrong for autonomous actors.12

The discipline that works treats agents as first-class identities with permissions scoped to the task and granted just in time rather than standing indefinitely, enforced at runtime rather than assumed at provisioning, and accompanied by full action traceability so that every step an agent takes can be tied back to a triggering request and a responsible owner.10 When an agent crosses from one platform to another, its identity and policy should travel with it rather than forcing a re-authentication that tempts engineers into over-broad grants.10 The accountability requirement is not bureaucratic overhead; without detailed logs, delegation graphs, and policy context, an organization cannot answer the three questions a regulator or an incident responder will ask first — who asked the agent to act, was the action within scope, and what exactly happened.10 Section seven shows, in painful detail, what it costs when those questions cannot be answered.

Operationally, this is where policy-as-code earns its place. Instead of permissions living as static entitlements granted at provisioning and forgotten, the agent’s authority is expressed as machine-readable policy evaluated at the moment of action: can this agent, acting for this principal, take this action on this resource, right now, given current risk signals.12 Pairing that runtime check with a complete audit and delegation graph — a record of who triggered the agent, what it was permitted to do, and what it actually did — turns governance from a documentation exercise into a control, and it is the only way to answer an incident responder’s questions after the fact rather than guessing.10 The three problems converge here: identity establishes who, schema establishes what, and permission establishes whether — and an agent that cannot satisfy all three at runtime is not production software, however capable its model.

CH 04The protocol layer

A Common Language for Connection

The most consequential development in enterprise agent strategy over the past two years has not been a model release. It has been the emergence of open protocols that turn the N×M problem from a per-company engineering burden into shared infrastructure. Two matter most, and they solve different halves of the connection.

04.1MCP: agents to tools and data

Anthropic introduced the Model Context Protocol in November 2024 as an open standard for connecting AI systems to the places data lives — content repositories, business tools, development environments — explicitly to replace fragmented, one-off integrations with a single protocol.7 The analogy that stuck is “USB-C for AI”: implement the protocol once in an agent, and it can speak to any system that exposes an MCP server, the way one port replaced a drawer full of proprietary cables.8 Architecturally it borrows from the Language Server Protocol — a host, clients, and servers exchanging capabilities over JSON-RPC — so that a server advertises its tools in natural language and the model decides which to call.8

Adoption was unusually fast for an infrastructure standard. By Anthropic’s own account the community built thousands of MCP servers within the first year, SDKs shipped for every major language, and developers now routinely build agents with access to hundreds or thousands of tools across dozens of servers.15 Independent trackers put SDK downloads in the tens of millions per month and the server ecosystem in the thousands, with production deployments at organizations including Block, Bloomberg, and Amazon and across hundreds of Fortune 500 companies.16 Crucially, the standard did not stay proprietary: OpenAI adopted MCP across its products in March 2025 and Google followed, and in December 2025 Anthropic donated the protocol to a newly formed Agentic AI Foundation under the Linux Foundation, co-founded with Block and OpenAI — an unusually clear signal that competitors had agreed to treat the connection layer as a public good.17

Under the hood, the protocol is deliberately small. An MCP server exposes a handful of primitive types — tools the model can invoke, resources it can read, and prompts it can reuse — and the host application mediates between the model and one or more servers, so that adding a capability means standing up a server rather than rewriting the agent.8 That minimalism is what let the ecosystem compound: once the interface was fixed, the work of integration became reusable, and a single well-built server for a system like a CRM or a ticketing tool could serve every agent that spoke the protocol.15 More recent practice has agents load tool definitions on demand and even execute code against those tools rather than calling them one message at a time — a refinement that keeps context windows manageable as the number of reachable tools climbs into the thousands.15

04.2A2A: agents to agents

Where MCP connects an agent down to tools, Google’s Agent2Agent protocol connects agents across to one another. Released on April 9, 2025 with more than fifty founding partners, A2A lets independently built agents discover each other’s capabilities, authenticate, and delegate tasks — over HTTP and JSON-RPC, using a structured “Agent Card” for discovery — and it was positioned from the start as complementary to MCP rather than competitive.18 The division of labor is clean: MCP is vertical integration, an agent reaching down to data and tools; A2A is horizontal integration, agents collaborating at the same level.18

The mechanics are built for a world of agents from different vendors that do not trust each other by default. An agent publishes a structured Agent Card describing its skills, endpoints, and authentication requirements; a client agent discovers that card, negotiates a secure channel, and delegates a task as a first-class object with its own lifecycle and status updates, all over ordinary web transport so that existing enterprise authentication and network controls still apply.18 The effect is to make agent-to-agent delegation look, to the surrounding infrastructure, like the kind of authenticated service-to-service call enterprises already know how to govern.18

A2A followed the same path to neutral governance, and faster. On June 23, 2025 the Linux Foundation announced the Agent2Agent project, seeded with Google’s transfer of the specification, SDKs, and tooling, expressly to address the challenge of scaling agents across enterprise environments.19 The founding roster read like a truce: Amazon Web Services, Cisco, Google, Microsoft, Salesforce, SAP, and ServiceNow, with more than a hundred companies supporting the protocol.20 IBM’s competing agent communication protocol was subsequently folded in, and supporter counts passed 150 organizations within months.18 For an enterprise, the practical meaning is that an agent built on one framework can increasingly interoperate with an agent built on another without a custom bridge — the multi-agent equivalent of the N×M collapse that MCP delivered for tools.

What the protocols do and do not solve

MCP and A2A standardize the connection — how an agent finds a tool, calls it, and exchanges data, or how two agents hand off a task. They do not, by themselves, solve identity, authorization, or governance. Security researchers flagged real risks early, including prompt injection and “poisoned” tools that can exfiltrate data through other connected tools, and the protocol’s own specification places the burden of consent flows, access controls, and tool-trust decisions on implementers.8

A standard plug does not make the wiring behind the wall safe. The protocol layer is necessary and was missing; it is not sufficient, and the three hard problems of the previous section do not disappear because a connection became easier to make.

CH 05The toolkit

What You Can Actually Buy and Build With

Below the protocols sits a maturing market of tools, and choosing among them is itself a strategic decision. The categories are distinct, they solve different layers of the problem, and conflating them is a common and expensive mistake. Four layers matter for an integration strategy.

05.1Unified APIs and integration platforms

The most direct answer to the schema problem for back-office systems is the unified API: a single, normalized interface that abstracts away the differences in authentication, pagination, and data models across dozens of providers in a category. For the systems an enterprise agent most often needs — HRIS, ATS, CRM, payroll, accounting — platforms such as Merge, Finch, Kombo, Knit, Apideck, Unified.to, and Nango let a team integrate once and reach a whole category, collapsing what was months of per-provider work and, by some estimates, cutting integration maintenance by up to 80%.21 Merge markets a single API spanning HR, payroll, accounting, ticketing, CRM, ATS, and file storage, with engineers reporting that building to it took roughly the same time as a single custom integration while unlocking dozens.22

What makes this category newly relevant to agents specifically is that the unified providers have begun exposing MCP servers over their connectors. Instead of writing traditional API integrations, an agent like Claude, Cursor, or Copilot can execute tasks against HRIS, ATS, payroll, and CRM data through standardized tool calls and natural-language commands — a meaningful shift in how HR and back-office integrations get built.23 Merge has gone further with an “Agent Handler” aimed at letting enterprises govern how employees connect Claude, ChatGPT, and other tools to third-party systems, and at letting agents take authenticated actions across connectors.22 Architecture matters here for security: some providers store a synced copy of customer data while others pass requests through without retaining payloads, and the pass-through model minimizes data-at-rest risk and audit scope — a distinction worth interrogating before an agent is pointed at employee records.21

For agents specifically, the security posture of a unified layer is not a detail to be settled later. An agent pointed at employee or customer records inherits whatever data-handling model its integration provider uses, so the difference between a provider that retains a synced copy of that data and one that passes requests through without storing payloads becomes the difference in how much sensitive information sits at rest, how large the audit surface is, and what a breach of the provider would expose.21 The shift toward exposing these connectors as protocol-native servers makes the question sharper still, because it puts an autonomous actor, rather than a reviewed line of application code, on the calling end of those credentials.23

Unified APIs should not be confused with integration platforms as a service. Tools like Zapier, Make, Workato, MuleSoft, and Boomi are workflow-automation and middleware platforms that connect applications through triggers and actions; they are infrastructure for orchestrating processes, where unified APIs are a normalized data layer a product consumes.21 Many enterprise agent deployments use both: a unified API for clean reads and writes, and an iPaaS layer for the multi-step business logic and monitoring around them.

05.2Orchestration frameworks

Above connectivity sits orchestration: the frameworks that define how an agent plans, holds state, calls tools, and hands off to other agents. The field consolidated through 2025 and 2026 around a handful of production-grade options — LangGraph for graph-based, auditable state machines with durable execution and human-in-the-loop checkpoints; CrewAI for fast role-based multi-agent prototypes; the Microsoft Agent Framework, which unified AutoGen and Semantic Kernel and reached general availability in 2026; OpenAI’s Agents SDK for handoff-style flows; Google’s ADK with native A2A support; and Anthropic’s Claude Agent SDK.24 Production footprints diverge sharply: LangGraph powers agents in production at companies including LinkedIn and Uber and hundreds of others, while CrewAI reports use across a majority of the Fortune 500.25

The framework choice matters less than vendors imply for capability and more than teams expect for cost. Every major framework now supports MCP and A2A natively or through adapters, which collapses the cost of swapping integrations between them.26 But independent evaluation finds that the orchestration scaffold can move an agent’s benchmark performance by up to 30 percentage points on an identical model, and that cost can vary as much as fiftyfold across agents achieving similar accuracy — with a roughly 37% average gap between lab benchmark scores and production behavior.26 The lesson is to choose a frontier model and a framework that fits the team’s operational maturity, then instrument cost and reliability as first-class metrics rather than chasing leaderboard accuracy.

The deeper differences between frameworks show up in operations, not features. Graph-based orchestrators model an agent as an explicit state machine with durable execution and checkpoints, so a long-running workflow can pause for human approval, survive a process restart, and be inspected step by step when it misbehaves — properties that matter enormously once an agent is touching production systems rather than answering questions.24 Lighter role-based frameworks trade some of that control for speed of assembly, ideal for prototypes and risky for regulated workflows. Because the gap between a framework’s benchmark behavior and its production behavior runs to roughly a third, and because cost at fixed accuracy varies by orders of magnitude, the mature posture is to treat evaluation, cost tracking, and observability as part of the framework decision rather than an afterthought bolted on once something is already live.26

05.3Application platforms, infrastructure, and identity

For many enterprises the entry point is not a framework but an application-layer platform that ships with integration and governance built in: Salesforce Agentforce, ServiceNow’s AI agents, Microsoft Copilot Studio, and SAP Business AI, with the cloud providers supplying the orchestration substrate through AWS Bedrock Agents, Google’s Agent Builder, and Azure AI Foundry.6 Running beneath all of it is the identity layer the previous section argued is most often missing — Okta and Microsoft Entra for workforce identity, and a wave of agent-specific entrants such as Strata, WorkOS, and hosted-MCP providers building the just-in-time, scoped, traceable access agents require.10

05.4The build-versus-buy decision

The toolkit makes a strategic question unavoidable: what to build and what to buy. McKinsey’s analysis of the agent-and-ERP divide argues that in a field evolving this fast, both extremes are high-risk — waiting for a perfect off-the-shelf solution and trying to custom-build everything — because AI changes too quickly for multiyear build cycles and re-creating foundational layers drains resources without creating differentiation.27 The resilient posture is to buy standardized capabilities such as ERP-integrated orchestration frameworks and predefined data products, and to reserve custom development for the narrow areas where domain-specific logic or proprietary workflows create real competitive advantage.27 And unlike the traditional SaaS world, the decision is not one-and-done: it demands continuous reassessment of what to create and what to consume.27 The same analysis issues a warning that bears directly on integration strategy — treating ERP and other systems of record as an unwieldy afterthought to the agent conversation dangerously undervalues them, because those systems hold the data and house the workflows the agent must actually touch.27

CH 06Case study

Klarna: The Integration Was the Product

No deployment has been cited more often, or understood less carefully, than Klarna’s. The headline numbers are genuinely striking. Launched globally in February 2024 and built on OpenAI’s models, the fintech’s assistant handled 2.3 million conversations in its first month — two-thirds of all customer-service chats — doing work the company equated to roughly 700 full-time agents. It matched human agents on customer-satisfaction scores, drove a 25% drop in repeat inquiries, cut average resolution time from eleven minutes to under two, operated across 23 markets in more than 35 languages, and was projected to add about $40 million in profit in 2024.28

The detail that the “replaced 700 agents” framing buries is the one that matters for an integration strategy. Klarna’s assistant was not a generic language model answering questions from a help page. It was embedded in the app and web flow with authenticated context about the customer’s purchases, payment status, and history before the first message — an agent with structured access to user state, not a chatbot bolted onto the side.29 Its outputs were deliberately constrained to a whitelist, retrieving information only from the help center and the customer’s own account data, which is what let it weave order-specific facts — the item, the merchant, the purchase date, the reason a transaction was declined — into otherwise generic answers while avoiding the hallucinations that sink less grounded systems.29 The performance was a function of the integration. The model was necessary; the authenticated, scoped connection to systems of record was what made it useful.

The system isn’t a generic model responding to questions. It is an agent with structured access to user state. That architectural detail is the part most retellings lose.

// On reading the Klarna deployment correctly

The second chapter is the one worth studying. Through 2025 the metrics held and grew — by the third quarter the company reported the assistant doing the work of around 850 agents, roughly $60 million in annual savings, response times 82% faster than before, and a customer NPS of 73 — even as Klarna publicly rebalanced.30 Chief executive Sebastian Siemiatkowski acknowledged that cost had been too predominant a factor in how support was organized, that the result was lower quality on the cases that needed a human, and that the firm was reopening hiring and moving to a model in which a customer can always reach a person.31 The chatbot still handled about two-thirds of inquiries; what changed was the recognition that the boundary between automated and human work is a design decision, not a static line.31

Two operating lessons generalize directly to enterprise agent integration. First, classify by risk band and tune for the quality of the automated subset rather than the size of it: a 50% automation rate at 98% accuracy beats a 75% rate at 90% accuracy in any regulated context, and the agent should refuse when confidence is low rather than guess.30 Second, the handoff is part of the integration. When an agent escalates, it must pass the classified intent, the retrieved context, the attempted response, and a confidence score to the human, because weak handoffs that force customers to re-explain themselves were exactly what eroded satisfaction on escalated cases.30 Klarna’s arc is not a cautionary tale about AI. It is an existence proof that deep, authenticated integration can absorb the high-volume tier of a workflow — paired with a real operating manual for where the human-in-the-loop floor sits.29

CH 07Case study

The Drift Breach: One Connector, 700 Companies

If Klarna shows what deep integration can earn, the 2025 Salesloft–Drift incident shows what careless integration can cost — and it is the single clearest illustration of why the identity and permission problems of section three are not theoretical. Between August 8 and 18, 2025, an intrusion cluster tracked as UNC6395 used stolen OAuth tokens from Salesloft’s Drift AI chat agent to reach the Salesforce environments of more than 700 organizations.32 The agent in question was a perfectly ordinary, widely deployed sales chatbot integrated with customers’ CRM systems. That was precisely the problem.

The mechanics are a textbook case of the master-key failure. The attackers first compromised Salesloft’s GitHub environment between March and June 2025, then stole the OAuth and refresh tokens that Drift used to connect into customer systems, and finally used those tokens to systematically query and export data across hundreds of organizations over roughly ten days.32 Because the tokens represented a trusted, pre-approved integration, the activity bypassed multi-factor authentication entirely and slipped past traditional monitoring, which was not watching a connection it had already blessed.33 The exposure was confined to data stored in Salesforce — contacts, account information, and support-case text — but that case text was not harmless: it contained embedded secrets such as cloud access keys and data-warehouse tokens, turning one breach into the seed of the next.33 The roster of confirmed victims included Cloudflare, Google, Palo Alto Networks, Zscaler, Proofpoint, and Tenable — companies whose own security maturity was not in question.33

What makes the incident a permanent teaching case is how ordinary the failure was at every step. No exotic exploit was required; the attackers used valid tokens through sanctioned channels, which is why the activity did not trip the controls built to catch intruders, and the breach surfaced through its downstream effects rather than at the point of access.33 The chain that led there — a source-code compromise, then stolen integration tokens, then ten days of quiet export — is a generic supply-chain pattern, not a vendor-specific one, which is why every enterprise wiring an agent into multiple systems should read it as a description of their own exposure.32

The structural lesson is in the blast radius. Researchers who tracked the campaign found that exploiting SaaS-to-SaaS integrations affected roughly ten times more organizations than a comparable credential-based breach that hit a single company directly — because the compromised integration was a shared dependency rather than an isolated target.34 Regulators drew the obvious moral: OAuth tokens grant broad and persistent access across cloud applications, and the incident underscored the necessity of monitoring SaaS integrations, enforcing least-privilege access, and not assuming a trusted connection is a safe one.35

Why this is an agent problem, not just a SaaS problem

An enterprise AI agent is, by design, exactly the kind of actor Drift was: a trusted integration holding standing credentials to multiple systems at once. The 90%-over-permissioned and long-lived-token patterns from section three are not abstract risks — they are the precise conditions that turned one compromised agent into a 700-organization incident.

Every argument for scoped, just-in-time, traceable, revocable agent credentials is, in the end, an argument for not building the next Drift. Treating an agent as a first-class identity is not compliance theater; it is the difference between a contained failure and a cascading one.

CH 08The value, measured

The Return on Integration

Cautionary tales should not obscure that well-integrated agents are delivering real, measurable value — provided the numbers are read with discipline. Across verified enterprise deployments, the wins cluster in functions with clean baselines and deep system access. Wiley, the publisher, replaced a basic chatbot with Agentforce-powered service agents and reported case resolution improving by more than 40% within the first weeks, seasonal agents onboarding 50% faster, and a 213% return with roughly $230,000 in documented savings.36 At platform scale, Agentforce passed $540 million in annual recurring revenue across 18,500 customers, its launch event saw attendees create more than 10,000 agents in three days, and Salesforce’s own internal help portal now handles over a million conversations a year at a 75% resolution rate, with adopters spanning Indeed, Finnair, Heathrow Airport, Saks, and SharkNinja.36

The pattern repeats elsewhere. ServiceNow’s internal “Now-on-Now” deployments report deflection as high as 54% on a common “report an issue” form, self-service gains of 14% for employees and 10% for customers, twelve to seventeen minutes of agent time saved per case, and around $5.5 million in annualized savings from case avoidance.37 JPMorgan runs more than 450 active AI agent use cases against an $18 billion technology budget, with systems that draft investment-banking presentations in seconds rather than the hours junior analysts once spent.38 And Uber’s internal engineering assistant, Genie, improved its share of acceptable answers by a relative 27% and cut incorrect advice by a relative 60% by moving from a traditional retrieval pipeline to an agentic approach that expands the query, retrieves across multiple sources, and re-queries until the answer clears a confidence threshold.39

It is worth holding these wins against the macro picture, because the two are easy to confuse. The same surveys that count near-universal adoption find that only a minority of organizations report enterprise-level financial impact, and the firms that do are disproportionately the ones that redesigned workflows around agents rather than dropping agents onto existing ones.4 The lesson is not that the headline cases are fabricated; it is that they are not yet typical, and what makes them atypical is exactly the depth of integration and process change that the median deployment skips.2

What unites these is not the model; it is the depth of integration. Klarna’s authenticated context, JPMorgan’s access across hundreds of internal systems, Uber’s multi-source retrieval, ServiceNow’s native presence in the workflow — in each case the agent earns its keep because it reaches into the systems where the work actually happens. The strongest single signal for whether an agent will deliver, as one survey of deployments puts it, is whether it integrates with the systems already running the operation or whether it first requires moving the data out.39

Read ROI claims like a skeptic

Many of the most-quoted figures — a 396% return on one customer-service platform, triple-digit returns on a copilot — come from vendor-commissioned studies modeled on composite organizations, and they belong in the input-range column of a business case, not the default column.40 The defensible anchors are independent: a peer-reviewed controlled trial found developers completed a coding task 55% faster with an AI assistant, which is a measured benchmark rather than a projection.40

And the macro picture stays humbling. The same body of survey work that records 88% adoption finds only single-digit percentages of organizations reporting genuine enterprise-wide financial impact — a reminder that scattered wins are not the same as a transformed P&L, and that the gap between them is the integration work this brief is about.40

CH 09First-class citizens

Agents Belong in the Architecture, Not Beside It

The deepest shift an enterprise agent strategy demands is conceptual. The companies stuck in pilot purgatory tend to treat each agent as a discrete project bolted onto the existing stack — a clever integration here, a copilot there — while the companies capturing value treat agents as first-class citizens of the architecture itself, with identity, lifecycle, governance, and a defined place in the system diagram. McKinsey’s name for the target state is the agentic mesh: a composable, distributed, and governed architecture that lets multiple agents reason, collaborate, and act across systems securely and at scale, becoming the connective tissue of the enterprise by coordinating agents, integrating with core systems, and evolving as the technology does.41

The figure renders the principle the mesh encodes: agents earn first-class status only when every layer beneath them is deliberate rather than improvised per project, and identity and governance run vertically through all of them rather than being bolted on at the end. The same research frames the transformation across six dimensions that no integration team can skip — an enterprise-wide strategy tied to real priorities, people equipped for human–agent collaboration, a governance framework that defines autonomy levels and decision boundaries, a technology architecture reshaped around agents as connective tissue, a data foundation, and systems for integration and monitoring designed in from day one rather than retrofitted.41 It also names new roles the org chart will need, such as the agent orchestrator and the human-in-the-loop designer.41

Infrastructure has to evolve to match. The recommended direction is a more modular, mesh-like design in which agents, tools, and enterprise systems connect through a shared orchestration layer, resting on a few foundational capabilities: repeatable actions made accessible as code with embedded policy checks; reliable operational data with clear sources of truth for ownership, dependencies, logs, and metrics; the pragmatic acceptance that imperfect data should not prevent progress, since many high-value use cases can be piloted even amid fragmented repositories; and controls and governance embedded directly rather than layered on afterward.42 This is not a side investment. More than a third of high performers are already committing over 20% of their digital budgets to AI, a level of spend that only makes sense if agents are becoming core infrastructure rather than experiments.42

Two architectural archetypes are settling out in practice: single-agent workflows, where one agent uses multiple tools and data sources in sequence, and multi-agent workflows, where specialized agents coordinate — the vertical and horizontal patterns the protocol layer was built to serve.3 Governing them works best as a federated model: business domains own the day-to-day governance of agent-enabled workflows, including the domain models and ontologies that give agents their semantics, while central data and AI teams maintain the shared platforms, guardrails, and oversight.3 Autonomy at the edge, accountability at the center.

The governance model that holds this together is federated by necessity. A central platform team cannot understand the nuances of every workflow an agent might touch, and a fully decentralized free-for-all cannot maintain consistent guardrails, so the workable arrangement gives business domains ownership of the policies, ontologies, and acceptable-autonomy levels for their own agent-enabled processes while a central function owns the shared substrate — the identity fabric, the orchestration layer, the audit pipeline, and the controls embedded in them.3 That division is only possible if the controls are built into the infrastructure rather than reinvented per team, which is the practical reason the recommended architecture embeds policy checks and observability directly into the orchestration layer instead of leaving them to each project.42

The organizational implications run deeper than a new team. When Moderna merged its HR and IT leadership, it was signaling that AI is not just a technical tool but a workforce-shaping force — a structural move toward an enterprise where the lines between who manages people and who manages systems blur because agents sit on both sides of that line.2 The blunt way McKinsey puts the architectural stakes is that in the agentic era, enterprise architectures are not merely the foundation of the business; they are the business, which is why even an incremental path — agents layered atop legacy systems, each insertion bringing efficiency and new data flows — has to be matched by a deliberate choice about what to build versus buy, revisited continuously as the technology moves.927 A bolt-on can be removed without anyone noticing. A first-class citizen has standing in the architecture, and that standing is the whole point.

CH 10The playbook

An Integration Playbook

The strategy resolves into a sequence. It begins, unglamorously, with assessment: implementations that started with a comprehensive readiness assessment — auditing data quality, integration architecture, and process maturity before writing code — delivered roughly 2.8 times better return than those that skipped straight to building, with success rates of 89% versus 34%.43 The same field data offers useful anchors for planning: a first-year total cost of ownership for an enterprise Agentforce-class deployment landing somewhere between $220,000 and $480,000, and conservative business cases modeling around 30% workflow automation in year one with an eighteen-to-twenty-four-month payback, integrated through middleware, OAuth 2.0, and REST APIs rather than point-to-point glue.43 What follows is the order that the evidence in this brief supports.

  1. Start where the systems are reachable

    Pick bounded, high-value workflows whose data and actions live in systems you can already connect to, the way Klarna began inside an app with authenticated customer state rather than with an open-ended assistant. Ambition without reach is how pilots die.2941

  2. Assess readiness before you build

    Audit data quality, integration architecture, and process maturity first. This single step separates the 89% that succeed from the 34% that do not, and it is far cheaper than discovering the gaps in production.43

  3. Unify identity before you scale access

    Give every agent a scoped, just-in-time, traceable, revocable credential and treat it as a first-class identity, not a service account. The Drift breach is what the alternative looks like at scale.1012

  4. Make data a product, not a scavenger hunt

    Build a shared semantic layer so agents inherit common definitions across HRIS, CRM, payroll, and ERP. Share meaning, not just data, so an agent never has to guess what a field means before it acts on it.3

  5. Use the protocol layer and unified APIs

    Adopt MCP and A2A and buy category coverage through unified APIs rather than hand-building connectors. Collapse the N×M problem into a sum instead of paying it as a product every quarter.21

  6. Design the handoff and the human floor

    Classify work by risk band, automate the low-risk tier fully, and make the agent refuse when confidence is low. When it escalates, pass intent, context, attempted response, and confidence so no human has to start from zero.30

  7. Instrument cost, reliability, and audit as first-class metrics

    Frameworks vary up to fiftyfold in cost at similar accuracy and roughly 37% between lab and production, so measure them, and keep the logs and delegation graphs that let you answer who acted, in scope, and how.2610

  8. Decide build-versus-buy deliberately, then revisit it

    Buy standardized capability, reserve custom work for genuine differentiation, and treat the decision as continuous rather than one-and-done, because the technology moves faster than any multiyear build.27

  9. Govern federally

    Let domains own the governance of their agent-enabled workflows and their ontologies while central teams own the shared platforms and guardrails. Autonomy at the edge, accountability at the center.3

  10. Tie it to the operating model and the P&L

    Anchor the program to enterprise priorities and to the income statement, not to a pilot’s vanity metrics — up to and including reshaping the org, as the firms that merged HR and IT leadership have begun to do.241

CH 11The connective decade

The Connective Decade

It is tempting to read the cancellation forecasts as a verdict on agents. They are better read as a correction in how agents get built. The market is early by any honest measure: roughly 17% of organizations have actually deployed AI agents while more than 60% intend to within two years, which means the dominant activity is still intention rather than production.44 Gartner’s projection that more than 40% of projects will be canceled is best understood as applying to the cohort proceeding without the prerequisites this brief has catalogued — clean and legible data, an integrated architecture, and governable identity — not as a statement that the technology fails when those foundations exist.44

The shakeout has a second, healthier dimension: it is clearing out the pretenders. Of the thousands of vendors claiming agentic capability, Gartner estimates only about 130 genuinely offer it, the rest being “agent washing” — rebranded chatbots and assistants — and that same analysis still projects that by 2028 around 15% of day-to-day work decisions will be made autonomously by agents and roughly a third of enterprise software will embed agentic AI.5 The direction is not in doubt; the discipline is. The enterprise agentic AI market sits on the order of $9–11 billion in 2026 and is projected to grow steeply, with one industry forecast putting nearly 45% of organizations orchestrating agents at scale by 2030.45 Headline ROI figures in the triple digits circulate freely; the responsible reading keeps them in the projection column and treats the foundational work as the thing that decides whether any of it materializes.45

The agents will keep improving regardless of what any single company does. The connection between those agents and the systems that run the business is the part you actually have to build — and it is the part that will separate the winners.

// The connective decade

The distinction that matters for a planner is between a market that is failing and a market that is maturing. Cancelled projects, consolidating vendors, and the exposure of “agent washing” are the visible signs of a field separating real capability from rebranded chatbots — a correction that tends to precede durable adoption rather than replace it, especially when the underlying trajectory still points toward agents making a meaningful share of routine decisions within a few years.445

Which returns us to where this began, and to the three priorities that an enterprise agent strategy lives or dies by. The first is to solve integration as the central problem, not the afterthought: secure, real-time, bidirectional access to the systems of record — HRIS, ATS, CRM, payroll, ERP — is the capability that turns a clever demo into an agent that finishes a job. The second is to win on the three hard problems underneath that access — authentication and identity for non-human actors, shared data schema and semantics across incompatible systems, and permission structures that are scoped, just-in-time, and traceable — because each is a place where deployments quietly fail and each is now solvable with deliberate effort. The third is to treat agents as first-class citizens of the enterprise architecture rather than bolt-ons to it: giving them identity, lifecycle, governance, and a defined place in the system through the protocol layer, the unified-API and orchestration toolkit, and a federated operating model.

The era’s defining advantage will not accrue to whoever has the most capable model; that capability is becoming a commodity available to everyone at once. It will accrue to whoever does the unglamorous work of connection — whoever makes their systems reachable, their data legible, and their permissions governable, and wires agents into the architecture as though they belong there. In the agentic era the enterprise architecture is the business, and the connective layer is the moat. The companies that understand this are not waiting for better agents. They are building the fabric those agents will run on.
