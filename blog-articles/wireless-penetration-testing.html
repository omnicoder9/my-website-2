01

The invisible attack surface

Every other domain a penetration tester touches has an edge you can point to. There is a cable, a switch port, a firewall, a building with a door. Wireless removes the edge. The moment a device modulates a carrier and lets it radiate, the network's effective boundary stops being the wall and becomes a propagation footprint — an irregular, weather-sensitive volume of space in which anyone with a receiver is, electrically, already inside. The wireless penetration tester's first job is to map that volume and treat everything within it as reachable, because to an attacker with a directional antenna in the parking lot, it is.

This manual treats wireless as a single discipline spanning several radio layers that share one property: they are all broadcast media. Wi-Fi, governed by the IEEE 802.11 family of standards, dominates the discussion because it carries the most valuable traffic, but the same adversarial logic applies down the frequency ladder to Bluetooth and Bluetooth Low Energy, to the 13.56 MHz and 125 kHz cards that open doors and authorize payments, and to the ultra-high-frequency tags tracking inventory.1 The current consolidated 802.11 standard defines the medium access control and physical layers that every Wi-Fi attack ultimately manipulates, and reading it the way an attacker does — as a list of unauthenticated management frames and negotiable parameters — is more instructive than any tool tutorial.1

What makes radio uniquely dangerous to defend is that the protocol itself leaks before any data is exchanged. An access point advertises its presence, capabilities, and supported ciphers in cleartext beacons many times a second. Clients, hunting for networks they have joined before, spray probe requests naming those networks, narrating their owner's travel history to anyone listening. None of this requires breaking encryption; it requires only a receiver placed in monitor mode and the patience to capture. The discipline of securing these networks is mature enough that the U.S. National Institute of Standards and Technology has published guidance dedicated to it, but mature guidance and deployed reality remain far apart.2

Authorization is the whole engagement

No part of this discipline is academic about legality, because wireless testing carries a double jeopardy that wired testing does not. The first hazard is the same one every penetration tester faces: accessing a computer system without authorization is a criminal offence. In the United States the governing statute is the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act, and cracking a Wi-Fi key you were not authorized to test, capturing another organization's traffic, or impersonating a network to harvest credentials can all fall within its reach regardless of how easy the act was technically.3 The second hazard is unique to radio: transmitting is itself regulated. Spectrum is a licensed public resource, and the unlicensed bands wireless gear uses are governed by rules — in the United States, the Federal Communications Commission's Part 15 — that constrain power levels and prohibit harmful interference. A deauthentication flood or a high-power rogue beacon is not just an attack; it is a transmission, and the act of jamming or interfering can be unlawful in its own right.4

The practical consequence is that a wireless engagement needs more careful scoping than almost any other. The rules of engagement must define not only which SSIDs and which BSSIDs are in scope, but the physical geography of testing, the permitted transmit behaviors, the handling of inevitably-captured third-party traffic from neighboring networks, and an explicit acknowledgement that radio does not respect property lines. A disciplined methodology anchors all of this. NIST's technical guide to security testing frames assessment as a repeatable progression — planning, discovery, attack, and reporting — and that skeleton maps cleanly onto wireless work, where discovery is reconnaissance of the spectrum, attack is exploitation of specific protocols, and reporting must translate radio findings into business risk.5

The perimeter is not where the wall is. It is where the signal stops being readable.

02

Wi-Fi and radio communications foundations

Before any attack makes sense, the tester has to think in the same terms the radio does: bands and channels, frames and states, the monitor-mode vantage point that turns a laptop into a passive sensor. Almost every Wi-Fi technique in this manual is a manipulation of one of three frame categories, and almost every failure to capture is a channel or interface problem rather than a tooling problem.

The spectrum you are operating in

Consumer wireless lives in shared, unlicensed bands. Wi-Fi historically occupied the 2.4 GHz industrial, scientific, and medical (ISM) band and the 5 GHz Unlicensed National Information Infrastructure (U-NII) bands; the Wi-Fi 6E generation extended operation into 6 GHz where regulators permit it, roughly doubling the available spectrum.6 The 2.4 GHz band is the most contested and the most instructive to understand, because its channels overlap. Each 802.11 channel in that band is roughly 22 MHz wide but the channel center frequencies are spaced only 5 MHz apart, so adjacent channels bleed into one another. Only channels 1, 6, and 11 are mutually non-overlapping in most regulatory domains — a fact that shapes both legitimate network planning and the practical question of which channels a tester must monitor to see all traffic.

Three kinds of frame, and why it matters

The 802.11 standard divides traffic into management, control, and data frames, and the security posture of each is wildly different.1 Data frames carry the payload and, on a protected network, are encrypted. Control frames coordinate medium access. Management frames — beacons, probe requests and responses, authentication and association, and, critically, deauthentication and disassociation — handle the lifecycle of a connection. The historical design flaw that underwrites a large fraction of Wi-Fi attacks is that management frames in the original and WPA2-era designs are unauthenticated. A station cannot cryptographically verify that a deauthentication frame claiming to come from its access point actually did, which is precisely what makes forced-disconnect attacks trivial and reliable. The 802.11w amendment introduced Protected Management Frames to close this gap, and its mandatory use is one of the meaningful security improvements that arrived with WPA3.

Monitor mode: the passive vantage point

An ordinary wireless adapter in managed mode only surfaces frames addressed to it. To assess a network, the tester puts a capable adapter into monitor mode, which surrenders all frames the radio can demodulate on the tuned channel regardless of destination — the wireless equivalent of promiscuous capture on a wired tap. The canonical toolchain for this is the Aircrack-ng suite, whose airmon-ng prepares the interface and whose airodump-ng performs the channel survey, enumerating access points, their BSSIDs, channels, cipher suites, and the clients associated with each.7 Because a radio can physically tune only one channel at a time, capture is a sampling problem: a tool either parks on a single channel to catch everything there, or hops across channels and accepts that it will miss frames during the hops. This single constraint explains most "the attack didn't work" confusion in the field — the tester was simply not listening on the right channel when the handshake flew by.

The card and short-range layers

Below Wi-Fi sit the technologies a tester reaches for when the target is a building's access control, an asset tag, or a payment card. These occupy distinct, much lower frequencies, and frequency dictates physics: how far the signal reaches, how much data it carries, and how an attacker must position to intercept or relay it. Low-frequency RFID at 125–134 kHz reaches only a few centimeters and carries almost no security; high-frequency systems at 13.56 MHz host the contactless smartcards and Near Field Communication that dominate access control and payments; ultra-high-frequency tags at 860–960 MHz reach meters and drive supply-chain tracking. Mapping these bands against Wi-Fi clarifies why each demands a different attack apparatus.

03

WPA2 attacks

WPA2 has been the default protection on the world's Wi-Fi for well over a decade, and the overwhelming majority of networks a tester encounters still run it. Its security rests on the Robust Security Network architecture and the four-way handshake, and almost every practical attack against it targets either the handshake's offline-crackable structure or specific protocol flaws discovered after the standard was set.

WPA2 is the Wi-Fi Alliance's certification of the security mechanisms defined in the IEEE 802.11i amendment, which introduced the Robust Security Network and replaced the broken WEP and interim TKIP designs with AES in Counter Mode with CBC-MAC Protocol (CCMP) for confidentiality and integrity.8 NIST's companion guidance walks through establishing these networks and remains a useful reference for understanding the intended security model against which real deployments are measured.9 In the pre-shared-key mode that dominates homes and small businesses, all the security ultimately derives from one passphrase, and that single point of failure is what the canonical WPA2 attack exploits.

The four-way handshake

When a client (the supplicant) joins a protected network, it and the access point (the authenticator) run a four-message exchange that proves both sides possess the Pairwise Master Key derived from the passphrase, and from it derives a fresh session key, the Pairwise Transient Key, without ever transmitting the master key. The exchange of nonces and message integrity codes is observable to a passive listener, and that is the crux: a captured handshake contains enough material to verify a guessed passphrase offline, at attacker-controlled speed, with no further interaction with the network.

Handshake capture and offline cracking

The classic attack has two steps. First, capture a handshake. A patient tester simply waits for a client to connect, but waiting can be replaced by a nudge: because deauthentication frames are unauthenticated, an attacker forges one to knock a client off the network, and the client immediately reconnects, performing a fresh handshake the tester records.1 Second, crack offline. With the handshake captured, the tester runs a wordlist against it; each candidate passphrase is used to derive a key and compute the expected integrity code, and a match reveals the passphrase.7 The entire economics of this attack come down to passphrase strength — a long, random passphrase is computationally out of reach, while a dictionary word or a predictable pattern falls in seconds. This is why the single most valuable recommendation a wireless report can carry is migration away from human-chosen pre-shared keys toward enterprise authentication or, at minimum, high-entropy passphrases.

The clientless PMKID attack

A significant 2018 refinement removed the need to wait for or deauthenticate a client at all. The hashcat project's lead developer demonstrated that on access points that include the Pairwise Master Key Identifier in the first handshake message, an attacker can solicit that field directly from the AP and crack the passphrase from it — no connected client, no deauthentication, no full handshake required.10 This clientless technique made WPA2-PSK auditing faster and quieter, and it is now a standard first move: query the AP, harvest the PMKID, and crack it offline exactly as before. It does not weaken a strong passphrase, but it eliminates the operational friction that previously protected networks with no active clients.

KRACK: breaking the handshake itself

Where dictionary attacks target weak passphrases, the Key Reinstallation Attack targeted the handshake protocol itself, and so affected strong passphrases too. In 2017 researchers showed that by capturing and replaying a specific handshake message, an attacker could force a victim to reinstall an already-in-use session key, resetting the associated nonce and replay counters. Because the encryption modes depend on never reusing a nonce with the same key, this reinstallation enabled decryption, and in some configurations replay or forgery, of frames.11 KRACK was a watershed because it was a flaw in the standard, present in every correct WPA2 implementation, rather than a misconfiguration. It was addressed through coordinated patches across the industry, and it is the strongest argument a tester can make that "we use WPA2 with a strong key" is not by itself a complete defense — patch currency matters as much as passphrase entropy.

WPS: the side door

Running parallel to the handshake story is Wi-Fi Protected Setup, a convenience feature meant to let users join a network with an eight-digit PIN. In 2011 a researcher showed the PIN's validation was structurally weak: the access point effectively reveals whether the first and second halves of the PIN are correct independently, collapsing the brute-force space from tens of millions to a few thousand attempts well within reach.12 The vulnerability was serious enough to warrant a formal coordination-center advisory, and it remains a live finding because WPS is frequently enabled by default and recovering the PIN recovers the passphrase regardless of how strong that passphrase is.13 A tester who finds WPS enabled has often found the fastest path in, and the remediation — disabling WPS entirely — is simple and high-value.

FragAttacks and the long tail

The discovery of design-level Wi-Fi flaws did not end with KRACK. In 2021 the same researcher disclosed FragAttacks, a collection of vulnerabilities in the frame fragmentation and aggregation features of the 802.11 standard — some present since the standard's earliest versions — that allow injection of frames or exfiltration of data under specific conditions.14 The lesson for the tester is structural: Wi-Fi's complexity guarantees a long tail of implementation and design issues, so a wireless assessment is never complete on protocol identification alone. Firmware versions, patch levels, and feature flags such as WPS and management-frame protection are all part of the attack surface. Even a purely insider concern persists — early WPA2 analysis showed that the shared group temporal key used for broadcast traffic lets one authorized client spoof the access point to others on the same network, an attack class sometimes called Hole196 that underscores why a valid network credential is not the same as trust.15

04

WPA3 attacks

WPA3 was designed specifically to retire the weaknesses a tester exploits in WPA2 — above all the offline dictionary attack. It largely succeeds at that goal, which changes the tester's job from cracking handshakes to probing implementations, transition modes, and side channels. The protocol is stronger; the deployments are not always.

The Wi-Fi Alliance introduced WPA3 in 2018 with two headline improvements relevant to personal networks.16 First, it replaced the pre-shared-key authentication exchange with Simultaneous Authentication of Equals (SAE), a password-authenticated key exchange that does not yield an offline-crackable artifact: an eavesdropper who captures a WPA3 authentication cannot run a wordlist against it the way they could against a WPA2 handshake. Second, WPA3 provides forward secrecy, so compromising the password does not retroactively decrypt previously captured sessions, and it mandates Protected Management Frames, neutralizing the unauthenticated-deauthentication trick that underpins so much WPA2 tradecraft.

SAE and the Dragonfly handshake

SAE is a specific instantiation of the Dragonfly key exchange, a balanced PAKE in which both parties prove knowledge of a shared password by deriving a common secret through elliptic-curve or finite-field operations, resisting both passive eavesdropping and offline guessing.17 In principle this is exactly the property a defender wants: an attacker who wishes to test passwords against SAE must do so online, one guess at a time, against a target that can rate-limit and lock out. The theoretical strength of the construction is real. The difficulty, as so often in cryptographic deployment, lies in the gap between the abstract protocol and the code that runs it on a battery-powered access point chip.

Dragonblood: side channels and downgrades

In 2020 researchers published Dragonblood, a systematic analysis of SAE and the related EAP-pwd that found several classes of weakness in the Dragonfly handshake as deployed.18 The findings fell into three practically important buckets. First were side-channel leaks: the password-derivation step in early implementations exhibited timing and cache-access behavior that varied with the password, letting an attacker who could run code on or near a victim device recover information sufficient to mount a password-partitioning attack — effectively reintroducing an offline-guessing capability through a side door rather than the front. Second were denial-of-service vectors, where the computational cost of processing SAE commit messages let a modest attacker exhaust an access point's resources. Third, and most important operationally, were downgrade attacks against transition mode.

Because the world cannot replace every WPA2 device overnight, WPA3 defines a transition mode in which an access point accepts both WPA3 and WPA2 connections on the same network. Dragonblood showed that an attacker can exploit this by standing up a rogue access point that advertises only WPA2 for the same SSID, coaxing a transition-capable client into the weaker protocol and then capturing a crackable WPA2 handshake — the full WPA2 attack chain, reached by denying the client its WPA3 path.18 The takeaway for the tester is precise and worth stating plainly in any report: a network advertised as "WPA3" but running transition mode often still carries WPA2's risk profile. The strongest configuration is WPA3-only with management-frame protection required, and verifying which mode a network actually runs is a core WPA3 assessment task rather than a footnote.

Rogue access points

A rogue access point is the cleanest illustration of why wireless erases the perimeter. It comes in two forms that share a name but differ in mechanism: an unauthorized AP attached to the inside of a trusted network, and an attacker-controlled AP that impersonates a trusted one. The first backdoors the wired network; the second hijacks the clients. Both are squarely in a wireless tester's remit.

The insider rogue: a bridge past every control

The first and most underappreciated category is the unauthorized access point installed by an insider — often a well-meaning employee who plugs a cheap consumer AP into an office Ethernet port for convenience. The security consequence is severe: that AP creates an unmanaged radio bridge directly onto the internal network, behind the firewall, the segmentation, and the monitoring that defend the wired perimeter. Every dollar spent on edge defenses is bypassed by a device broadcasting an open or weakly-secured network from a conference room. NIST's WLAN guidance treats the detection and removal of unauthorized devices as a baseline control precisely because this bridge is so dangerous and so easy to create.2

This is not merely a theoretical concern; it is a compliance-mandated test. The Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard requires organizations that handle cardholder data to maintain an inventory of authorized wireless access points and to actively test for the presence of unauthorized ones, whether through periodic scanning or continuous wireless monitoring.19 For a tester, this means a rogue-AP assessment has both a technical and a regulatory dimension: finding an unauthorized AP is a finding in its own right, and an absence of any detection capability is a separate, structural finding.

The attacker rogue: luring the client

The second category flips the direction of attack. Rather than bridging into a network, the attacker stands up an access point designed to attract victim clients — broadcasting an enticing or familiar network name, then intercepting and manipulating whatever traffic the lured clients send. This is the technique cataloged in adversary behavior frameworks as a rogue Wi-Fi access point, frequently chained with adversary-in-the-middle positioning to capture credentials or inject content.20 Its most refined and dangerous expression is the evil twin, treated in the next section, where the rogue AP precisely mimics a specific legitimate network the victims already trust.

What the tester actually does

A rogue-AP engagement has three deliverables. The first is discovery: walk the facility with a monitoring rig, enumerate every AP within the building's RF footprint, and reconcile that list against the authorized inventory, flagging anything unexplained. The second is impact demonstration: where an unauthorized AP is found bridging the internal network, show — within the agreed rules of engagement — what an attacker reaches through it. The third is control validation: test whether the organization's wireless intrusion-detection or prevention system actually notices a newly introduced rogue, by introducing a benign test AP and measuring whether and how fast it is detected and contained. The absence of that capability is often the most consequential thing the engagement uncovers, because it means the organization has no way of knowing when the bridge appears.

06

Evil twin attacks

The evil twin is the rogue access point perfected: an attacker-controlled AP that clones the name, and often the look, of a network the victim already trusts, so that the victim's own device — or its owner — connects without suspicion. It is the dominant technique for harvesting Wi-Fi credentials and for inserting an attacker into the middle of a victim's traffic, and it exploits human and protocol trust simultaneously.

The mechanism rests on a fact about how clients choose networks. A station identifies a network primarily by its name, the SSID, not by the access point's hardware address. An attacker who broadcasts the same SSID as a legitimate network — often with a stronger local signal, achieved simply by being closer or using more antenna gain — presents the victim's device with what appears to be the same network. If the attacker also knocks the victim off the real AP with forged deauthentication frames, the victim reconnects, and the device's roaming logic may select the attacker's stronger signal. From that point the attacker is the network, positioned to intercept, alter, or redirect everything that flows.

Captive portals and credential harvesting

An evil twin becomes a credential trap when it presents a forged captive portal — the splash page familiar from hotels and cafés. Having drawn the victim onto its network, the attacker's AP serves a convincing imitation of the expected login or "Wi-Fi password required" page, and the victim, conditioned to such prompts, types the very passphrase or corporate credential the attacker wants. This blends a social-engineering attack with the radio attack: the protocol delivers the victim, and the portal collects the secret. Against pre-shared-key networks the harvested passphrase is the keys to the kingdom; against enterprise networks the attacker may instead capture the authentication exchange itself for offline attack, which is why rogue enterprise APs are a distinct and serious sub-discipline.

KARMA and the device that betrays itself

The evil twin's most elegant variant requires no knowledge of the target network at all, because the client volunteers it. Devices that remember preferred networks broadcast probe requests asking, by name, whether any of those networks are nearby. The KARMA technique, introduced in 2005, observed that an attacker can simply answer "yes" to every such request, impersonating whatever network the device asks for and luring it to connect automatically.21 A laptop that once joined an airport's open network will, untouched in its owner's bag, try to rejoin "AirportWiFi" anywhere it hears that name advertised — and an attacker listening for that probe can become "AirportWiFi" on demand. Modern operating systems have hardened probe behavior to limit this, but the underlying lesson endures: a client's memory of past networks is an attack surface, and minimizing saved open networks is a genuine defensive control.

Tooling and the tester's framing

Evil-twin operations are well served by general network-attack frameworks. Bettercap, a successor to the older Ettercap, bundles Wi-Fi reconnaissance, deauthentication, and the man-in-the-middle and traffic-manipulation capabilities an evil twin needs into a single scriptable tool.22 Purpose-built tools layer rogue enterprise-AP and captive-portal automation on top. But the tester's contribution is not running a tool; it is demonstrating, concretely and within scope, that a specific population of users and devices can be lured and harvested, and then translating that into the two controls that actually help: strong mutual authentication that lets clients verify the network's identity (server-certificate validation on enterprise Wi-Fi), and user awareness that resists portal phishing. A finding that "users connected to our evil twin and submitted credentials" lands with an organization in a way that abstract protocol commentary never does.

Scope discipline. Evil-twin testing inevitably attracts whatever devices are in range, including those of uninvolved third parties. Rules of engagement must define how non-target associations and any incidentally captured credentials are handled, isolated, and destroyed. Broadcasting a lure is a transmission with real spillover, and the geography and timing of the test must be constrained accordingly.

Bluetooth security

Bluetooth is everywhere a tester is not always looking: in the keyboard, the headset, the medical sensor, the car, the building's access fob. It is two protocols wearing one name — Classic and Low Energy — with different pairing models and different failure modes, and its assessment has matured from novelty attacks into a body of rigorous protocol research.

Bluetooth shares the crowded 2.4 GHz ISM band with Wi-Fi but behaves very differently, using adaptive frequency-hopping spread spectrum to skip rapidly across many narrow channels, which both reduces interference and makes naive interception harder. The technology splits into two stacks defined in the Bluetooth Core Specification: Basic Rate / Enhanced Data Rate (BR/EDR), the "Classic" Bluetooth of audio and file transfer, and Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE), the low-power variant that dominates wearables, beacons, and the Internet of Things.23 They are not interoperable at the radio level and they fail in different ways, so a tester must identify which is in use before reasoning about its security. NIST maintains a dedicated guide to Bluetooth security that catalogs the threat model and recommended configurations across both stacks, and it is the natural anchor for a Bluetooth assessment.24

Pairing, bonding, and the weak-by-default problem

Security in both stacks is established at pairing, when two devices agree on keys that protect subsequent communication. Modern Bluetooth offers strong pairing modes — numeric comparison and passkey entry provide protection against man-in-the-middle interception — but it also offers a mode called Just Works, which establishes an encrypted link with no authentication of the peer at all, precisely to support devices with no display or keypad. Just Works is extraordinarily common in BLE accessories, and a link paired this way is vulnerable to an active attacker who interposes during pairing. The recurring theme in Bluetooth assessment is that the protocol's strongest options exist but its weakest options are chosen by default in shipping products, and the tester's job is to find where the convenient-but-insecure path was taken.

KNOB: negotiating security down to nothing

The most striking demonstration of Bluetooth's negotiation weaknesses is the Key Negotiation of Bluetooth attack. In 2019 researchers showed that the BR/EDR specification allowed two devices to negotiate the entropy of their encryption key down to as little as a single byte, and — critically — that an attacker positioned between two pairing devices could force this minimal entropy without either device alerting its user.25 With the key reduced to one byte of entropy, brute-forcing it becomes trivial, and the encrypted link is effectively transparent to the attacker. KNOB was a specification-level flaw affecting compliant devices broadly, and its remediation required mandating a minimum key length — a fix that, like KRACK's, depends on the device actually being patched and on both ends enforcing the floor.

BIAS, BlueBorne, and the impersonation family

Two further bodies of work round out the modern Bluetooth threat picture. The Bluetooth Impersonation Attacks demonstrated in 2020 that weaknesses in the authentication procedures of both legacy and Secure Connections pairing let an attacker impersonate a previously-bonded device without knowing the long-term key, defeating the assumption that a device which has paired before can be trusted on sight.26 Earlier, in 2017, the BlueBorne disclosure collected a set of vulnerabilities across multiple operating-system Bluetooth implementations that could allow code execution or interception over the air without the victim pairing, clicking, or taking any action — a reminder that the Bluetooth stack itself, not just its cryptography, is attackable software.27 Together with the historical bluejacking and bluesnarfing techniques, these define a discipline in which both the protocol's negotiation logic and its implementation surface are fair game.

Sniffing the hop

Capturing Bluetooth off the air is harder than capturing Wi-Fi because of frequency hopping: a sniffer must follow a rapidly changing channel sequence rather than parking on one frequency. Dedicated hardware solves this. The open-source Ubertooth platform was built specifically to give researchers an affordable window into Bluetooth — and especially BLE — traffic, enabling the passive observation that underpins much Bluetooth research and assessment.28 For BLE in particular, where a great deal of traffic is unencrypted or weakly protected and where address-randomization is inconsistently implemented, a sniffer frequently reveals device behavior, identifiers, and data that the designers assumed were private. The defensive prescriptions follow directly: use Secure Connections with authenticated pairing, enforce minimum key lengths, limit discoverability and connectability to when they are needed, and keep the stack patched against implementation flaws.

08

RFID attacks

Radio-frequency identification is the technology between a tester and most physical security: the badge that opens the door, the tag on the asset, the card in the wallet. Its security ranges from genuinely strong to literally none, and the difference is almost entirely a function of frequency and the chip the customer chose to buy.

An RFID system is a reader and a tag. Passive tags carry no battery; the reader's field powers them, and they answer by modulating that field. What an attacker can do depends overwhelmingly on which of three frequency tiers the system uses, because frequency sets read range, data rate, and — by correlation with the chips that operate there — the level of cryptographic protection typically present. The relevant standards are tiered the same way: low- and high-frequency proximity and vicinity behavior is defined principally in the ISO/IEC 14443 and 15693 families, while ultra-high-frequency item management follows the ISO/IEC 18000 series.2930 NIST's guidance on securing RFID systems frames the risks — cloning, eavesdropping, tracking, and unauthorized access — that a tester sets out to demonstrate.31

Low frequency: identity without secrecy

The 125–134 kHz low-frequency tier is the most thoroughly broken because it was never designed for security. Common low-frequency access credentials transmit a fixed identifier and nothing else — no challenge, no response, no cryptography. Reading the identifier and writing it to a blank card produces a perfect functional clone, and the read can be performed from a short distance with a concealed antenna. For a physical-security assessment this is frequently the single most impactful finding: an organization with serious network defenses may gate its doors with a credential that an attacker can copy in seconds from a badge glimpsed on a lanyard. The remediation is not a configuration change but a technology change — moving to cryptographic credentials — and that scale of recommendation is exactly why physical and wireless testing belong together.

High frequency: where the cryptography lives, and breaks

The 13.56 MHz high-frequency tier under ISO/IEC 14443 is where contactless smartcards operate, and where real cryptography appears — sometimes well, sometimes catastrophically. The defining cautionary tale is MIFARE Classic, for years the world's most widely deployed contactless access card, which relied on a proprietary stream cipher called Crypto-1. In 2008 researchers reverse-engineered Crypto-1 and demonstrated fundamental weaknesses that allowed recovery of card keys and full cloning, ending the card's credibility as a security control while leaving it deployed in countless buildings and transit systems for years afterward.32 The episode is the canonical argument against security through obscurity: a secret cipher inspired confidence right up until it was extracted, after which every card became forgeable. Modern high-frequency credentials based on open, vetted cryptography with proper key diversification are a different proposition — but a tester must verify which generation of card is actually in use, because "we use smartcards" says nothing about whether they use a broken one.

Ultra-high frequency: range as the threat

The 860–960 MHz ultra-high-frequency tier under the ISO/IEC 18000 series serves supply-chain and inventory tagging, and its distinguishing risk is range.33 Where proximity cards must be read at a few centimeters, UHF tags are designed to be read at meters, which makes both unauthorized inventorying and covert tracking practical at a distance. Many UHF tags carry little or no access control, and the threat model is less about cloning a credential than about an adversary reading an organization's tagged assets — counting them, identifying them, and tracking their movement — from outside any controlled boundary. The defensive measures here are about access passwords, tag data minimization, and, where appropriate, the ability to disable tags after their useful life.

The card-hacking bench

The dominant tool for RFID research and assessment across these tiers is the Proxmark3, a versatile platform that can read, emulate, clone, and attack tags at both low and high frequencies, descending from early independent work on RFID transponder analysis.34 Alongside it, emulators and field tools let a tester capture a credential and replay it at a door. The workflow mirrors the rest of this manual: identify the technology, capture the credential, and demonstrate — within authorized scope — the act of cloning or replay that an attacker would perform, then recommend the credential generation and configuration that resists it.

09

NFC security

Near Field Communication is high-frequency RFID with a tighter range and a richer protocol, and it carries some of the highest-value transactions a tester will ever touch: contactless payments, transit, and increasingly identity. Its security story turns on one assumption — that proximity equals presence — and on the attacks that quietly break that assumption.

NFC operates at 13.56 MHz, the same high-frequency band as the smartcards of the previous section, and is built atop those proximity-card standards while adding its own interface and protocol defined in ISO/IEC 18092 and the NFC Forum specifications.35 Its defining characteristic is a deliberately short operating range — on the order of a few centimeters — which is presented as a security feature: a device must be very close to interact, so the reasoning goes, an attacker cannot easily interpose. NFC supports three operating modes that a tester must distinguish: reader/writer mode, in which a device reads passive tags; peer-to-peer mode, in which two active devices exchange data; and card-emulation mode, in which a phone behaves like a contactless card, the foundation of mobile payments.

Payments and the tokenization shield

The most security-critical NFC application is contactless payment, governed by the EMVCo contactless specifications that define how cards and phones present payment credentials to terminals.36 Mobile payment platforms layer strong protections on top: rather than transmitting the real card number, they present a device-specific token and a per-transaction cryptogram, so that a captured transaction cannot be replayed and a stolen token is useless elsewhere. This tokenization model is genuinely effective against naive skimming and replay — the data an attacker captures from a single tap cannot simply be reused. It does not, however, address every attack, and the residual risk that most concerns assessors is the relay.

The relay attack: defeating proximity

The relay attack is the conceptual heart of NFC and contactless security, because it directly dismantles the proximity assumption on which the whole model rests. The foundational practical demonstration, against ISO/IEC 14443 proximity cards, showed that an attacker need not break any cryptography at all: they place one device near the victim's card and another near the legitimate reader, and relay the protocol messages between them over a fast, separate communications channel.37 The card and reader believe they are centimeters apart and complete the transaction normally, while in reality the victim's card is far away — in a pocket, across a room, in another building. Crucially, this defeats per-transaction cryptograms too, because the attacker is not forging or replaying anything; they are faithfully passing the live, valid exchange between two genuine endpoints that should never have been able to reach each other.

Tags, NDEF, and the rest of the surface

Beyond payments, NFC's tag-reading mode introduces a more pedestrian but widespread risk. Passive NFC tags carry structured records — most commonly NDEF-formatted data such as a URL — that a phone may act on automatically when tapped, opening a web page or prompting an action. A maliciously written tag placed where people tap, such as on a poster or a payment stand, becomes a phishing or social-engineering vector that requires only that the victim bring a phone near it. Add the perennial possibilities of eavesdropping (achievable at greater than the nominal range with sufficient equipment) and active data corruption on the link, and the full NFC attack surface emerges. The defensive themes are consistent: rely on tokenization and per-transaction cryptography for value transactions, require deliberate user authorization rather than automatic action, treat the round-trip timing of a transaction as a security signal where the platform allows it, and educate users that "tap to do something" is an instruction a hostile tag can also issue.

The core toolkit

Three open-source tools recur in almost every wireless engagement, and they divide the work cleanly: Aircrack-ng cracks Wi-Fi, Kismet watches the spectrum, and Bettercap attacks across layers. Knowing what each is genuinely for — and what it is not — separates a methodical assessment from a pile of disconnected command output.

Aircrack-ng — the Wi-Fi auditing suite

Aircrack-ng is not a single program but a suite of focused command-line tools for assessing Wi-Fi security, and the name on the box, aircrack-ng, is just the cracker at the end of the pipeline.7 The suite's division of labor maps directly onto the WPA2 attack chain described earlier. airmon-ng places an adapter into monitor mode; airodump-ng surveys the band and captures frames, including the all-important four-way handshake; aireplay-ng injects frames, including the forged deauthentication that nudges a client into reconnecting and the fragmentation and replay attacks used against weaker protections; and aircrack-ng itself runs the captured handshake against a wordlist. A companion, airbase-ng, can even act as a software access point for rogue-AP scenarios. In practice the suite is frequently paired with a GPU-accelerated cracker for the final brute-force step, but Aircrack-ng remains the reference toolchain for getting from "an adapter and a target" to "a crackable handshake."

Kismet — the passive spectrum sensor

Where Aircrack-ng is oriented toward attacking a chosen target, Kismet is oriented toward seeing everything. It is a wireless network detector, sniffer, and intrusion-detection system that operates passively — it can map an environment without ever transmitting, which makes it both stealthy and safe to run in sensitive settings.38 Kismet channel-hops to discover access points and clients, including networks with hidden names that do not advertise themselves, and it tracks devices over time, logging what it sees to structured data for later analysis. Critically, it is no longer Wi-Fi-only: through a modular capture architecture and software-defined-radio support it can ingest Bluetooth, BLE, and other radio protocols, making it a single pane of glass for the whole RF environment. With location data it becomes the engine of wardriving and site survey, building maps of where networks are detectable. For the rogue-AP and reconnaissance work at the heart of an assessment, Kismet is the monitoring backbone, and its passive nature is exactly what lets a tester observe without disturbing.

Bettercap — the cross-layer offensive framework

Bettercap is the active counterpart to Kismet's passive watching: a scriptable framework for reconnaissance and attacks across multiple network layers, often described as a Swiss Army knife for the job.22 Its relevance to wireless is that it does not stop at Wi-Fi. Bettercap modules cover Wi-Fi reconnaissance, deauthentication, and handshake capture; Bluetooth Low Energy discovery and enumeration; attacks against wireless human-interface devices such as keyboards and mice; and the classic man-in-the-middle, traffic-manipulation, and credential-capture capabilities needed to exploit the access an evil twin or a spoofing attack provides. It exposes its functionality through an interactive session, scriptable "caplets," and a web interface, which makes it well suited to chaining a wireless foothold into traffic interception. Bettercap is where many wireless engagements turn a radio position into a demonstrated business impact.

How they fit together. A representative flow: run Kismet passively to inventory the RF environment and identify targets and anomalies without transmitting; use Aircrack-ng to capture and crack a chosen WPA2 network's handshake; and use Bettercap to stand up an evil twin or man-in-the-middle position and demonstrate what an attacker reaches once on the network. These three are anchors, not the whole bench. Hidden among real engagements are GPU crackers for offline work, dedicated radios such as the Ubertooth and Proxmark families for Bluetooth and card work, software-defined radios for arbitrary signal capture, and purpose-built field hardware for rogue-AP and credential-harvesting automation. But a tester fluent in Aircrack-ng, Kismet, and Bettercap can carry the core of nearly any wireless assessment, and the rest of the toolkit slots in around them.

Methodology and standards

Tools without method produce noise. A wireless engagement is a structured progression that begins long before the first capture and ends with risk translated into language a business can act on. The same testing standards that govern all penetration testing apply, with wireless-specific emphasis on scoping the physical and spectral boundaries that radio refuses to respect on its own.

The phased model from NIST's testing guide — planning, discovery, attack, and reporting — provides the spine.5 Around it, three bodies of practice add rigor. The Penetration Testing Execution Standard structures the engagement end to end, from pre-engagement scoping and intelligence gathering through threat modeling, exploitation, post-exploitation, and reporting, and its insistence on disciplined pre-engagement is exactly what wireless work needs given its legal and spillover hazards.39 The Open Source Security Testing Methodology Manual goes further toward measurement, treating wireless and spectrum security as a distinct testing channel with its own operational controls and metrics.40 And where wireless leads into the software behind it — captive portals, device management interfaces, and the web and IoT applications of connected devices — OWASP's testing guidance and project ecosystem provide the methodology for the application layer that a radio foothold so often exposes.41

The phases, made wireless

  1. Scope and rules of engagement. Define in-scope SSIDs and BSSIDs, the physical area of testing, which transmit behaviors (deauthentication, rogue beacons, lures) are permitted, and how inevitably-captured third-party traffic and credentials are handled and destroyed. Acknowledge explicitly that the test will radiate beyond property lines.
  2. Reconnaissance. Survey the spectrum passively first — enumerate access points, clients, channels, cipher suites, and coverage, and reconcile against the authorized inventory. Passive-first protects sensitive environments and reduces legal exposure.
  3. Threat modeling. Map which assets are reachable over which radio, and prioritize: a weak guest network is a different risk than a WPA2-PSK network bridging into production.
  4. Exploitation. Execute the scoped attacks — handshake capture and offline cracking, WPS recovery, evil twin and rogue AP, transition-mode downgrade, and the Bluetooth, RFID, and NFC techniques as authorized.
  5. Post-exploitation and pivot. The finding that matters is impact. Demonstrate, within scope, that a wireless foothold reaches internal systems, because an unauthorized bridge or a cracked key is abstract until its consequences are shown.
  6. Reporting. Translate radio findings into business risk and concrete remediation, and rank by exploitability and impact rather than by protocol novelty.

Why this is never a one-time test

Wireless security resists the "assess once, certify, move on" model because the protocol-level attacks keep arriving. The cadence is steady across two decades — a roughly biennial drumbeat of standards-level breaks affecting correctly-implemented, fully-patched devices — and each one resets the baseline of what "secure" means. Mapping discovered findings to adversary behavior frameworks helps a defender connect a specific weakness to the techniques a real attacker would use, and keeps an assessment honest about which threats it actually addressed.20

Training, labs and certifications

Wireless is the most stubbornly hands-on specialty in offensive security, because the attacks live in physical radio behavior that no purely virtual lab fully reproduces. Building skill means building a bench — real adapters, real radios — alongside structured training, and the certifications that matter are the ones that test you on live spectrum.

Certifications worth the time

The most focused credential in this space is the Offensive Security Wireless Professional, earned through OffSec's wireless attacks course and a practical examination in which the candidate must compromise live networks rather than answer multiple-choice questions — the hands-on format that suits the subject.42 For broader RF coverage, the SANS Institute's wireless penetration testing course leads to the GIAC Assessing and Auditing Wireless Networks certification, spanning Wi-Fi alongside Bluetooth, Zigbee, RFID, and software-defined radio, and is well regarded for breadth across the spectrum.43 More general credentials place wireless within a wider curriculum: EC-Council's Certified Ethical Hacker includes wireless attack content as part of a broad ethical-hacking syllabus, and CompTIA's PenTest+ offers a vendor-neutral, foundational penetration-testing certification that situates wireless among other domains.4445

Regulations and best practice

Wireless testing is constrained by two distinct legal regimes and informed by a clear, if imperfectly-followed, set of defensive standards. Knowing both is part of competence: the tester must stay inside the law on two axes at once, and the report must point the client toward the configurations that actually close the findings.

The two regulatory axes

The first axis governs accessing systems. Unauthorized access to a computer or network is a criminal matter, and in the United States the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act is the principal statute; equivalent laws exist across other jurisdictions, and a tester operating internationally must understand the local equivalent.3 The second axis governs using the spectrum. Radio transmission is regulated independently of any computer it touches: in the United States the FCC's Part 15 sets the terms for unlicensed operation and prohibits harmful interference, and in Europe the corresponding regime — the ETSI harmonized standards such as those for 2.4 GHz wideband transmission systems — performs the same function.446 The practical upshot is that active wireless attacks are transmissions and may be unlawful as transmissions — jamming and deauthentication flooding are the clearest examples — quite apart from any unauthorized-access question. Compliance regimes add a third consideration: standards such as PCI DSS impose affirmative wireless obligations, including maintaining an authorized-AP inventory and scanning for rogues, so that a wireless assessment is sometimes a regulatory requirement rather than a discretionary exercise.19

Captured data is regulated data. A wireless test sweeps up identifiers and traffic from devices and people who never consented, including from neighboring networks outside scope. Data-protection law applies to this material; the rules of engagement must specify how it is minimized, isolated, and destroyed, and the tester must follow that specification to the letter.

The defender's configuration, by layer

The consolidated best practice for wireless local-area networks is well documented, and a wireless report is at its most useful when it maps each finding to one of these controls.2 Across the layers this manual covers, the prescriptions are consistent and mutually reinforcing.

Two cross-cutting controls deserve emphasis because they recur in every layer. The first is patch currency: KRACK, KNOB, Dragonblood, and FragAttacks were all standards- or implementation-level breaks whose only practical remedy was a firmware update, so an unpatched device negates otherwise-correct configuration.24 The second is treating wireless as inherently untrusted: the most resilient architectures place every wireless network behind the same segmentation, authentication, and monitoring that protect any other untrusted entry point, so that even a fully compromised radio reaches only what a hostile network should — namely, very little. Securing the radio layers individually matters, as NIST's RFID guidance and the rest of the standards make clear, but assuming the radio will eventually fail and containing that failure is the posture that survives the next biennial break.31
