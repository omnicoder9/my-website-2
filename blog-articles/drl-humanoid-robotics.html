Abstract

Humanoid robots—machines embodied in human morphology and tasked with operating across human environments—represent one of the most challenging frontiers in both robotics engineering and artificial intelligence. Deep reinforcement learning (DRL) has emerged as a central methodology for endowing such systems with adaptive, general-purpose motor intelligence. This review synthesises the theoretical foundations of DRL, with particular attention to policy gradient methods, value-based architectures, and actor-critic algorithms; examines their application to canonical humanoid control problems including locomotion, whole-body coordination, and dexterous manipulation; and surveys the practical challenges of sim-to-real transfer and cross-embodiment generalisation. Complementary paradigms—imitation learning, diffusion-based policy representations, and vision-language-action models—are assessed as synergistic components of a broader research agenda. Key tools, simulation platforms, and landmark empirical results are catalogued throughout. The review concludes by mapping the open problems that must be resolved to achieve robust, scalable, and genuinely general humanoid robot intelligence.

§ 1Introduction

Humanoid robotics occupies a unique position at the intersection of mechanical engineering, control theory, computer vision, and artificial intelligence. Unlike purpose-built industrial manipulators or wheeled service robots, humanoid platforms are designed to navigate and act within spaces constructed for the human body—ascending staircases, manipulating door handles, operating hand tools—a requirement that imposes an extraordinary breadth of motor competence.1 For decades, the dominant design paradigm relied upon model-based control: carefully engineered dynamical models, hand-crafted controllers, and analytically derived stability guarantees.1 While such approaches yielded impressive demonstrations, they proved brittle in the face of environmental variability and notoriously difficult to scale.

The emergence of deep learning as a general-purpose function approximator, combined with the reinforcement learning (RL) framework for sequential decision-making under uncertainty, offered a compelling alternative.2 When these two disciplines were united in the mid-2010s, first dramatically in the context of Atari game-playing by Mnih and colleagues at DeepMind,2 the field of deep reinforcement learning (DRL) was effectively born. Subsequent years witnessed the rapid migration of DRL techniques from game-playing benchmarks to physically grounded continuous control problems, culminating in their application to high-dimensional humanoid locomotion and manipulation tasks.22

The appeal of DRL for humanoid robotics is threefold. First, it dispenses with the need for a precise analytical model of the robot's dynamics, instead learning a policy directly from interaction with an environment.1 Second, deep neural networks provide the representational capacity to map raw sensory observations—joint encoders, inertial measurement units, camera images—to motor commands in a single end-to-end architecture.38 Third, when deployed at scale within physics-based simulation, DRL policies can be trained on billions of experience samples at a fraction of the cost and risk of equivalent real-world trials.33

§ 2Foundations of Deep Reinforcement Learning

2.1 The Reinforcement Learning Framework

Reinforcement learning formalises the problem of sequential decision-making as a Markov Decision Process (MDP), defined by the tuple (𝒮, 𝒜, 𝒫, ℛ, γ), where 𝒮 is the state space, 𝒜 the action space, 𝒫 : 𝒮 × 𝒜 → Δ(𝒮) the transition dynamics, ℛ : 𝒮 × 𝒜 → ℝ the reward function, and γ ∈ [0,1) the discount factor.1 An agent interacts with the environment by selecting actions according to a policy π : 𝒮 → Δ(𝒜), receiving scalar reward signals, and transitioning between states. The objective is to find a policy that maximises the expected discounted cumulative reward:

J(π) = 𝔼τ∼π[∑t=0∞ γt r(st, at)]

In the deep RL setting, the policy πθ is parameterised by the weights θ of a deep neural network, enabling generalisation across continuous and high-dimensional state and action spaces that are characteristic of robotic systems.38

2.2 Value-Based Methods and Deep Q-Networks

Value-based approaches learn an action-value function Qπ(s,a) = 𝔼π[∑t≥0 γt rt | s0=s, a0=a], from which a greedy policy can be derived.1 The seminal contribution of Mnih et al. (2015) was the Deep Q-Network (DQN), which demonstrated that a convolutional neural network could learn Q-functions directly from raw pixel inputs, achieving superhuman performance on 49 Atari 2600 games.2 DQN incorporated two critical stabilisation techniques: an experience replay buffer to decorrelate training samples, and a periodically updated target network to reduce oscillation in the Bellman target.2

While transformative for discrete action spaces, DQN is inapplicable to the continuous action spaces of robot control without architectural modification. The Q-value maximisation step over a continuous action set is generally intractable, motivating the development of actor-critic and policy gradient methods described in subsequent sections.

2.3 Policy Gradient Methods

Policy gradient algorithms optimise J(θ) directly by computing the gradient ∇θJ(θ) and ascending it via stochastic gradient ascent. The foundational result, the policy gradient theorem, expresses this gradient as:1

∇θJ(θ) = 𝔼τ∼πθ[∑t=0T ∇θ log πθ(at|st) · Ψt]

where Ψt is a return estimator, typically the advantage function Aπ(s,a) = Qπ(s,a) − Vπ(s). The REINFORCE algorithm of Williams (1992) instantiated this theorem using Monte Carlo return estimates, establishing the prototype for the policy gradient family.3 A persistent challenge is high variance in gradient estimates, necessitating the introduction of variance-reduction techniques such as baselines and advantage normalisation.

2.4 Trust Region Policy Optimisation (TRPO)

Vanilla policy gradient methods suffer from instability: a single poorly chosen update can destroy months of learned behaviour, as the policy may collapse if steps are too large. Schulman et al. (2015) addressed this by formulating an optimisation problem that maximises a surrogate objective subject to a constraint on the Kullback-Leibler divergence between successive policies:4

maximiseθ 𝔼[πθ(a|s) / πθ_old(a|s) · Ât] s.t. 𝔼[KL(πθ_old ‖ πθ)] ≤ δ

Trust Region Policy Optimisation (TRPO) solves this constrained problem via the conjugate gradient algorithm with a line search, yielding monotonically improving policies under certain conditions.4 TRPO demonstrated substantially more stable training than preceding methods on continuous control benchmarks including simulated locomotion tasks, and remains theoretically significant even as computationally lighter alternatives have superseded it in practice.

2.5 Proximal Policy Optimisation (PPO)

Proximal Policy Optimisation (PPO), proposed by Schulman et al. (2017), retains the core insight of TRPO—conservatively constraining policy updates—while replacing the computationally expensive constrained optimisation with a clipped surrogate objective amenable to first-order gradient methods:5

ℒCLIP(θ) = 𝔼t[min(rt(θ) Ât, clip(rt(θ), 1−ε, 1+ε) Ât)]

where rt(θ) = πθ(at|st) / πθ_old(at|st) is the probability ratio and ε is a hyperparameter typically set to 0.1–0.2.5 The clipping prevents excessively large updates without requiring second-order computation. PPO rapidly became the dominant algorithm in humanoid and legged robot research owing to its robustness, simplicity, and empirical effectiveness across diverse tasks, and is the default choice in the majority of contemporary DRL robotics papers.17,18

2.6 Actor-Critic Architectures

Actor-critic algorithms maintain two function approximators in parallel: an actor πθ that selects actions, and a critic Vφ or Qφ that evaluates them. The critic's bootstrapped value estimates are used to compute low-variance advantage estimates, combining the stability of value-based methods with the continuous action support of policy gradients.1

The Asynchronous Advantage Actor-Critic (A3C) of Mnih et al. (2016) distributed training across multiple CPU workers running parallel environment instances, achieving wall-clock speed-ups that made complex locomotion tasks tractable for the first time.9 Soft Actor-Critic (SAC), developed by Haarnoja et al. (2018), augmented the standard reward with an entropy bonus, encouraging exploratory behaviour and yielding a maximum-entropy policy:7

JSAC(π) = 𝔼[∑t γt(rt + α · ℋ(π(·|st)))]

SAC's off-policy nature and temperature parameter α—which can be tuned automatically—make it sample-efficient and robust to reward scaling, properties that proved highly beneficial for real-robot learning scenarios. Twin Delayed DDPG (TD3) of Fujimoto et al. (2018) addressed the overestimation bias inherent in actor-critic methods through a clipped double-Q trick and delayed policy updates, achieving state-of-the-art performance on MuJoCo continuous control benchmarks at the time of publication.8

Policy gradient methods offered something model-based control could not: a general mechanism for discovering complex motor behaviours from experience alone, without requiring a human to specify how the robot should move.

§ 3The Humanoid Robot Control Problem

3.1 Historical Context

The engineering pursuit of bipedal walking machines predates modern computation. Waseda University's WABOT-1 (1973) constituted the first full-scale anthropomorphic robot capable of rudimentary ambulation. Subsequent decades witnessed iterative progress through projects including Honda's ASIMO (2000), which employed zero moment point (ZMP) planning for statically stable locomotion, and Boston Dynamics' ATLAS (2013 onward), which demonstrated remarkably dynamic whole-body behaviours including backflips and parkour traversal under model-predictive control.

3.2 Why Humanoids Are Difficult

The humanoid control problem is characterised by several properties that render classical analytical methods insufficient. First, humanoids are underactuated systems: they possess fewer independent actuators than degrees of freedom (DoF) relative to the unconstrained dynamics, because the feet may leave the ground. Maintaining postural stability thus requires dynamic balancing rather than static support, demanding real-time coordination across the entire kinematic chain.1

Second, typical humanoid platforms such as Agility Robotics' Digit, Figure 01, or the Berkeley Humanoid possess 30–53 DoF,28 generating a state space that may exceed 200 dimensions when proprioceptive measurements—joint positions, velocities, accelerations, and torques—are concatenated with inertial and contact data. Designing reward functions that elicit stable, natural, and efficient movement across this space is a non-trivial engineering challenge in its own right.

Third, contact dynamics—the transient forces arising when the robot's feet, hands, or body interact with the environment—are discontinuous, difficult to model precisely, and highly sensitive to surface geometry and friction coefficients. Any learned policy must implicitly reason over contact events without explicit symbolic representations thereof.

3.3 Reward Function Design

The specification of reward functions for humanoid locomotion is a form of inverse problem: the engineer must encode, in a scalar signal, the full suite of desiderata including forward velocity, energy efficiency, joint limit avoidance, postural naturalness, and robustness to perturbation. Poorly specified rewards lead to reward hacking—the discovery of degenerate behaviours that maximise the numerical objective while violating intended design goals, such as hopping rather than walking.41

Standard practice employs composite rewards of the form r = rtask + λ1rvel + λ2rsmooth + λ3rcontact, with penalty terms for joint limit violations, excessive torques, and base orientation deviations.17 Reference motion imitation (Section 4.2) offers an alternative by replacing scalar reward engineering with trajectory-level demonstrations.

§ 4DRL for Humanoid Locomotion

4.1 Locomotion as a DRL Benchmark

The MuJoCo-based control suite, released with the OpenAI Gym (now Gymnasium) interface and later formalised by Duan et al. (2016) as a continuous control benchmark,22 established simulated humanoid locomotion as a canonical test for RL algorithms. The 21-DoF MuJoCo Humanoid environment—requiring a simulated figure to walk forward without falling—became a standard evaluation environment against which TRPO, PPO, SAC, TD3, and their successors were benchmarked. These early demonstrations, while informative, produced gaits that were energetically inefficient and lacked the qualitative naturalness of human movement.

4.2 Reference Motion Imitation: DeepMimic

A pivotal methodological advance was introduced by Peng et al. (2018) in DeepMimic, which augmented the DRL objective with an imitation reward measuring the proximity of the simulated character's state to reference motion captured from real human subjects:12

rimitation(s, a) = exp(−∑i wi ‖qi − q̂i‖2)

where qi and q̂i denote the simulated and reference joint configurations at frame i. By grounding the RL objective in human motion data, DeepMimic produced physically plausible characters capable of backflips, cartwheels, and diverse acrobatic skills from motion capture inputs, establishing reference motion imitation as a standard component of humanoid skill learning pipelines.12

4.3 Agile Locomotion and Parkour

Building on reference motion imitation, subsequent work demonstrated increasingly agile locomotion across challenging terrain. Peng et al. (2020) trained policies that could imitate animal gaits—trotting, bounding, leaping—from video observations of real animals rather than structured motion capture, significantly expanding the data sources available for reference motion learning.30

Zhuang et al. (2023) applied DRL to robotic parkour, training legged robots to vault over obstacles, perform precision jumps, and negotiate clutter without explicit planning, relying instead on a learned perception-action policy trained with privileged information in simulation and subsequently distilled for deployment.27 The Adversarial Motion Priors (AMP) framework of Peng et al. (2021) replaced hand-crafted imitation rewards with a learned discriminator—trained on motion capture data—that provides a style reward, allowing task and motion style objectives to be jointly optimised without frame-level correspondence.16

4.4 Massively Parallel Training: Learning to Walk in Minutes

Rudin et al. (2022) demonstrated that, when training is parallelised across thousands of simultaneous simulation instances on a single GPU—exploiting the Isaac Gym physics engine's CUDA-native tensor API—locomotion policies for complex legged robots could be trained to deployable quality in under ten minutes of wall-clock time.17 This result, which would have required weeks of computation in prior frameworks, fundamentally altered the economics of DRL policy development and enabled rapid iteration on reward functions, curriculum designs, and network architectures.

Key Result: Learning to Walk in Minutes

Rudin et al. (2022) trained locomotion policies for a 12-DoF quadruped using 4,096 parallel environments on a single NVIDIA A100 GPU. The resulting policy, trained in approximately 10 minutes, achieved robust outdoor locomotion and was successfully transferred to hardware with minimal tuning.17

4.5 Humanoid Locomotion as Token Prediction

Radosavovic et al. (2024) proposed reframing humanoid locomotion as an autoregressive sequence modelling problem, training a transformer-based policy on large datasets of robot experience using a next-token prediction objective analogous to language model pretraining.25 This approach, termed Humanoid Locomotion as Next Token Prediction, demonstrated that architectures developed for natural language processing could be adapted to continuous control, achieving robust locomotion on the UC Berkeley Humanoid platform with improved generalisation to novel terrains relative to conventional RL policies.25

§ 5Whole-Body Coordination and Teleoperation

5.1 The Whole-Body Control Challenge

Whole-body control (WBC) concerns the simultaneous regulation of a humanoid's full kinematic chain—including arms, torso, and legs—towards a common task objective. While locomotion policies typically treat the upper body as a passive load, practical humanoid deployment demands coordinated arm swing for balance, simultaneous loco-manipulation (carrying objects while walking), and reactive whole-body responses to perturbation. The coupling between limbs means that upper-body actions necessarily perturb the base dynamics, precluding independent control of the two subsystems.

5.2 Expressive Whole-Body Control

Cheng et al. (2024) introduced an expressive whole-body control framework for humanoid robots that learns to track upper-body motion commands—derived from human motion retargeting—while maintaining stable locomotion with the lower body.23 The system employed a hierarchical architecture: a high-level motion capture retargeting module extracted desired upper-body joint trajectories from a human operator, which were passed as reference commands to a low-level RL policy responsible for tracking while preserving balance. Crucially, the policy was trained entirely in simulation and successfully transferred to a full-sized humanoid platform.23

5.3 Real-Time Human-to-Humanoid Teleoperation

He et al. (2024) demonstrated real-time whole-body teleoperation of a humanoid robot from a human operator's motion, captured by a minimal sensor suite comprising a head-mounted display and hand controllers.24 A retargeting algorithm mapped the human's skeletal configuration to the robot's morphology, accounting for kinematic differences between human and robot embodiments. The RL policy, trained to track the retargeted commands under perturbation, achieved expressive behaviours including tool use, pouring, and object handover at latencies compatible with closed-loop teleoperation.24

§ 6Dexterous Manipulation

6.1 The Dexterity Problem

Dexterous in-hand manipulation—regrasping objects, rotating them to arbitrary orientations, and performing precise finger gaiting—is among the hardest problems in robotics. A five-fingered hand with four DoF per finger presents a 20-dimensional action space in which contact topology changes discontinuously, finger-object friction is poorly characterised, and visual occlusion by the hand itself limits observability. Classical planning approaches struggle with the combinatorial contact mode space, while DRL must contend with extraordinarily sparse reward signals for tasks such as solving a Rubik's cube.

6.2 OpenAI Dactyl

The most celebrated early demonstration of DRL-based dexterous manipulation is OpenAI's Dactyl project. Andrychowicz et al. (2020) trained a Shadow Dexterous Hand to reorient a Rubik's cube to arbitrary goal configurations using PPO in simulation, with domain randomisation applied to physics parameters including surface friction, object mass, joint damping, and tendon dynamics.14 The resulting policy, trained on approximately 13,000 years of simulated experience across 6,144 CPU cores, transferred to the physical hand without additional real-world training.14 Subsequent work by the same team demonstrated the system solving the Rubik's cube one-handed, while an automatic domain randomisation (ADR) scheme continuously expanded the distribution of simulated environments to maintain the policy at the edge of its competence.29

Achieving dexterous in-hand manipulation with reinforcement learning required training on more simulated experience than a human accumulates in several lifetimes — and domain randomisation extreme enough to encompass the physical world's variability.

6.3 Scaling Manipulation Learning

Dexterous manipulation research has increasingly moved towards data-driven scaling. The Open X-Embodiment collaboration (2023) compiled a dataset of 22 million robot trajectories across 22 robot platforms and 527 skills, training RT-X models that demonstrated improved cross-embodiment generalisation compared to single-robot baselines.36 RoboCat (Bousmalis et al., 2023) demonstrated a self-improving foundation agent for manipulation that fine-tuned a pre-trained visual model on small task-specific datasets before generating autonomous training data to further improve itself.44

§ 7Sim-to-Real Transfer

7.1 The Reality Gap

Simulation-trained policies frequently fail when deployed on physical hardware, a phenomenon termed the sim-to-real gap or reality gap. Sources of discrepancy include: imprecise actuation models (motor friction, backlash, rotor inertia), unmodelled dynamics (cable routing, thermal effects, joint elasticity), contact model inaccuracies (Coulomb friction simplifications, compliant contact versus rigid contact assumptions), and sensor noise characteristics not captured in simulation.41 For humanoids, the consequences of policy failure extend beyond task failure to potential hardware damage and safety hazards.

7.2 Domain Randomisation

Domain randomisation (DR), introduced to the robotics context by Tobin et al. (2017), addresses the reality gap by training policies over a broad distribution of simulated environments parameterised by randomised physical properties.13 The underlying hypothesis is that if the real world corresponds to a draw from this distribution—or lies within the envelope of generalisable behaviours learned from it—the policy will transfer without additional adaptation. Parameters subject to randomisation in humanoid locomotion research typically include link masses, joint frictions, motor strength, ground friction, floor height perturbations, and external push forces.14,17

Limitation: Domain Randomisation Bounds

Domain randomisation is not a panacea. Excessively wide randomisation can degrade nominal performance (a policy trained for any friction may be mediocre at all), and some properties—e.g. complex deformable object dynamics—are difficult to parametrise and randomise efficiently. Systematic unmodelled dynamics may lie outside any feasible randomisation envelope.41

7.3 Rapid Motor Adaptation (RMA)

Kumar et al. (2021) proposed Rapid Motor Adaptation (RMA), a two-phase training procedure specifically designed for sim-to-real transfer of legged locomotion.18 In the first phase, a base policy conditioned on an explicit environmental latent vector z—encoding ground friction, payload, and terrain type—is trained with standard RL. In the second phase, an adaptation module is trained to estimate z from proprioceptive history alone, enabling the deployed policy to adapt on-the-fly to the real environment without any privileged information. RMA achieved robust walking on challenging outdoor terrain including sand, mud, grass, and gravel within 20 simulation steps (approximately 0.5 s) of real deployment, without any real-world fine-tuning.18

7.4 Privileged Information and Teacher-Student Distillation

A complementary approach exploits the gap between simulated and real observation spaces during training. A "teacher" policy trained with access to privileged simulation state—precise terrain maps, contact forces, object positions—is first trained to solve the task. A "student" policy, observing only sensors available in deployment, is then trained by behavioural cloning or distillation to reproduce the teacher's action distribution given its restricted observations.27 The teacher-student framework decouples the difficulty of learning from the difficulty of sensing, and has been applied effectively to humanoid locomotion and parkour settings.27

§ 8Cross-Embodiment Generalisation

A fundamental question in robot learning is whether policies can generalise across different hardware platforms—different kinematic structures, sensor modalities, and actuator characteristics. Cross-embodiment generalisation is practically significant because training data from diverse platforms could be pooled to train more capable shared representations, reducing the per-robot data requirement.36

The Open X-Embodiment project operationalised this question at scale, demonstrating that a single transformer-based policy trained on heterogeneous multi-robot datasets could exhibit positive transfer across embodiments.36 However, the degree of transfer was found to be sensitive to kinematic similarity: policies trained on data from morphologically similar robots transferred more effectively than those bridging radically different kinematic structures.36

Hierarchically decoupled architectures (Hejna et al., 2020) address cross-embodiment transfer by separating morphology-independent task representations from morphology-specific motor primitives, enabling high-level skills to transfer while low-level motor control is retrained per embodiment.43 The advantage-weighted regression (AWR) framework of Peng et al. (2019) offers a related mechanism, using offline data from one embodiment to initialise policies for another via importance-weighted behavioural cloning.42

§ 9Complementary Learning Paradigms

9.1 Imitation Learning

Imitation learning (IL) encompasses methods that derive policies from expert demonstrations rather than (or in addition to) scalar reward signals. Behavioural cloning (BC) treats IL as supervised regression from states to expert actions, but suffers from compounding errors under distribution shift—the policy encounters states the expert never visited, leading to cascading failures.1

Generative Adversarial Imitation Learning (GAIL), introduced by Ho and Ermon (2016), frames IL as a two-player game between a policy generator and a discriminator trained to distinguish policy-generated state-action pairs from expert demonstrations.15 By treating the discriminator output as a shaped reward, GAIL circumvents the need for explicit reward engineering while inheriting the expressive power of deep RL. GAIL-derived rewards have been applied extensively in humanoid control as style or motion quality signals.16

9.2 HumanPlus: Humanoid Shadowing and Imitation

Fu et al. (2024) introduced HumanPlus, a system enabling a full-sized humanoid to learn whole-body skills from human demonstrations via a two-stage pipeline.26 In the first stage, a whole-body controller trained with RL enables the robot to shadow a human operator's body pose in real time, generating paired robot-action datasets without kinesthetic demonstration. In the second stage, sensorimotor transformer policies trained by imitation on these datasets generalise to autonomous task execution, demonstrating skills including folding clothes, typing, and warehouse picking—tasks requiring coordinated loco-manipulation without the operator in the loop.26

9.3 Diffusion Policies

Diffusion Policy, proposed by Chi et al. (2023), reimagines robot action generation as a denoising diffusion process.19 Rather than learning a deterministic or unimodal stochastic mapping from observations to actions, a diffusion-based policy learns to reverse a Markov chain that progressively adds Gaussian noise to expert action sequences. At inference, actions are generated by iteratively denoising a random initial sample conditioned on the current observation. This formulation naturally handles multimodal action distributions—crucial when demonstrations reflect diverse solution strategies—and empirically achieves state-of-the-art performance on both simulation and real-robot manipulation benchmarks.19

9.4 Vision-Language-Action Models

The integration of large pre-trained vision-language models (VLMs) into robot control pipelines represents a fundamental paradigm shift from specialist towards generalist robot intelligence. RT-1 (Brohan et al., 2022) demonstrated that a robotics transformer trained on 130,000 real-robot demonstrations could generalise to novel instructions specified in natural language, outperforming imitation learning baselines by a significant margin on both seen and unseen instructions.20

RT-2 (Brohan et al., 2023) took this further by directly co-training a large vision-language model (VLM) on robot action data, enabling the model's web-scale semantic knowledge to inform action selection.21 Actions were tokenised and appended to the VLM's output vocabulary, allowing the model to "speak" robot commands in the same framework it used to reason about images and text. RT-2 demonstrated emergent capabilities including multi-step reasoning, concept transfer from internet knowledge to physical manipulation, and the ability to follow novel instructions such as "move the object that represents the country that invented the Eiffel Tower."21

π0 (Black et al., 2024), developed at Physical Intelligence (Pi), extended the VLA paradigm to whole-body humanoid control using a flow matching action head—related to diffusion but using continuous normalising flows—enabling dexterous loco-manipulation conditioned on language instructions.35 π0 was pre-trained on a large cross-embodiment dataset before fine-tuning on task-specific demonstrations, demonstrating the viability of foundation model pretraining for general robot intelligence.35

Vision-language-action models do not merely learn motor skills. They import the accumulated knowledge of human language and visual understanding—a vast prior over the physical world—into the control loop.

10.1 Physics Simulation Environments

MuJoCo Sim

Todorov et al. (2012).11 Fast, accurate contact physics; standard for continuous control research. Now open-source under DeepMind stewardship. Used in nearly all academic DRL locomotion benchmarks.

Isaac Gym / Isaac Lab Sim

NVIDIA's GPU-accelerated RL simulation platform (Makoviychuk et al., 2021).33 Supports thousands of parallel environments on a single GPU. Isaac Lab is the successor, with improved modularity and ROS 2 integration.

Python bindings for Bullet physics; widely used in early DRL robotics research. Less performant than MuJoCo/Isaac but highly accessible and free. Supports humanoid URDF models.

Emerging GPU-native differentiable simulator (2024) supporting rigid bodies, deformables, and fluids. Designed for large-scale parallelism and differentiable physics for gradient-based policy optimisation.

Drake Sim

MIT's robotics toolbox for model-based control and simulation. Provides rigorous multibody dynamics and is used for generating reference trajectories that can seed RL policies.

Webots Sim

Open-source robot simulator from Cyberbotics. Supports ROS integration; widely used in education and research for humanoid robot prototyping before hardware deployment.

Stable-Baselines3 RL

Raffin et al. (2021).31 Reliable, well-documented PyTorch implementations of PPO, SAC, TD3, A2C, DDPG, and HER. De facto standard for research reproducibility and algorithm benchmarking.

Liang et al. (2018).32 Distributed RL library within the Ray ecosystem. Supports heterogeneous hardware and large-scale multi-agent training; used in production robotics pipelines.

rl_games RL

Maintained fork of OpenAI Gym by Farama Foundation. Provides the standard environment API, including MuJoCo Humanoid-v4, Ant, HalfCheetah, and Hopper environments used in benchmark comparisons.

Modular reinforcement learning library with native Isaac Gym / Isaac Lab support. Provides clean interfaces for multi-agent RL, real-robot deployment hooks, and integration with robot learning research workflows.

CleanRL RL

Single-file RL implementations emphasising transparency and reproducibility. Each algorithm (PPO, SAC, etc.) is self-contained in a single Python script, making it ideal for understanding and auditing implementations.

Pinocchio Control

Open-source rigid body dynamics library. Provides fast analytical computation of Jacobians, mass matrices, and inverse dynamics used by both classical WBC and as differentiable layers in hybrid RL-MPC controllers.

ROS 2 Control

Robot Operating System 2; middleware for hardware abstraction, sensor integration, and inter-process communication. Nearly all deployed DRL-trained humanoid policies interface with hardware through ROS 2 nodes.

WBC / IHMC Libraries Control

Model-predictive whole-body controllers from IHMC Robotics and similar groups, often used in hierarchical architectures where a DRL policy generates high-level references that are tracked by a lower-level analytical WBC.

Lerobot Data

Hugging Face library for robot learning. Provides datasets, pre-trained policies (ACT, Diffusion Policy), and standardised evaluation; lowers entry barrier for imitation learning and VLA model fine-tuning research.

§ 11Open Problems and Research Frontiers

11.1 Long-Horizon Task Planning

Current DRL methods excel at short-horizon skills—locomotion, grasping, reorientation—but composing these into coherent long-horizon task execution remains challenging. A robot tasked with "prepare a meal" must decompose the instruction into a sequence of interdependent sub-tasks, navigate between them, and recover from failures at each stage. Hierarchical RL, language model planning, and VLA models (Section 9.4) represent active approaches to this challenge, but robust long-horizon generalisation on physical humanoids remains an open problem.41

11.2 Safe Exploration and Constraint Satisfaction

Standard DRL algorithms treat safety constraints as penalty terms in the reward function, providing no formal guarantees on constraint satisfaction during training or deployment. For physical humanoids, safety violations during training—unexpected joint torques, falls, collisions—can damage hardware or harm nearby humans. Constrained policy optimisation methods, including Constrained Policy Optimisation (CPO) and Lagrangian-relaxed RL, address this through formal constraint formulations, but remain less empirically studied in the humanoid setting than unconstrained methods.41

11.3 Contact-Rich Manipulation at Scale

While diffusion policies and VLA models have improved contact-rich manipulation performance, generalisation to novel object geometries, materials, and task configurations remains limited. A humanoid robot must be capable of competent manipulation of arbitrary household objects without prior training on each specific instance—a requirement that current methods only partially address. Improved tactile sensing, deformable object simulation, and data-driven contact models are identified as key enabling technologies.19

11.4 Sample Efficiency and Foundation Model Integration

Despite GPU-accelerated simulation, the sample complexity of DRL remains orders of magnitude higher than that required by humans to learn equivalent motor skills. Foundation models pre-trained on internet-scale data offer a mechanism for injecting prior knowledge—geometric, semantic, causal—into the policy learning process, potentially reducing the required number of environment interactions. The boundary between pre-training and fine-tuning in this context, and the optimal architecture for integrating VLM representations with continuous motor control, are active areas of investigation.25,35

11.5 Evaluation Standards and Reproducibility

The field suffers from a lack of standardised evaluation protocols for humanoid robot policies. Simulation benchmarks are fragmented across simulators with different physics fidelity, and hardware demonstrations are often presented under controlled conditions that do not reflect real-world deployment variability. The robotics community would benefit from standardised evaluation suites, analogous to ML benchmarks such as ImageNet or GLUE, that assess locomotion, manipulation, and loco-manipulation capabilities in a reproducible and comparable manner.22

§ 12Conclusion

Deep reinforcement learning has undergone a remarkable transformation from a technique capable of playing Atari video games to one enabling bipedal robots to navigate outdoor terrain, perform acrobatic locomotion, and execute dexterous in-hand manipulation. The theoretical toolkit comprising policy gradients, TRPO, PPO, and actor-critic algorithms provides a principled foundation for continuous robot control, while massively parallel simulation environments have democratised access to the computational scale these methods require.5,17

The sim-to-real transfer problem, long considered a fundamental barrier, has been substantially ameliorated through domain randomisation, rapid motor adaptation, and teacher-student distillation, enabling policies trained in simulation to operate on physical hardware with increasing reliability.13,18 Complementary paradigms—imitation learning, adversarial motion priors, diffusion policies, and vision-language-action models—enrich the methodological landscape, providing mechanisms for incorporating human knowledge, naturalness constraints, and internet-scale pre-training into the robot learning pipeline.16,19,21,35

The frontier challenges that remain—long-horizon task composition, safe constraint satisfaction, contact-rich manipulation at arbitrary scale, and the principled integration of foundation model priors—are not merely engineering challenges but scientific problems whose resolution will require advances in optimisation theory, physics simulation, perception, and the cognitive science of how humans themselves acquire motor competence. The humanoid robot, as a platform, is simultaneously a practical engineering objective and a uniquely informative model system for studying the computational principles of embodied intelligence.

The convergence of DRL, large-scale simulation, and foundation model pre-training observed in the period 2022–2024 suggests that the pace of progress in humanoid robot capabilities will continue to accelerate. Whether this trajectory converges on genuinely general humanoid intelligence—or reveals new fundamental barriers—will be among the defining scientific questions of the coming decade.
