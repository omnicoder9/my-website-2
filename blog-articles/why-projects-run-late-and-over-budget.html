The Anatomy
of the Overrun

Projects come in late and over budget with a regularity that looks less like bad luck than like a law of nature. Across more than 16,000 large projects, only 8.5% hit both their budget and their schedule, and only half a percent hit budget, schedule and benefits together. This is what the evidence says about why — and what the handful of organisations that beat the odds actually do differently.

Prologue Sydney, 1957–1973   —   +1,357%

The house on Bennelong Point

In January 1957, the government of New South Wales announced that a Danish architect named Jørn Utzon had won its competition for a national opera house. The estimate was seven million Australian pounds. The building would be finished, the state said, by Australia Day 1963. It opened in October 1973, ten years late, at a final cost of a hundred and two million dollars. Measured against the number that was used to get the project approved, the Sydney Opera House came in roughly fourteen times over budget — a cost overrun of about 1,357%.1,2

The reasons are not mysterious, and they are worth stating plainly, because they will recur in every case in this article. Construction on the podium began in 1959, two years before the structural engineers had worked out how to build the roof. The client wanted spades in the ground before the next election, and the design was not finished, so the design was finished while the concrete was setting. The shells that make the building famous went through at least twelve schemes before Utzon and Ove Arup arrived at the spherical geometry that could actually be cast. The programme was rebuilt from scratch several times. In 1966, having been starved of fee payments by a new state government that had decided the project was a scandal, Utzon resigned and left the country. He never returned to see it finished.2,3

Fifty years on, the Opera House is on the World Heritage list. It is on the coins. It is the reason a certain number of people go to Australia at all. If you asked the citizens of New South Wales today whether they would take the deal — ten years, a hundred million, one ruined career, and the most recognisable building of the twentieth century — almost all of them would take it without blinking. So it is possible to argue that the Opera House was not a failure at all. That is a real argument and we will come back to it.

But notice what the argument concedes. It does not say the estimate was right. It says the estimate did not matter. And that is a different claim, and a much more dangerous one, because the seven-million-pound figure was not a private guess. It was a public promise, made to secure a decision, and it was wrong by a factor of fourteen. Somewhere between the promise and the building, an enormous quantity of money and time appeared from nowhere, and a man's professional life disappeared into the gap.

The question is not why this project went wrong. The question is why almost all of them do.

That is the subject of this essay. Not the Sydney Opera House, which is merely the most photogenic example, but the phenomenon it belongs to: the near-universal tendency of planned work to cost more and take longer than the people who planned it said it would. Software releases, kitchen renovations, particle accelerators, tram lines, tax systems, aircraft carriers, video games, PhD theses. The domains have nothing technical in common. The pattern is identical.

And it is a genuine pattern, not a selection of horror stories. If overruns were random errors — honest noise around an honest guess — roughly half of all projects would come in under budget. They do not. The distribution is not symmetric. Something is pushing systematically in one direction, and it has been pushing in that direction for as long as anybody has been keeping records.

What follows is an attempt to say what that something is. It turns out to be four somethings, stacked on top of one another: a mind that cannot forecast, a politics that does not want it to, a mathematics that guarantees slippage even when the first two are cured, and an organisational machinery that converts all of it into delay. Each layer is sufficient on its own to wreck a schedule. In most real projects, all four are running at once.

Part One The base rates

What the numbers actually say

Before diagnosing the disease, it is worth establishing that there is one. The evidence is unusually good, unusually consistent, and unusually old.

The largest body of evidence comes from Bent Flyvbjerg, who has spent three decades assembling a database of large projects at Oxford and elsewhere. It now runs to more than sixteen thousand projects across some twenty-odd sectors and more than a hundred countries, and for each one it records the budget approved at the moment of the decision to build against the outcome that was eventually delivered. The headline results are these. Just under half of the projects — 47.9% — came in on budget. Only 8.5% came in on budget and on time. And only 0.5% came in on budget, on time, and delivered the benefits that were promised when the money was voted.1

Read that last number again. One project in two hundred does what it said it would do, for the money it said it would cost, by the date it said it would be finished. Flyvbjerg calls the pattern the iron law of megaprojects: over budget, over time, under benefits, over and over again.4 The phrasing is deliberately fatalistic, and it is meant to be provocative, but the arithmetic behind it is dull and solid.

These are not the only numbers, and they are not the first. In 1987, Peter Morris and George Hough reviewed the record of major projects across sectors and reached the same conclusion by hand: overruns were not aberrations but the standard case, routinely in the range of 40% to 200%.5 In 2002, Flyvbjerg and colleagues examined 258 transport infrastructure projects worth some $90 billion across twenty countries and five decades. Nine in ten had cost overruns. Rail was worst, at an average of 45%; bridges and tunnels came in at 34%; roads at 20%.6 Most striking was a negative finding: estimation accuracy had not improved in the seventy years for which data existed. Seventy years of computers, of project management as a profession, of critical path analysis and earned value and risk registers, and the errors were exactly as large at the end as at the beginning.6,7

Nor is the problem confined to concrete. In information technology it is, if anything, worse. A McKinsey study conducted with the University of Oxford looked at 5,400 large IT projects, each with a budget above $15 million, and found average cost overruns of 45% and schedule overruns of 7%, while delivering 56% less value than had been predicted. One large IT project in six ran so far out of control that it threatened the existence of the company that commissioned it.8,9

The report everyone quotes, and why you should be careful with it

Any discussion of project failure eventually collides with the Standish Group's CHAOS Report. The 1994 edition reported that only 16.2% of software projects were delivered on time and on budget; that 31.1% were cancelled before completion; and that the 52.7% in the middle cost, on average, 189% of their original estimate.10 Those figures have been repeated for thirty years. The 2015 edition put success at 29%, challenged at 52%, and outright failure at 19%, which is where the widely quoted “only about 30% of projects succeed” comes from.11

The figures are probably too pessimistic, and it matters that we say so. Magne Jørgensen and Kjetil Moløkken-Østvold went through the 1994 methodology and found that the 189% overrun figure is far out of line with every comparable survey; other reviews of software effort estimation put the typical overrun at 30% to 40%, not 189%. They concluded that the Standish sampling was probably skewed towards failing projects, and that respondents were being asked, in effect, to nominate their disasters.12,13 Others have gone further, arguing that Standish's definitions of success and failure are unstable across editions and that the resulting time series is close to meaningless.14 A separate, independently sampled study of 412 IT projects found something less lurid: most projects landed reasonably close to budget and schedule, and the disasters were concentrated in the large and the volatile.15

Why the correction does not save the argument

Suppose the sceptics are entirely right and the true average software overrun is 35% rather than 189%. That is still a systematic, one-directional error of about a third, on a class of work where the estimates are made by the people who will do the work, using tools built for the purpose, in an industry that has been trying to fix this since 1968. The critique changes the size of the number. It does not change its sign. Every serious dataset, from every discipline, points the same way.

The tail is the story

Averages are, in this field, actively misleading. Project outcomes are not normally distributed. They are fat-tailed — in some sectors, so fat that the theoretical mean does not converge at all. Flyvbjerg and colleagues examined 5,392 IT projects and found that cost overrun follows a power law: 18% of IT projects overrun by more than 50%, and within that group the average overrun is 447%.16 An earlier study of the same phenomenon put it in more visceral terms: one IT project in six is a black swan, with an average cost overrun of 200% and a schedule overrun of almost 70%.9

The Olympic Games are the extreme case, and the cleanest natural experiment we have. Every single Games since 1960 has run over budget — a 100% failure rate, which no other project type achieves — at an average cost overrun of 172% in real terms. Montreal 1976 overran by 720% and took the city thirty years to pay off. The distribution of Olympic costs, Flyvbjerg's team showed, is a power law with, formally, infinite mean and infinite variance: not a distribution you can plan against, only one you can be destroyed by. They call the phenomenon regression to the tail. It is only a matter of time before a new record overrun is set, and then another.17

Large dams tell the same story from a different sector: three out of four go over budget, with actual costs on average 96% above estimate and schedules 44% longer than planned.18 Electricity infrastructure, examined across 401 projects in 57 countries, ranks in the order you would expect if repetition were the governing variable: nuclear power overruns by 117% on average, hydroelectric by 71%, thermal plant by 13%, wind by 8%, and solar by 1%.19

And the tails are not merely statistical curiosities. Nassim Taleb's argument about domains where the extreme observation dominates the average applies with unusual force here: in a fat-tailed sector, the mean overrun tells you almost nothing about your exposure, because your exposure is set by the observation you have not seen yet.20

The third number nobody reports

Cost and schedule are the two failures that make headlines. The third — benefits — is the one that actually determines whether the project was worth doing, and it is systematically worse than either.

Flyvbjerg's work on demand forecasting found that for rail projects, passenger forecasts were overestimated in nine cases out of ten, with an average overestimate of 106%: the typical new railway carried roughly half the passengers its business case promised. Road traffic forecasts were less biased but wildly imprecise, wrong by more than 20% in half of all cases.21,22 Put the two errors together and the picture is bleak: the costs come in roughly double, and the benefits come in roughly half, which means the benefit–cost ratio that justified the decision was wrong by a factor of about four.

The aggregate waste is difficult to measure and certainly enormous. The Project Management Institute's global survey work has for years put the share of investment lost to poor project performance at somewhere around one dollar in ten.23 For a world that spends something in the region of a fifth of its economic output through projects, that is not a rounding error.

The type-by-type data is the single most useful thing in this section, so let us be explicit about what it implies. A solar farm is not intellectually easier than a nuclear station, but it is the same thing done many times, and a nuclear station is one thing done once. The difference between a 1% overrun and a 117% overrun is not competence. It is repetition.

Part Two 1896–2023   —   the tools improved; the numbers did not

A century of trying to fix it

Every generation has believed that the previous generation's failures were a technique problem, and has invented a technique. The techniques were real advances. None of them moved the numbers.

The first recognisable project schedule was drawn not by Henry Gantt but by a Polish engineer, Karol Adamiecki, who in 1896 developed what he called the harmonogram to co-ordinate work in the rolling mills of the Russian Empire. He published in Polish and Russian; nobody outside read it. Gantt's chart, developed independently in the United States around the First World War and popularised by Wallace Clark in 1922, became the standard visual grammar of scheduled work and remains so a century later.24,25

The next leap came in a single extraordinary eighteen months. In 1957, Morgan Walker and James Kelley at DuPont and Remington Rand formalised the Critical Path Method: model the work as a network of activities with dependencies, compute the longest chain through it, and you have both the minimum duration of the project and the list of tasks where delay is fatal.26 In 1958, the US Navy's Special Projects Office, working on the Polaris submarine-launched missile, produced PERT, which added probability: each activity got an optimistic, a most likely and a pessimistic estimate, and the arithmetic produced an expected duration with a variance attached.27 Polaris came in ahead of schedule. PERT was credited, and swept the world.

The Polaris myth

In 1972 the political scientist Harvey Sapolsky published a study of the Polaris programme based on interviews with the people who ran it. His conclusion was uncomfortable: PERT had almost nothing to do with the programme's success. The charts were maintained for external consumption; the engineers largely ignored them. What actually delivered Polaris was an unusual degree of managerial autonomy, a ruthless technical leadership, and a public-relations operation of genius — of which PERT itself was the crown jewel, a device for convincing Congress that the Navy had science on its side.28

Sapolsky's point was not that planning is useless. It was that the appearance of control is a political product with its own market, and that market will pay for charts whether or not the charts do anything. Stephen Lenfle and Christoph Loch later extended the argument: the great projects of the 1940s and 1950s — Manhattan, Atlas — were run with parallel trials, deliberate redundancy and constant redesign, and the profession then took exactly the wrong lesson from them, enshrining control and conformance to plan as the discipline's core value at the precise moment the evidence pointed to flexibility.29

The rest of the century followed the pattern. In 1967 the US Department of Defense issued the Cost/Schedule Control Systems Criteria, the origin of earned value management: a rigorous method for comparing what you have spent, what you planned to spend, and what you have actually accomplished, all in the same units.30 In 1968, at a NATO conference in Garmisch, a group of computing's founders coined the phrase software crisis to describe an industry that could not reliably deliver anything.31 In 1975 Fred Brooks published The Mythical Man-Month, drawn from the wreckage of IBM's OS/360, and gave the field its first law.32 In 1997 Eliyahu Goldratt published Critical Chain, arguing that the schedule was being destroyed by the behaviour of the humans inside it.33 In 2001 seventeen software practitioners met at a ski resort in Utah and wrote a manifesto that proposed, in effect, to stop making the promises altogether.34

Set the two columns side by side and the problem becomes visible. Critical path analysis tells you which tasks control the end date — given the durations you supplied. PERT gives you a probability distribution for the finish — given the three-point estimates you supplied. Earned value tells you, with beautiful precision, how far you have diverged from the baseline — the baseline you supplied. Every one of these instruments is a measuring device calibrated against a number that came from the very people whose optimism, or whose interests, are the thing being measured.

This is the central structural fact about project control, and almost everything else in this essay follows from it. We built a magnificent apparatus for detecting deviation from the plan, and almost nothing for detecting deviation of the plan from reality.

Terry Williams put the indictment in academic terms in 2005: the dominant discourse of project management — decomposition, control, conformance to baseline — is a body of theory that is structurally incapable of explaining the overruns it is supposed to prevent, because its founding assumption is that the baseline is sound.35 A method whose first axiom is the thing most likely to be false is not a method. It is a liturgy.

Part Three Honest error

The mind: why we cannot see our own future

The first layer is cognitive, and it is the one people find easiest to forgive, because everybody who has ever promised to be somewhere in ten minutes has done it.

The planning fallacy

The term is Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky's, from 1979: the tendency to make predictions about a task that are close to the best-case scenario, even when one has abundant experience that tasks of that type almost never go according to the best case.36 The crucial word is even. This is not ignorance. The planning fallacy is remarkable precisely because it survives contact with the evidence. People who know perfectly well that their last five projects ran long will confidently predict that this one will not.

The canonical demonstration is Roger Buehler, Dale Griffin and Michael Ross's 1994 study of psychology students finishing their honours theses. Asked when they would submit, students gave a mean estimate of 33.9 days. The actual mean was 55.5 days. Only about 30% finished within their own predicted time. The researchers then did the thing that makes the study famous: they asked the same students for a worst-case estimate — “if everything went as poorly as it possibly could” — and the students' catastrophic scenario still came in ahead of what actually happened, for roughly half of them.37 Their worst case was optimistic.

People's own worst-case estimates are, on average, beaten by reality.

Buehler, Griffin & Ross, 1994

The mechanism is now well characterised.38,39 When we forecast a task, we construct a plausible narrative of how it will go: first this, then that, then it is finished. Narrative construction is a forward-looking, scenario-building activity, and it has a fatal property. It has no natural slot for the unknown. You cannot include in your story the specific thing that will go wrong, because if you could name it, you would have already dealt with it. The result is that the mental simulation is a chain of successful steps, and its duration is the sum of best cases — while the real project's duration is the sum of actual cases, each of which has a long right tail.

It is worth noting the honest caveats. A large review by Torleif Halkjelsvik and Magne Jørgensen found that the effect is not universal and not uniform: for very short tasks people sometimes overestimate, and the magnitude of the bias depends heavily on how the question is asked.40 The planning fallacy is a robust tendency, not a law of physics. But in the domain that matters here — long, complex, novel work — it points reliably in one direction.

The inside view and the outside view

Kahneman's own account of how he learned this is the most useful story in the literature, and he has told it many times.39,41 In the 1970s he assembled a team to write a new curriculum for the Israeli Ministry of Education. About a year in, he asked the group to write down, privately, how long they thought the project would take. The estimates clustered around eighteen to thirty months. Then he turned to Seymour, the group's curriculum expert, and asked a different question: think of other teams like ours, that have done work like this. How long did they take?

Seymour went quiet, and then said something that should be printed on the wall of every project office: of the teams he could think of, a substantial fraction never finished at all, and of those that did, none took less than seven years. Asked whether the present team was better than the ones he was thinking of, he thought about it and said: no, we are somewhat below average. The team carried on anyway. The project took eight years, and by the time it was finished the Ministry had lost interest and the curriculum was never used.

The two questions Kahneman asked are the two views. The inside view builds a forecast from the details of this specific case: our plan, our people, our particular circumstances. The outside view ignores the details and asks what happened to the class of things this thing belongs to. The inside view feels like expertise and is nearly worthless as a forecast. The outside view feels like an insult and is nearly always right.

Uniqueness bias

The reason people resist the outside view is that they do not believe their project belongs to a reference class at all. Flyvbjerg calls this uniqueness bias: the conviction that this project is genuinely unlike the others, so their data cannot apply.1 It is almost always false, and it is self-serving in a specific way — it converts a discouraging base rate into an irrelevant one. The right response is not to argue about whether the project is special. It is to observe that every project in the reference class was also thought to be special, by people who were also experts, and the reference class is what happened to them anyway.

Anchoring, and the poison of the first number

Estimates do not form in a vacuum. They form in a room, usually with a number already floating in it.

Tversky and Kahneman's original anchoring experiments showed that people's numerical judgements are pulled towards whatever number they happened to see last, even when that number is manifestly arbitrary — even when it was generated in front of them by spinning a wheel.42 Jorge Aranda and Steve Easterbrook reproduced this precisely in software estimation. They gave professional developers an identical specification, varying only an offhand remark from a fictitious manager — “I think this will take about two months” versus “about twenty months” — and explicitly told some participants that the manager had no relevant expertise whatsoever. The estimates still moved dramatically toward the anchor.43 Jørgensen and Grimstad found the same effect with irrelevant information embedded in requirements documents, and could not train it away.44

The practical consequence is severe and widely ignored. The moment a sponsor says out loud what they hope the project will cost, every subsequent estimate in that organisation is contaminated. The engineers are not lying when they come back with a number close to the sponsor's; they have simply been anchored, and anchoring is invisible from the inside. This is why serious estimating practice insists that the cost of a thing be estimated by people who have not been told what it is supposed to cost.45

The illusion of progress

Two folk laws capture the rest of the cognitive picture better than any formal literature. The first is Hofstadter's Law, from Gödel, Escher, Bach: it always takes longer than you expect, even when you take into account Hofstadter's Law.46 The joke is a serious observation about recursion: applying a correction factor to an estimate does not escape the bias, because the corrected estimate is produced by the same mind that produced the original.

The second is the ninety-ninety rule, attributed to Tom Cargill of Bell Labs and preserved by Jon Bentley: the first 90% of the code accounts for the first 90% of the development time; the remaining 10% of the code accounts for the other 90% of the development time.47 Every engineer laughs at this, and every engineer has lived it. We will see in Part Five that it is not a joke at all but a precise description of a dynamic that can be modelled, and that the phenomenon has a name: the ninety per cent syndrome.48

Part Four Useful error

The politics: why the wrong number gets chosen

Optimism is an explanation that flatters everyone involved. It says the forecasters were sincere. Very often they were not, and the evidence for this is stronger than most practitioners realise.

In 1989 the transport planner Martin Wachs published a short and devastating paper in the Journal of the American Planning Association titled “When planners lie with numbers.” Wachs had interviewed consultants and public officials involved in producing ridership and cost forecasts for American rail transit projects. What he found was not error. It was a practice. Forecasters were routinely instructed, sometimes explicitly, to produce the numbers that would clear the federal funding thresholds. Analysts who produced honest numbers were replaced by analysts who did not.49,50

Thirteen years later, Flyvbjerg, Mette Skamris Holm and Søren Buhl gave a statistical form to the same suspicion in a paper whose title poses the question directly: Underestimating costs in public works projects — error or lie? Their argument runs as follows. If cost underestimation were the product of honest technical error, then over fifty years and hundreds of projects, we would expect two things: errors in both directions, and improvement over time as forecasters learned. Neither is observed. The errors are overwhelmingly in one direction, and their magnitude is stable across seven decades. That is not the signature of error. That is the signature of a process that is producing the number it is supposed to produce.6

Underestimation cannot be explained by error and is best explained by strategic misrepresentation: lying.

Flyvbjerg, Skamris Holm & Buhl, 2002

Why the lie is rational

It is important to see that the behaviour is not pathological. Within the incentive structure that actually exists, it is the correct move.

Consider a portfolio of competing proposals: five rail schemes, one budget. Each promoter produces a business case. The scheme that wins is the one with the most attractive combination of low cost and high benefit. Now suppose that all five promoters are honest, but their estimates carry random error. The scheme that wins will systematically be the one whose errors happened to run most favourably. Selection alone — with no dishonesty anywhere in the system — guarantees that approved projects overrun on average, because approval is conditional on having made an optimistic mistake. Jonas Eliasson and Mogens Fosgerau formalised exactly this and showed how much of the observed overrun it can explain without anybody lying at all.51

This is the same mathematics as the winner's curse in auctions, identified in 1971 by petroleum engineers analysing oil-lease bidding: when many bidders estimate the same uncertain value, the winner is not the best bidder but the one who most overestimated the prize.52,53 Public infrastructure is an auction in which the prize is permission to build, and the winning bid is the lowest credible estimate. Now add even a little strategic behaviour to that structure and the outcome is over-determined. A promoter who forecasts honestly loses to a promoter who does not, so honest promoters are selected out.

The construction market applies the same filter a second time, at procurement. In a competitive tender for an incompletely specified scope, the lowest bid tends to come from the contractor who has most underestimated the work — or who has correctly understood that the profit is not in the bid but in the variations, and has priced accordingly. Economists have a formal treatment of this: when a contract must be adapted after signature, competitive fixed-price bidding transfers the argument from the tender to the change-order process, where the contractor holds all the power.54

A dissenting view, taken seriously

Not everyone accepts the deception thesis. Peter Love and colleagues have argued that Flyvbjerg's framing over-attributes to malice what is better explained by an under-defined scope at the moment of estimation, by genuinely stochastic ground conditions, and by the routine misuse of early-stage estimates as budgets.55 They have a point, and it is not merely a technical one: it changes what you should do about the problem. If the cause is lying, you need accountability. If the cause is premature commitment, you need better front-end engineering. The honest position is that both mechanisms are real, they are not mutually exclusive, and they reinforce each other — a culture that rewards low numbers will also be a culture that commits before the scope is defined, because defining the scope would reveal the number.

The hiding hand, benevolent and otherwise

In 1967 the economist Albert Hirschman offered a defence of the whole business that has been quoted approvingly ever since. Reviewing development projects around the world, he proposed the principle of the hiding hand: because we systematically underestimate both the difficulties of a project and our own creativity in overcoming them, the two errors cancel, and our ignorance functions as a benign trick that gets us to attempt things we would otherwise have refused.56 It is a seductive idea. It says that the planning fallacy is a feature.

Flyvbjerg and Cass Sunstein tested it against data. If Hirschman were right, we would see projects where the underestimation of cost is matched or exceeded by an unexpected windfall of benefits. In Flyvbjerg's sample, this happened in a small minority of cases. The far more common pattern was the opposite: costs came in higher than forecast and benefits came in lower. They named it the principle of the malevolent hiding hand.57 Ignorance is occasionally your friend. It is much more often just ignorance.

Lock-in and the point of no return

Once a project is underway, a second political mechanism takes over from the first, and it is arguably more destructive: the sponsor's inability to stop.

The foundational study is Barry Staw's, from 1976, with the wonderful title “Knee-deep in the big muddy.” Staw gave business students a hypothetical firm and a failing division and asked them to allocate further funds. The finding, replicated many times since, was that people commit more resources to a course of action when they are personally responsible for the original decision and it is going badly — not less.58 Escalation of commitment is not simply throwing good money after bad; it is throwing good money after bad specifically because it was your bad money. Hal Arkes and Catherine Blumer gave the phenomenon its popular name in 1985 and demonstrated the sunk-cost effect in a dozen everyday settings.59 Joel Brockner's review established that the effect is driven substantially by self-justification: withdrawing means admitting the first decision was wrong.60

Richard Dawkins and Tamsin Carlisle, writing about animal behaviour in 1976, called it the Concorde fallacy, after the aircraft that both the British and French governments continued to fund long after it was clear the aircraft would never pay for itself, on the grounds that they had already spent so much.61 Concorde's development ran roughly eleven times over budget. Every extra pound spent made the next extra pound easier to justify.

In the project world this hardens into what Cantarelli, Flyvbjerg and colleagues call lock-in: the point at which a project has accumulated enough commitment — contracts signed, land bought, ministers photographed in hard hats, alternatives formally rejected — that cancellation becomes politically more expensive than completion at any price.62 The lock-in point typically arrives long before the true cost is known, and this is not an accident. A sponsor who wants a project to happen has an interest in reaching lock-in fast, precisely so that the cost discovery arrives too late to matter. Mark Keil's studies of runaway software projects document the same dynamic inside corporations, where the mechanism is a manager's reputation rather than a minister's.63

The watermelon report

None of this would survive contact with honest reporting, which is why honest reporting does not survive.

The relevant psychology is old. In 1970 Sidney Rosen and Abraham Tesser named the MUM effect: people are reluctant to transmit bad news, even when they bear no responsibility for it and even when transmitting it costs them nothing.64 Layer an organisational hierarchy on top of that reluctance and you get systematic attenuation. Andrew Snow and Mark Keil modelled software project status reporting and found two distinct failures compounding: the reporter often does not know the true status, and the reporter biases the status they do know in the optimistic direction. The two errors do not cancel; they stack.65

Keil and Daniel Robey documented the receiving end of the same pipe, and named it the deaf effect: even when someone does report the bad news, the recipient frequently declines to act on it, because acting would require an admission that the project is in trouble.66 The result is the phenomenon every practitioner knows by its nickname: the watermelon report. Green on the outside, red all the way through. Status is reported as amber at worst, all the way up the chain, until the week before the deadline, at which point it becomes red without ever having passed through any intermediate state.

Diane Vaughan's study of the Challenger disaster gives this its most careful treatment, under the name normalisation of deviance: an organisation that repeatedly accepts a small anomaly without consequence gradually reclassifies it as normal, until the accumulated normal anomalies are catastrophic.67 Every project that reports amber for eighteen months and then slips a year is doing exactly this with schedule variance.

Goodhart's law, applied to project reporting

Marilyn Strathern's compact formulation of Charles Goodhart's observation is the one worth remembering: when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure.68,69 Project reporting is a machine for turning measures into targets. The moment “percentage complete” determines whether a manager is having a good quarter, percentage complete stops describing the project and starts describing the manager. This is why projects are reported at 90% complete for months: 90% is the highest number you can report without triggering questions about delivery.

Part Five Inevitable error

The mathematics: why honest, competent plans still slip

Now assume everybody is telling the truth. Assume the estimates are unbiased, the sponsors are saints, and the reporting is candid. The project will still run late, and here is why.

The flaw of averages

Begin with a task that will take, on average, ten days. What does that sentence mean? It means that if you ran this task a thousand times, the mean duration would be ten days. It does not mean the task will take ten days. It does not even mean ten days is the most likely outcome. And it certainly does not mean there is a 50% chance of finishing in ten days — that would require a symmetric distribution, and task durations are not symmetric. A task cannot take less than zero days, but it can take five times as long as expected. Every duration distribution in project work is skewed right.

Sam Savage's name for the consequence is the flaw of averages: plans built by substituting the average value of every uncertain quantity are systematically wrong, and wrong in a predictable direction.70 The formal statement is Jensen's inequality — the average of a function is not the function of the average — and it bites hardest exactly where projects live: in networks of dependent tasks with skewed durations.

The merge point, where slack goes to die

Here is the single most important piece of arithmetic in project scheduling, and it is arithmetic a schoolchild can do.

Suppose a milestone depends on eight parallel workstreams. Each workstream is honestly estimated and each has a genuine 90% chance of hitting its date. What is the probability that the milestone is hit? The milestone requires all eight. If the streams are independent, the answer is 0.98, which is 0.43. Eight streams, each of which is 90% reliable, produce a milestone that is 43% reliable. With twenty streams — a modest number for a real programme — it is 12%.

This is merge bias, and it is why real projects are late even when their component estimates are good. Richard Schonberger demonstrated it in 1981 by hand-simulating PERT networks and showed that the standard PERT calculation, which computes the expected duration of the critical path and stops, systematically underestimates the true project duration precisely because it ignores the possibility that a non-critical path becomes critical.71 Salah Elmaghraby's later work formalised how badly the notion of “the” critical path breaks down under uncertainty: in a stochastic network, criticality is a probability, not a property, and a path that is nominally 20% slack can be the one that kills you.72

The asymmetry is what makes this vicious. When a workstream finishes early, the milestone does not move forward, because it is still waiting for the other seven. When a workstream finishes late, the milestone moves back by the full amount. Early finishes are absorbed. Late finishes are transmitted. A project is a machine for discarding good luck and accumulating bad.

The utilisation trap

The second mathematical guarantee comes from queueing theory, and it is the reason that the most reasonable-sounding management instruction in the world — make sure nobody is idle — is a schedule-destroying error.

When work arrives at a resource with variability, and the resource is busy, the work waits. The relationship between how busy the resource is and how long the work waits is not linear. In its simplest form, the expected wait is proportional to ρ / (1 − ρ), where ρ is utilisation.73,74 At 50% utilisation, work waits about as long as it takes to do. At 80%, four times as long. At 90%, nine times. At 95%, nineteen times. As utilisation approaches 100%, the wait approaches infinity — not metaphorically, but as a limit.

Donald Reinertsen's Principles of Product Development Flow is the definitive treatment of what this means for knowledge work, and his central point is an accounting one: the cost of idle capacity is visible, and the cost of the queue is not.75 A manager can see an engineer with nothing to do. Nobody can see the three weeks of delay that the fully-loaded engineer will inject into six other people's critical paths next month. So organisations optimise the thing they can see and destroy the thing they cannot.

Little's law supplies the corollary.76 The average time an item spends in a system equals the average number of items in the system divided by the throughput. If you want to reduce cycle time and you cannot increase throughput, the only lever left is to reduce the number of things in progress. Every organisation that responds to being late by starting more work in parallel is pressing the accelerator to stop the car.

Brooks's law, and the price of talking to each other

The most famous single sentence in project management is Fred Brooks's: adding manpower to a late software project makes it later.32 It is often quoted and less often understood. Brooks gave two reasons. The first is ramp-up: new people must be trained, and the training is done by the people who were already productive, so the immediate effect of adding staff is to reduce output. The second, and the one that scales, is communication.

A team of n people has n(n − 1) / 2 potential communication channels. Five people have ten. Twelve have sixty-six. Twenty-four have two hundred and seventy-six. The work grows linearly with headcount; the co-ordination cost grows quadratically. At some point — and it arrives sooner than anyone expects — the marginal person costs more in co-ordination than they contribute in output.

Melvin Conway's 1968 observation is the structural companion: organisations which design systems are constrained to produce designs which are copies of the communication structures of these organisations.77 If your project is split across four teams, your system will have four modules and three painful interfaces, and the schedule risk will live in the interfaces — because the interfaces are the places where nobody is in charge. Almost every catastrophic integration failure in this essay is, at bottom, an interface between two organisations that each believed the other one understood the contract.

The rework cycle: why progress is a lie

The final mathematical mechanism is the most under-appreciated, and it comes from system dynamics rather than from probability.

Kenneth Cooper spent years modelling large engineering programmes, initially in support of shipbuilding litigation, and formalised what he called the rework cycle.78,79 The insight is simple to state and horrible in its consequences. When a task is completed, some fraction of it is done correctly and some fraction is done wrong — and crucially, nobody knows which fraction is which. The defective work does not announce itself. It sits in the pile of “work done” and gets built upon. It surfaces later, usually at integration or test, and by then it has contaminated everything downstream of it.

David Ford and John Sterman gave the resulting pathology its name: the ninety per cent syndrome.48,80 A project sails to 90% complete on schedule, and then spends as long again getting to 100%, because the last 10% is where the accumulated rework finally becomes visible. This is not a failure of will and it is not sandbagging. It is the arithmetic of a system in which the measure of progress — tasks marked done — systematically overstates the quantity that matters, which is tasks that will still be done at the end.

Ward Cunningham's metaphor for the financial character of this stock is now standard, and it deserves to be understood precisely as he meant it: technical debt is not sloppy work, it is a deliberate decision to ship something you know to be provisional in order to learn faster — and, like any debt, it is a perfectly rational instrument right up to the moment you stop paying the interest.81 The projects in this essay did not take on debt. They took on debt, denied that they had, and then were surprised by the bailiff.

Ford and Sterman also identified the aggravating factor. Engineers frequently know that some of their output is provisional — that it depends on an assumption that has not been confirmed — but they do not report it, because reporting it looks like admitting to unfinished work. The rework is not merely undiscovered; it is partly concealed. And concurrent engineering, which was adopted precisely to compress schedules by starting downstream work before upstream work is finished, is a machine for maximising exactly this quantity.

Tight coupling and the shape of the failure

Charles Perrow's Normal Accidents was written about nuclear plants and air traffic, but its two axes describe project risk perfectly.82 Interactive complexity is the degree to which parts of the system can affect each other in ways nobody anticipated. Tight coupling is the degree to which there is no slack between them — no buffer, no substitute, no time to intervene. Systems that are both complex and tightly coupled will fail in ways that cannot be designed out, and the failures will look, in retrospect, like a series of small things nobody could have predicted.

A megaproject with a fixed opening date, a single integrated design, hundreds of interdependent contracts and no float is the definition of a complex, tightly coupled system. This is precisely why the Olympic Games have the worst overrun record of any project type on earth: the deadline cannot move, so every shock in the system must be absorbed by cost or by quality.17

Part Six Manufactured error

The machinery: how organisations turn risk into delay

The first three layers explain why the plan is wrong. This one explains how the organisation takes a wrong plan and makes it worse.

Scope creep, and its nastier cousin

Scope creep is the familiar one: the gradual, unbudgeted accretion of features. Each request is small. Each is reasonable. Each is delivered by someone who wants to be helpful and who does not have the standing to say no. And each one is priced at its marginal cost of implementation rather than its true cost, which includes the retesting, the documentation, the interface it perturbs, and the two weeks of the architect's attention that will not now be spent on the thing that was actually on the critical path. Scope creep is not the sum of the features added. It is the sum of the features added plus the second-order damage, and the second-order damage is invisible at the moment of approval.

The more destructive relative is what practitioners have taken to calling scope grope: the condition in which the requirements were never really defined in the first place. The project is authorised on a scope that is a page of aspirations. Nobody discovers what the sponsor actually wanted until they see the first deliverable and recoil from it. What follows is not creep — it is the retrospective invention of the requirement, and everything built to the old assumption is now waste.

The industrial evidence on this is unambiguous. Edward Merrow's analysis at Independent Project Analysis, drawn from a proprietary database of over three hundred industrial megaprojects, found that the single most reliable predictor of catastrophe was the quality of front-end loading: the depth of definition and engineering completed before the project was sanctioned. Projects that were sanctioned with poor front-end definition failed at extraordinary rates. Merrow's overall finding is worth stating without softening: 65% of industrial megaprojects above $1 billion fail to meet their business objectives.83

Merrow's second finding is the one that ought to be tattooed on sponsors: schedule pressure is the leading cause of cost overrun. Not the consequence — the cause. Projects that are driven to an aggressive date skip front-end engineering, overlap phases that should be sequential, order long-lead equipment against unfinished designs, and pay for all of it several times over.83 The desire to save six months at the front reliably costs two years at the back.

Software has an equivalent, and it was quantified more than forty years ago. Barry Boehm's cost models identified what he called the impossible region: below roughly 75% of the nominal schedule for a given quantity of work, no amount of additional staff will deliver the project, because the co-ordination and rework costs rise faster than the added capacity.84 Compression beyond that point does not produce a fast project. It produces a failed one, and the interesting thing is that the failure does not announce itself at the moment of compression. It announces itself eighteen months later, in test.

Parkinson's law and student syndrome: the disappearance of float

Even when a schedule contains slack, the slack does not survive.

C. Northcote Parkinson published his law in The Economist in 1955 as a piece of civil-service satire, and it has outlived every serious management theory of its decade: work expands so as to fill the time available for its completion.85 Give a task four weeks and it will take four weeks, whether or not it needed four weeks. The engineer who finishes early will polish, gold-plate, or simply not report the early finish — because reporting it invites the observation that the estimate was padded, and next time the padding will be removed.

Eliyahu Goldratt paired this with the behaviour he named student syndrome: given a deadline with slack, people start at the last responsible moment.33 The float that was inserted at the front of the task to protect against surprises is consumed before any surprise occurs, so that when the surprise arrives it lands on a task with no protection left. The buffer was real. It was simply spent on procrastination rather than on risk.

Put the two together with merge bias and you have a complete account of how a schedule with generous, honest contingency in every task produces a project with none. Every task carries its own private buffer. Parkinson and student syndrome consume the buffers regardless of whether the risk they were protecting against materialised. The tasks finish on time or late, never early. And at each merge point, the late ones are transmitted and the on-time ones are absorbed.

Safety hidden inside individual tasks is not safety. It is a rounding error that has been distributed until it cannot be used.

The core insight of critical chain

Goldratt's remedy — strip the padding out of every individual task, aggregate it into one visible buffer at the end of the chain, and manage the project by watching the rate at which the buffer is consumed — is elegant and mathematically well-founded: aggregated variances add as the square root, so a single pooled buffer can be smaller than the sum of the individual ones and still provide more protection. It should be said that the empirical record for critical chain in practice is more mixed than its advocates claim, and researchers have pointed out that the method's treatment of resource contention is less rigorous than its rhetoric suggests.86 But the diagnosis is correct even where the prescription is contested.

The multitasking tax

Organisations respond to schedule pressure by running more projects at once, because each individual project appears to be moving too slowly and the obvious inference is that people are underemployed. The inference is exactly backwards.

Gerald Weinberg's estimate — rough, but it has held up in spirit — is that each additional concurrent project costs roughly 20% of an individual's available time in context-switching overhead. Two projects: 40% each, 20% lost. Three: 20% each, 40% lost. By five concurrent projects, a majority of the person's time is being spent on the transitions.87 Combine this with Little's law and you have the fundamental pathology of the modern portfolio: an organisation that starts everything finishes nothing, and then concludes that it needs to start more things.

The death march

The last piece of machinery is cultural, and it is the one that converts a recoverable problem into an unrecoverable one.

Edward Yourdon coined death march for the project whose schedule, budget or staffing is so far below what the work requires that the shortfall is not a risk but a certainty — and where everybody involved knows it.88 The defining feature is not the overtime. It is the shared, unspoken knowledge that the plan is fiction, combined with the impossibility of saying so.

The dynamics are grimly predictable, and they close the loop with everything in Part Four. A date is set by people who will not do the work. The people who will do the work are told to make it happen. Raising a concern is coded as negativity, or as a lack of commitment, or as an admission of inadequacy. So concerns stop being raised. Status goes green. The engineers work the weekends, and the good ones leave, which removes exactly the people whose institutional knowledge was the only remaining defence. And because nobody said anything, the slip is not discovered incrementally at a manageable size — it is discovered all at once, six weeks before the launch, at its full and unrecoverable magnitude.

Unknown unknowns are not an excuse, but they are real

Some of what goes wrong genuinely could not have been foreseen: a supplier's parent company collapses, a pandemic closes the borders, a regulator reinterprets a rule. Known risks can be quantified and priced. Unknown ones, by construction, cannot.

But two things must be said. First, the correct response to unquantifiable risk is not to ignore it — it is to hold a management reserve sized from the historical record of how badly projects of this class have been surprised, which is precisely what reference class forecasting does. Second, and more uncomfortably: most of what gets classified as an unknown unknown after the fact was a known unknown that nobody wrote down, because writing it down would have raised the estimate. The category is a genuine feature of the world and it is also, in practice, the most heavily abused excuse in the profession.

The early-warning paradox

Here is the cruellest fact in this entire literature, and it belongs at the end of this section because it indicts the machinery rather than the plan.

Analysis of large defence acquisition contracts using earned value data established something remarkable about the cost performance index — the ratio of work accomplished to money spent. Once a project passes roughly 15% to 20% completion, the cumulative index becomes stable: it does not, in practice, recover. Whatever cost efficiency the project has achieved by the one-fifth mark is, to a good approximation, the cost efficiency it will have at the end. If anything it deteriorates.89,30,90

Read together with the escalation literature, this is the whole tragedy in one picture. The information required to stop a doomed project reliably exists before the project has spent a quarter of its money. It is not acted upon. Instead the baseline is restated — a manoeuvre that makes the variance disappear without changing a single thing about the physical work — and the project proceeds with a new plan that is wrong in exactly the same way as the old one.

Part Seven Six autopsies and four survivors

Case files

Theory is cheap. What follows are six projects in which the mechanisms described above can be watched, in sequence, doing their work — and then four in which they did not.

Denver International Airport: the baggage system

Denver — 1989–1995

Denver's new airport was to have an automated baggage system of unprecedented ambition: destination-coded vehicles running on twenty-two miles of track, sorting bags for the entire airport with no human handling. It was conceived late — the airport was already under construction when the decision was taken to build it — and it was contracted to BAE Automated Systems on a schedule that BAE itself had said was impossible for a system of that scale.

The engineering failure modes were textbook: destination-coded vehicles derailing, bags being flung from carts, jams cascading through a system with no slack. But the project failure modes were more interesting. The city acted as its own integrator without the technical capacity to do so. Design changes were still being issued after fabrication had begun. The system was tightly coupled to the airport's opening date, which had been announced, and which the mayor could not move without political cost. And the airlines — the customers — changed their requirements repeatedly after the contract was signed.91,92

The airport's opening slipped by sixteen months. The delay cost the city roughly $1.1 million a day in interest and standing costs. When the airport finally opened in February 1995, it did so with a conventional, manual baggage system for most of its operation. The automated system limped along for United Airlines alone until 2005, when it was abandoned. Ramon Montealegre and Mark Keil used Denver as the definitive case study of de-escalation: the extraordinary organisational effort required to admit that a project must be stopped.93

Berlin Brandenburg Airport

Berlin — 1991–2020

Planning for a single airport to serve reunified Berlin began in 1992. Construction began in 2006 against a budget of roughly €2.8 billion and an opening date of October 2011. The airport opened on 31 October 2020: nine years late, after six abandoned opening dates, at a cost variously reported between €6 billion and €7 billion.94,95

The proximate cause of the most famous failure — the cancellation of the June 2012 opening, four weeks before the ceremony, with invitations already sent — was the fire safety system. Rather than extracting smoke upwards through the roof, as every other large terminal does, BER's designers chose to draw it downwards through a network of underground ducts. This was novel, it was bespoke, and it never passed certification as designed. But the fire system was a symptom.

The structural causes were governance. The airport company chose to act as its own general contractor rather than appoint one, so co-ordination across hundreds of subcontractors landed on an owner without the capability to do it. The supervisory board consisted largely of politicians — the Mayor of Berlin, the Minister-President of Brandenburg, federal ministers — who lacked the technical standing to evaluate the warnings they were being given, and whose instinct on receiving bad news was to wait. Capacity targets were revised upward after construction had started, forcing rebuilds of finished sections. And a manager was later arrested on bribery charges connected to the fire-suppression contractor, which subsequently went bankrupt.94

The Central Artery / Tunnel Project — the “Big Dig”

Boston — 1982–2007

Boston's decision to bury its elevated central artery and drive a tunnel to Logan Airport was, in engineering terms, a triumph: it was executed under a live city, under an operating harbour, over an operating subway, without shutting down the road it was replacing. It is also the most expensive highway project in American history.

The 1982 estimate was $2.6 billion. The project was substantially complete in 2007 at roughly $14.8 billion in nominal terms; with the financing costs of the bonds issued to pay for it, the burden on the Commonwealth of Massachusetts runs past $20 billion and continues into the 2030s.96 In real terms Flyvbjerg's database records the overrun at about 220%.1

Virginia Greiman, who served as deputy counsel to the project, catalogued the causes from inside: an estimate made at a stage of definition that could not possibly support it; a scope that grew through more than a decade of environmental mitigation commitments negotiated with communities as the price of consent; ground conditions in reclaimed harbour fill that were understood only as they were excavated; and a delivery structure in which the design consultant was also the construction manager, an arrangement that removed the check that was supposed to catch precisely these problems.96

The National Programme for IT in the NHS

England — 2002–2011

Announced in 2002 after a meeting in Downing Street that lasted less than an hour, the NPfIT was to deliver a single electronic care record for every patient in England, with national infrastructure and regional systems procured centrally from a small number of very large suppliers. It was, at the time, the largest civilian IT programme in the world.

It was dismantled in 2011. The National Audit Office's assessments and the Public Accounts Committee's subsequent report describe a programme whose costs approached — and on some accountings exceeded — £10 billion against an original figure of £6.2 billion, and which never delivered the detailed care record system that was its central purpose.97,98

The failures are almost a checklist of this essay. The scope was set centrally by people who would not use the system, with essentially no clinical engagement, which meant the requirements were not wrong so much as absent — scope grope at national scale. The contracts were vast, monolithic and long, which maximised lock-in and destroyed the option to change course cheaply. Suppliers who fell behind could not be replaced, because there was nobody else. And the political cost of admitting failure rose year by year with the sunk cost, so the programme was restructured repeatedly rather than stopped.

The Airbus A380 wiring harness

Toulouse and Hamburg — 2005–2007

The A380 is the purest available demonstration of Conway's law. Each aircraft contains something on the order of 500 kilometres of wiring, 100,000 wires and 40,000 connectors, routed through a fuselage where every millimetre is contested. The design of the harnesses was distributed across Airbus's national partner sites, in the political settlement that made the company possible.

The German and Spanish sites were using CATIA version 4. Toulouse and the British sites had moved to version 5. The two versions handled three-dimensional models differently, and the digital mock-ups did not reconcile. The mismatch was not discovered on a screen. It was discovered when the harnesses, manufactured to the German models, were brought to the assembly line in Toulouse and found not to fit.99

Thousands of harnesses had to be redesigned and remanufactured by hand. Deliveries slipped by roughly two years. EADS told the market that the delays would cost it several billion euros of operating profit over the following four years. The engineering error was, in the end, trivial — a version mismatch in a CAD tool. The organisational error was that the interface between two design authorities had no owner, and each side assumed the other had checked.

The James Webb Space Telescope

Greenbelt, Maryland — 1996–2021

Webb belongs here for a different reason than the others: it is the case that shows why the word “overrun” needs handling with care.

Early in its life the telescope was discussed in terms of a cost around $1 billion and a launch around 2010. It launched on Christmas Day 2021 having cost, on NASA's own accounting, roughly $9 to $10 billion. Depending on which baseline you choose, that is an overrun of 76%, or 450%, or 900%.

The 2010 Independent Comprehensive Review Panel — the Casani report — examined the programme and reached a conclusion that ought to be far better known. The technical work was, in the main, being executed competently. The problem was that the budget and schedule baseline had never been sound: it had been constructed without adequate reserves, in an environment where a realistic number would have caused the project to be cancelled, and the annual funding profile made an efficient build physically impossible.100 The GAO's later reviews traced the remaining slips to integration and test — the phase where, as Cooper's rework cycle predicts, the accumulated undiscovered defects of a decade become visible all at once.101

And the telescope works. It is doing science that nothing else can do, and will for twenty years. Which raises the question the Opera House also raises, and which no accounting framework can answer: what exactly was the failure — the project, or the promise?

Six more, in one paragraph each

Space forbids a full treatment of the rest of the field, but the reader should know the shape of it. The Scottish Parliament at Holyrood was presented to the public in 1997 at £10–40 million and completed in 2004 at £414 million; Lord Fraser's inquiry found a project in which nobody could establish who had approved what, and in which the cost estimate had been treated as a political fact rather than an engineering one.102 Crossrail, London's Elizabeth line, was to open in December 2018 for £14.8 billion; it opened in May 2022 for something near £19 billion, and the National Audit Office's post-mortem identified a governance culture in which the delivery organisation reported confidence in a date that its own internal data could not support — the watermelon report, in a British accent.103 Vogtle units 3 and 4, the only new American nuclear reactors in a generation, were expected to cost $14 billion and enter service in 2016 and 2017; they cost roughly $35 billion and entered service in 2023 and 2024.104

The nuclear cases are worth one further observation, because they demolish a comforting assumption. One might expect that building the fortieth reactor would be cheaper than building the first. Arnulf Grubler's study of the French programme — the most standardised, most centrally directed nuclear build in history, and therefore the best case anyone could construct for the learning curve — found the opposite. Real construction costs rose over the course of the programme. He called it negative learning by doing.105 Later work assembling construction costs across the global fleet found the escalation was not uniquely French, though it was not universal either.106 Scale, in this sector, does not deliver the learning it promises, because each unit is enough of a one-off that the learning does not transfer.

The survivors

It would be dishonest to present the iron law as inescapable, because it is not. A small number of very large projects have come in on time and on budget, and they have enough in common to be instructive.

Four projects that did not overrun

The control group

The Empire State Building (1930–31) rose in roughly thirteen months, ahead of its schedule and under its budget of about $50 million, finishing near $41 million. It was designed to be built: a repeated structural bay, a standardised floor, a construction sequence worked out in advance in ferocious detail, and a design frozen before steel was ordered. Depression-era deflation helped. Planning helped more.107,1

Hoover Dam (1931–36) was delivered more than two years ahead of schedule, by a consortium that was contractually punished for lateness and rewarded for speed, on a design that had been settled before the contract was let.108

Heathrow Terminal 5 (2002–08) opened on its planned day, at its planned cost of £4.3 billion, after the longest public inquiry in British history. The mechanism was contractual. Under the “T5 Agreement”, the client — BAA — held all of the risk itself and paid its suppliers their costs plus an agreed profit, explicitly removing the incentive for contractors to bid low and litigate later. Integrated teams were co-located, problems were surfaced rather than concealed, and the whole terminal was rehearsed as a system before it was built.109

The Guggenheim Bilbao (1993–97) opened on time and on budget, because Frank Gehry's office used aerospace software to build the entire building in a computer, and to build physical models of every difficult junction, before anybody poured concrete. The iteration happened where iteration is cheap.1

Common factorsA design frozen before delivery began. Repetition or rehearsal wherever it was available. A contract that did not reward the lowest liar. And an execution phase kept as short as possible, because time on site is exposure, and exposure is where the risk lives.

The uncomfortable footnote to Terminal 5

Terminal 5 opened on time and on budget, and on its opening day the baggage system collapsed. Staff could not park, could not get through security, and could not log in. Some 500 flights were cancelled and tens of thousands of bags went astray over the following ten days.110

The construction project had succeeded. The operational readiness of the organisation that would run the building had not been tested at full scale, because it was owned by a different part of the business. This is the last word on the iron triangle: on time and on budget is not the same thing as successful, and any measure that treats them as identical will eventually be gamed.111

Part Eight Diagnostics

The risk register

Before you can judge whether a project is in trouble, you have to know what kind of number you were given in the first place.

What kind of number is that?

AACE International, the professional body for cost engineers, maintains a classification of cost estimates that every journalist, every minister and most executives ought to be required to read before they are allowed to quote a figure. It sorts estimates into five classes by the maturity of the underlying project definition, and it states the accuracy range that is expected of each.112

A Class 5 estimate — made when between zero and two per cent of the project has been defined — carries a typical accuracy range of about −50% to +100%. That is not a failure of the estimator. That is the correct, professional, competently produced accuracy of a number made when there is nothing to estimate from. It is only at Class 1, when the project is essentially designed, that the range narrows to something like −10% to +15%.

Now consider what actually happens. The number that enters the public record — the number in the press release, the number in the manifesto, the number that will be used forever afterwards to compute the “overrun” — is generated at Class 5 or Class 4, because that is when the decision to proceed is taken. The moment it is uttered it stops being an estimate with a range and becomes a commitment with a point value. The range is discarded. The plus-one-hundred-per-cent that the profession's own standard says is a normal outcome is now, in the public conversation, a scandal.

Steve McConnell puts the distinction as clearly as anyone. An estimate is an unbiased analytical assessment of a probable outcome. A target is a statement of a desirable business objective. A commitment is a promise to deliver defined functionality at a specific level of quality by a certain date. All three are legitimate. Confusing them is the single most common failure in the industry, and organisations do it because a target is easier to defend than a distribution.45

Half the overruns in the world are not overruns at all. They are Class 5 estimates that were treated as Class 1 promises.

This does not exonerate anybody. The projects in Part Seven really did cost far more than they should have, and the cost of the Big Dig was not merely an artefact of accounting. But it does mean that “the project overran” and “the project was badly run” are two different propositions, and conflating them prevents the diagnosis that would actually help. If your project's problem is that it was baselined too early, hiring a better project manager will not fix it.

What actually predicts trouble

Aggregating the empirical literature — Merrow's industrial database, Flyvbjerg's megaproject database, the systematic review by Denicol, Davies and Krystallis, and the software estimation research — produces a reasonably stable list of the structural features that predict overrun.83,113,114,15 It is worth being explicit that these are properties of the project, not of the team. They are visible before a single person is hired.

Aaron Shenhar and Dov Dvir's contribution is to insist that these properties should determine the management approach rather than being managed around: they classify projects along axes of novelty, technology, complexity and pace, and argue that applying a single project-management doctrine across all four quadrants is the root error of the discipline.115 A repeat build of a known design and a first-of-a-kind development programme are not the same activity, and the fact that both are called “projects” has done a great deal of damage.

Bespoke, one-off design Nothing is repeated, so nothing is learned. Fat tails. Every element is “tailored to our needs.” Modularise. Build the biggest thing you can build many times.
Immovable deadline Schedule is removed as a control variable, so all variance is absorbed by cost and quality. An opening ceremony, an election, a regulatory cliff. Buy schedule float with money early, when it is cheap.
Long duration Exposure. Technology, politics, prices and personnel all change underneath the plan. Delivery measured in presidential terms. Compress delivery. Plan slowly, build fast.
Weak front-end definition The estimate is Class 5 and the commitment is Class 1. Scope grope follows. Sanction before the design is complete. Front-end loading. Spend real money before you spend enormous money.
Many interfaces Co-ordination cost grows quadratically; interfaces have no owner. An organisation chart with more boxes than the system has modules. Own the interfaces explicitly. Integrate early and continuously.
Client without capability Nobody on the buying side can evaluate what the seller says. The integrator role is unfilled, or filled by the designer. Build an intelligent client. It is the cheapest money you will ever spend.
Fixed price on undefined scope Winner's curse at tender; the profit migrates to change orders. The lowest bid is far below the others. Target cost with pain/gain share; score bids on realism.
Political sponsorship Strategic misrepresentation at the front, lock-in at the back. The benefit-cost ratio lands just above the funding threshold. Independent assurance; make forecasters accountable for forecasts.
Subsurface or brownfield work Genuinely irreducible uncertainty in ground conditions. “We will find out when we dig.” Investigate early; price the range, not the mean; hold real reserve.
100% resource loading Queueing. Utilisation near 1 makes wait times explode. Every named person is on the plan every week. Hold utilisation near 80%. Limit work in progress.
Concurrent phases Downstream work built on unstable upstream assumptions maximises undiscovered rework. Detailed design starting before concept freeze. Overlap only where the upstream output is genuinely stable.
Status reported by the delivery chain MUM effect, deaf effect, watermelon reporting. Amber for eighteen months, then red. An assurance function that does not report to the project.
Large size, in absolute terms Every mechanism above scales with size, and several scale superlinearly. The word “mega”. Break it into projects that can fail independently.

Part Nine Countermeasures

What actually works

Nothing in this section removes uncertainty. Everything in it is designed to stop uncertainty from being silently converted into a promise.

1. Take the outside view, and put a number on it

The single highest-value intervention available is also the oldest, and it descends directly from Kahneman and Tversky's 1979 paper. It is called reference class forecasting, and it has three steps.36,116

  • Identify a reference class of past projects that are similar to yours in the relevant respects — not in their subject matter, but in their structural risk profile.
  • Establish the distribution of outcomes for that class: not the average overrun, but the whole distribution, including the tail.
  • Position your project within it, and adjust your own estimate to the distribution — not the other way round.

The method has been operational for two decades. The UK Department for Transport adopted it in 2004, with guidance written by Flyvbjerg and COWI, and the American Planning Association endorsed it the following year.117 Its power comes from the fact that it does not require the forecaster to be honest, or unbiased, or even competent. It replaces judgement with data at exactly the point where judgement is known to fail.

2. Uplift the estimate before anyone argues about it

The blunt-instrument version of the outside view is an optimism bias uplift: an empirically derived percentage that is added to the estimate, by rule, before any project-specific risk is even considered. The UK Treasury's Green Book mandates this for all central government business cases, using values derived from Mott MacDonald's 2002 review of large public procurements.118,119

Practitioners hate these uplifts, and their objection is always the same: but our project is different. That objection is the uniqueness bias, arriving on schedule, and the uplift exists precisely because the objection is always made and is almost never right.

3. Budget a distribution, not a number

Once you accept that a cost estimate is a distribution, the question “what is the budget?” becomes “which percentile are we funding?” Mature practice, in Australian and British infrastructure especially, separates the two: the delivery team is funded at the P50 — the median outcome, which they have a fifty-fifty chance of hitting — while the sponsor holds a contingency, centrally and visibly, up to the P80. The team is not given the contingency, because Parkinson's law will consume it. The sponsor does not pretend the contingency is unnecessary, because it will be needed.

Getting the distribution right requires Monte Carlo simulation of the schedule network rather than PERT's closed-form approximation, because — as we saw in Part Five — the closed form ignores merge bias and therefore lies in a specific, predictable direction.71,72 It also requires that the input distributions come from the reference class rather than from the estimators, or you have merely simulated the planning fallacy with great precision.

4. Run a pre-mortem

Gary Klein's technique is almost embarrassingly cheap and remarkably effective.120 Before the plan is approved, gather the team and say: it is eighteen months from now. The project has failed, catastrophically and expensively. Write down why.

The device works because of a specific quirk of cognition. Asking “what could go wrong?” invites people to defend the plan; asking people to explain a failure that has, in the fiction, already happened, licenses them to say what they actually think. Klein reports that prospective hindsight of this kind substantially increases the number and specificity of the risks identified. It is also, in practice, the only mechanism most organisations possess for extracting the objection that the junior engineer has been afraid to raise for three months.

5. Modularise: build the biggest thing you can build many times

This is the deepest structural lesson in the data, and it is the one with the largest effect size. Look again at Figure 06. Solar overruns by 1%; nuclear by 117%. Wind by 8%; hydroelectric dams by 71%. The difference is not difficulty. It is that a solar farm is one small thing repeated ten thousand times, and each repetition is a rehearsal that makes the next one cheaper and more predictable, while a dam is one enormous thing done exactly once, in a place where nothing like it has been done before.19,1,121

The prescription that follows is unglamorous and unpopular: find the smallest unit of your project that delivers value, make it repeatable, and then repeat it. Ship the first module, learn, ship the second. This is what agile methods are for, when they are used properly — not as a licence to avoid planning, but as a way of converting one enormous irreversible bet into a series of small reversible ones.34 It is also what China's high-speed rail programme did, and what the Madrid metro expansion did, and what every shipping container in the world does.

What agile does and does not fix

Iterative delivery genuinely attacks several of the mechanisms in this essay. It shortens the exposure window. It surfaces rework early, when it is cheap, instead of at integration. It makes progress observable rather than self-reported, which defeats watermelon reporting. And it converts scope grope into a discovery process rather than a catastrophe.

It does not repeal merge bias, or queueing, or Brooks's law, or the politics of the original approval. An organisation that sets an impossible deadline, funds it at a fictional number, and then runs two-week sprints towards it has changed nothing except the frequency with which it is disappointed. Agile is a delivery method. Most overruns are decided before delivery begins.1

Two techniques worth knowing by name

The Last Planner System. Glenn Ballard's method attacks merge bias and the rework cycle at the level of the weekly plan, by refusing to schedule any task until its prerequisites are demonstrably ready, and by measuring the percentage of promises kept rather than the percentage of tasks complete.122 It converts a schedule from a forecast into a chain of commitments that can be audited, and it makes the reasons for non-completion visible instead of aggregated away.

Decision hygiene. Kahneman, Sibony and Sunstein's work on noise — unwanted variability in judgements that should agree — supplies the procedural counterpart: independent judgements collected before discussion, structured into components, and only then aggregated.123 Applied to estimation, this means the estimators do not meet before they estimate. The first person to speak in an estimation meeting has already set the answer.

6. Fix the incentives at the tender

If the lowest bid wins, the winner's curse selects for the bidder who most underestimated the work, and you have imported an overrun into the contract before the first day.52,54 The counters are known and they are used, though not often enough.

  • Score bids on realism as well as price. A bid that is 30% below the others is not a bargain; it is a warning.
  • Target cost with pain/gain sharing. Both parties share overruns and underruns against an agreed target, which aligns the contractor with the outcome rather than with the claim.
  • The Terminal 5 model. The client holds the risk and pays cost plus a fee, on the explicit theory that risk transferred to a contractor who cannot bear it is not transferred at all — it is merely deferred, and it will come back as a claim.109

7. Pre-commit to the conditions under which you will stop

Escalation of commitment cannot be defeated in the moment, because in the moment every incentive points towards continuing. It has to be defeated in advance, when nobody has anything at stake yet.58,63

The mechanism is a kill criterion: a specific, numerical, pre-agreed condition — written down before the first pound is spent — under which the project will be stopped. If the cost performance index is below 0.85 at the 20% gate, we stop. If the pilot does not achieve X by date Y, we stop. Stage-gate governance exists to institutionalise this, and Robert Cooper's original formulation was explicit that a gate at which nothing is ever killed is not a gate but a ceremony.124

The reason this works is that it converts the decision from a judgement made by people who are personally invested into a rule agreed by people who were not yet invested. It is Ulysses and the mast.

8. Make the forecast someone's problem

Flyvbjerg's proposal here is more radical than most organisations will accept, and it is worth stating for exactly that reason. If cost underestimation is substantially a matter of incentive rather than of cognition, then it will not be fixed by better methods. It will be fixed by accountability: forecasters and promoters who are wrong should bear a personal or financial consequence, and forecasts should be subject to independent audit by parties who are paid for accuracy rather than for approval.6,116

The weaker, more palatable version of this is independent assurance: a review function that does not report to the project it is reviewing, and cannot be fired by it. It is the only structural answer to the watermelon report, because it is the only reporting line that has no reason to lie.65,66

9. Protect the slack

Three concrete rules follow directly from Part Five, and they are the cheapest changes on this list.

  • Do not load people to 100%. Aim for 75–85% and treat the remainder as insurance, not waste. The queueing arithmetic is not negotiable.75,74
  • Aggregate the buffer. Strip padding from individual tasks and hold it centrally at the end of the chain, where it can protect whatever actually needs protecting, and where its consumption is visible.33
  • Limit work in progress. Every extra concurrent project lengthens every other one. Little's law is arithmetic, not philosophy.76,87

10. Think slow, then act fast

The synthesis Flyvbjerg draws from his own database is a single sentence, and it is a genuinely counter-intuitive one for anyone raised on a bias for action: plan slowly and in exhaustive detail; then build as fast as you possibly can.1

The logic is exposure. Planning is cheap, reversible, and safe: a mistake in a model costs a modelling week. Delivery is expensive, irreversible, and dangerous: a mistake in steel costs a year. Time spent in delivery is time during which the world can change underneath you — ministers, interest rates, technologies, executives, wars. So the correct strategy is to move the iteration into the phase where iteration is cheap, and to keep the window during which the project is exposed as short as it can be made.

This is what Gehry did at Bilbao, what the Empire State Building's builders did in 1929, and what Pixar does before it animates a single frame. It is also the exact opposite of what almost every political sponsor demands, which is spades in the ground before the election and detail figured out afterwards. The Sydney Opera House began construction two years before anyone knew how to build the roof, and paid for that decision for fourteen years.

Coda Two clocks

The budget does not move, so reality has to

Return, at the end, to Bennelong Point.

The Sydney Opera House cost fourteen times its estimate and took ten years longer than promised. It is also, by any measure anyone would actually use — cultural, economic, civic, touristic — one of the most successful public investments of the twentieth century. Both statements are true, and holding them together is the beginning of thinking clearly about this subject.

What failed at Sydney was not the building. It was the number. And the number failed not because the people who produced it were stupid, but because they were asked to produce a Class 5 estimate and hand it over as a Class 1 promise, in a political environment that rewarded a low figure and punished an honest one, for a structure that had never been built before and whose geometry had not yet been solved. Every single mechanism in this essay was present on that site in 1957, and they were present in the same combination that produced Berlin's airport sixty-three years later.

The persistent, uncomfortable finding of the research is that the overrun is not manufactured during delivery. It is manufactured at the moment of the promise. Flyvbjerg's formulation is the sharpest: projects do not go wrong so much as they start wrong.1 By the time the contractors are on site and the software teams are sprinting and the earned value reports are turning amber, the outcome has largely been determined by decisions taken years earlier by people who will not be there to see them land.

A project runs on two clocks. One is a piece of paper. The other is the world. When they disagree, the paper does not move.

That is why reality has to. The budget is a fixed artefact, defended by the people whose reputations are attached to it, and it does not bend. So the bending is done by the things that can bend: the quality, which is quietly reduced; the scope, which is quietly cut; the people, who are quietly worked into the ground; and finally, when nothing else is left, the schedule and the money, which slip all at once and in public, at the end, when it is far too late to do anything about it.

None of the countermeasures in Part Nine are difficult. Reference class forecasting is arithmetic. A pre-mortem takes ninety minutes. Not loading people to 100% costs nothing but nerve. The Green Book uplift is a lookup table. Kill criteria are a paragraph in a governance document. The reason they are not used is not that they are hard. It is that every one of them makes a project look worse on the day it is proposed, and no organisation has yet found a way to reward the person who came in with the honest number and lost the funding to the person who did not.

Until that changes — until we stop selecting our projects by holding a competition to see who can be most wrong — the iron law will keep holding. Over budget, over time, under benefits, over and over again. And the buildings will keep going up anyway, some of them magnificent, most of them late, all of them more expensive than we were told.
