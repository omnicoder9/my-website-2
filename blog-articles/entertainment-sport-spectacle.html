Animals in Human Culture

The Spectacle
of Suffering

A systematic examination of how entertainment, sport, and spectacle function as engines of animal exploitation — from captive display and trained performance to competitive killing — and what advocates can do about it.

2025  ·  Long-form analysis
I. Captive Display II. Performance III. Rodeo & Sport IV. Racing V. Fighting VI. Hunting & Fishing VII. Strategies References

Entertainment built on animal bodies is among the most culturally normalised and economically entrenched forms of animal exploitation. Unlike the food system, which can invoke necessity, or research, which can invoke medical progress, the use of animals in spectacle is frankly elective: it exists solely because people derive pleasure from it, and because significant financial interests have developed around that pleasure. This makes it, paradoxically, both one of the most morally straightforward targets for advocates and one of the most resilient, insulated as it is by tradition, nostalgia, nationalism, and the structural inertia of billion-dollar industries.

This article maps the full landscape — captive display institutions, performing animal industries, competitive animal sports, racing, fighting, and recreational killing — with attention to what the scientific evidence shows about animal experience, where the strongest ethical arguments lie, and what advocacy strategies have proven most effective. The tone throughout is analytical rather than emotive, not because the suffering described here is not moving, but because sustained change is more likely to follow from persuasive evidence than from outrage alone.

A note on framing: these categories are not entirely distinct. Zoos stage performances. Racing involves captivity. Rodeo intersects with hunting culture. The common thread is instrumentalisation: the reduction of a sentient individual to a unit of spectacle, to be bred, trained, drugged, hazarded, and disposed of in the service of human amusement.

Section I

Captive Display: Zoos, Aquariums & Marine Parks

The 800 or so accredited zoos worldwide hold tens of thousands of individual animals whose existence is defined entirely by their value as exhibits. Their welfare and the institutions' conservation claims deserve scrutiny in proportion to their cultural authority.

The Welfare Case Against Captivity

The physiological and behavioural evidence that captivity causes harm to wild animals is extensive and has grown substantially since the early 2000s. A comprehensive review published in the journal Animal Conservation found that captivity can produce persistent changes in glucocorticoid levels (a primary marker of chronic stress), immune suppression, weight loss, and reproductive failure in many species — and that these effects can persist for months or years, indicating that some species may never fully adapt to confinement.[1]

Behavioural abnormalities — known as stereotypies — are a widely accepted welfare indicator in captive animals. Common examples include pacing, repetitive swaying, overgrooming, and sham-chewing. These are not incidental quirks; they are responses to environments that fail to provide the complexity and space animals' nervous systems require.[2] The pacing of big cats in zoo enclosures, and the self-directed biting of isolated primates, are documented indicators of stress rather than adaptive coping, even when they help the animal modulate its distress.[3]

Key Welfare Issues Across Captive Settings
  • Restricted movement and range deprivation
  • Chronic stress from social, spatial, and sensory impoverishment
  • Breeding for display, including surplus animal management
  • Stereotypic behaviours indicating psychological distress
  • Visitor-induced stress (negative behaviours observed in 57% of zoo observations)[4]
  • Medical complications from captive diets and flooring
  • Social disruption from artificial grouping
  • Loss of agency and predictive control over environment

Orcas and Cetaceans in Marine Parks

No captive species has attracted more evidence of welfare failure than the orca (Orcinus orca). In the wild, females who survive infancy live an average of 46 years, with some reaching 70–80; wild males average 31 years with a maximum around 50–60.[5] In captivity, orcas routinely die in their 20s and 30s. One study published in Marine Mammal Science found that captive orca mortality rates were approximately 2.5 times higher than those of wild orcas.[6] A more recent literature review in the Journal of Veterinary Behavior documented the physiological mechanisms underlying this pattern — chronic stress suppresses immunity, leading to the infectious diseases that are the predominant cause of captive orca death.[7]

The physical sequelae of captivity are visible and well-documented: dorsal fin collapse is observed in 60–90% of captive male orcas. This condition — caused by gravity acting on connective tissue that is not counteracted by the constant swimming motion natural to wild orcas — is virtually absent in wild populations.[5]

Wild orcas have no recorded history of killing humans. Captive orcas have been responsible for hundreds of documented attacks and four human deaths — behaviours researchers attribute to chronic stress, frustration, and psychopathology in captive conditions.[8]

The Conservation Argument: Scrutinised

Zoos routinely defend their existence with reference to conservation. This claim deserves careful analysis. Accredited zoos do maintain some genuine conservation programmes — captive breeding has contributed to the recovery of species including the Arabian oryx and the scimitar-horned oryx, and some institutions conduct meaningful field research.[9] A 2018 study in Scientific Reports evaluated North American zoo contributions to species listed under the US Endangered Species Act, finding that while zoos conduct useful research and population management, their direct impact on wild population recovery is often limited, and education programmes that demonstrably change conservation behaviour are underutilised.[10]

The deeper structural critique is this: accredited zoos represent a small proportion of captive animal facilities globally. The far larger landscape includes roadside zoos, petting zoos, unaccredited private collections, and poorly regulated facilities in many countries, where conservation claims are minimal or absent and welfare standards are routinely inadequate. Even among accredited institutions, large wide-ranging species — lions, elephants, polar bears — show the highest rates of stereotypy and welfare compromise, precisely the species most featured as headline exhibits.[11]

The individual animal in a zoo enclosure is not a conservation unit. It is a sentient being whose interests in movement, social complexity, and psychological wellbeing are systematically curtailed. The question is whether the institutional benefits justify that cost — and for most zoo animals, the honest answer is that they do not.

— Analytical synthesis of welfare literature

Petting Zoos, Safari Parks, and Roadside Facilities

Below the level of major accredited zoos lies a range of facilities with weaker regulatory oversight and more acute welfare problems. Petting zoos expose young animals to repeated handling stress; "cub petting" operations — where big cat cubs are rented for photo opportunities — typically involve removal of cubs from mothers shortly after birth, disrupting bonding and increasing stress hormones, and are often linked to commercial breeding operations that generate surplus animals.[12] Safari parks vary considerably in enclosure quality but frequently involve large animals in spaces inadequate for their ranging behaviour. Roadside zoos — a term covering informal captive animal exhibits along roadsides and at tourist attractions — are subject to the least regulatory scrutiny in most jurisdictions and generate a disproportionate share of documented welfare violations.

Section II

Circuses, Performances, and Trained Animal Acts

The use of wild and domestic animals in staged performances has declined significantly in the developed world following sustained advocacy campaigns — but it has not ended, and the mechanisms by which animals are compelled to perform remain largely unchanged.

How Animals Are Trained to Perform

The techniques that produce circus-ready animals are not trade secrets. Documented undercover investigations — including by Animal Defenders International in the United States, which observed multiple circus operations — found pervasive use of physical coercion: bullhooks (fireplace-poker-like implements targeting sensitive areas behind elephants' ears and on their legs), electric prods, whips, tight chains, and isolation.[13] The industry describes this as "training"; the mechanism is negative reinforcement in its most literal and brutal form — inflicting pain until the animal performs the required behaviour, then withdrawing pain as reward.

Many of the tricks performed are biomechanically problematic. Headstands, hind-leg stands, and other extreme postures place repeated abnormal stress on muscles and joints, producing cumulative musculoskeletal injury.[14] Elephants trained in circuses typically die before age 30; their wild counterparts commonly live to 60 or 70.[15]

Species-specific concerns

Elephants are among the most cognitively and emotionally complex land mammals, with documented long-term memory, grief responses, and complex social bonds. In circuses they are typically chained for most of the day — including entire days when not performing — in conditions that produce the swaying and head-bobbing stereotypies indicative of chronic psychological distress.[16] Foot infections and arthritis, accelerated by prolonged standing on hard surfaces, are the leading causes of death among captive elephants.[16]

Bears in performance contexts often learn to "dance" through a conditioning procedure in which they are placed on heated metal surfaces while music plays, associating the sound with pain. In their natural range they may roam vast territories, forage across seasons, and hibernate. Circus life offers none of these possibilities.[17]

Dolphins in show settings — most prominently at marine parks — perform via positive reinforcement training, which is less directly coercive than bullhook-based methods, but the welfare critique remains: these animals are held in environments radically impoverished relative to their natural cognitive and social needs, may travel hundreds of miles daily in the wild, and exhibit stress behaviours in captivity consistent with the orca evidence reviewed above.

Legislative Progress and Remaining Gaps

The global trend is clearly toward restriction. More than 35 countries have introduced full or partial bans on wild animals in circuses, including the UK, Ireland, Austria, Belgium, and many others. In the United States, where federal law is weaker, a patchwork of state and local bans exists; major circuses including Ringling Bros. and Barnum & Bailey have phased out animal acts following public pressure, though smaller operations continue. Globally, the shift has been driven by a combination of undercover investigations generating public outrage, legislative pressure from welfare organisations, declining ticket sales, and the increasing difficulty of obtaining municipal permits.[18]

Section III

Rodeos, Bullfighting, and Competitive Animal Sport

Events in which animals are stressed, injured, or killed as the mechanism of competition occupy a complex cultural space — particularly in regions where they are framed as living heritage. The welfare evidence, however, is unambiguous.

Rodeo

Rodeo events — bull riding, bronc riding, calf roping, steer wrestling, and barrel racing — share a common feature: animal stress and potential injury is not an unfortunate side effect but an intrinsic part of the spectacle. Bulls and horses buck because they are frightened and in pain, from spurring and the tightening of flank straps around their sensitive underbellies. Their response is not performance but a fear reflex identical to the response to predator attack.[19]

The welfare evidence on calf roping is particularly clear. A 2023 study published in Animals (University of Sydney) analysed video footage of calves across all phases of roping events — chase, lasso, catch and restraint, leg-tie, and release — and found signs of fear and distress throughout every phase.[20] Physiological research has confirmed significant increases in cortisol, creatinine kinase, and lactate dehydrogenase in roped calves, consistent with acute stress and muscle damage.[21] Physical injury risks include windpipe damage from the lasso, broken ribs, bruising, neck soft-tissue injury, and choking.[19]

Calves show signs of distress from the moment they are chased, through lassoing, being thrown to the ground, and tied — right through to release. Every part of this event is frightening and distressing for the young animals.

— Dr Di Evans, RSPCA Australia, commenting on University of Sydney research[20]

In steer wrestling, competitors drop from horseback onto a running steer and wrestle it to the ground. Injury risks include horn damage, windpipe injury, and musculoskeletal trauma.[22] Rodeo bulls and horses sustain injuries including broken bones, torn ligaments, and internal injuries, though industry reporting of these incidents is inconsistent and often incomplete.

Bullfighting

Approximately 180,000 bulls are killed in bullfights globally each year, concentrated in Spain, Portugal, France, Mexico, Colombia, Venezuela, Peru, and Ecuador.[23] The structure of a bullfight is designed to progressively weaken and disorient the bull through lancing (picadors), banderilla placement, and blood loss, before the matador administers the killing blow. The process from entry to death typically takes 15–20 minutes and involves significant pain and terror throughout.

A study evaluating Portuguese public opinion found that nearly 85% of respondents believed the bull suffered during bullfighting — yet 65% would support a ban on animal cruelty grounds, suggesting a gap between perception and legislative willingness that advocates can target.[24] In Spain, polling by Ipsos MORI found that 58% of adults oppose bullfighting, compared to 19% who support it.[25] Support is lowest among young, urban demographics — the trajectory of public opinion runs consistently toward abolition.

A 2025 study of Korean traditional bullfighting, while examining a variant in which bulls fight each other rather than humans, found avoidance behaviour rates of 41.2% and injury rates of 62.3% across the 131 contests examined — providing quantitative welfare data applicable to all bull-fighting contexts.[26]

The Running of the Bulls and Related Events

Bull-running events — most famously Pamplona's San Fermín festival — are defended as cultural heritage but involve forcing bulls through narrow urban streets in conditions that produce extreme terror. The bulls are subsequently killed in the associated bullfight. The cultural heritage defence has been successfully rebutted in several jurisdictions through a combination of public opinion data, welfare evidence, and arguments about evolving cultural norms — the same arguments that have ended other traditions including bear-baiting and public execution.

Section IV

Racing: Horse, Greyhound, and Beyond

Racing industries present welfare problems that span the entire lifecycle of the animal — from selective overbreeding to injury, doping, and disposal when commercial utility ends.

Horse Racing

Horse racing is a global industry with a well-documented injury and fatality record. Data from the US Equine Injury Database (EID) — the most comprehensive available — recorded a fatality rate of 1.11 per 1,000 starts in 2024, the lowest since the database's inception in 2009.[27] While the industry presents this as a measure of progress, the figure represents a substantial absolute number of deaths given the scale of the industry, and the rate on dirt tracks (1.18/1,000) remains significantly higher than on turf (0.88/1,000). Non-fatal injuries — fractures, tendon ruptures, joint injuries — occur at far higher rates and often end careers.

Beyond the race track, the structural welfare problems of horse racing are systemic. Thoroughbreds are selectively bred for speed at the cost of skeletal integrity; early racing of two-year-olds — before bone plates are fully formed — is routine despite evidence of elevated injury risk. Doping to mask pain or enhance performance, while regulated, remains a persistent enforcement challenge. And the fate of horses that do not perform competitively — "wastage" in industry terminology — is rarely tracked: many end their lives in slaughterhouses in countries where horse meat is traded.[28]

Greyhound Racing

Greyhound racing has a more acute welfare profile than horse racing relative to its smaller economic scale. Research conducted in Western Australia over seven years (2017–2023) reported 281 race fatalities, an average of 40 deaths per year and a death rate of 4.5% of dogs who raced.[29] In Ireland, where the industry is more concentrated, Greyhound Racing Ireland data shows that 715 greyhounds were killed at tracks between 2015 and 2020, with 202 deaths in 2024 alone — the highest recorded total.[30] In the UK, analysis of a 10-year period found over 40,000 dogs injured and more than 18,000 who did not race again.[31]

The overbreeding problem is severe: estimates suggest that many times the number of dogs required for racing are bred annually. In Ireland, reports indicate overbreeding by around 1,000%, resulting in up to 6,000 dogs culled annually because they did not meet qualifying times.[30] Dogs who fail to perform are routinely killed, abandoned, or exported to countries with minimal welfare oversight. The New Zealand government has announced a phase-out of greyhound racing specifically on welfare grounds.[29]

Other Racing Contexts

Camel racing — practised across the Gulf states, North Africa, and parts of South Asia — has historically involved the use of child jockeys (now replaced by robotic riders in many jurisdictions following international pressure), with ongoing welfare concerns around breeding, training methods, and post-racing disposal. Pigeon racing involves a significant mortality rate from weather, predation, and exhaustion, with birds frequently lost. Sled dog racing — most prominently the Iditarod in Alaska — involves animals covering extreme distances in harsh conditions; documented deaths from exhaustion, aspiration pneumonia, and trauma are reported in major events annually.

Section V

Animal Fighting

Dogfighting, cockfighting, and their variants represent a category of exploitation that is illegal in most jurisdictions yet operates on a massive, organised, transnational scale — intertwined with gambling, organised crime, and other serious criminal enterprises.

Dogfighting

Dogfighting involves placing dogs of similar weight in an enclosed pit and goading them to attack each other. Fights may last more than an hour, resulting in severe lacerations, broken bones, haematomas, and death. Dogs that perform poorly are routinely killed by drowning or electrocution, or used as bait animals to provoke aggression in other dogs.[32] The psychological consequences of the training regimes — isolation, forced aggression induction, intermittent reinforcement of violence — are distinct welfare harms in addition to the physical injuries of combat.

Dogfighting is illegal across all 50 US states and in most countries, but enforcement rarely matches the scale of the problem. Research published in Trends in Organized Crime found that professional dogfighting operations resist effective local enforcement partly because syndicates are too large for local agencies, and partly because the sub-culture operates through reputation networks that are difficult to penetrate.[33]

Cockfighting

Cockfighting operates on a far larger scale than dogfighting globally. The USDA estimates there are as many as 20 million fighting birds in the United States alone, many destined for illegal pits domestically and legally-operating fighting venues in the Philippines, Mexico, and elsewhere.[34] In 2022, more than $13 billion was wagered on cockfights in the Philippines.[35] American cockfighters supply international markets through established trafficking networks using postal services and commercial airlines, despite federal prohibitions under the Animal Welfare Act.[36]

In cockfighting, roosters are fitted with metal gaffs (curved blades) or knife attachments to their natural spurs, designed to inflict deep and mortal wounds. Fights end in the death or severe injury of one or both birds. Beyond the welfare harm to the animals, cockfighting has been documented as a vector for avian influenza and virulent Newcastle disease — representing a public health risk of substantial concern.[36]

The inattention to dogfighting and cockfighting must end. These events are meeting places for criminals — a law enforcement net would gather up animal cruelty, illegal gambling, drug trafficking, weapons violations, and gang activity simultaneously.

— Animal Wellness Action advocacy coalition[37]

Other Fighting Contexts

Bear baiting — in which dogs are set upon a chained bear — persists in parts of Pakistan despite widespread condemnation, often as a form of rural entertainment and gambling. Fish fighting occurs in parts of Southeast Asia and is increasingly subject to welfare legislation. Insect fighting (most notably cricket fighting in parts of China) occupies a less legally scrutinised space but raises questions about the sentience and capacity to suffer of invertebrates that the precautionary principle suggests should not be dismissed.

Section VI

Hunting, Fishing, and Recreational Killing

The deliberate killing or injuring of animals for recreation is among the most normalised forms of animal exploitation in many cultures — and among the hardest to dislodge, given its deep entanglement with identity, tradition, rural economy, and in some cases genuine conservation complexity.

Trophy Hunting

Trophy hunting — in which hunters pay large sums to kill particular animals, primarily in sub-Saharan Africa, North America, and Central Asia — is defended by its proponents on three grounds: economic benefit to local communities, conservation revenue generation, and the stimulation of wildlife-friendly land use. Each claim warrants scrutiny.

On conservation: CITES trade database analysis by the International Fund for Animal Welfare found that at least 11,000 lion trophies were traded internationally between 2004 and 2013, with African lions showing the strongest statistically significant increase in trophy hunting trade among the top 20 threatened taxa.[38] Research on lion populations in trophy hunting areas has produced conflicting results — some studies find sustainable harvest compatible with population stability at low harvest intensities, while others document localized population declines from over-hunting.[39] A 2016 US House of Representatives report found "many troubling examples of funds either being diverted from their purpose or not being dedicated to conservation in the first place" and "no merit to claims that hunting deters poaching."[40]

Canned hunting — a variant in which lions are bred in captivity specifically to be shot at close range in enclosed areas — has no plausible conservation justification. An estimated 10,000–12,000 captive lions are held in South Africa's canned hunting industry.[41] The fees charged for hunting a captive African lion range from $24,000 to over $71,000, with little evidence that revenue reaches community conservation efforts.[41]

Research modelling alternative revenue streams has found that well-designed ecotourism, including a proposed "lion protection fee" charged to visiting international tourists, can match or exceed trophy hunting revenue while preserving living animals — with 84.2% of surveyed international tourists expressing support for such a fee.[42]

Recreational Hunting and Trapping

Recreational hunting encompasses a wide range of practices — rifle hunting, bowhunting, falconry, and trapping — with welfare profiles that vary considerably. Bowhunting is associated with elevated rates of wounding, with wounded animals frequently not recovered. Leg-hold traps cause prolonged pain and distress; many jurisdictions have banned specific trap types on welfare grounds while permitting others. The individualistic and often rural cultural identity attached to recreational hunting makes it one of the most challenging areas for advocacy, requiring approaches that engage hunters' own values around conservation and fair chase rather than frontal opposition.

Sport Fishing and Catch-and-Release

Whether fish experience pain in a morally relevant sense has been a contested scientific question, but the current evidence increasingly supports the conclusion that fish have the biological architecture for nociception and show behavioural and physiological responses consistent with pain experience. A review of welfare literature on recreational angling, published in the Journal of Applied Animal Welfare Science, concluded that "all recreational fishing results in some level of injury and stress" and that fish likely experience pain and fear during capture.[43]

Catch-and-release fishing — often presented as an ethical alternative — does not resolve these concerns. Post-release mortality depends heavily on species, water temperature, hook type, handling time, and air exposure. A PubMed-indexed review found that capture time, handling time, and air exposure are significant determinants of physiological stress responses in fish and should be minimised.[44] Germany and Switzerland have banned catch-and-release fishing on welfare grounds, with German law finding no "good reason" adequate to justify causing pain to an animal when no food is consumed.[45]

Recent research applying the Welfare Footprint Framework to fish slaughter documented that air asphyxiation — a common kill method — can cause excruciating pain for ten minutes or more, and that electrical stunning is one of the most cost-effective welfare interventions known, potentially sparing 60–1,200 minutes of suffering per dollar spent.[46]

Section VII

Advocacy Strategies

Entertainment exploitation is a domain where advocacy has produced measurable, durable victories — circus animal bans, greyhound racing phase-outs, captive orca legislation. Understanding what worked, and why, is essential to strategic prioritisation.

What Has Driven Progress

The most significant wins in this domain share common features: undercover documentation that made private cruelty publicly visible; mass media amplification (Blackfish, The Cove); economic pressure on venue operators and sponsors; legislative campaigns with clear policy asks; and coalitions that extended beyond the animal advocacy community to include tourism operators, medical researchers, and environmental groups.

  1. Undercover documentation and strategic media placement. Filmed evidence of circus training abuse, marine park conditions, or dogfighting operations — professionally produced and placed in mainstream outlets — has consistently outperformed abstract argument. Partner with skilled investigators and plan media strategy before footage is released.
  2. Venue and permit campaigns. Circus animal acts require municipal permits to perform. Campaigns targeting local governments to refuse permits have been highly effective and require significantly less political capital than national legislation. Begin locally, build precedent, then scale.
  3. Sponsor boycotts and corporate campaigns. Racing, bullfighting, and rodeo events depend on corporate sponsorship. Documented welfare harms give sponsors clear reputational risk; systematic, polite, evidence-led communication to CSR departments has led to sponsor withdrawals that significantly increase financial pressure on target industries.
  4. Legislative coalition-building. Frame animal entertainment legislation in terms that attract cross-partisan support — public safety (escaped circus animals; cockfighting as disease vector), organized crime (gambling on animal fights), tourism (photo-prop cubs in conservation areas). Avoid framing that reduces the issue to animal rights philosophy.
  5. Economic alternatives advocacy. Where industries claim conservation or community-livelihood benefits — particularly in trophy hunting — actively research, fund, and publicise viable alternatives. The "lion protection fee" modelling is an example of the kind of rigorous alternative-proposition work that neutralises the most durable industry counterargument.
  6. Engage hunters and anglers through their own values. Recreational hunting and fishing communities have internal conservation ethics — "fair chase," habitat protection, sustainable harvest — that can be leveraged. Welfare arguments are more durable when they are framed as extensions of values the target audience already holds, not as attacks on identity.
  7. Target demographic trends. Public opposition to bullfighting, circus animals, and marine park shows is highest among younger and urban demographics. Long-form educational content, social media, and school-level engagement do not change the views of current consumers but shape the next generation of cultural expectations — the timeline for which is shorter than it appears.
  8. Support legislative enforcement, not just prohibition. Animal fighting bans mean little without enforcement resources. Advocacy for increased funding for animal fighting prosecution units, and for mechanisms (such as citizen suit provisions) that allow private enforcement action, extends the practical reach of existing law.

Priority Targets by Tractability

Not all of these issues are equally tractable at any given moment. The following rough ranking is based on current public opinion trends, legislative momentum, and advocacy track record:

High tractability: Marine park captive cetacean legislation (momentum from Canada, California, and ongoing state-level bills); greyhound racing phase-outs (NZ precedent, declining attendance, strong RSPCA evidence base); roadside zoo regulation (bipartisan appeal, documented public safety harms).

Medium tractability: Circus animal bans in remaining US states and jurisdictions; trophy hunting import bans (UK Hunting Trophies Bill trajectory); canned lion hunting prohibition.

Longer horizon: Recreational fishing (fish sentience is not yet culturally embedded); bullfighting in Spain and Mexico (cultural resistance is significant, though trends are favourable); rodeo (deep rural identity attachment).

The Blackfish Effect

The 2013 documentary Blackfish provides a case study in narrative-driven advocacy. Following its release, SeaWorld's stock fell significantly, major corporate sponsors withdrew, and the company announced the end of its captive orca breeding programme in 2016. Legislative action followed at state and federal levels. The film demonstrates that well-evidenced, emotionally engaging narrative — when placed in mainstream media — can compress the legislative timeline by years.
