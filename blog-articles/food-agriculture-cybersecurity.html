01 — Framing

The shape of the sector

For most of its history, the American food system was protected by physics, not firewalls. A plow does not have an attack surface; a grain elevator does not run a TCP/IP stack; a herd of cattle cannot be encrypted. That dispensation is over. The same sensors, controllers, telematics units and cloud platforms that have made farming and food production extraordinarily productive have also made the sector legible to ransomware crews, nation-state operators and opportunists scanning the internet for anything that will pay. The food and agriculture sector did not so much join the cyber threat landscape as get conscripted into it.

In the U.S. critical-infrastructure taxonomy, Food and Agriculture is one of sixteen designated sectors, and an unusually sprawling one. It is composed almost entirely of private enterprise — farms, ranches, feedlots, grain elevators, processors, manufacturers, cold-storage warehouses, distributors, restaurants and retailers — and it accounts for roughly one-fifth of the nation's economic activity.2 Concretely, that footprint spans on the order of 2.1 million farms, more than 935,000 restaurants and over 200,000 registered food-related facilities, collectively producing about a fifth of national economic output.5 No single regulator sits atop it. Responsibility is shared: the U.S. Department of Agriculture and the Food and Drug Administration (within Health and Human Services) serve as the joint Sector Risk Management Agencies, co-chairing the Government Coordinating Council that works alongside an industry-led Sector Coordinating Council.324 The FDA alone is responsible for the safety of roughly 80% of the food Americans eat.2

The agencies that steward the sector frame its risk landscape around four enduring categories: food contamination and disruption (whether accidental or intentional), disease and pests, severe weather, and — increasingly — cybersecurity.3 For most of the sector's modern history, the first three dominated planning and the last barely registered. The reasons are now obvious in retrospect: cyber risk was abstract, the equipment was electromechanical, and executives had concrete, visible problems — spoilage, drought, avian influenza, foreign animal disease — competing for attention and capital. As one industry account put it, for years cyberattacks were simply low on the priority list, with leadership focused on sickened cattle and spoiled wheat until ransomware and nation-state espionage became too disruptive to ignore.52

How agriculture became "critical"

The sector's place in homeland-security doctrine was settled in two strokes in the early 2000s. In December 2003, Homeland Security Presidential Directive 7 (HSPD-7) on critical-infrastructure identification added agriculture and food to the protected sectors — correcting an omission in the 1998 directive (PDD-63) it superseded. Weeks later, in January 2004, HSPD-9, "Defense of United States Agriculture and Food," established a standing national policy to defend the food and agriculture system against terrorist attacks, major disasters and other emergencies.67 HSPD-9's animating concern was explicit and physical: America's food system is "an extensive, open, interconnected, diverse, and complex structure," and therefore a target whose compromise could carry "catastrophic health and economic effects."6 That language was written with agroterrorism, pathogens and adulteration in mind. Two decades on, the same adjectives — open, interconnected, complex — describe precisely why the sector's digital surface is so hard to defend.

The governing framework has since modernized. The Obama-era Presidential Policy Directive 21 (PPD-21) institutionalized the sixteen-sector structure and the Sector Risk Management Agency model. On 30 April 2024, the White House replaced PPD-21 with National Security Memorandum 22 (NSM-22), explicitly acknowledging that the threat environment had shifted "from counterterrorism to strategic competition," driven by nation-state cyber activity and new technology such as artificial intelligence.8 NSM-22 preserved the sixteen sectors and the SRMA leadership model but designated the Director of CISA as National Coordinator for the Security and Resilience of Critical Infrastructure and signaled a clear drift from purely voluntary partnership toward "minimum requirements and effective accountability mechanisms," including "aligned and effective regulatory frameworks."9 Running alongside it, the Cyber Incident Reporting for Critical Infrastructure Act of 2022 (CIRCIA) will, once rules are final, require covered critical-infrastructure entities — food and agriculture included — to report significant cyber incidents within 72 hours and ransom payments within 24 hours.10

Field note — why this sector is structurally exposed

Five characteristics, none of them new, compound to make food and agriculture an attractive and brittle target:

  • Private, fragmented ownership. Defense is voluntary and uneven across millions of operators with wildly different maturity.
  • Consolidation at the chokepoints. A handful of meatpackers, a few input cooperatives, and a small number of software vendors carry disproportionate load — single points of failure for the whole chain.
  • Thin margins, thin IT. Many operators cannot afford dedicated security staff and lean heavily on managed-service providers, inheriting their risk.
  • Just-in-time, perishable product. There is little slack. A days-long outage during a critical window does not merely defer revenue; it spoils inventory and empties shelves.
  • Time-bound operations. Planting and harvest are immovable. Adversaries know it, and they time their pressure accordingly.

That last point is not conjecture. As the incident record below will show, federal agencies have formally warned that ransomware operators appear to deliberately strike agricultural cooperatives during planting and harvest, when the cost of downtime — and the temptation to pay — is highest.34 The sector's calendar is itself an attack parameter.

02 — Technology & attack surface

The stack, from soil sensor to SCADA

To reason about the sector's cyber risk, you have to see it as a stack of very different technologies stitched together end to end. The agronomic edge and the processing floor are not one system; they are several, each with its own protocols, lifecycles, vendors and failure modes. What unites them is that they are now networked, and that the people running them are frequently not security specialists.

Precision agriculture: the instrumented field

Precision agriculture is the umbrella term for data-driven farming: using GPS/GNSS positioning, satellite and drone imagery, in-field IoT sensors, variable-rate application, autosteer and increasingly autonomous machinery to optimize inputs and yields. The productivity case is real — more crop, fewer inputs, less labor. But the same instrumentation rebuilds the farm as a cyber-physical system. A useful mental model, drawn from the agronomic-security literature, decomposes a precision-agriculture deployment into four layers, each with characteristic threats: a perception layer of sensors and actuators; a network layer that may braid together cellular 4G/5G, satellite (e.g., Starlink), LoRaWAN and Bluetooth; an application layer of farm-management and decision software; and an execution layer of machinery and actuators.11 Threats map cleanly onto the layers: sensor spoofing and "sleep-deprivation" attacks that drain battery-powered field devices; denial-of-service and forged control injection against actuators; and — the signature precision-agriculture threat — GNSS jamming and spoofing.11

The distinction matters operationally. GNSS jamming floods the receiver's band with noise, denying a fix; GNSS spoofing feeds the receiver counterfeit signals so it computes a false position.11 Autonomous and semi-autonomous platforms that depend on positioning for split-second decisions are the most exposed class of systems to both, and jamming hardware is cheap and widely available.13 In an agricultural context the consequence is rarely a fiery crash — autonomous tractors operate in controlled fields, not traffic — but it can be ruined passes, mis-registered application maps, equipment downtime and damaged crops. And the attack surface is not only the machine. Consider fertilizer: a 2021 research disclosure noted that manipulating application logic could create a denial-of-service in a field, and that dramatically over-applying chemicals without alerting the operator could render that field infertile.16 The risk is not science-fiction sabotage of the food supply so much as the quiet corruption of the data and control loops a modern farm now trusts.

The John Deere problem: long-lived, locked-down, exposed

No episode crystallized the security state of farm equipment better than the work of the researcher known as Sick Codes. At DEF CON 2021 he and collaborators disclosed a string of trivially exploitable flaws in John Deere's developer and operations web properties — among them a default-credential weakness in a Pega business-process tool that yielded remote access, and reflected cross-site scripting found almost immediately upon logging into Deere's developer site — vulnerabilities that could expose equipment-owner data and, in the worst framing, manipulate operations.1615 The following year, at DEF CON 2022, he went further: a hardware-level jailbreak of Deere 2630 and 4240 displays — running a Wind River Linux build — that achieved root and, in the demonstration's now-famous flourish, ran a corn-themed port of Doom on the tractor's screen.141517 The jailbreak was not a remote exploit, but it laid bare a deeper truth: much of the most vital equipment in the food supply runs on outdated or unsupported operating systems, and Deere — which sells roughly twice as much equipment as its two nearest competitors combined — anchors that exposure.14

The Deere saga also exposes a genuine tension that recurs throughout this sector: the same digital locks that frustrate the right-to-repair movement are sold as security, and the same jailbreaks that let farmers fix their own machines reveal fundamental insecurities that a malicious actor could exploit or chain.14 Security-through-obscurity and a manufacturer's remote control over field equipment are not the same as defensible architecture — a point made vivid when, by widely circulated accounts, Russian forces looted a John Deere dealership in occupied Ukraine and trucked the machines home, only to discover how much control the manufacturer still retained from afar.12 The episode is a reminder that "who can reach this equipment, and how" is now a first-order security question on the farm.

The processing floor: ICS, SCADA and the cold chain

Move downstream from the field to the plant and the technology changes character entirely. Slaughterhouses, mills, bottling lines, bakeries and pack houses are industrial environments governed by operational technology — programmable logic controllers (PLCs), distributed control systems, human-machine interfaces and supervisory control and data acquisition (SCADA) systems — speaking industrial protocols such as Modbus, DNP3, EtherNet/IP and PROFINET. The canonical reference for how these networks should be structured is the Purdue Enterprise Reference Architecture, which separates enterprise IT (upper levels) from supervisory and basic-control layers (lower levels) and, crucially, prescribes segmentation between them.21 The defining OT-security challenge of the last decade is that this separation has eroded: the push to add connectivity between layers and to bolt IoT sensors and remote access onto historically air-gapped control systems has blurred the IT/OT boundary that the Purdue model was meant to police.23

That convergence is what makes a "business" ransomware infection in food processing so dangerous. Encrypting an enterprise file server should not, in principle, stop a production line. In practice — when networks are flat, when an HMI shares a domain with corporate laptops, when a single managed-service compromise reaches both — it does. The result, repeatedly, has been precautionary shutdowns of physical production to contain a threat that began in IT, with operators choosing to halt plants rather than risk the controllers.

One OT subsystem deserves singular attention because it sits at the intersection of food safety, worker safety and the chemical sector: industrial ammonia refrigeration. The cold chain that keeps the food supply viable runs overwhelmingly on anhydrous ammonia as the working refrigerant — it is, in effect, the most common refrigerant in the largest-scale industrial systems, and virtually any chilled food or beverage reaching a U.S. store has passed through an ammonia refrigeration facility.20 Anhydrous ammonia is also acutely hazardous: it is corrosive and toxic, immediately dangerous to life and health at 300 ppm, and flammable at roughly 15–28% by volume in air, capable of explosion in an enclosed space with an ignition source.18 Facilities holding 10,000 pounds or more fall under EPA Risk Management Program and OSHA Process Safety Management requirements.20 These systems are controlled and monitored by the very OT that ransomware and intrusions target. The point is not that a typical ransomware crew wants to vent ammonia — it is that the control layer governing a life-safety hazard now shares an attack surface with ordinary enterprise IT.

The enterprise edge and the managed-service dependency

Finally, the prosaic but most-attacked layer: enterprise IT. Cooperatives, processors and distributors run ERP, warehouse- and transport-management systems, electronic data interchange with trading partners, e-commerce and ordinary email — the same software, and the same vulnerabilities, as any mid-market firm. What is distinctive is the sector's heavy reliance on third parties. Because many agricultural companies cannot afford to staff IT and security roles, they depend on managed-service providers for protection — which means a single MSP or shared-software compromise can fan out to dozens of downstream agricultural clients at once.36 That structural dependency, as much as any field sensor, is the sector's defining cyber exposure — and the next section's incident record is, in large part, a record of it being exploited.

03 — The incident record

Anatomy of a sector under fire

Theory is one thing; the casualty list is another. The modern security history of U.S. food and agriculture can be read as a short, sharp escalation — from collateral damage in someone else's cyberwar, to a watershed ransomware shutdown, to a documented pattern of seasonally timed extortion, to the present condition in which the sector is a deliberate target of both ransomware syndicates and nation-state operators.

NotPetya (2017): collateral in a state cyberwar

The sector's first nine-figure cyber loss was not aimed at it at all. NotPetya, the destructive wiper masquerading as ransomware that Russia unleashed via a compromised Ukrainian accounting package in June 2017, tore through global enterprises and inflicted an estimated $10 billion in worldwide damage.24 Among its corporate victims was the snack-food giant Mondelēz — maker of Oreo and Ritz — which, by its own court filings, lost more than 1,700 servers and 24,000 laptops to the malware, alongside disrupted supply and distribution.24 Mondelēz's roughly $100 million insurance claim collided with a "hostile or warlike action" exclusion: insurer Zurich initially denied coverage on the theory that a Russian state attack was an act of war, and the multi-year litigation that followed — settled confidentially in 2022 — became the defining test case for how cyber loss intersects with "war exclusion" clauses.25 A parallel suit by pharmaceutical maker Merck over $1.4 billion in NotPetya losses produced a New Jersey ruling that the war exclusion did not apply.25 For food and agriculture, NotPetya delivered two lessons at once: a state-grade weapon can hit you simply for being networked, and your insurance may not pay when it does.

JBS (2021): the watershed

If NotPetya was abstract, the May 2021 ransomware attack on JBS — the world's largest meat processor — was visceral. The REvil (Sodinokibi) group, the same ransomware-as-a-service operation later tied to other high-profile incidents, encrypted JBS's environment after a months-long intrusion that included reconnaissance from February and data exfiltration through late May.2628 All nine of the company's U.S. meatpacking plants temporarily shut down. The significance lay in JBS's share of national supply: in the United States alone the company accounts for roughly a quarter of beef and a fifth of pork production.26 After negotiating an initial demand of $22.5 million down, JBS paid approximately $11 million in Bitcoin — a decision its U.S. CEO called difficult but justified to protect customers, and one made even though most facilities were already operational again.2726 The payment drew sharp congressional criticism for incentivizing future attacks, and the episode focused new attention on how consolidation in meatpacking turns a single corporate outage into a national supply event.27

The grain-cooperative wave (2021)

Four months after JBS, the threat moved to the agronomic input side of the chain. In September 2021, Iowa's NEW Cooperative — a major farm-services provider handling feed, grain, fertilizer and crop-protection for the nation's top corn state — was hit by the BlackMatter group, which demanded roughly $5.9 million and claimed to have stolen financial, HR and R&D data along with the source code for the cooperative's SoilMap platform.2930 NEW Cooperative took its systems offline to contain the attack; in arguing for its own significance, it claimed that 40% of the grain industry runs on its software — a figure that, if accurate, makes a single mid-sized cooperative a genuine national chokepoint.31 Tellingly, BlackMatter's self-declared "off-limits" list covered only power and water treatment; by that logic a grain cooperative was fair game.31 Days later, Minnesota's Crystal Valley Cooperative — serving more than 2,500 farms across southern Minnesota and northern Iowa — was struck by a second attack that disrupted daily operations and even knocked out card payments at its fuel pumps.32 Both incidents landed at the onset of harvest. Because OT compromise can be invisible from the outside, security responders at the time could not even rule out that the cooperatives' control systems had been reached.29 In October 2021, CISA, the FBI and the NSA issued a joint advisory naming BlackMatter's targeting of multiple critical-infrastructure organizations, including two in food and agriculture.33

The seasonal-timing warning (2022)

By April 2022 the pattern was clear enough for the FBI to formalize it. A Private Industry Notification — issued with USDA and CISA — warned that ransomware actors "may be more likely" to strike agricultural cooperatives during the critical planting and harvest windows, when time-sensitive operations make victims both more disruptable and more willing to pay.34 The Bureau was already aware of six grain-cooperative ransomware attacks during the fall 2021 harvest and two more in early 2022 with the potential to disrupt the planting-season supply of seed and fertilizer.35 The notification catalogued a striking diversity of variants used against the sector in that window — Conti, BlackMatter, Suncrypt, Sodinokibi and BlackByte among them — and noted the recurring intrusion vectors: known-but-unpatched vulnerabilities and the compromise of shared network resources or managed services.35 One software-supply-chain incident illustrated the cascade vividly: a business-management software vendor hit by HelloKitty/FiveHands ransomware (with a $30 million demand) seeded secondary infections at downstream clients, several of them agricultural cooperatives.3536

Dole (2023): the lettuce shortage

The clearest public demonstration of just-in-time fragility came in February 2023, when produce giant Dole suffered a ransomware attack that forced it to shut down production plants across North America and halt shipments.37 Dole moved quickly to contain the malware by taking systems offline, and characterized the operational impact as "limited" — yet grocery stores in Texas and New Mexico went days without Dole's pre-packaged salad kits, and the shortage rippled to fast-food chains that depend on its bagged lettuce.3738 The episode produced an unfamiliar sentence — "ransomware cyberattack" and "pre-packaged salad" in the same breath — and a precise lesson from analysts: the just-in-time nature of food supply chains, with production and distribution tightly coupled to minimize waste, is exactly what makes them so vulnerable to financially motivated disruption.39 When there is no buffer, a software outage becomes a shelf outage in days.

The present picture: deliberate, diversified, and increasingly nation-state

The sector is no longer suffering opportunistic spillover; it is being hunted. The clearest window into the current threat is the work of the Food and Agriculture Information Sharing and Analysis Center (Food and Ag-ISAC), whose annual reporting now quantifies a maturing adversary set. Its 2025 analysis identified 72 active threat actors targeting the sector — drawn from a pool of more than 330 monitored adversaries — scored through a "Predictive Adversary Scoring System" that weighs recency, frequency, sophistication and motivation.43 The geography is lopsided: Russia accounted for nearly 59.3% of observed adversary activity and China about 25.4%, reflecting both the ransomware ecosystems operating beyond Western law enforcement and state-backed operations pursuing agricultural research and intellectual property.43 The named actors span the spectrum — nation-state groups such as Lazarus, Moonstone Sleet, APT41 and APT18, versatile crews like Scattered Spider, and ransomware brands including Qilin, LockBit 5.0 and Akira.44

The raw ransomware volume keeps climbing. The Food and Ag-ISAC, with the IT-ISAC, tracked roughly 6,377 ransomware incidents across all sectors in 2025 — an 82% jump from 3,508 the prior year — of which 265 hit food and agriculture, about 4.2% of total volume.42 That continues a steady rise from 167 sector incidents in 2023 and 212 in 2024 (5.5% and 5.8% of volume respectively).40 Ransomware now represents on the order of 53% of all known cyber threats facing the industry.40 And the tradecraft is mostly unglamorous and preventable: roughly 90% of observed actor techniques rely on readily available tools or living-off-the-land methods, around 83% of attacks against organizations involved targeted spear-phishing, and about 80% involved custom malware or tooling.40

04 — Consequences

Risks & consequences, by type

The incident record points to a consequence profile that differs in emphasis from most other sectors. In financial services or healthcare, the headline harm is usually data — breach, exfiltration, privacy. In food and agriculture the headline harm is more often disruption: the inability to slaughter, mill, pack, chill or ship on a perishable clock. The Food and Ag-ISAC's own guidance makes the reorientation explicit — in this sector the primary risk is not data theft but disruption, and incident-response plans should treat operational continuity as the "north star," because a breach that stops sixty trucks has already defeated a data-recovery plan.48 The blast radius of a food breach is measured less in leaked records than in spoiled inventory and bare grocery shelves.48 With that framing, the consequence taxonomy resolves into six overlapping categories.

A — Operational & supply disruption

The dominant and best-documented harm: precautionary or forced shutdowns of production and distribution. JBS halted nine plants; Dole stopped North American production; cooperatives lost administrative function during harvest. Because the sector runs lean and perishable, downtime converts rapidly into spoilage, missed delivery windows and consumer-visible shortages — and the absence of slack is precisely what makes operators willing to pay.39

B — Food safety & cyber-physical harm

Where OT governs a physical process, a compromise can in principle degrade the safety of product or facility. Manipulated control logic could alter formulation, thermal processing or cold-chain integrity; corrupted application data could over- or under-dose chemicals in the field, with the documented potential to render farmland infertile.16 The convergence of food safety with worker safety is sharpest in ammonia refrigeration, where the controlled hazard is acutely toxic and flammable (see §2 and §5).18

C — Economic & market harm

Beyond the victim's own losses and ransom, sector incidents transmit into prices and markets. Shortages from a major processor or distributor move retail prices; disruptions to input cooperatives during planting threaten seed and fertilizer availability for an entire region's season.34 Because agricultural commodities, lending, crop insurance and futures are tightly interlinked, a sufficiently large physical disruption is also a financial event — a cross-sector channel taken up in §5.

D — Data, IP & strategic-research theft

The sector does hold prized data: proprietary platforms (the SoilMap source code BlackMatter claimed), agronomic and genetic research, breeding and crop-science intellectual property, and operational data on national production. Nation-state interest here is explicitly about degrading U.S. advantage and acquiring research and IP — a motive distinct from extortion and one that favors quiet, persistent access over noisy encryption.4143

E — Public-health & safety harm

At the extreme, the sector's HSPD-9 lineage is about catastrophic health effects — contamination or adulteration that reaches consumers. A cyber pathway to such harm is not the typical ransomware objective, but the directive's framing remains the worst-case bound: an open, interconnected food system whose compromise could carry catastrophic health and economic effects.6

F — National-security harm

Finally, the sector is now treated as a lifeline whose disruption is strategically valuable to an adversary. NSM-22's reframing toward strategic competition, and the documented pre-positioning of state actors on U.S. critical infrastructure (§3, §5), place food and agriculture inside the calculus of national resilience rather than ordinary commercial risk.8

The asymmetry that defines the sector

In most sectors, defenders optimize the classic confidentiality–integrity–availability triad with confidentiality often first among equals. Food and agriculture inverts the priority: availability and integrity of physical operations dominate, and the clock is perishable. An adversary does not need to steal anything to do catastrophic damage — stopping the line, during the wrong week, is enough. That is why ransomware, not espionage, is the sector's most common threat, and why "uptime-linked" controls and recovery should anchor its defensive posture.48

05 — Cross-sector cascade

Food & agriculture as a linchpin

No critical-infrastructure sector stands alone, but food and agriculture is unusually entangled — both dependent on, and depended upon by, the rest of the sixteen-sector lattice. CISA describes the sector's own external dependencies plainly: it relies on Water and Wastewater Systems for clean irrigation and process water, Transportation Systems to move product and livestock, Energy to power production and processing, Chemical for the fertilizers and pesticides crop production consumes, and Information Technology throughout.1 Add Communications (the connectivity precision agriculture rides on) and Financial Services (agricultural lending, crop insurance, commodity and futures markets), and the picture is of a sector wired into nearly half the national infrastructure base. A cyberattack rarely respects these boundaries; it follows the wiring.

Inbound dependencies: how other sectors break the food system

The food system inherits the fragility of everything it depends on. A cyber disruption to water and wastewater degrades both irrigation and the potable and process water that food manufacturing requires; an energy disruption stops processing lines and, critically, the refrigeration that keeps perishable product viable; a transportation disruption strands livestock and product in a system with no slack; and a chemical-sector disruption chokes the fertilizer and pesticide supply on which crop production depends. The interconnection is not theoretical at the adversary level either: state pre-positioning campaigns (below) have specifically compromised water and wastewater, energy, transportation and communications entities — the very lifelines the food sector rides.45

Outbound cascade: how the food system breaks others

The reverse flow is just as real. Three cascade channels are worth drawing out:

Chemical & emergency services — the ammonia channel. The cold chain's reliance on anhydrous ammonia means a food or cold-storage facility is also a chemical-hazard facility. A loss-of-containment event at an ammonia refrigeration system is a high-consequence incident: historically, ammonia-refrigeration failures at food-processing facilities have produced mass-casualty disasters, including factory fires in 2013 that killed 135 people.19 Whatever the cause, the control and monitoring layer governing that hazard is OT — the same class of system that intrusions target — which is why a food-sector cyber incident can become, in the worst case, a chemical-release event demanding an emergency-services response.18

Financial services — the market channel. Agriculture and finance are inseparable: production is financed by agricultural lending, hedged in futures, and insured through crop insurance, while commodity prices key off physical supply. A disruption large enough to move supply — a major processor offline, an input cooperative down at planting — is therefore also a market and credit event. The NotPetya and JBS episodes additionally exposed a second financial channel: the insurance system itself, where "war exclusion" disputes over state-attributed attacks left nine-figure losses contested for years.25

Information technology — the shared-software channel. The most efficient cascade is digital. Because so many agricultural operators depend on the same managed-service providers and software vendors, a single upstream compromise propagates to many downstream victims at once — exactly the dynamic in the HelloKitty/FiveHands incident, where one software vendor's breach seeded infections across multiple cooperatives.35 Here the IT sector is not a bystander to food-sector risk; it is the medium through which that risk spreads.

The strategic overlay: pre-positioning across lifelines

The cross-sector picture takes on a different cast once nation-state intent is added. In February 2024, CISA, the NSA, the FBI and international partners assessed that the People's Republic of China state-sponsored group tracked as Volt Typhoon was pre-positioning on U.S. IT networks — using living-off-the-land techniques to blend into normal activity — to enable disruptive or destructive attacks against critical infrastructure in the event of a major crisis or conflict.45 The confirmed footprint spanned communications, energy, transportation, and water and wastewater — the lifeline sectors on which food production depends — and the assessment was blunt that the activity was about positioning for disruption rather than mere espionage.46 Microsoft's parallel reporting documented the same hallmark tradecraft: near-exclusive reliance on built-in tools and hands-on-keyboard activity, with traffic routed through compromised small-office/home-office routers to evade detection.46 Food and agriculture's own threat reporting now mirrors this, attributing a quarter of observed adversary activity to China and identifying state groups whose interest is degrading U.S. capability and harvesting research.43 The cascade, in other words, is not only an accident waiting to happen — for some adversaries it is the objective.

06 — Mitigation

Frameworks, standards & defense

There is no single regime that "secures" food and agriculture; defense is assembled from general cybersecurity governance, OT-specific engineering standards, sector institutions and an evolving policy layer. The good news embedded in the threat data is that most attacks are not exotic — they ride preventable entry points like phishing, unpatched software and exposed remote access — which means foundational controls return disproportionate value.40 The structure below moves from governance to engineering to institutions to policy.

Governance frameworks

At the top sits the NIST Cybersecurity Framework, whose 2.0 revision added a sixth core function — Govern — alongside Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond and Recover, formally elevating cyber risk to an organizational governance concern rather than a purely technical one. For the OT-heavy parts of the sector, the indispensable companion is NIST Special Publication 800-82, the Guide to Operational Technology Security, whose Revision 3 (September 2023) was a substantial update: it aligns fully to CSF 2.0 (including the new Govern function), incorporates zero-trust principles, explicitly addresses OT-targeted ransomware, expands coverage of cloud-connected OT and IIoT, and treats supply-chain and third-party/remote-access risk as first-class concerns.21 SP 800-82 uses the Purdue model to describe recommended segmentation boundaries and access policies while acknowledging that modern cloud-connected architectures do not always map cleanly onto the hierarchy.21

Where SP 800-82 and CSF describe what to achieve, the ISA/IEC 62443 series (maintained jointly as ANSI/ISA-62443) describes how to structure an industrial-automation security program to get there, and the two are explicitly complementary.22 Its defining contributions are the zone-and-conduit model — grouping assets of similar criticality into security zones and controlling the conduits between them — and a four-level Security Level (SL 1–4) scheme that calibrates required protections to the capability of the adversary they must withstand, from casual or accidental exposure (SL 1) up to well-resourced, automation-knowledgeable state actors (SL 4).22 In practice these standards translate into a recognizable set of OT controls for a food plant: rigorous network segmentation separating engineering, operations and IT; whitelisting-based endpoint protection on HMIs and engineering workstations rather than traditional antivirus; firmware verification where controllers support it; and passive, protocol-aware network monitoring (Modbus, DNP3, EtherNet/IP, PROFINET) that observes without injecting traffic that could perturb deterministic control loops.22

Sector institutions: the information-sharing keystone

If one development has most improved the sector's defensive posture, it is the belated arrival of a credible information-sharing body. In May 2023, the food-and-agriculture special-interest group within the IT-ISAC was elevated into the standalone Food and Ag-ISAC, founded by major operators including PepsiCo, Tyson Foods, Cargill and Conagra.5250 Its creation filled a gap that experts had long called uniquely dangerous: a prior food-sector ISAC, launched in 2002, had collapsed by 2008 because members were reluctant to share with competitors and wary of antitrust exposure.51 What changed in the intervening years were new federal legal protections for sharing and the trust relationships built through the IT-ISAC, which made companies willing to try again.52 The Food and Ag-ISAC today supplies members with a queryable ransomware-tracker database, more than 330 adversary "attack playbooks" of tactics and techniques, the PASS adversary-scoring model, quarterly and annual threat reporting, and a vendor-neutral collaboration platform — and it has expanded through partnerships with industry associations to reach down toward farms and small operators.4243 Alongside it, USDA and FDA as co-SRMAs convene the Government and Sector Coordinating Councils, and CISA offers the sector free cyber-hygiene services (vulnerability and web-application scanning), a sector-specific cybersecurity factsheet and its cross-sector Cybersecurity Performance Goals as a baseline.1

Resources — a working reference shelf

For practitioners building or auditing a program in this sector, the authoritative primary sources cited throughout this piece are the place to start:

  • CISA — Food & Agriculture Sector page, cybersecurity factsheet, free Cyber Hygiene services, and the Cybersecurity Performance Goals.1
  • NIST SP 800-82r3, Guide to Operational Technology Security, and the NIST CSF 2.0 core.21

  • Food and Ag-ISAC annual threat reports and its SMB cybersecurity guide.42

The control set that actually moves the needle

Synthesizing the standards and the threat data, the highest-leverage controls for this sector are unglamorous and well within reach of even modest operators: multi-factor authentication across email, remote access and cloud applications; disciplined patching of internet-facing systems; tested, offline and air-gapped backups with continuity-of-operations plans rehearsed against an OT shutdown; network segmentation between IT and OT; endpoint detection and response in IT and protocol-aware passive monitoring in OT; rigorous third-party/MSP risk management given the sector's dependency; and software-supply-chain hygiene. Because so many intrusions begin with social engineering and credential abuse — and because adversaries increasingly impersonate staff to defeat help-desk and onboarding controls — identity verification deserves particular emphasis: verifying the human, not merely the credential, at the help desk and during remote onboarding.48

The policy layer

Policy is converging on the same conclusion from above. NSM-22's shift toward minimum requirements and accountability signals that the sector's long era of purely voluntary security is narrowing, and CIRCIA's reporting mandates will, once finalized, force a degree of incident visibility the sector has never had.910 Sector-specific legislation has advanced in parallel: the bipartisan Farm and Food Cybersecurity Act, first introduced in 2024 and reintroduced in 2025 (H.R.1604 / S.754), would direct USDA — in coordination with CISA — to conduct a biennial risk assessment of cybersecurity threats and vulnerabilities across the agriculture-and-food critical-infrastructure sector and to run an annual cross-sector crisis-simulation exercise for a food-related emergency or disruption.49 Provisions of the measure were folded into the House Agriculture Committee's 2024 farm-bill work, and it has drawn support from operational-technology and industry coalitions as a way to institutionalize coordination and resilience exercises the sector has historically lacked.49 CISA's national exercise program has, for its part, begun treating the sector as a primary scenario — the April 2024 Cyber Storm IX exercise centered on food and agriculture as a possible cyberattack target.3

07 — Open problems

Where research & innovation must go

For all the recent progress, the sector's defensive maturity lags its threat exposure, and several problems remain genuinely unsolved — technical, economic and institutional. These are the frontiers where research, investment and policy will determine whether the sector hardens faster than adversaries adapt.

1 — The long-lived OT problem

Agricultural and food-processing equipment has lifecycles measured in decades, not years. Combines, controllers and plant systems purchased a decade ago — and expected to run for another — frequently rely on outdated or unsupported operating systems, the unpatched Linux and Windows CE the John Deere disclosures laid bare.14 Patching is hard when downtime is unacceptable and when the vendor no longer ships updates. Industries with heavy legacy OT and industrial control systems are, for exactly this reason, perceived as easier ransomware targets.40 Bringing decades-old fleets to a defensible baseline — through compensating controls, segmentation and secure-by-design replacement — is a multi-year, capital-intensive undertaking with no shortcut.

2 — The small-operator long tail

The headline incidents involve household names, but ransomware crews increasingly use automated scanning that finds vulnerable systems regardless of size, and small and mid-sized operations are hit regularly.53 A five-figure theft from a family farm never reaches national news yet can destroy the business; a fifty-person processor with weak security is as exploitable as a national brand.53 Reaching this long tail — operators with no IT staff, thin margins and deep MSP dependence — is the sector's hardest distribution problem, and the reason the Food and Ag-ISAC's affordable, SMB-focused guidance and association partnerships matter as much as any high-end control — even as sector leaders acknowledge that many farms and small operators remain underprepared.4247

3 — The research and zero-day gap

Agricultural software and equipment are under-studied relative to the systems the security-research community usually scrutinizes — and a high proportion of zero-days observed against the sector reflects that the researchers who normally surface vulnerabilities in other software are not sufficiently focused on the software the food and agriculture community runs.41 The structural reason is partly economic: with per-device costs for some agricultural equipment in the high six and low seven figures, even well-funded security firms can scarcely buy a combine merely to test it.15 Closing this gap calls for shared testbeds, vendor cooperation, coordinated disclosure programs and academic partnerships of the kind only now beginning to form.

4 — Consolidation and single points of failure

The sector's chokepoints are not bugs to be patched; they are features of its industrial structure. When one cooperative plausibly underpins 40% of the grain industry's software, when a handful of meatpackers carry a quarter of beef supply, and when many operators share the same MSPs, the system's resilience is hostage to a few nodes.3126 Concentration is efficient and, in cyber terms, dangerous. Whether resilience can be engineered back into a consolidated system — through redundancy, interoperability and capacity buffers — is as much an industrial-policy question as a security one.

5 — Right-to-repair versus security-by-obscurity

The Deere episodes exposed a tension the sector has not resolved: manufacturers' digital locks are defended as security and contested as anti-repair, while the jailbreaks that restore farmers' control simultaneously reveal exploitable insecurity.14 A durable answer requires moving past security-through-obscurity toward genuinely defensible, owner-respecting architectures — equipment that is both repairable and secure. That is a design and standards problem the equipment industry has only begun to confront.

6 — Resilient positioning, navigation and timing

Precision agriculture's dependence on GNSS makes positioning a strategic single point of failure, and jamming hardware is cheap and accessible.13 Hardening the sector's PNT — through anti-spoofing receivers, multi-constellation and authenticated signals, sensor fusion and resilient timing — is an active research front whose urgency grows as autonomy spreads across the field.11

7 — AI and autonomy: new surface, new tooling

The next wave compounds the problem from both directions. The sector is rapidly adopting autonomous machinery and informal "vibe-coded" software even as it draws more than seventy active threat actors, and defenders must now contend with AI-enabled social engineering among the leading near-term predictions for the sector's threat landscape.48 AI is simultaneously a defensive opportunity — for anomaly detection across water and agricultural systems — and an offensive accelerant; getting the balance right, especially for resource-constrained operators, is unresolved.

8 — Insurance, attribution and the war-exclusion gap

The NotPetya litigation left a structural uncertainty unresolved: when a state-attributed attack inflicts collateral damage, who pays? The Mondelēz settlement set no binding precedent, and the market is still reconciling "war exclusion" language with the reality of nation-state cyber operations that hit private firms as bystanders.25 Until that is settled, the financial-resilience layer beneath the sector remains shakier than its operators assume.

9 — From voluntary to mandatory, without breaking the small operator

Finally, the regulatory trajectory itself is an open problem. NSM-22 and CIRCIA signal a shift from voluntary partnership toward minimum requirements and mandatory reporting, but the sector has no mandatory cybersecurity standard of its own, and the progression from voluntary guidance to enforced compliance is delicate in an industry of millions of uneven operators.910 Calibrating accountability so that it raises the floor without crushing the family farm — and so that requirements "continuously evolve to keep pace with the nation's risk environment," as NSM-22 demands — is the governance challenge of the coming decade.9

08 — Coda

The weakest digital link

The arc of this sector's security story is a transition from obscurity to target. For decades, food and agriculture was protected by being analog, dispersed and overlooked; its planners reasonably worried more about drought and disease than about adversaries on the network. The instrumentation that transformed the sector's productivity also transformed its exposure — and the threat actors arrived faster than the defenses. NotPetya proved a networked food company could absorb a state-grade blow as collateral; JBS proved a single ransomware payment could be a national supply event; the cooperative wave and the FBI's seasonal warning proved adversaries had learned the sector's calendar; Dole proved a software outage becomes a shelf outage in days; and the current threat reporting proves the sector is now deliberately hunted by both extortionists and nation-states.

The defensive lesson is bracingly ordinary. The attacks that hurt this sector mostly arrive through preventable doors — phishing, unpatched systems, exposed remote access, shared service providers — which means the foundational controls return outsized value, and the recent institutional progress, above all a credible information-sharing body and a modernizing policy framework, is closing the gap from the right end. What remains genuinely hard is structural: legacy OT measured in decades, a long tail of operators who cannot easily defend themselves, chokepoints baked into the industry's consolidation, and the dependencies that wire food and agriculture into half the national infrastructure base. The farm-to-fork chain at the head of this piece is only as strong as its weakest digital link — and in a perishable, just-in-time, consolidated system, that link is closer to the surface, and closer to the dinner table, than almost anywhere else in critical infrastructure.
